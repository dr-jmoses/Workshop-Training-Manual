Abstract

Colour and colour patterns seem to be especially important visual warning signals for predators, which might have innate or learned ability to avoid aposematic prey. To test the importance of larval colour pattern of the aposematic ladybird Harmonia axyridis (Pallas) (Coleoptera: Coccinellidae), an invasive alien species in Europe, we presented the plasticine models of aposematic larvae to wild and naïve birds. We studied the attacks on aposematic larvae of various patterns and colours in nature and in an outdoor aviary. The larvae were cryptic (green), aposematic (resembling those of the H. axyridis larvae), and semi‐aposematic (i.e., black but missing the typical orange patches of H. axyridis larvae). We detected attacks on 71 larvae out of 450 (i.e., 2.6% daily predation). Twenty‐nine attacks were made by birds, 37 by arthropods, and five by gastropods. Wild birds attacked green and black larvae significantly more often than aposematic larvae. Colour did not have an effect on attacks by arthropods. The experiment with naïve birds was conducted in an outdoor aviary, where naïve great tits, Parus major L., were offered the same artificial larvae as in the first experiment. In total, 57 of 90 exposed larvae were attacked by birds (i.e., 28% daily predation), and green larvae were attacked significantly more than the aposematic larvae (but not more than black larvae). Our results imply that aposematic larvae of H. axyridis are more than 12× less likely to be predated by birds than green larvae in nature. The aposematic pattern represented a more effective signal than the semi‐aposematic signal. The ability to reject aposematic prey seemed to be innate in our birds.

Introduction

The term aposematism is applied to an anti‐predatory strategy by which the prey signals its unprofitability to predators (Alatalo & Mappes, 1996). Aposematism involves an advertising (warning) signal such as conspicuous colouration, sounds, odours, or other perceivable characteristics (Rowe & Guilford, 1999). Colouration seems to be an especially important warning signal for mostly visually oriented predators and its importance and evolution has been studied using natural or artificial prey offered to naïve and wild‐caught birds (Lindström et al., 1999; Exnerová et al., 2007; Dolenska et al., 2009).

The aversion towards an aposematic prey can be innate (Guilford, 1988), or an inexperienced predator learns to avoid aposematic prey in a single or few trials (Engen et al., 1986; Fabricant & Smith, 2014). In previous studies, naïve juvenile great tits, Parus major L., did not hesitate before attacking an aposematic prey longer than before attacking non‐aposematic prey (Svádová et al., 2009). However, the birds showed an innate bias against aposematic prey, when a non‐aposematic (Lindström et al., 1999) or less conspicuous (Fabricant et al., 2014) alternative prey was present.

Ladybirds (Coleoptera: Coccinellidae) are a textbook example of aposematic insects. Most species possess chemical substances (alkaloids and pyrazines) providing protection against avian as well as invertebrate predators (Marples et al., 1989; Nedvěd et al., 2010; Pettersson, 2012). Adults, mainly in the tribes Coccinellini and Chilocorini, are brightly coloured often with contrasting red/black or yellow/black patterns (Escalona et al., 2017). Several species of ladybirds (Dolenska et al., 2009) and diverse colour morphs (Průchová et al., 2014) were shown to provoke avoidance in birds (great tit), although they differ in the level of toxicity (Brakefield, 1985; Marples et al., 1989). Only tree sparrow, Passer montanus (L.), consumed ladybirds without adverse reactions (Kristin, 1986; Veselý et al., 2017).

Larvae of ladybirds have entirely different body shape from adults. However, similar to adults they possess toxins (Hautier et al., 2011), and they are usually also aposematically coloured with dark (grey, black) and bright (yellow, orange, red) areas (Holloway et al., 1991; Nedvěd, 2015). Few, if any, experimental tests have demonstrated the anti‐predator protection of the developmental stages of ladybirds against vertebrates. Only a recent study showed that the predators – experienced P. major and naïve Gallus gallus domesticus (L.) – avoided both Harmonia axyridis (Pallas) larvae and pupae (Aslam et al., 2019) in winter cage experiments, whereas avoidance of adult H. axyridis was well known before (Marples et al., 1989; Dolenska et al., 2009; Průchová et al., 2014; Veselý et al., 2017). In contrast to our previous study, the current study focuses on the importance of the full and partial aposematism for naïve and experienced birds in the wild, for the first time.

Harmonia axyridis is an invasive alien species in Europe and many other parts of the world (Roy et al., 2016). Larvae of H. axyridis are quite large (up to 12 mm), grey to black with a pair of bright orange stripes on the abdomen (Nedvěd, 2015) and, hence, its aposematic signal is apparent to visually foraging predators from a distance. Harmonia axyridis exhibits a higher level of chemical protection compared to the common seven‐spot ladybird, Coccinella septempunctata L. (Nedvěd et al., 2010), and it appeared in many studies dealing with importance of aposematism. However, all existing studies focused on adult beetles only.

Ecological aspects of aposematic colouration are often studied using artificial prey made of various materials easy to colour. Artificial caterpillars from plasticine have extensive application in predation surveys (Howe et al., 2009; Roslin et al., 2017; Mrazova & Sam, 2018) and are proven to be useful in studies of aposematism and warning colouration too (Dattilo et al., 2016; França et al., 2017). In a study using artificial aposematic millipedes, warning colouration pattern involving yellow spots along dark plasticine body mediated avian predator avoidance (Iniesta et al., 2017). In a slightly different system, plasticine models of frogs were used to study aposematic and cryptic colouration on predation by birds in tropical forest (Noonan & Comeault, 2008).

Experimental studies with adult ladybird beetles were mostly concerned with the model interaction of a single prey and a single predator species in laboratory settings (Komarek, 1998). However, under natural situations, one prey species usually faces attacks from various predators. Predators vary with respect to their foraging ecology (diet preferences, foraging behaviour, microhabitat), sensory and cognitive abilities, as well as a possibility of risk related to devouring poisonous prey (Morris & Reader, 2016; Beneš & Veselý, 2017). All these features may have impact on the predation of aposematic prey in nature.

In our study, we used artificial larvae to answer simple, yet little explored questions, involving wild and naïve birds and their predation of cryptic vs. aposematically coloured larvae. Specifically, we addressed the following questions. (1) Do visually oriented predators (i.e., birds) attack conspicuously coloured artificial larvae (black) more than cryptic larvae (green), or does the black colouration serve as semi‐aposematic signal? (2) Are purely black larvae eaten more than aposematic (black and orange) larvae? (3) Do also invertebrate predators distinguish between the colour morphs? (4) How does the reaction of naïve and experienced birds to aposematic larvae differ? We predict that the black colour itself is sufficient to decrease predator attack (in contrast to attacks on green colour). We also predict that (5) the complete orange and black pattern will prevent predators from attacks significantly more than black colour only. If the ability of the birds to react to aposematic colouration is not innate, but birds need some earlier negative experience with the signal, we expect that naïve birds will not be deterred by black colour or orange and black pattern and will attack all larvae with similar intensity.

Materials and methods

Experimental area

We conducted our study at the former military area north‐west from Ceske Budejovice, Czech Republic (48°59′34″N, 14°26′39″E, 380 m a.s.l.) and in aviaries located within the campus of University of South Bohemia (48°58′36″N, 14°26′46″E). The landscape of the military area is mostly occupied by shrubs, with scattered trees. A dominant shrub species in the area, which we used for our field experiment as well as previous experiments conducted in this area (Mrazova & Sam, 2018), is grey willow (Salix cinerea L., Salicacaea).

Thirty individual shrubs were selected for the experiment within an area of about 12 ha. Each experimental shrub was tagged by a number for identification. Shrubs were at least 5 m from the nearest neighbour. The branches of each selected shrub with experimental leaves were at least 30 cm apart. Dummy preys exposed 30–50 cm apart were considered to be statistically independent in previous studies (Tvardikova & Novotny, 2012; Bereczki et al., 2014; Dattilo et al., 2016). However, to account for potential non‐independence, we nested the individual larvae into the tree in the statistical analyses. Only branches at 1.5–2.5 m high were used. The leaves of selected branches did not have any obvious signs of leaf damage or deformations caused by herbivores or pathogens. Great tits, blue tits [Cyanistes caeruleus (L.)], Eurasian blackcaps [Sylvia atricapilla (L.)], and fieldfares (Turdus pilaris L.) are among the most common insectivorous birds in this area (Mrazova & Sam, 2018).

Experimental design

Experiment 1 – wild birds

To study the response of the foraging predators to the artificial larvae, we used plasticine (Howe et al., 2009; Tvardikova & Novotny, 2012; Sam et al., 2015a) to make artificial larvae. We pressed modelling plasticine (Koh‐I‐Noor Hardtmuth, Ceske Budejovice, Czech Republic) through a syringe and made smooth‐surfaced cylinders (3 mm diameter, 12 mm long), which we then pressed laterally. We made 150 pieces of each of three colours: green, black, and aposematic (Figure 1). The cryptic artificial larvae were made of green plasticine, conspicuous larvae of black plasticine, and aposematic larvae of black plasticine with a pair of painted orange stripes (Univerzal SU2013 orange 7550 acrylic paint; Colorlak, Stare Mesto, Czech Republic) resembling larvae of H. axyridis commonly found on shrubs in Central Europe. We measured the spectral characteristics of experimental larvae and all used materials by USB4000‐XR1‐ES Ocean Optics Spectrometer (Ocean Insight, Stuttgart, Germany) and SpectraSuite Software (Ocean Insight) (Figures S1‐S4). We correlated and hierarchically clustered (based on the Euclidean distances) obtained data to document similarities in the spectral characteristics (Figure S5).

Artificial larvae were randomly exposed on the stalks of leaves of all selected shrubs with the help of entomological pins (Sam et al., 2015a) on Sunday 8 July 2018 between 10:00 and 11:00 hours. We placed five larvae of each type on each shrub (i.e., 15 larvae on one shrub and 450 larvae in total). On the 6th day of exposure between 10:00 and 11:00 hours, the artificial larvae were collected into plastic Petri dishes, labelled, and inspected for possible signs of attack from natural enemies by two authors independently, similar to previous studies (Posa et al., 2007; Tvardikova & Novotny, 2012; Low et al., 2014; Sam et al., 2015a,b; Roslin et al., 2017; Mrazova & Sam, 2018). We then discussed the three occasions where the identification of the attack disagreed. An artificial larva was considered attacked by a predator if it had some unequivocal bite marks distinctive for a specific predator group.

Experiment 2 – naïve birds

The second experiment was conducted in an outdoor aviary (10 × 15 m), where naïve great tits were offered the same artificial larvae as in the first experiment. The great tits were hand‐reared from 10 days of age; therefore, they did not have any previous experience with foraging in trees. Furthermore, they were not fed with natural food. They were provided with ad libitum water, sunflower seeds, a commercial dry mixture (proteins, trace elements, minerals, and vitamins), a fresh mixture of eggs and carrots, and live mealworms.

In total, 15 saplings (1‐1.2 m tall) of grey willow, planted in 60‐l buckets, were placed into the aviary on Friday 7 September 2018 at 14:00 hours. Twenty‐nine birds were released to the outdoor aviary. After 24 h, larvae were pinned to the potted grey willows and offered to the birds. On each of the 15 saplings, two green, two black, and two aposematic plasticine larvae were placed. The artificial caterpillars were collected on Monday 10 September 2018 at 10:00 hours and inspected for signs of attack from the birds.

Ethical note

To take nestlings of great tits, we obtained permission from the Environmental Department of Municipality in Ceske Budejovice, Czech Republic, and permission for laboratory experimentation with birds from the Czech Animal Welfare Commission. Each bird was ringed (under licence from the Czech Ringing Centre Praha, to principle investigator Katerina Sam and Petr Vesely) and released back to the locality of its origin within a few days after termination of the experiment. All the birds were released first from the small breeding cages to the outdoor aviary, where this experiment was conducted. Birds were allowed to acclimatize for 10 days after the experiment, and the doors of the aviary were kept open after that, so the birds could freely fly in and out to feeders placed inside and in the surrounding of the aviary.

Data analysis

Prior to analyses of the first experiment with wild birds, we excluded all lost larvae (2%) from the analysis, as their fate was unclear. After that, we calculated percentages of larvae attacked by all predators together, and by different predators separately. Percentages of caterpillars attacked at each sampling tree were arcsine transformed to meet conditions of normality. The effects of type of larva, predator, and their interaction were tested as linear model (R‐software, package nlme, lme function) with tree (1–30) as random effect, in which five larvae of each type were nested. Tukey’s post hoc test was performed to inspect differences between the three types of artificial larvae and predator taxa. We proceeded in a similar way with the data from the aviary where only naïve birds had access to larvae. Therefore, we tested the effect of type of larvae only. We ran a power analysis to find out the power of our statistical analyses, considering the sample size which we based on previous studies (Tvardikova & Novotny, 2012; Sam et al., 2015a,b; Mrazova & Sam, 2018). Test with the smallest recommended effect size (u = 2, v = 447, f2 = 0.02) resulted into a power of 78%. When we considered medium effect size (u = 2, v = 447, f2 = 0.15), the analyses reached the power of 100%.

Results

Experiment 1 – wild birds

After 6 days of exposure of a total of 450 artificial larvae, we found marks of 71 attacks (i.e., 15.8% of all the larvae were attacked which represents daily predation of 2.7% of larvae), and nine larvae were missing (i.e., 2% in total, or 0.35% daily loss). We identified that 29 attacks were made by birds, 37 attacks by arthropods, and five by gastropods. Number of attacks differed between the types of larva (F2,145 = 71.2, P = 0.001), but was not affected by the type of predator (bird vs. arthropods: F1,145 = 0.44, P = 0.51), nor by their interaction (F2,145 = 1.29, P = 0.28). Birds attacked more green (11.8% in 6 days; i.e., 2.1% daily) and black (9% in 6 days, 1.6% daily) larvae than aposematic ones (0.66% in 6 days, 0.11% daily) (adjusted P = 0.0015 and 0.026, respectively; black vs. green: P = 0.65; Figure 2). Predation by arthropods on different types of larvae did not differ (adjusted P = 0.17‐0.88; Figure 2).

Experiment 2 – naïve birds

In the second experiment with naïve, hand‐raised birds in an aviary, 57 of 90 exposed larvae were attacked by birds during the 3 days. Type of larva had an effect on predation (F2,74 = 8.11, P<0.001). Aposematic larvae were attacked less often than green larvae (Z = 4.03, P<0.001), but predation did not differ between aposematic and black larvae, nor between black and green larvae (both Z = 2.01, P = 0.11; Figure 3).

Discussion

Although protection via aposematism has been studied in adult ladybird beetles (Marples et al., 1989; Dolenska et al., 2009; Průchová et al., 2014; Veselý et al., 2017), our study is the first to extend this to ladybird larvae in the wild. In accordance with our predictions, we found that aposematically coloured larvae had about 18.7× lower daily predation by wild birds than green larvae. However, aposematic colouration did not protect larvae against arthropod predators, which daily attacked only 2× fewer aposematic than green larvae. Wild birds were somewhat more cautious when attacking black larvae in comparison to green larvae. We hypothesized that black larvae are more visible than green larvae. Therefore, we expected black larvae to be predated by birds more often, assuming that the black colouration does not represent a partial aposematic signal. Black larvae in our experiments were predated more often than aposematic larvae, but less than green larvae. This finding would imply that black colouration functions at least partially as an aposematic signal, as it was reported for some completely black insects (Brandley et al., 2016).

The daily predation observed on green larvae in our current experiment is comparable with the daily predation rate recorded on green caterpillars in the same region. Mrazova & Sam (2018) reported 4.3 ± 0.9% daily predation rate on green caterpillars assessed in July 2016. Our slightly lower daily predation might be due to the smaller size of the bait (1.2 vs. 2.5 cm), as birds usually prefer larger prey.

In the experiments in the wild, we unfortunately do not know which bird species attacked our larvae. Many birds of various species and ages occurred regularly in the study site. Bird species may react differently towards a warning signal of aposematic prey (Exnerova et al., 2003; Endler & Mappes, 2004). Even closely related species may have different impacts on populations of aposematic prey owing to specific foraging strategies (Brower, 1988) or may differ in the origin of avoidance (Exnerová et al., 2007). In our previous laboratory experiment, experienced great tits captured in winter often avoided larvae of H. axyridis (Aslam et al., 2019). The results of the current study confirm the avoidance of H. axyridis larvae by experienced wild birds during the breeding season, and in an aviary by naïve birds, and quantify the predation rate of aposematic larvae in comparison to less aposematic and cryptic larvae. In the same study (Aslam et al., 2019), tree sparrows attacked and ate moderate numbers of ladybird larvae. It was previously shown that P. montanus ate ladybirds without significant physiological problems, whereas cadavers of P. major from the same experiments showed evidence of severe liver damage (Kristin, 1986; Marples et al., 1989).

Our experiment with naïve, hand‐raised great tits implies that the ability to recognize unsuitable prey from the suitable prey may by innate in this species. Although our naïve birds attacked some of the aposematic larvae, the difference in predation of aposematic and green larvae was significantly lower. Our results thus contrasted with findings of Exnerová et al. (2007) who observed behaviour of wild and naïve hand‐reared birds of four bird species (blue tits, great tits, crested tits, and coal tits) to aposematic and non‐aposematic firebugs, both novel for them. The naïve great tits (used also in our experiment) and crested tits behaved differently than the wild‐caught conspecific adults; most of the wild‐caught birds avoided the aposematic firebugs, whereas the naïve birds usually did not show any initial avoidance and had to learn to avoid the aposematic prey. In contrast, behaviour of the naïve blue tits and coal tits was similar to that of the wild‐caught birds; most of them did not attack the firebugs. In another study, Exnerová et al. (2015) observed significant variation in predator behaviour toward aposematic prey in birds from Konnevesi (Finland) and from Prague (Czech Republic). Prague birds avoided attacking aposematic prey and Konnevesi birds were attacking both aposematic and palatable prey. There might be some behavioural differences between our (Ceske Budejovice) population and the Prague population of wild P. major. The overall high predation rate in our aviary experiment might have been affected by the length of the experiment and rather high density of the birds in there. Furthermore, naïve birds were fed mostly with sunflower seeds, which they had available at several places in the aviary to support their occupancy and search for food in a natural way. The artificial ladybird larvae look a little bit like sunflower seeds. A possibility that the naïve birds may not have avoided the aposematic larvae but just something that looked unfamiliar to them should also be considered. However, the green larvae were as unfamiliar as the aposematic larvae (birds were not fed with any green items in cages), and the birds often attacked them. Despites these potential problems, our data seem to imply that naïve great tit avoids the aposematic larvae.

Contrary to birds, arthropods attacking our larvae did not seem to distinguish the colouration of the artificial prey. Arthropods orient themselves mostly chemically. In real ladybird larvae, methoxypyrazines are likely responsible for protection of the attacked individuals (Moore et al., 1990; Daloze et al., 1994). In another laboratory experiment, we demonstrated the aversion of ants against extracts from adult ladybirds (M Aslam & O Nedvěd, unpubl.). In another field experiment, we offered dead crickets and ladybird adults and larvae to scavengers. Whereas most crickets disappeared within a few hours, the ladybirds remained mostly untouched (M. Aslam & O Nedvěd, unpubl.). In the present study, methoxypyrazines were not present in our models made of mineral salts and fatty compounds, which represent potentially attractive resource for arthropods.

Abstract

  1. In the last decade, artificial intelligence (AI) has increasingly been applied to help solve applied ecology problems. Partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs) are one such example. POMDPs have been applied in conservation, applied ecology and natural resource management to solve problems such as deciding when to stop managing or surveying threatened species that are difficult to detect. POMDP solvers are useful to find optimal sequential decisions under imperfect detection. However, POMDPs remain inaccessible to most applied ecologists.
  2. Here, we present the shiny r package smsPOMDP that solves the problem of ‘When to stop managing or surveying cryptic threatened species?’ (Chadès et al., 2008). We developed this package to address a common and challenging problem faced by conservation managers. It has broad applications for decision‐makers and ecologists by supporting them to focus efforts and resources where they are most likely to provide benefits to threatened species.
  3. Our r package smsPOMDP includes a set of functions that call a POMDP solver, allowing users to solve any stop, manage or survey problems that share the same structure as the original problem. Our Shiny r app also allows users to run simulations of optimal management and graphically represent the optimal solution. The smsPOMDP package and documentation are hosted at https://github.com/conservation‐decisions/smsPOMDP.
  4. In artificial intelligence, POMDPs are acknowledged as the Swiss army knife of decision models. However, POMDP's application in applied ecology remains seldom despite repeated evidence of their flexibility. Our package smsPOMDP is fast and provides an entry point to further develop POMDP apps, contributing to further uptake of AI research to solve ecological problems.

1 INTRODUCTION

Artificial intelligence (AI) provides a unique opportunity to fast track applied ecology research. AI is a research field from Computer Science that aims to solve two types of problems: perception problems (e.g. image recognition and sound recognition) and control problems (e.g. decision‐making under uncertainty). While the use of AI tools for perception problems in ecology is developing at a fast rate (Christin et al., 2019; Norouzzadeh et al., 2018; Torney et al., 2019), AI tools for decision problems have not encountered the same success (Marescot et al., 2013; Nicol et al., 2015; Polasky et al., 2011). Of particular interest to us, partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs; Smallwood & Sondik, 1973; Sondik, 1978) have shown great potential to help guide management under uncertainty in conservation, applied ecology and natural resource management. For example, POMDPs have been used to inform management and monitoring of cryptic species such as the critically endangered Sumatran Tigers (Chadès et al., 2008; McDonald‐Madden et al., 2011), or to optimally manage invasive plants that are difficult to detect (Regan et al., 2011; Rout et al., 2014). POMDPs have also been employed to adaptively manage migratory shorebirds threatened by the uncertain consequences of sea level rise (Nicol et al., 2013, 2015), or more recently, to avoid over‐exploitation of fisheries (Memarzadeh & Boettiger, 2018, 2019).

Despite their demonstrated usefulness, on‐ground applications of POMDPs remain seldom in applied ecology. More generally, several reasons can be envisaged for the lack of on‐ground applications of POMDPs. First, POMDPs deal with making decisions over time under imperfect detection, a concept that can be difficult to grasp. Contrary to its completely observable counterpart Markov decision processes (MDPs), there is no primer on POMDPs for ecologists (Marescot et al., 2013), but see Kaelbling et al. (1998) for an AI primer. Knowing and modelling an ecological problem as a POMDP is therefore the first hurdle to overcome. Second, once a problem is formulated as a POMDP, solving a POMDP efficiently requires using state‐of‐the‐art AI algorithms such as sarsop (Kurniawati et al., 2008). While these algorithms often have a toolbox, their usage may require advanced skills. Third, POMDP solutions are difficult to represent, interpret and explain (Dujardin et al., 2015, 2017). Fourth, like many academic fields, interactions between end‐users and researchers are often lacking limiting the potential impact of research.

In the state of New South Wales (NSW), Australia, there are almost 1,000 threatened species and communities. With the NSW threatened species programme—Saving our Species—we identified the opportunity to apply POMDP conservation research and applications to support their allocation of resources to these species. Conservation managers are faced with decisions everyday about where to focus their limited resources to protect species, but with limited tools to support decision‐making. Maximising the number of threatened species being conserved requires consideration of the resources needed to manage a species, the likelihood of success of management actions and detectability. Our r package (R Core Team, 2020), named smsPOMDP, stems from collaboration with the NSW Saving our Species programme and aims to address this issue by providing a tool that directly addresses a common conservation problem. We chose to implement our package in r, because it is the programming language primarily used by conservation ecologists, and hope that we can also contribute to the uptake of AI tools in ecology.

2 METHODS

2.1 Problem formulation

The problem we are focusing on is a problem of resource allocation: at each time step, managers allocate their budget to keep a population of threatened species extant. However, threatened species are difficult to detect due to their low numbers. Managers are prone to two types of risks: they could be managing locally extinct species or giving up too soon on extant species (Chadès et al., 2008). Managers need informed guidance to allocate resources and decide when to stop, manage or survey, denoted here as the SMS problem.

We present a formulation of the SMS problem as a POMDP. A Markov decision process (MDP) is defined as a time‐discrete stochastic decision model satisfying the Markov property. POMDPs are the generalisation of MDPs, where a manager only has a partial knowledge of the current state of the system. POMDPs are a convenient tool to deal with trade‐offs between decisions to gain rewards and decisions to gain information (Memarzadeh & Pozzi, 2016; Sigaud & Buffet, 2008). Consequently, the value function can be represented by a finite set of ‐dimensionnal vectors called α‐vectors. The value function can compactly be reformulated using α‐vectors Because to each α‐vector corresponds an action , the optimal policy can be expressed as a policy function with the index of the optimal α‐vector.

Conveniently, α‐vectors help representing the optimal policy as a policy graph. A policy graph is a directed graph with |Γ| nodes (each node corresponds to one α‐vector, thus to one action) and at most |Z|*|Γ| edges (each edge corresponds to an observation, e.g. seen or not seen, |Z| is the size of the observation set). One can easily grasp that larger graphs are more difficult to interpret for human decision‐makers. For example, assuming the default parameters of the sms package, the solution has |Γ| = 13 and 26 links (Figure 2a). Fortunately, the same solution can be represented with three nodes and four links (Figure 2b, Chadès et al., 2008). However, adding states, actions or observations changes the structure of the optimal policy, and render interpretation challenging (interested readers can refer to Regan et al., 2011; Rout et al., 2014 for examples). Automatically building interpretable POMDP solutions is an active area of research in AI (e.g. Dujardin et al., 2015; Dujardin et al., 2017).

2.2 The sarsop algorithm

Solving exactly a POMDP is a computationally challenging problem. Indeed, finite horizon POMDPs are PSPACE‐complete (Papadimitriou & Tsitsiklis, 1987), and infinite‐horizon POMDPs are undecidable (Madani et al., 1999). However, advances in AI have allowed us to use efficient algorithms such as sarsop (successive approximations of the reachable space under optimal policies; Kurniawati et al., 2008). sarsop is a point‐based POMDP algorithm (Pineau et al., 2003; Shani et al., 2013) which focuses on finding an approximate belief space reachable under an optimal policy from a given starting belief point and solving the problem only for the identified reachable belief space. sarsop has been applied successfully to many applications, for example, robotics (Ong et al., 2010), natural resource management (Memarzadeh & Boettiger, 2018, 2019), conservation (Chadès et al., 2012) and invasive species management (Péron et al., 2017). Despite its demonstrated efficiency in different domains, sarsop has not been used widely most likely because sarsop requires advanced computing skills—the algorithm is encoded in C++. Here, we build on an existing sarsop r wrapper (https://github.com/boettiger‐lab/sarsop) to call sarsop from r (Boettiger et al., 2018).

2.3 The smsPOMDP r package

Our r package smsPOMDP provides a set of functions (Supporting Information 1) to help solve the SMS problem with a Shiny r app (Chang et al., 2018). Shiny is an r package that enables the user to build interactive web applications with r. We provide guidance on building a shiny app to solve POMDPs in the Supporting Information 2. The smsPOMDP package and documentation are hosted at https://github.com/conservation‐decisions/smsPOMDP. The online version of the app is also available at https://conservation‐decisions.shinyapps.io/smsPOMDP/.

Figure 3 presents the flowchart illustrating the different functionalities of the app. The user starts by setting the parameters of the problem—default values corresponds to the original Sumatran Tiger problem (Figure 3a). Once the parameters of the problem are provided, the user interface of the app provides interactive graphs (Figure 3b). The user can then set a history of past actions and observations (Figure 3c). Next, the app solves the POMDP (Figure 3d), derives the optimal policy graph from the user's inputs and runs several simulations of management (Figure 3e). The interactive plots are updated consequently (Figure 3f).

3.1 Decision graph of the optimal policy

The app solves the POMDP and provides the decision diagram (Figure 2b), assuming that the starting belief corresponds to the species known to be extant (b0 = [1 0]). Figure 2b recommends to start managing the species for 10 years in absence of detection. If the species is not observed during this period, it is recommended to survey the species for 2 years, before surrendering in absence of detection. If the species is observed at any time, the optimal strategy would be to manage the species for 10 years and so on.

3.2 Setting a history of past actions and observations

Our app allows managers to provide a history of past actions and observations. This information can be used to update the current belief state and the optimal strategy to implement. Using this information, the app solves a POMDP and returns a decision graph adapted to the collected information. Figure 4e,f illustrate how to add a history of past actions and observations (2 years of managing, then 2 years of surveying and then stop, without detecting the species). The optimal strategy now recommends to keep surveying for 1 year, and then stop in absence of detection. If the species is detected at any time, then the belief that the species is extant is updated to 1 and the optimal strategy resets to Figure 2b.

3.3 Difference in performance between POMDP optimal policies and other policies

Our app offers the possibility to compare the difference in performance between the POMDP optimal policies and user‐derived decisions by calculating the predicted difference benefit of using optimal policies on the belief state of the species (Figure 4f) and on the expected rewards over time (Figure 4g).

For the first two time steps (t = −5 and t = −4), the user has implemented ‘manage’ while the species was not seen. This is analogous to the optimal policy; therefore, the user's belief state fits the optimal trajectory (red and blue lines on Figure 4f). For time steps t = −3 to −1, the actions of the user are no longer optimal: 2 years of ‘survey’ and 1 year of ‘stop’, while the optimal policy recommends ‘manage’ according to Figure 2b. During this period, both the user's belief and expected rewards decline drastically. In the future, even if the user applies the optimal trajectory, the belief state stays under the optimal trajectory (0.41 at the 10th time step for the optimal and 0.06 for the user's optimal future trajectory). We have the same conclusion for the expected rewards ($32.9 at the 10th time step for the optimal trajectory and $4.4 for the user's optimal future trajectory).

4 DISCUSSION

We present the smsPOMDP package to solve the POMDP problem: When to stop managing or surveying cryptic threatened species? This is the first POMDP web application designed to solve a conservation problem. An increasing number of conservation problems have been solved using POMDPs such as managing invasive species under imperfect detection (Regan et al., 2011; Rout et al., 2014), the management of multiple or meta populations (Chadès et al., 2011; McDonald‐Madden et al., 2011) and adaptive management problems in conservation and natural resource management (Memarzadeh & Boettiger, 2018, 2019). We hope that our package will inspire others to provide web applications such as ours as part of their research outcomes. In particular, web applications could be designed to solve POMDP problems with a higher number of states and/or actions (seeRegan et al., 2011; Rout et al., 2014), and non‐stationary dynamics (see Nicol et al., 2013, 2015).

While programming in r comes at an additional cost to computer scientists, it is a prevalent programming language in applied ecology. The dependence on r as a programming language is a barrier to uptake of AI tools that are rarely developed with r, but see MDPToolbox for an exception (Chadès et al., 2014). Here, we were able to overcome this issue using the r sarsop wrapper (Boettiger et al., 2018). Through the development of smsPOMDP, we have shown that it is possible to build a shiny r web applications when solving a POMDP problem. Here, we have chosen to interactively solve ‘manage, survey or stop’ allowing users to change parameters for their case study. This was possible due to the structure and simplicity of the optimal POMDP solution (Figure 2b) smsPOMDP calls the POMDP solver, sarsop, and provides instant recommendations. However, many POMDP problems require more computational time to be solved. In this case, we recommend solving POMDP problems offline and building an app that allows simulations and representations of POMDP solutions. While this means less opportunities to analyse the influence of POMDP input parameters on the optimal solutions, it will still allow users to interact with a given solution and gain mechanistic insights into the best management options.

The motivation for building this package stemmed from an identified need to provide a decision‐making tool to support the Saving our Species programme to decide when to invest in threatened species that are difficult to detect. Future work will identify which POMDP parameters are difficult to populate (Table 1, e.g. economic value) and investigate adequate solutions (Pandit et al., 2015; Subroy et al., 2019). Further testing with ecologists and managers using New South Wales species data will also be used to identify opportunities for future development, for example using qualitative assessment when estimation of parameter values is impossible. A promising application of the SMS problem could be at the evaluation and reporting stage of conservation programmes on species recovery.

Through the development of this package, we have learned that gaining an understanding of the users' need and interactions improved the app over time. Designing fit for purpose web app is in itself a research field that requires acquiring new skills. This is an added challenge for scientists that aim to achieve on ground impact. Finally, we hope that this package will contribute and encourage the uptake of AI tools in applied ecology more broadly.

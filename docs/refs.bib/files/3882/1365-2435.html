Summary

  1. Plant leaf litter comprises the major common source of energy and nutrients in forested soil and freshwater ecosystems world‐wide. However, despite the similarity of physical and biochemical processes, generalizations across aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems regarding litter decomposition drivers remain elusive.
  2. We re‐analysed data from a published field decomposition experiment conducted in two ecosystems (forest floors and streams) across five biomes (from the tropics to subarctic) with increasing decomposer community complexity (microbes, microbes and mesofauna, microbes and meso‐ and macrofauna).
  3. Using a wide litter quality gradient (15 litter combinations), we aimed to disentangle the roles of decomposer community complexity from that of leaf litter traits (18 traits encompassing four broad trait categories: nutrients, C quality, physical structure and stoichiometry) on litter C and N loss. Comparisons of decomposition drivers between ecosystems were evaluated across and within biomes.
  4. Differences in environmental conditions (e.g. climate, soil/water fertility) and litter nutrients – with a particular focus on Mg and Ca – across biomes were the major drivers of litter C loss in both ecosystems, but decomposer complexity also played a prominent role in streams. Within biomes, we observed consistent effects of litter nutrients and stoichiometry on litter C and N loss between ecosystems, but the effects of decomposer complexity differed between streams and forest floors in the temperate, Mediterranean and tropical biomes.
  5. Our results highlight that, beyond the litter traits commonly identified as controlling decomposition (e.g. C, N and lignin), micronutrients (e.g. Mg and Ca) can also play an important, and globally consistent, role in both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. In addition, in forest streams the complexity of decomposer communities had similar importance as litter traits for predicting litter C and N turnover across all five biomes.
  6. The identification of common drivers in our large‐scale ecosystem comparison suggests a basis to develop common models across aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems for C and N dynamics during decomposition. Future modelling efforts should account for the global similarities (litter micronutrient effects) and biome‐level differences (contingent decomposer effects) found between ecosystems.

Introduction

Terrestrial plants produce annually ca. 120 billion tons of organic carbon (Beer et al. 2010), with only a small portion of this plant production being sequestered (Battle et al. 2000) or consumed by herbivores (Cebrián & Duarte 1995). As a result, up to 90% of the organic matter produced globally is recycled during decomposition (Cebrián 1999), highlighting the significance of this process in regulating the carbon (C) cycle and nutrient dynamics (Gessner et al. 2010). Leaf litter is the dominant common source of energy and nutrients for heterotrophic communities in forested soil and stream ecosystems, with streams playing a key role in transporting C and nutrients across the landscape (Gessner, Chauvet & Dobson 1999; Wallace et al. 1999). Despite these linkages and the substantial contribution of inland waters to the global C cycle (Battin et al. 2008, 2009), global decomposition models are biased towards terrestrial ecosystems (Cole et al. 2007). In addition, multisite experiments disentangling the factors controlling litter decomposition have been carried out independently in aquatic (Boyero et al. 2011; Woodward et al. 2012) and terrestrial (Parton et al. 2007; Wall et al. 2008; Makkonen et al. 2012) ecosystems. Therefore, large‐scale experimental investigations for understanding drivers of litter decomposition are needed in both ecosystem types, in order to examine whether commonalities exist, and to integrate across larger spatial scales as it is needed in predictive models.

Leaf litter decomposition in aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems shares common abiotic and biotic drivers. These include climate and ambient nutrient availabilities (Boyero et al. 2011; García‐Palacios et al. 2013), the structure and diversity of decomposer communities (bacteria, fungi and detritivorous invertebrates) (Heemsbergen et al. 2004; Frainer, McKie & Malmqvist 2014), and the quality traits of the leaf litter that falls both onto the forest floor and into streams (Enriquez, Duarte & Sand‐Jensen 1993; Cornwell et al. 2008). Environmental conditions can have direct (changes in moisture, temperature and nutrients) and indirect (mediated by changes in litter resource quality and decomposer phenotypes and/or community composition) effects on litter decomposition (Wardle et al. 2004). However, differences in the relative importance of these drivers between ecosystems are likely, due to the contrasting spatial and temporal scales at which organic matter is decomposed in different habitats (Wagener, Oswood & Schimel 1998). Direct environmental effects on litter decomposition may be stronger in terrestrial than in aquatic ecosystems, given that both vertical (i.e. above‐ground–below‐ground) and horizontal environmental heterogeneity are generally greater in soils than in freshwater ecosystems (Gessner et al. 2010). On the other hand, in aquatic ecosystems the constant water flow and continuous nutrient supply from upstream results in decomposition occurring over shorter periods of time than in terrestrial ecosystems (Wagener, Oswood & Schimel 1998). Thus, the proportion of environmental effects on litter decomposition indirectly mediated by litter quality and decomposers might be greater in streams than in forest floors.

Biome‐specific characteristics might also affect the relative importance of both biotic and abiotic drivers for litter decomposition in aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. At lower latitudes, the role of invertebrate decomposers may be less important in aquatic than in terrestrial ecosystems, as litter decomposition in equatorial lowland streams appears to be mostly driven by microbial decomposition (Boyero et al. 2011; Bruder et al. 2014). This reflects a lack of a true detritivore fauna in many tropical streams, although exceptions to this general pattern exist (Irons et al. 1994). In contrast, in terrestrial systems the relative impact of soil fauna increases with increasing mean annual temperature (García‐Palacios et al. 2013). When comparing the importance of litter quality for decomposition between aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems, further differences may arise among biomes. For example, the effects of litter quality on terrestrial decomposition in colder biomes (e.g. boreal or subarctic) are potentially overwhelmed by extreme microclimatic conditions (Aerts 2006), an effect less likely to occur in the more buffered thermal environments of stream ecosystems (Gessner et al. 2010). In contrast, the relative importance of litter quality may be similar between ecosystems in warmer climates (e.g. Mediterranean or tropical).

The lack of a global‐scale comparison of how litter quality and decomposer communities drive litter decomposition in aquatic vs. terrestrial ecosystems has precluded the development of common models across these systems for C and nitrogen (N) dynamics during decomposition. In addition, for logistical reasons, most large‐scale decomposition experiments have addressed the influence of a reduced number of litter quality levels (Wall et al. 2008; Woodward et al. 2012). Here, we re‐analysed previously published data (Makkonen et al. 2012; Handa et al. 2014) from a unique global litter decomposition study (five biomes, from the tropics to subarctic) including a broad range of litter qualities (15 litter combinations) and conducted in both aquatic (forest stream) and terrestrial (forest floor) sites in each biome. Using structural equation modelling, we investigated how variation in 18 litter traits encompassing four broad trait categories (macro‐ and micronutrients, C quality, physical structure and stoichiometry), and increasing complexity of decomposer communities (achieved by regulating access to the litter by organisms of different size classes, from micro‐organisms to macrofauna) drive losses of litter C and N during decomposition. Our focus on C and N loss, rather than litter mass loss, facilitates the interpretation of our results within a broader biogeochemical framework (Wickings et al. 2012). We tested the following hypotheses: (i) litter C and N loss across biomes will be controlled by the same hierarchy of drivers in both streams and forest floors, (ii) the portion of biome effects on litter C and N loss modulated by litter traits will be greater in aquatic ecosystems. In addition, when comparing both ecosystems within each biome, (iii) the relative importance of litter traits for C and N loss will be more similar between ecosystems in the warmer biomes (Mediterranean and tropical) than in the colder part of the gradient, but (iv) the role of decomposer community complexity will be lower in aquatic than in terrestrial ecosystems towards the equator.

Materials and methods

Study sites, experimental design and field litter incubations

The data we analysed were collected as part of a global litter decomposition experiment (Handa et al. 2014) conducted in forests of five different biomes encompassing a large latitudinal gradient: subarctic, boreal, temperate, Mediterranean and tropical (Appendix S1, Supporting information). One aquatic (stream) and one terrestrial (forest floor) study site was selected in each biome. Site‐specific, native leaf litter was collected in each biome in 2007, and included woody species belonging to four different plant functional types: N‐fixing, rapidly decomposing deciduous plant, slowly decomposing deciduous plant, and broadleaved evergreen (Appendix S2, Supporting information). The selected species encompassed wide variation in litter quality within biomes, allowing investigation of a broad gradient in litter traits, while the categorization of these species into plant functional types facilitated identification of common mechanisms across ecosystem types and biomes. We investigated the decomposition of the four functional litter types in isolation and in mixtures containing all possible litter combinations, resulting in a total of 15 litter treatments (four single species and 11 species mixtures). Litter mixtures were included to increase the spread in trait and decomposability variability within the litter quality gradient, beyond that established with the four litter monocultures. This aspect is fundamental in large‐scale decomposition studies to: (i) represent realistic litter layers, as litter in undisturbed forest floors and streams normally consists of multiple species, which in turn can drive interactions among microbes and detritivores (Gessner et al. 2010); (ii) increase the litter quality range used to predict litter decomposition (Parton et al. 2007).

Three increasingly functionally complex decomposer communities were included as an additional treatment at all sites by constructing field microcosms of varying mesh size that regulated access to the litter for decomposers of different size classes. Coarse mesh microcosms (5 mm) allowed access to microbes, mesofauna and macrofauna. Medium mesh microcosms (1 mm) excluded macrofauna, while fine mesh microcosms excluded both meso‐ and macrofauna. The size of the fine mesh differed between forest floor (50 μm) and stream (250 μm) ecosystems, in line with differences in definition of the components of the decomposer food web in the two ecosystems. Stream microcosms were constructed in a tetrahedral shape (17 cm × 25 cm), attached to chains and anchored to iron bars. Forest floor microcosms were made of cylindrical polyethylene (height 10 cm, ø 15 cm), with two windows (5 × 18 cm) on the sides of the cylinder covered with the appropriate mesh size. The top and bottom of forest floor microcosms were covered with 50 μm mesh, and microcosms were slightly inserted into the top soil (to a depth of 1·5 cm) to promote an intimate contact of the litter with the soil surface.

These treatments were replicated five times in a randomized block design within each ecosystem type, and across all five biomes, yielding a total of 2250 field microcosms (15 litter treatments × 3 decomposer treatments × 5 blocks × 2 ecosystem types × 5 biomes). Exposure time differed across biomes and ecosystems (terrestrial: 278–714 days, aquatic: 48–97 days) to account for different leaf litter decay rates. We aimed at retrieving microcosms when the rapidly decomposing deciduous litter had reached 40–50% remaining mass at each site. This procedure allowed an assessment of litter C and N dynamics at a comparable stage in the decomposition process, across ecosystem types and biomes (Wall et al. 2008; Frainer, McKie & Malmqvist 2014; Handa et al. 2014), but precluded evaluation of temporal dynamics in litter decomposition drivers, which is beyond the scope of our study. While we retrieved all litter treatments based on the remaining mass of the fastest decomposing species at each site, the mass loss of the other 14 litter treatments contrasted strongly, driving variation within sites. A full description of the experimental design is given in Appendix S3 (Supporting information).

Measurements of the initial leaf litter traits and litter C and N loss

Chemical and physical litter trait data were determined in an earlier study conducted at the same sites and using the same litter material (Makkonen et al. 2012). These data included leaf litter concentrations of N, P, K, Ca, Mg, Na, C fractions (C, hemicellulose, cellulose, lignin and water‐soluble compounds), total phenols, soluble phenols, condensed tannins, leaf toughness, water saturation, specific leaf area (SLA) and leaf three‐dimensionality. We also calculated several litter stoichiometry ratios, including ratios of lignin‐, C‐, total phenols‐ and condensed tannins to N and P. A community‐weighted mean (CWM) approach (Garnier et al. 2004) was used to determine the trait values of litter mixtures. Litter C and N loss data were obtained from Handa et al. (2014) and calculated as follows. At retrieval, all litter was sorted by species, oven dried at 65°C to constant weight, weighed to calculate litter dry mass and ground with a ball mill. Subsamples of 3 mg were analysed for C and N concentrations. Litter C and N loss (%) during field incubation was calculated for every treatment as 100 × [(Mi × CNi) ‒ (Mf × CNf)]/(Mi × CNi), where Mi and Mf are the initial and final litter dry mass, respectively, and CNi and CNf are the initial and final C or N concentration (% of litter dry mass).

Analytical procedures

We used structural equation modelling (SEM), an analytical procedure ideal for partitioning complex relationships in multivariate data sets (Shipley 2002), to disentangle linkages between litter traits and increasing complexity of decomposer communities (from micro‐organisms to macrofauna) as drivers of litter C and N loss. We constructed two sets of SEM models. Firstly, we compared differences between ecosystem types across biomes, to assess whether similarities in the hierarchy of drivers controlling litter decomposition occur at global scales. Secondly, we compared streams and forest floors at the biome level, to test for regional/biome contingencies in the relative importance of different drivers between ecosystems.

Before building the models, we reduced the dimensionality of the litter trait data set by generating multivariate axes of trait variability suitable for use in SEM. We first grouped all the measured litter traits into four matrices, representing broad litter trait categories known to exert strong control on decomposition: (i) nutrients (micro‐ and macronutrients: N, P, K, Ca, Mg and Na; Makkonen et al. 2012), (ii) C quality (C, lignin, hemicellulose, cellulose, water‐soluble compounds, total phenols, soluble phenols and condensed tannins; Hättenschwiler & Bracht‐Jørgensen 2010), (iii) leaf physical structure (toughness, water saturation, SLA and three‐dimensionality; Cornelissen & Thompson 1997) and (iv) stoichiometry (lignin:N, C:N, total phenols:N and condensed tannins:N ratios, and lignin:P, C:P, total phenols:P and condensed tannins:P ratios; Talbot & Treseder 2012;). Principal components analysis (PCA) was then used to extract a reduced number of variables (multivariate axes) capturing most of the variance in these trait categories across the 15 litter combinations in each biome, reducing redundancy among suites of correlated traits. Before conducting the PCA, we checked for collinearity between litter traits using the variance inflation factor (VIF) according to Kutner, Nachtsheim & Neter (2004). Only PCA axes with eigenvalues >1 were retained to represent the effects of each of the four trait categories on litter C and N loss in the SEM (Quinn & Keough 2002).

For the cross‐biome analysis, we proposed an a priori model of hypothesized relationships among predictor (biome, litter traits and decomposer community complexity) and response (litter C and N loss) variables within a path diagram (Fig. 1). The model structure proposed was based on current concepts of the decomposition process, allowing a causal interpretation of the model outputs (Shipley 2002). However, SEMs are contingent on the structure imposed by the modellers (Shipley 2002); hence, our model is only valid for the type of relations introduced, and does not exclude the possibility of other types of relationships, such as litter traits modulating the effects of decomposers on C and N loss.

We tested the fit of the a priori model against the field data. The same a priori model was hypothesized for both streams and forest floors (Fig. 1). The overall influences of ‘biome’ (five levels) and ‘decom’ (decomposer community complexity, three levels) on litter C and N loss were modelled as exogenous categorical effects using dummy variables (Grace 2006). The influence of biome on litter C and N loss was evaluated with both direct (variation attributable to non‐measured environmental parameters such as climate, soil/water fertility or other site‐specific characteristics; Parton et al. 2007) and indirect (variation attributable to changes in the composition of litter traits among the species studied in each biome; de Deyn, Cornelissen & Bardgett (2008)) relationships. As the total effects are the sum of direct plus indirect effects (Grace 2006), the percentage of total absolute effects indirectly mediated by litter traits can be calculated. To increase degrees of freedom, any path with a coefficient <0·10 was removed from the model when not significant, based on a chi‐square goodness. The RMSEA (root mean square error of approximation) index was also considered due to its greater robustness to small sample size (Grace 2006). In addition, and because some variables were not normally distributed, we confirmed the fit of the model using the Bollen–Stine bootstrap test (Schermelleh‐Engel, Moosbrugger & Müller 2003). See Appendix S3 for further explanations.

For the biome‐level ecosystem comparisons, we hypothesized an additional a priori model (Appendix S4, Supporting information) and tested it as 10 different groups (five biomes times two ecosystems) with SEM multigroup analysis (Byrne 2004). The multigroup analysis tests whether the value of a particular path coefficient differs significantly between the stream and the forest floor model, to investigate whether the biotic drivers of decomposition differed among ecosystems in each of the five biomes evaluated (see Appendix S3 for further explanations). Due to sample size restrictions, we only focused on the litter traits that accounted for a larger litter C and N loss variation in the cross‐biome models (e.g. litter nutrients and stoichiometry). All SEM analyses were performed with amos 22.0 (Amos Development Co., Armonk, NY, USA).

Results

Litter trait distribution among biomes

The PCA showed a marked difference among biomes in all four categories of litter traits evaluated (Fig. 2; see Appendix S2 for mean litter traits per species). In all cases, the first principal component explained more than 50% of the variance and was the only component with an eigenvalue >1. Accordingly, only component 1 from each PCA was kept for further analyses. Component 1 of the litter nutrients PCA was mainly associated with increasing Mg and Ca concentrations, which were higher in the subarctic and lower in the tropical litter combinations, with the other biomes intermediate (Fig. 2a). The biomes were also distributed from the subarctic to the tropical along component 1 of the litter C quality PCA, which was positively related with lignin and C concentrations, but negatively related with the concentration of cellulose (Fig. 2b). Component 1 of the litter stoichiometry PCA was characterized by increasing C:N and lignin:N ratios from the temperate to the subarctic biome (Fig. 2c). The litter physical PCA was positively related with leaf toughness but negatively associated with SLA. This PCA also described variation across biomes, with tropical species characterized by greater leaf toughness and lower SLA, in contrast to the boreal species (Fig. 2d).

The relative importance of litter traits and decomposer community complexity across biomes

The two structural equation models constructed separately for each ecosystem type and pooled across all biomes had good data‐fit, and explained about 50% of the variance in C and N loss in streams and about 75% in forest floors (Fig. 3). In both ecosystems, biome (r = 0·51 in streams and r = 0·78 in forest floors) and litter nutrients (r = 0·46 in streams and r = 0·62 in forest floors) accounted for most variation in C loss. We found the highest C loss in subarctic streams and temperate forest floors. In the Mediterranean biome, N loss was especially low in streams, whereas it peaked in the tropical and temperate forest floors (Appendices S5 and S6, Supporting information).

Litter nutrients were positively related to C and N loss in both ecosystems, indicating faster C and N release with higher litter Mg and Ca concentrations, that is the two micronutrients accounting for most of the variance among biomes (Fig. 2a). However, component 1 of the litter nutrients PCA was also positively correlated with P, K and N (ρ = 0·67, 0·57 and 0·35, respectively, Appendix S7, Supporting information), which consequently may also have contributed to the positive link with C and N loss. Litter stoichiometry was negatively related with C loss in the forest floor model and with N loss in both ecosystems, which indicates more rapid C and N release when litter C:N and lignin:N ratios are lower. This runs counter to the positive influence of litter C quality (correlated with C and lignin concentrations) on N loss found in the forest floor model, suggesting that effects of stoichiometric ratios on litter decomposition are driven by litter N concentrations rather than differences in litter C quality. Litter physical traits had no significant influence on litter C and N loss in streams, and played a minor but significant role in forest floors. In the stream model, the positive effects of increasing decomposer community complexity on C (r = 0·41) and N (r = 0·34) loss were as important as that of biome and litter traits, whereas decomposer effects were considerably smaller in the terrestrial model (C: r = 0·10, N: non‐significant).

When partitioning the total biome effects into direct and indirect effects (Fig. 4), we found that litter traits represented a larger part of the absolute total biome effect in streams (42 and 31% for C and N loss, respectively) than in forest floors (20 and 23% for C and N loss, respectively). This was due to the similar magnitude in biome direct and indirect effects in streams (Fig. 4). In contrast, direct biome effects on C and N loss in forest floors were considerably higher than the indirect effects mediated through changes in trait composition.

The relative importance of litter traits and decomposer community complexity within biomes

The multigroup SEM analysis on individual ecosystems and biomes indicated high similarity in the effects of litter nutrients and stoichiometry on C and N loss between streams and forest floors across all biomes excepting the subarctic (Table 1). The consistent influence of litter nutrients on both C and N loss was positive, whereas that of litter stoichiometry was negative (Table 2). The positive link between decomposer complexity and C loss was similar between ecosystems in the subarctic and boreal biomes, as P > 0·05 in the multigroup comparisons (Table 1). However, the strength of this relationship was significantly higher in streams than in forest floors in the mid‐ and low‐latitude biomes (temperate, Mediterranean and tropical; Table 2). The same pattern was found for N loss, except for the Mediterranean biome.

Discussion

To our knowledge, this study represents the first comprehensive large‐scale comparison of the effects of leaf litter traits and decomposers on litter decomposition in forest streams vs. forest floors, complementing prior site‐specific studies (Merritt & Lawson 1992; Hutchens & Wallace 2002). Through our direct comparison of C and N dynamics during litter decomposition between aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems, we identify common drivers which can be used in modelling C and nutrient cycles in detritus‐based food webs (Battin et al. 2008; Gessner et al. 2010). Across biomes, our structural equation models indicate that litter micronutrients (Mg and Ca) and environmental conditions were the major drivers of C and N loss during litter decomposition in both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. When biomes were analysed separately, the effects of litter micronutrients and stoichiometry (C:N and lignin:N ratios) were generally consistent between ecosystems, but the effects of decomposer complexity (varied by manipulating access to our litter packs by organisms of different size) differed between streams and forest floors in the lower latitude biomes. Although the spatial range of our study (five biomes, from the tropics to subarctic) is unique, the lack of replication of ecosystem types within each biome may constrain the generalization of our biome‐specific comparisons between ecosystems, a limitation that should be addressed in future large‐scale litter decomposition studies.

The importance of micronutrients for litter decomposition: globally consistent patterns in aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems

We provide evidence that in both streams and forest floors, litter C loss is largely controlled by the same hierarchy of drivers across biomes, firstly by biome‐specific environmental conditions and secondly by initial litter micronutrient (Mg and Ca) concentrations, supporting our first hypothesis. This result extends beyond the current ‘fast’ plant traits (e.g. high N, low lignin concentration) that have been commonly identified as controlling decomposition (Parton et al. 2007; Cornwell et al. 2008; Reich 2014) and suggests that micronutrients also have a globally consistent control over C turnover during litter decomposition in aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. Such control was observed regardless of the temporal scales at which decomposition operated in both ecosystems (68 days in streams and 436 days in forest floors were needed on average to achieve 40–50% remaining litter mass). As expected, initial litter quality (overall litter traits) explained greater proportions of the variance in C and N loss across biomes in the forest floors than in streams, reflecting the more rapid initial leaching and litter fragmentation characteristic of aquatic compared to terrestrial environments (Gessner et al. 2010). In contrast with C loss, our results suggest that controls over N release during litter decomposition are less consistent between streams and forest floors, with biome‐specific environmental conditions having a major influence in both ecosystems. Interestingly, litter micronutrients were more important for N loss in streams than litter stoichiometry, despite the speed of initial leaching in streams (typically 1–2 days), whereas litter stoichiometry was the main driver of N cycling during litter decomposition in the forest floors. We can think of two potential mechanisms to explain these contrasting findings: i) in streams, external dissolved nutrients enter the litter pool through microbial uptake from the water column (Ferreira, Gulis & Graça 2006a; Rosemond et al. 2010), reducing the importance of litter stoichiometry for N loss, whereas ii) in forest floors, enhanced N immobilization with higher litter C:N and lignin:N ratios (Manzoni et al. 2010; Talbot & Treseder 2012) increases the importance of litter stoichiometry for N loss.

Based on the same data set as we examined here, Handa et al. (2014) reported prevalent positive litter diversity (number of plant functional types and composition) effects on the cycling of C and N during decomposition. These litter diversity effects were explained in part by apparent N‐transfer from litter of N‐fixing plants to rapidly decomposing litter (Handa et al. 2014). Interestingly, the concentrations of Mg and Ca, the two litter traits we identified among the best predictors for C and N loss across ecosystems and biomes, were highest in litter from the N‐fixing functional type (Appendix S2). Together with N‐transfer, the contribution of relatively high Mg and Ca may be an additional mechanism for why the presence of litter from the N‐fixing functional type is associated with faster net decomposition across ecosystems and biomes (Wickings et al. 2012). Litter Ca can regulate decomposition through its positive effects on the growth and activity of aquatic hyphomycetes (Jenkins & Suberkropp 1995) and terrestrial white rot fungi (Eriksson, Blanchette & Ander 1990), enhancing fungal capacity to transfer N between litter types. Calcium can also increase the abundance of consumers, particularly of earthworms (Reich et al. 2005). In fact, liming experiments have found strong stimulation of litter decomposition rates after Ca addition in streams (McKie, Petrin & Malmqvist 2006) and forest floors (McCay, Cardelús & Neatrour 2013), especially in systems in which ambient Ca concentrations are low. Mg is a key component of invertebrate diets (National Research Council 2005) and has been identified as a key trait for terrestrial litter decomposition across biomes previously (Makkonen et al. 2012).

While most of the decomposition studies have investigated litter macronutrient effects driven by N or P, observational (Jenkins & Suberkropp 1995; Reich et al. 2005) and experimental studies (Kaspari et al. 2008; Powers & Salute 2011) suggest that micronutrients (e.g. Na, Mg, Ca, or Zn) may also play a role. Here, we provide evidence for an important influence of Mg and Ca on litter decomposition across biomes, supporting the results of Makkonen et al. (2012) for forest floors and indicating a similar pattern in streams. Although component 1 of the litter nutrient PCA was mostly driven by Mg and Ca (Fig. 2a), it also exhibited a significant, but weaker, positive correlation with N and P concentrations. Thus, these macronutrients, which exert a major influence at a global scale on litter decomposition in terrestrial (Parton et al. 2007) and aquatic ecosystems (Enriquez, Duarte & Sand‐Jensen 1993), may have played a role for decomposition in addition to or independently of, litter Ca and Mg. It is notoriously difficult to distinguish between different nutrient‐related traits and their importance in driving decomposition, especially when they covary among each other. Our representation of litter quality based on four broad trait categories is more comprehensive than previous large‐scale decomposition studies (Parton et al. 2007; Cornwell et al. 2008). Nevertheless, only component 1 of the litter traits PCA was retained for the sake of model simplicity (Fig. 2), an approach that may have missed some other important influences in the multivariate data sets (Zwick & Velicer 1986). We also evaluated whether this procedure excluded some traits that have been found to strongly control litter decay in specific biomes. For example, condensed tannins and C:P ratios play an important role in tropical biomes (Coq et al. 2010; Manzoni et al. 2010), but their inclusion did not substantially alter the outcome of the models in either ecosystem (Appendix S8, Supporting information).

Compared to litter traits, decomposer community complexity effects on litter decay differed considerably between streams and forest floors. Decomposers exerted a positive influence on litter C and N loss in both ecosystems, supporting the results of previous large‐scale litter decomposition studies (Wall et al. 2008; Woodward et al. 2012; García‐Palacios et al. 2013). However, in streams, the fraction of variation in decomposition attributable to detritivores (i.e. assessed from coarse mesh bags) was of similar magnitude to that of environmental conditions and litter traits. This result likely reflects the joint influence of decomposer activity and constantly moving water on physical fragmentation of the litter (Gessner et al. 2010). The interplay between water current and the activities of detritivores is characteristic of decomposition in stream environments, and represents an important difference in decomposition dynamics not only in soils, but also from still‐water aquatic environments such as lakes. Nevertheless, the biological contribution to this interplay is crucial. Only when velocities are very high (Ferreira et al. 2006b) or in the presence of suspended sediments (Heard et al. 1999), neither of which applied to our streams, does water current drive significant decomposition in its own right.

Our second hypothesis stated that the portion of biome effects on litter C and N loss modulated by litter traits would be greater in aquatic ecosystems. In accordance with this, 42% and 31% (C and N loss) of such effects were explained by differences in litter traits in streams, and 20% and 23% (C and N loss) in forest floors. This indicates that the indirect influence of climatic and other environmental differences among biomes on litter traits (de Deyn, Cornelissen & Bardgett 2008) represents a potentially larger contribution to large‐scale variation in C and N cycling in aquatic than in terrestrial ecosystems. In contrast, the direct effects of biome on litter C loss were stronger in the forest floors. If biome effects largely reflect differences in climate and the availability of nutrients in soil and water, then this result lends support to the prediction that such environmental factors will have stronger direct influences on decomposition in terrestrial environments (Gessner et al. 2010). However, as fitted in our models, the biome term may capture variation not only in environmental parameters, but also site‐specific shifts in the abundance and diversity of decomposers that have been shown to differ considerably among biomes in both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems (Handa et al. 2014). Regardless of the exact underlying mechanisms, our results indicate that consequences of direct and indirect environmental effects (e.g. shifts in litter quality) on litter decay may be very different for aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems, and should be taken into account by global decomposition models.

Biome‐level comparisons of aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems

The relationships between litter nutrients (mostly Mg and Ca) and stoichiometry (mostly C:N and lignin:N ratios) and litter C and N loss were similar between ecosystems in all biomes evaluated, except for the subarctic. This relationship found at the biome level partially supports our third hypothesis, stating that the relative importance of litter trait variation on C and N loss is more similar between aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems at the warmer end of the latitudinal gradient. Terrestrial habitats at the subarctic site were characterized by both the coldest and driest conditions (Appendix S1), which may have limited the litter trait influence on decomposition in the forest floor compared to the stream in this biome (Gessner et al. 2010). Within‐site litter trait variation is usually larger than between‐site variation across large spatial scales (Cornwell et al. 2008), but we instead reported a higher variation in litter traits across the latitudinal gradient than within particular biomes (Fig. 2). Thus, despite capturing a broad range of site‐specific litter trait variation, our experimental design is likely to have underestimated overall trait variation in leaf litter produced by the local plant communities, especially in the tropics. However, this study did not aim to evaluate the full site‐specific litter trait variation, but to represent wide gradients in leaf litter traits and litter decomposability within each biome, while ensuring that the same broad functional types were represented across biomes.

Increasing decomposer community complexity generally leads to increased litter decomposition rates in terrestrial (García‐Palacios et al. 2013; Handa et al. 2014) and aquatic ecosystems (Schindler & Gessner 2009; Handa et al. 2014). However, our biome‐specific results do not support our fourth hypothesis, stating that the influence of decomposer community complexity would decline more strongly towards the equator in the stream than forest floor ecosystems, with most decomposition in the tropical stream expected to be microbially mediated (Boyero et al. 2011; Bruder et al. 2014). Instead, we saw no consistent decline in the effects of decomposer community complexity on litter C or N loss from the subarctic to the tropical biome in either the aquatic or terrestrial ecosystems. Rather, the influence of decomposer complexity peaked in the temperate stream, reflecting the presence of highly efficient amphipod detritivores at this site (Graça 2001; Handa et al. 2014). For N loss, the effects of decomposer community complexity were higher in the tropical biome in the forest floor than in the stream, confirming the important effects of soil macrofauna on litter N dynamics found at the same site (Hättenschwiler & Bracht‐Jørgensen 2010).

Conclusions

Litter decomposition is generally controlled by environmental conditions, decomposer communities and litter quality traits, but the relative importance of these drivers can differ between ecosystems. Our results indicate that for both terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems, specific combinations of litter traits can be used to generate a reliable predictive framework of litter decomposition at a global scale. However, the complexity of decomposer communities also needs to be included in addition to litter traits, in particular for forest streams. Controlled fertilization experiments designed to tease apart the effects of individual macro‐ and micronutrients on litter decomposition are likely to be useful for confirming the important influence of litter micronutrients found in both ecosystems. The identification of common drivers in our large‐scale ecosystem comparison points towards a basis for developing common models for C and N dynamics during decomposition across aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. Future modelling efforts should account for the global similarities (litter nutrients with a particularly important and so far underestimated Mg and Ca effect) and biome‐level differences (consistent litter traits but contingent decomposer effects) found between ecosystems observed here.

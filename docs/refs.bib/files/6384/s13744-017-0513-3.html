Abstract

Herbivory is an important factor to generate spatial mosaics with variations in a plant community composition and organization. The objective of this work was to determine the impact of Atta vollenweideri Forel 1893 nests on herbaceous and shrub vegetation in a degraded native forest of the Espinal ecoregion. The study was carried out in the Protected Area and Multiple Use Nature Reserve called Estancia “El Carayá” (Entre Ríos, Argentina). Ten A. vollenweideri nests were selected by simple random sampling through internal roads, and two transects were drawn from the center of the nest (0 m) up to 60 m away in opposite directions. The line intercept method was used to quantify the percentage of vegetation cover of herbaceous and shrub species, while the floristic composition was estimated by the Canfield method. Afterwards, a nonparametric test between positions and a conglomerate analysis to evaluated distance were applied. Grass species, legumes, and sedges fell in the adjacent areas to nests, highlighting the bare soil at the crest and base of the nests. Fifteen plant species were identified, and two families correspond to monocotyledonous and dicotyledonous species. In conclusion, the nests of A. vollenweideri affect the community of herbaceous and shrub vegetation of the studied degraded native forest of the Espinal ecoregion since these ants perform a high selection of herbaceous species considered as pioneers of plant successions.

Introduction

Natural grasslands are the classical agroecosystems of native forests in the northern center of the province of Entre Ríos (Argentina) and are degraded, which is evidenced by specific floristic composition (Wilson & Sabattini 2001). Herbaceous species composition of natural grasslands determines its potential as a forage resource, directly affecting its secondary productivity (Sabattini et al 2008).

The diversity of plant communities, and in particular their organization in terms of biotic and abiotic factors, constitutes a key issue of botany and ecology (Begon et al 2005). In particular, herbivory is an important factor in the evolution of species (Agrawal 2011), in the structure of communities (Maron & Crone 2006, Allan & Crawley 2011), and in the generation of spatial mosaics modifying the organization and composition of the plant community (Olff and Ritchie 1998). The way that herbivory insects affect plant population and community ecology and ecosystem processes is well studied (Belsky 1986, Maron & Crone 2006) and raises new questions about leaf-cutting ants.

Ants play a dominant ecological role in nearly every terrestrial environment on Earth (Wheeler 1910, Wilson 1971, Hölldobler & Wilson 1990). Throughout the neotropics, the leafcutter ants (LCA) (Formicidae: Attini) are top herbaceous consumers (Hölldobler & Wilson 2008) and use a unique means of food production that has been described in great detail (Weber 1972, Hölldobler & Wilson 2010). LCA modify soil properties via nest construction and, by doing this, modulate soil resources for other organisms. In this sense, ants are recognized as ecosystem engineers (Jones et al 1994, Folgarait 1998, Meyer et al 2013, Leal et al 2014) because they modify, maintain, and create habitats for other species. Indeed, the local effects of active ant nests on soil and the indirect effect on plants are well known (e.g., Nowak et al 1990, Brown & Human 1997, Farji-Brener & Ghermandi 2004; Sosa & Brazeiro 2010). These insects collect 15% of the vegetation per year (Wirth et al 2003) and about 50% of the plant species (Vasconcelos & Fowler 1990) within their foraging areas. These herbivory activities reduce the reproductive success of cut species (Barbosa 2009), create small openings in the forest canopy that promote heterogeneity in the light regime, and modify the adaptation of plant structures (Meyer 2008, Correa et al 2010). In the foraging area, also, these herbivory activities lead to an increase in nutrient availability (Moutinho et al 2003, Sousa-Souto et al 2008) by means of both the accumulation of material used in fungiculture (Sternberg et al 2007) and soil permeability (Moutinho et al 2003). Such microclimatic and edaphic changes cause alterations in species composition, structure, and dynamics of plant communities in the adjacent areas to nests (Garrettson et al 1998, Correa et al 2010, Meyer et al 2011).

Studies carried out in an open dry forest of the Eastern Republic of Uruguay indicate that environments with Atta vollenweideri nests increase biodiversity of woody plants at a landscape scale (Sosa & Brazeiro 2012); however, in the vicinity of living nests, the richness of woody species is reduced (Sosa 2006). In addition, high density of nests of A. vollenweideri in native forest ecosystems of Entre Ríos was determined, where the structure of the dominating species is Trithrinax campestris Drude & Griseb., Butia yatay Becc., and Aspidosperma quebracho-blanco Schltr. with important areas covered by degraded natural grasslands (Sabattini et al 2015a, b).

The objective of this work was to determine the impact of the presence of A. vollenweideri nests on herbaceous and shrub vegetation in a degraded native forest of the Espinal ecoregion being our hypothesis that the specific floristic composition of plant communities in an open forest ecosystem with LCA is changed according to the distance between such communities and the center of the nest.

Material and Methods

Work area

The study was carried out in the Protected Area and Multiple Use Nature Reserve (PA-MUSR) called Estancia “El Carayá” (30°40′S, 58°50′W), located in Atencio district (south of Feliciano department) in the north of the province of Entre Ríos, Argentina (Fig 1). According to Rojas & Saluso (1987), this region has a humid temperate climate typical of plains. The average annual temperature is 18.9°C, the average maximum temperature is 24.8°C, and the average minimum temperature is 12.0°C (Plan Mapa de Suelos [Soil Mapping Plan] 1986). Average annual rainfall is 1300 mm, rains being concentrated between October and March. The study is located 64 m above sea level.

Native forests are heterogeneous as regard the appearance and structure of tree and shrub strata development, depending on soil diversity and the profuse hydrographic network across the region (Sabattini et al 1999). In general, the current state of the native forest of the region is characterized by the position of a plant succession, and it is possible to identify the following: virgin or pristine forests that constitute the final stage of the succession (climax); successional forests that correspond to intermediate stages, where diversity is richer but the native forest is not yet stabilized; and renewable forests at the beginning of the succession after deforestation, where the usually dominating species belong to the invasive type (Sabattini et al 2015c).

According to Cabrera (1976), the study area corresponds to successional forests of the Espinal ecoregion, which is characterized by xerophilous forests dominated by Prosopis nigra Griseb., Prosopis affinis Spreng., and Vachellia caven Molina. In addition, there are “Riverside Forests with Shrud Jungle” corresponding to the Paranaense ecoregion (Sabattini 2015).

Soils are imperfectly drained because of the irregular surface that fluctuates from flat to very gently undulating areas, where rainwater is excessively retained and accumulated on the surface. They have a loam epipedon, below 10 cm, which is followed by a waterproof horizon and is impenetrable by roots (Plan Mapa de Suelos [Soil Mapping Plan] 1980). According to Sabattini et al (2015b), the dominant soil cartographic unit in the study area is Grecco (Vertic Epiaqualfs), located in well-defined small plains, which are relatively higher within the landscape of long and slight slopes. Those areas are dominated by the native forest with thick and short species such as T. campestris, B. yatay, and A. quebracho-blanco and significant areas without natural grassland.

Sampling design

Ten nests of A. vollenweideri were selected by simple random sampling through internal roads to PA-MUSR, and two transects were drawn in each nest from the center (0 m) up to 60 m away in opposite directions. The sampling units to determine the presence and cover of herbaceous and shrub plants were located at 0, 5, 15, 30, and 60 m, perpendicularly to the main transect.

The line intercept method (Canfield 1941, Kent & Coker 1992)—where the transect line is thought of as a vertical plane that is perpendicular to the ground—was used and modified according to Sione et al (2006) to quantify vegetation cover percentage of herbaceous and shrub species, and eight fractions, namely grass, legumes, sedges, bare soil, shrubs, saplings, branches, and chaff, were identified. Each transect was represented by a rectangle whose area was 6 m2, and the vertical projection was quantified with respect to the soil of the vegetal species corresponding to each fraction. Afterwards, a correlation analysis of the fractions was performed as a function of distance to the nest, and the means obtained between distances for each fraction were compared, using the Kruskal-Wallis nonparametric test. The floristic composition of the herbaceous and shrub vegetation was determined in each nest for the aforementioned positions by means of the Braun-Blanquet scale (1979).

Subsequently, a group analysis of sites listed for each position was carried out, the similarity criterion being the square of the Euclidean distance between the percentages of total cover of each species (Kent & Coker 1992). Data were analyzed using Infostat 2015 version (Di Rienzo et al 2015).

Results

Cover of grass, legume, and sedge species decreased significantly in the adjacent areas of nests (Table 1), highlighting bare soil at the crest and base of the nests (79.3 and 45.0%, respectively). A dominance of grass cover (47.2%) was noted 60 m away from the nest, followed by bare soil (13.8%), legumes (13.7%), and sedges (9.4%). The fractions of shrubs, saplings, branches, and chaff involved approximately 10% of the ground in all sampling units of the transect.

Five meters away from the nest, D. repens and other species of dicotyledons dominate with 24.4 and 17.8% respectively, followed by S. indicus (11.9%) and sedge species (10.8%). It is also noteworthy the increase in shrub species cover (Table 2).

However, 15 m away from the nest, there was a decrease in the percentage of shrub species (A. gratissima, B. ulicina, and T. campestris), S. indicus (12.7%) being the most abundant and Cynodon dactylon Pers. (9.9%), Setaria spp. P.Beauv. (8.8%), Eragrostis lugens Wolf (8.6%), and Cloris spp. Sw. (7.3%) constituting the grass group. On the other hand, a great decline in dicotyledonous species was observed at 5 m away from the nest (Table 2).

Thirty meters away from the nests, the most abundant were Setaria spp. and E. lugens, followed by S. indicus, Stipa spp., and Chloris spp. Other species such as Paspalum spp. and Pitochaetium montevidensis J.Presl were filed within the group of grasses at 60 m away. On the other hand, the cover of sedge species at 30 and 60 m of distance doubled at 15 m (20 and 10.9%, respectively), while D. repens and dicotyledonous cover had a 50% decrease with respect to the 5-m distance to the nest. It is important to mention that Schizachyrium microstachyum Nees, Senecio grisebachii Baker, and Sida rhombifolia L. were companions in the transect, presenting lower cover percentages (2%) (Table 2).

Three species groups can be identified in the nest: group I dominates with 10% of the vegetation cover (D. repens and dicotyledonous); group II is formed by accompanying species of shrub (B. ulicina and A. gratissima) and herbaceous strata with sedges and S. indicus and another group of species that is present with a low cover percentage (<4%) corresponding to group III. Five meters away from the nest, the same number is kept for the dominating group of D. repens (group I), whose cover is more than 20%, followed by another group of abundant and thick species that represent 10–18% of the cover (group II), which consists of dicotyledons, S. indicus, and sedge and shrub species of medium and large sizes. Group III species are companions with low cover percentage (<8%) (Figs 3A, B and 4).

Fifteen meters away from the nest, however, the number of species that makes up each group is balanced (Fig 3C). Grasses dominate within the group of greater vegetation cover (group I) that consists of Setaria spp., E. lugens, and C. dactylon, among others. Group II represents dicotyledon species accompanied by medium-sized shrubs. Setaria spp. and E. lugens (group I) were grouped in relation to the 30-m distance away from the nest (Fig 3D, E), while group II presents grass and dicotyledonous species in a lower cover percentage.

Discussion

Herbaceous and shrub plant composition and cover on the nests and near them were different from those in adjacent areas, in agreement with other studies (Woodell 1974, Anderson 1982, Horvitz & Schemske 1986). The nests were recorded in native forests with serious problems of degradation due to the absence of many tree species, great cover of bare soil, and increased percentage of vegetation cover consisting of invasive herbaceous and shrub species such as T. campestris, A. gratissima, B. ulicina, S. grisebachii, and S. rhombifolia (Sabattini et al 2015c). These vegetation conditions could be determined by the presence of LCA because they facilitate the emergence of invasive species as stated by Bucher (1982), Folgarait et al (1996), and Farji-Brener & Margutti (1997). In addition, they accelerate the course of plant successions (Jonkman 1977).

It is important to mention that some recorded herbaceous species are adapted to the physical and chemical conditions of these soils, with low cover and biomass since they are pioneer species within the colonization process (Sabattini et al 2015c).

All species presented in this study coincide with those described by Landi et al (1990) in a bordering field with equal agroecological conditions and by Sabattini et al (1999) in native forests of the northern center of Entre Ríos. In particular, C. dactylon and S. indicus are adapted to moderate salinity (Burkart 1979, 1987), doubling the cover percentage found in a native forest without nests (Table 2, Fig 4), since they react positively to bioturbation in association with nest construction and maintenance (Fagúndez 2003). These species are considered pioneers at the beginning of the succession and highly preferred by ants because they have low concentrations of carbon, fibers, tannins, and terpenoids (Howard & Wiemer 1986, Urbas 2004), thus being more exposed to herbivory (Coley et al 1985, Coley & Aide 1991).

In general, species of genus Atta and Acromyrmex differentiate between those plant species they cut for cultivating fungi (Vasconcelos & Fowler 1990, Wirth et al 2003). The appearance of higher vegetation cover of dicotyledonous species near the nest—including D. repens—(Fig 4) could indicate selective foraging behavior that A. vollenweideri realizes on the grasses as described by Fowler et al (1986). This behavior occurs in ecosystems with poor diversity of plant species—e.g., native forests at the beginning and middle of the plant succession—where the foraging activity of ants is related to dominant species (Blanton & Ewel 1985, Shepherd 1985) and mainly pioneer species (Oliveira et al 2004, Santos et al 2008). In addition, these species exhibit high tolerance to soil moisture conditions (Casermeiro et al 2001). Adjacent areas to the nest presented a topographically flat and concave surface that retains water after rainfall due to the textural class of soil (Sabattini et al 2015b). Despite the high content of sodium in nest surroundings, these species can develop due to a greater soil moisture concentration and lower soil compaction, generating a highly favorable microhabitat (Sosa 2006).

In addition, the percentage of coverage of the species of Paspalum spp. and Stipa spp. were significantly lower in the adjacent areas to the nest than 60 m away from the nests (Table 2, Fig 4). In this regard, ants select their food by making a strict balance between a low concentration of secondary metabolites and a high content of proteins and carbohydrates, since this affects fungi cultivation in the nest (Leal et al 2012).

These native forests that are located at the beginning of the plant succession presented reduced foraging areas and high herbivory rates (Urbas et al 2007, Silva et al 2009), leading to an increase in colony density (Wirth et al 2007, Meyer et al 2009, Silva et al 2009). In these conditions, the role of underground herbivores is noticed, which alter the composition of species and increase plant diversity through the construction of galleries and the generation of earth mounds (Huntly & Reichman 1994, Reichman & Seabloom 2002). LCA may favor species coexistence and diversity—via pioneer control—across disturbed or degraded habitats, which without LCA would be progressively dominated by few species (Tabarelli et al 2012).

Conclusion

Nests of A. vollenweideri affect the community of herbaceous and shrub vegetation of a degraded native forest of the Espinal ecoregion, performing a high selection of pioneer herbaceous species in a plant succession.

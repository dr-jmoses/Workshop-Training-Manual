    Abstract

    1. Productivity is a key driver of ecosystem structure and function, so long‐term studies are critical to understanding ecosystems with high temporal variation in productivity. In some deserts, productivity, driven by moisture availability, varies immensely over time (rainfall) and space (landscape factors). At high productivity, species richness is expected to be driven in opposing directions by abundance (More Individuals Hypothesis – MIH) and competition. While studies investigating the impacts of spatial variation in productivity on community structure are common, the impacts of temporal variability on productivity are poorly understood.
    2. We tested how well rainfall predicted the activity, species numbers and assemblage composition of ants and if responses were moderated by landscape position. We also asked whether the number of species (richness per sampling unit and estimated species richness) responded directly to rainfall or was moderated by ant activity or competition from dominant ants.
    3. Over a 22‐year period, when annual rainfall fluctuated between 79 mm and 570 mm, we sampled ants using pitfall traps in paired dune and swale habitats in the Simpson Desert, Australia. We used climate records over this period to model changes in ant assemblages.
    4. Activity of dominant ants responded primarily to long‐term rainfall, increasing exponentially, while subordinate ants responded to short‐term weather and time. Consistent with the MIH, the number of ant species was best predicted by activity, particularly that of subordinate ants. Activity of dominant ants had a declining positive effect on numbers of species. Landscape position strongly predicted species composition, while long‐term rainfall determined composition at genus level but not species level. Over time, species composition fluctuated, but several genera consistently increased in activity.
    5. Productivity moderators such as long‐term rainfall and landscape position are key drivers of ant activity and composition in the study ecosystem, acting indirectly on numbers of species. Numbers of species were explained largely by ant activity, making a strong case for the MIH, but not competition. Longer periods of low rainfall may indirectly reduce species richness in desert ecosystems. However, a trend to increasing richness over time may indicate that conservation management can ameliorate this impact.

    1 INTRODUCTION

    Ecosystem productivity is an important determinant of the structure and function of ecological communities (Borer, Grace, Harpole, MacDougall, & Seabloom, 2017; Cusens, Wright, McBride, & Gillman, 2012; Popic, 2013; Worm & Duffy, 2003). This relationship is particularly evident in arid ecosystems, where primary productivity is often limited by water (Fischer & Turner, 1978; Heisler‐White, Knapp, & Kelly, 2008; Huxman et al., 2004; Morton et al., 2011). Rainfall in these challenging environments is not only low, but also highly variable (Van Etten, 2009). In hot deserts, for example, inter‐annual rainfall may vary ten‐fold (Fischer & Turner, 1978; Heisler‐White et al., 2008; Huxman et al., 2004). Arid ecosystems are therefore shaped by extreme climatic events (Holmgren et al., 2006; Noy‐Meir, 1973). Episodic events such as drought or unusually wet periods in desert ecosystems regulate plant and animal populations (Ehleringer, Phillips, Schuster, & Sandquist, 1991; Letnic & Dickman, 2010; Noy‐Meir, 1973). High variability in desert rainfall also compels biota to adapt to both survive long periods of drought and to optimize uptake of resources following sustained rainfall, resulting in pulsed growth of individuals or populations (Noy‐Meir, 1973; Ward, 2016). At the extremes, these ecosystems are considered to go through periods of boom and bust (Greenville, Wardle, & Dickman, 2012).

    Productivity pulses may shape ecological communities by boosting the abundance of individuals across all species. The well‐known relationship between species richness and productivity (Currie, 1991; Evans, James, & Gaston, 2006) is often explained through the “More Individuals Hypothesis” (MIH), whereby large populations in more productive systems buffer rare species from local extinction, thus increasing diversity (Evans, Warren, & Gaston, 2005; Preston, 1962; Srivastava & Lawton, 1998; Wright, 1983), although the sampling effects might also be responsible (Kaspari, Yuan, & Alonso, 2003). Positive relationships between species richness, abundance and productivity might also result from increased specialization in more productive environments (Abrams, 1995; Hurlbert & Jetz, 2010). For consumers, species richness is positively or unimodally related to abundance and productivity both in observational studies across large spatial scales (e.g. birds, Hurlbert, 2004) and in small‐scale experiments (but see Srivastava & Lawton, 1998; e.g. pitcher plant protozoans, Kneitel & Miller, 2002; e.g. tree hole invertebrates, Yee & Juliano, 2007; e.g. ants, McGlynn, Weiser, & Dunn, 2010). A recent review suggests that the relationship is humped at small spatial scales, but stronger and monotonically increasing over large spatial scales (Storch, Bohdalkova, & Okie, 2018). Temporal studies of abundance–productivity relationships show weak correlations (e.g. Dobson, Sorte, Manne, & Hawkins, 2015), perhaps as a result of temporal lags in productivity responses (Graham & Duda, 2011), especially in desert ecosystems (e.g. Gibb, Grossman, Dickman, & Wardle, 2018a). Where species richness–abundance/productivity relationships are unimodal, the descent of species richness at high abundances is commonly attributed to increased competitive dominance at high productivity (e.g. Parr, Sinclair, Andersen, Gaston, & Chown, 2005). In agreement, experimental manipulations suggest that the effects of competition on community structure increase with productivity (Gibb, 2011). We might therefore predict that any declines in species richness associated with periods of high productivity might be better predicted by competition pressure than by productivity.

    While rainfall drives temporal variation in productivity in deserts, spatial variation in productivity also may result from differences in local topography and geology (Ludwig et al. 1997; Morton et al., 2011). Even with similar rainfall regimes, plants occupying different parts of the landscape with different soil types might experience large differences in the availability of soil moisture. This is because soil texture controls the infiltration depth, water holding capacity and hydraulic conductivity of water (Schwinning, Sala, Loik, & Ehleringer, 2004). With low rainfall, sandy soils can support higher productivity than loamy soils because water is more biologically available to deep‐rooted plants (Noy‐Meir, 1974). In turn, spatial differences in plant growth can regulate animal populations, with the scale of the effect depending on animal mobility. Spatial and temporal differences in productivity might be expected to interact to determine assemblage responses. In desert dune fields, for example, the effects of productivity pulses should vary between assemblages on crests and swales, with biota on sandy crests responding quickly but transiently before conditions dry, and more slowly but with greater amplitude in the swales where water pools and nutrients accumulate (Wardle et al., 2015).

    The high temporal variability in productivity of desert ecosystems means that long‐term data are critical to our understanding of how these systems function and how they might respond to climate change. Although several long‐term studies have been carried out on vertebrates in deserts (Greenville, Wardle, Nguyen, & Dickman, 2016a, 2016b; Shenbrot, Krasnov, & Burdelov, 2010; Thibault, Ernest, White, Brown, & Goheen, 2010), few have focussed on invertebrates (c.f. Langlands, Brennan, & Pearson, 2006 [spiders, 14 years]; Kwok, Wardle, Greenville, & Dickman, 2016 [invertebrate orders, 6 years]). Our lack of understanding of invertebrate responses to rainfall fluctuations is problematic because invertebrates not only make up the bulk of terrestrial animal biodiversity, they are also of considerable functional importance in ecosystems (e.g. Colloff, Pullen, & Cunningham, 2010; Evans, Dawes, Ward, & Lo, 2011; Rader et al., 2016). Ants, in particular, make up a large fraction of animal biomass (King, Warren, & Bradford, 2013), perform a range of ecosystem functions (Del Toro, Ribbons, & Pelini, 2012; Folgarait, 1998) and are a food source for many other animals in most terrestrial communities (e.g. Pianka & Vitt, 2003). Competition has long been considered the hallmark of ant community ecology (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990), but its importance may depend on ecosystem productivity (Gibb, 2011).

    The broad aim of this study was to understand how variation in rainfall, the key driver of productivity in the arid zone, affects ant species assemblages. By using a broad temporal scale and local assemblages, we directly link changes in local abundances to changes in productivity, providing a strong test of the More Individuals Hypothesis. Further, in linking species richness to abundance, we consider the limitations imposed on species richness by a key interaction: competition. Over a 22‐year period, we used standardized methods to collect ants from paired dune–swale sites in the Simpson Desert, central Australia. We predicted that climate (long‐term rainfall and temperature), weather (short‐term rainfall and temperature) and landscape position would influence: (a) the activity of dominant and subordinate ants, and (b) ant assemblage composition. We further predicted, (c) that long‐term rainfall and temperature, landscape position and ant activity (More Individuals Hypothesis) would positively influence species density, but that high activities of dominant ants might reduce species density through competition. This work contributes to our understanding of the drivers of species assemblages in environments exposed to productivity pulses and the potential implications of climate change for desert ant communities.

    2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

    2.1 Study site

    We conducted the study on Ethabuka Reserve (214,000 ha) in the Simpson Desert, central Australia. Sampling was conducted at five sites within 10 km of “Main Camp” (23°46'S, 138°28'E; Figure 1) using standard monitoring methods of the long‐term ecological research (AusLTER) site. The study landscape is dominated by parallel sand dunes running in a north‐north‐west to south‐south‐easterly direction. The dunes are up to 10 m in height and 0.6–1 km apart (Purdie, 1984). Each of the sites comprised a crest and swale sampling point to account for the gradient in spinifex (hummock) grass (Triodia basedowii) cover and associated differences in vegetation composition (Wardle & Nano, 2017). Dune crests are relatively open and have a low, but variable, cover of vegetation, including grasses, ephemeral herbaceous plants, sub‐shrubs and shrubs including Acacia ligulata, Dodonaea viscosa, Crotalaria eremaea and Grevillea stenobotrya. Spinifex cover is higher on dune swales (up to 60%), and the composition of grasses, forbs and scattered shrubs is similar. Swales have heavier clay soils and include patches of Georgina gidgee Acacia georginae woodland ranging from 0.5 to 10 ha (Wardle et al., 2015). Summer daily temperatures usually exceed 40°C; winter minima are below 5°C (Purdie, 1984). As in other hot deserts, rainfall is highly temporally and spatially variable; more notably, it is unpredictable (Greenville et al., 2012). During the study period, annual rainfall averaged 217 mm/year (range: 79–570 mm/year). Ethabuka Reserve is a former cattle station (stock density was <1 animal per 100 ha, Frank, Dickman, & Wardle, 2012), but it was destocked in 2004 when it was purchased by Bush Heritage Australia. The region experienced extensive wildfires in 2001–2002, but these did not affect our ant sampling sites (Greenville, Dickman, Wardle, & Letnic, 2009).

    2.2 Invertebrate collection

    We collected ants between August 1992 and August 2013. Here, we have included sampling sessions carried out in spring (September, October or November) and winter (June, July or August) each year, where possible (Table S1). Vertebrate pitfall traps were arranged in grids of six by six traps, with each trap separated by 20 m, such that the grid covered an area of 1 ha. The first line of six traps was set along a dune crest and the sixth line 100 m distant in the corresponding swale (Figure 1d). Ants and other invertebrates were trapped around one vertebrate trap on the dune crest and around another in the swale using wet pitfall traps. Six wet pitfall traps (40 mm diameter, 90 cm deep; filled with 3% formalin solution) were placed in a grid of 2 × 3 (each trap separated by ~3 m) in dune and swale at each site (i.e. a total of 12 pitfall traps per site). Wet pitfall traps were buried flush with the ground and generally left open for three consecutive days and nights, occasionally for four days, before removal. In the laboratory, trap contents were transferred to 80% ethanol. We counted ants and identified them to morphospecies (Oliver & Beattie, 1996), and a reference collection was identified to species by Prof. Alan Andersen (Charles Darwin University, Darwin). Ants were classified as dominant (Iridomyrmex was the only genus of dominant ants present) or subordinate (i.e. not dominant: all other genera), based on Andersen (1990). Counts of individual ant workers per pitfall trap are reported as ant activity, and counts of ant species per pitfall trap are reported as ant species density. Because some traps were disturbed (and therefore excluded from the study) and some trapping periods were longer than 3 days, activity and species density data were converted to individuals or species per trap day for analysis. We also estimated species richness (described under statistical analyses).

    2.3 Vegetation data

    We sampled composition and cover of vegetation at six vertebrate trap locations, one on each of the six traplines. Only those data for the traplines where ants were sampled were used in the analyses here (Table S1). Plant species were identified, and visual estimates of cover were recorded to the nearest 5% in 5 m circular plots, centred on the mammal trap. We scored flowering and seeding on a scale of 1—low to 5—high for each species to track changes in plant resources. We calculated total plant cover, total seed index (i.e. sum of seeding indices) and total flowering index for each sampling location separately for Triodia and other plants.

    2.4 Climate and weather data

    Although two local weather stations were available (within 2 km of the field sites), rainfall records had significant temporal gaps, making it impossible to calculate annual precipitation. We therefore used climate data from the Bureau of Meteorology (BOM) for our analyses. Data were averaged from the nearest BOM weather stations at Glenormiston (104 km from Main Camp), Boulia (172 km), Birdsville (190 km), Marion Downs (113 km), Sandringham (65 km) and Bedourie (116 km; BOM, 2016). A regression test showed that monthly rainfall averaged across the six BOM stations was consistent with monthly rainfall averaged across the local weather stations (F1,138 = 19.6, p < 0.0001, R2 = 0.74) and was therefore a reasonable estimate of the rainfall experienced at the study sites.

    We used long‐term rainfall and temperature to represent the long‐term conditions that might influence colony establishment success, persistence and size (population) and short‐term rainfall and temperature to influence activity of colonies. We therefore expected that long‐term climate would directly influence species density or estimated richness and that short‐term weather would influence species density/estimated richness through its effects on activity. Measures selected for use in analyses were those that were most closely correlated with ant activity and ant species density in a previous study (Gibb, Grossman, et al., 2018a). For long‐term rainfall, we used cumulative rainfall in the 12 months prior to sampling. For long‐term temperature, we used average minimum monthly temperature in the 12 months prior to sampling. Weather was expected to indirectly affect species density by changing activity, so we were interested primarily in effects of short‐term rainfall and temperature on activity. We therefore used the weather variables best related to activity (again from Gibb, Grossman, et al., 2018a): cumulative rainfall in the 18 days prior to sampling and average minimum daily temperature in the 3 days prior to sampling.

    2.5 Statistical analyses

    To avoid problems with multi‐collinearity in our data, we tested the association of season with climate and vegetation variables with climate and position (dune or swale). All vegetation variables and season were strongly associated with climate and/or position (Table S2). Vegetation data also were gathered only on 76% of sampling times (Table S1). We therefore excluded season and vegetation variables from further analysis.

    We used species density (the mean number of species per trap at each site) and three species richness estimators: the Chao, jack1 and boot estimators (Chao, 1987; Smith & van Belle, 1984 calculated using the r package Vegan, Oksanen et al., 2010) as our measure of the number of species. Species density was calculated for all site–date combinations with at least one undamaged trap (212 data points); species richness estimation was performed for those site‐date combinations with four or more of the six pitfall traps intact, resulting in inclusion of 163 of the original 212 data points.

    To determine whether the form of the relationship between dominant ant activity and numbers of species was indicative of competitive suppression, we compared the fit of linear mixed models predicting species density and estimated species richness using log–log, polynomial–log and negative exponential–log relationships. Models also included sampling date, long‐term precipitation and temperature, landscape position, activity of subordinate species and the random effect of site. We compared model fits using marginal (fixed effects; ) and conditional (fixed + random effects; ) R2 values (Nakagawa & Schielzeth, 2013) from the package MuMin (Barton, 2011) in r (R Development Core Team, 2014) to select the best form of dominant ant activity to use in piecewise structural equation models.

    We used piecewise structural equation modelling (SEM; piecewiseSEM in r, Lefcheck, 2016) to address our first and third questions simultaneously. Piecewise SEM allows incorporation of multiple linear equations, which are evaluated individually and allows greater flexibility in distributions and sampling designs and lower replication than traditional SEM (Shipley, 2009). It also helps to disentangle direct and indirect effects. We specified three paths (equations; Figure 3): the first two predicted the activity of dominant and subordinate ants from climate (long‐term rainfall and temperature), weather (short‐term rainfall and temperature) and landscape position (dune or swale), with activity of dominant ants also included as a predictor of activity of subordinate ants. The third path predicted the number of ant species (species density and estimated species richness) from climate, landscape position and the activity of dominant and non‐dominant ants. Preliminary models included the landscape position: long‐term rainfall interaction to test for a role of landscape position in modifying the effect of rainfall, but this term never improved the models. The form of the relationship between species richness and dominant ant activity was chosen based on best R2 for log, polynomial and negative exponential models, as described above. All models also included sampling date (days since 1 January 1900) and site (random). All climate terms and date were scaled in the models and activity, and species richness and subordinate activity data were log10‐transformed. In addition to the testing for multi‐collinearity described above, we calculated the variance inflation factor (VIF) for each predictor in the piecewise SEM using the package usdm (Naimi, 2015). The VIF indicates the proportion of variance in each predictor in the model that is explained by other predictors. No predictors in the model had a VIF <2 (as recommended by Zuur, Ieno, & Elphick, 2010). The fit of the piecewise SEM was evaluated using Fisher's C statistic, where a value of p > 0.05 indicates a good fit of the model to the data. We report standardized effect sizes for variables with significant relationships in the piecewise SEM and both marginal (fixed effects; ) and conditional (fixed + random effects; ) R2 values (Nakagawa & Schielzeth, 2013) for each of the three paths.

    To test the effect of our predictors (sampling date, position (crest or swale), climate (long‐ and short‐term rainfall and temperature), the long‐term climate interaction and site) on ant assemblage composition, we used simultaneous generalized linear models (ManyGLM, Warton, 2011; Wang, Naumann, Wright, & Warton, 2012). Ant assemblage composition was a matrix of activities at species (n = 106) or genus level (n = 20) by sites across all years (n = 212 rows). We conducted this test for both species and genera because genera include functionally similar species. Exploratory tests also included all interactions between long‐term rainfall, long‐term temperature and landscape position, but these terms were not significant in the larger models. ManyGLM analyses were conducted in mvabund (Wang et al., 2012) in r, and residual plots were checked to ensure that the selected response distribution (negative binomial) was appropriate. Adjusted post‐hoc tests were used to examine associations for individual genera (20 genera) where whole assemblage effects were significant. Adjusted post‐hoc tests were considered overly conservative for the species‐level analysis (76 species with >3 occurrences tested), so p‐values of 0.001 and less in unadjusted tests were considered significant.

    3 RESULTS

    We collected 35,990 ant workers belonging to 106 species in 20 genera. The most speciose genera were Melophorus (19 species), Iridomyrmex (17), Monomorium (17), Camponotus (9), Tetramorium (9) and Pheidole (8). The catch was dominated by two very abundant species: Iridomyrmex rufoniger group sp. 5a (42.4% of captures) and Iridomyrmex chasei (39.8% of captures). Only 65 alate ants were collected.

    Species composition fluctuated considerably over time, although the same genera dominated the pitfall catch (Figure 2). Assemblages were almost always dominated by species of Iridomyrmex (Figure 2, shown in blues), although the identity of the dominant species changed over time. Monomorium (yellows) and Melophorus (reds) were also often highly abundant, but which species was common at particular sites again fluctuated among years, suggesting significant year‐to‐year variation in the identity of colonies close to the pitfall locations.

    3.1 Do climate, weather and landscape position structure ant assemblages?

    The best‐fitting model for the relationship of species richness with activity of dominant ants used the negative exponential of dominant ant activity for all species richness estimators (boot: log:  = 0.40; polynomial:  = 0.42; negative exponential: = 0.46; Chao: log: = 0.30; polynomial: = 0.32; negative exponential: = 0.36; jack1: log: = 0.33; polynomial: = 0.34; negative exponential: = 0.38). For species density, polynomial and exponential models had similar fits (log: = 0.55; polynomial: = 0.57; negative exponential: = 0.57). We therefore used the negative exponential for this relationship in the piecewise SEM.

    The piecewise SEM was a good fit to the observed data for species density (Fisher's C(4) = 7.07, p = 0.132) and for all species richness estimators (boot: C(4) = 1.99, p = 0.878; Chao: C(4) = 2.12, p = 0.714; jack1: C(4) = 1.58, p = 0.813; Figure 3). Prediction of species numbers was best for species density ( = 0.55; = 0.57) and the boot estimator ( = 0.54; = 0.58), followed by the jack1 ( = 0.46; = 0.50) and Chao ( = 0.40; = 0.43) estimators; we therefore report standardized estimates for species density and the boot estimator.

    The same variables were significant predictors in models for species density and the boot estimator, and standardized estimates for those variables were also very similar (Figure 3). Subordinate ant activity increased with time (date) and short‐term temperature and precipitation, although the relationship was not strong (species density: = 0.15; = 0.20; boot estimator: = 0.16; = 0.19). Dominant ant activity increased with long‐term precipitation, with a marginally non‐significant increase with short‐term temperature for species density (species density: = 0.20; = 0.24; boot estimator: = 0.22; = 0.25). Ant species density increased with the activity of subordinate ants, followed by time (date), and long‐term temperature and precipitation. The negative relationship between ant species density and the negative exponential of dominant ant activity indicates a strongly positive relationship at low activities, moving to a null relationship at high activities. Estimated ant species richness showed similar responses to dominant ant activity, but the relationship with activity of dominant ants was stronger and long‐term precipitation was not significant.

    Multivariate models (ManyGLM) at the species level showed that assemblage composition differed according to landscape position, climate (long‐term rainfall and temperature and their interaction and short‐term temperature) and site (Table 1, Figure 4). Similar patterns were observed for genus‐level analyses, although long‐term temperature was not a significant predictor of genus‐level composition, while sampling date was. Crests and swales differed in composition, with adjusted post hoc tests significant for one species that preferred crests and four species that preferred swales (Table 1); two genera, Nylanderia and Rhytidoponera, were more active in swales (Figure 4a,b). Activities of Iridomyrmex sp. 5a, I. rufoniger gp. and Melophorus were greatest when long‐term rainfall was higher, but long‐term temperature was low (Figure 4c). Higher long‐term rainfall was also associated with greater activity of Monomorium (Figure 4d). Two Melophorus species, Monomorium fieldi and the genus Melophorus, increased activity when short‐term minimum temperatures were higher (Figure 4e). Four genera, Nylanderia, Paraparatrechina, Tapinoma and Tetramorium, increased in abundance over time (Figure 4f–i). One Camponotus species from the discors group and the genera Camponotus and Nylanderia were associated with particular sites.

    4 DISCUSSION

    Long‐term datasets are critical for understanding species responses to resource availability in environments that experience extreme temporal variation in rainfall. Using an unparalleled 22‐year dataset on the activity of desert ants, we asked how variation in climate (long‐term temperature and rainfall) and weather (short‐term temperature and rainfall) interacted with landscape position to shape ant assemblages. Long‐term rainfall drove ant assemblages by exponentially enhancing the activity (our measure of ant abundance) of dominant ant species, although its impacts on the activity of subordinate ants were less clear. Species density and estimated species richness (our measures of species richness) were strongly associated with ant activity, as predicted by the More Individuals Hypothesis (Srivastava & Lawton, 1998), but there was no evidence that competition suppressed richness. The direct effects of time and climate were also important in determining species richness. Similar drivers determined species composition, which fluctuated immensely over time. Landscape position became important when species identity was considered. Below, we examine how these factors structure desert ant assemblages and consider the implications for the resilience of this system to extreme events, including a change in climate.

    4.1 Long‐term rainfall increases activity

    Increases in long‐term rainfall were associated with exponential increases in the activity of dominant ants (Iridomyrmex). However, subordinate ants, other than the genera Monomorium and Melophorus, did not respond to long‐term rainfall. Over a shorter timeframe, the exponential response of dominant ants to increased precipitation might be interpreted as a boom‐bust response to extreme events. However, our long‐term dataset shows a continuum of rainfall values over which there is no threshold at which dominant ant activity suddenly booms. Unexplained variation might be partially attributed to the large scale over which the climate data were collected (six weather stations spread over 381 km2), although the relationship with precipitation from local weather stations was good (R2 = 0.74). Increases in rainfall could boost ant abundance either directly, by improving soil moisture conditions and reducing physiological stress, or indirectly, by increasing food resources through enhanced growth of vegetation and the production of food resources such as flowers and seeds. The direct effects of soil moisture have largely been ignored as a driver of ant assemblages, but are particularly important for colonizing reproductives in arid areas (Johnson, 2006). More alates are produced in wet years (Cahan, 2001; Ryti & Case, 1988), and soil moisture acts as a trigger for alate flights (Bartz & Hölldobler, 1982; Davidson, 1982; Mull & Crist, 1993) and determines the success of colonizing queens by increasing survival, condition and number of brood (Johnson, 1998, 2000). While mature colonies can survive long periods of drought, founding queens are much more vulnerable to desiccation (Johnson, 2006). Greater colonization success following rain could therefore boost colony density, which would be reflected in our measure of ant activity. Unfortunately, our sampling was not targeted to collect alates, so we are unable to test the association of alate flights with rainfall in this system. Many ant species can also reproduce through colony fission or colonize with multiple queens, which might be more successful in increasing colony density in adverse conditions (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990).

    Dominant ant activity may also increase following rainfall due to indirect effects, whereby food resources from vegetation are enhanced. This was particularly pronounced for Iridomyrmex sp. 5a, rufoniger gp., although rainfall effects on this species were moderated by temperature. Higher rainfall in deserts is associated with a suite of changes in vegetation, with time lags of 2 to 11 months (Gibb, Grossman, et al., 2018a). In the study area, greater long‐term rainfall was associated with increased seeding of Triodia and increased cover, flowering and seeding of other plants at varying time lags (Gibb, Grossman, et al., 2018a; Kwok et al., 2016). Increased plant cover increases the diversity and abundance of herbivore prey, floral and extra‐floral nectaries and honeydew‐producing mutualists (Aranda‐Rickert, Diez, & Marazzi, 2014; Siemann, 1998). Increased seeding also provides more resources for a variety of ant species (Briese, 1982; Briese & Macauley, 1981; Morton, 1985). Rainfall therefore enhances a range of resources used by ants, but different resources peak with different time lags (Gibb, Grossman, et al., 2018a). Although Melophorus (seed harvester generalists) and Monomorium (generalist foragers; Shattuck, 1999; Agosti, Majer, Alonso, & Schultz, 2000; Gibb, Sanders, et al., 2018c) responded to long‐term rainfall, the variation in lag times for different resources may explain the poor relationship between overall subordinate ant activity and long‐term rainfall because the “subordinate ants” included a large array of trophic groups. The stronger and more consistent response of the dominant (Iridomyrmex) ants to long‐term rainfall (illustrated by the piecewise SEM in Figure 3) might be a result of their general dependence on honeydew, coupled with consistent changes in honeydew availability with rainfall.

    4.2 Species density increases with ant activity

    Higher long‐term rainfall was also associated with increased species density (but not estimated species richness), comparable with findings for chafer beetles in semi‐arid Australia (Gibb, Verdon, et al., 2018b). Model fits were stronger for species density and estimated species richness than for activity largely because activity, particularly of subordinate ants, predicted species density. Species density was positively associated with activity, consistent with the More Individuals Hypothesis. Greater colony densities and survivorship across all species resulting from more favourable soil moisture or higher plant productivity are expected to reduce local extinction rates of rare species, thus increasing diversity (Preston, 1962; Srivastava & Lawton, 1998; Yee & Juliano, 2007). Alternatively, improved conditions might result in increased species density through the sampling effect: all species are more abundant and therefore more likely to occur at and be detected at a site (Kaspari et al., 2003).

    Although more than 80% of the catch consisted of dominant Iridomyrmex species, the activity of subordinate ants was a better predictor of the number of species than that of dominant species. Our best fit models included a negative exponential relationship with Iridomyrmex, indicating that, although richness increases rapidly with Iridomyrmex activity at low abundances, at high abundances, further increasing Iridomyrmex activity does not increase richness. This is probably because Iridomyrmex made up only 16% of species and a single species was often hyperabundant in a trap, so most of the variation in species composition was contained within other taxa. Further, Iridomyrmex can competitively suppress congeneric species (Fox, Fox, & Archer, 1985; Gibb & Hochuli, 2004), decreasing the likelihood that increased abundances of this genus will result in increased species richness. Notably, high dominant ant activity was not associated with declines in richness, suggesting that competitive suppression of other ant genera may not be important in this ecosystem.

    4.3 Landscape moderation of rainfall effects

    Landscape position was a key determinant of ant assemblage composition, but not activity or species density. The effective productivity of an area depends on the availability of soil water to plants, and this may differ among components of a landscape that experience the same level of rainfall. Dune crests and swales present very different water availability environments to plants, and this is reflected in plant productivity: plant cover was 34% higher in dune swales than on crests. Plant productivity differences were mirrored by differences in ant assemblage composition between dunes and swales, with several species more abundant in swales and only one more abundant on crests. Similarly distinct dune‐ and swale‐associated assemblages have been observed for other taxa (e.g. lizards, Downey & Dickman, 1993; birds, Tischler, Dickman, & Wardle, 2013), although they are rare for mammals (but see Dickman, Greenville, Tamayo, & Wardle, 2011).

    Rainfall pulses might be expected to act differently in environments differing in primary productivity (Yang et al., 2010). However, we found no evidence that this was the case: preliminary analyses showed that interactions between landscape position and rainfall did not improve our models for activity or species density and no interaction terms including position were significant in the initial models predicting species composition. This suggests that species dwelling in dunes and swales are equally vulnerable to variation in rainfall.

    4.4 Temporal change

    Over time, activity and species density increased and composition changed, with large turnover among both dominant and subordinate species (Figure 2). The increase in activity and species density is in contrast to studies in agricultural or urban settings showing long‐term declines in insect populations (Biesmeijer et al., 2006; Hallmann et al., 2017) and of stability in tropical ant assemblages (Donoso, 2017). Seeding of plants other than Triodia and short‐term rainfall increased over time and could be drivers of this pattern, as could variables that we did not measure. A major management change (removal of cattle) that occurred in 2004 might also be responsible. Disturbances, such as grazing, are a major determinant of ant assemblage structure globally (Gibb, Sanders, et al., 2018c; Gibb et al., 2015), so removal of grazers would be expected to alter assemblages. Flowering at the study sites increased following destocking (compared with control sites), showing that some resources increased detectably (Frank, Wardle, Dickman, & Greenville, 2014), which likely favoured ants. However, temporal change in activity and species density were not clearly associated with the destocking date (Figure 2) and it is difficult to assess the impact of this change without appropriate control sites or knowledge of lag times.

    5 CONCLUSIONS

    On a background of highly fluctuating species composition, species richness was clearly driven by increases in activity of all ants, in support of the MIH. Temporal productivity, that is long‐term rainfall, was a strong predictor of activity, driving exponential increases in dominant ants, but acting largely indirectly on species richness. Along with climate, spatial productivity, in the form of landscape position, shaped the composition of arid zone ant assemblages. Long‐term increases in overall activity, richness and the activity of some genera suggest that long‐term declines in insects observed elsewhere may not be a general feature of ecosystems in the Anthropocene, or may be preventable through conservation actions, such as the removal of introduced grazers. Global warming is predicted to increase variability in rainfall (productivity), and the likely consequence is an increase in the length and severity of droughts. This will pose a challenge, particularly to dominant ants, which declined substantially in activity with declining rainfall. However, the outcome for ant diversity at a regional scale may depend on the uniformity of productivity peaks and troughs across the landscape and continued conservation efforts.

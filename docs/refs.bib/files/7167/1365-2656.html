Abstract

  1. In generalized mutualisms, species vary in the quality of services they provide to their partners directly via traits that affect partner fitness and indirectly via traits that influence interactions among mutualist species that play similar functional roles. Myrmecochory, or seed dispersal by ants, is a generalized mutualism with ant species varying in the quality of dispersal services they provide to their plant partners. Variation in ant species identity can directly impact seed dispersal patterns and plant community composition; however, we know less about how interactions among seed‐dispersing ant species indirectly influence plant partners.
  2. The invasive ant Myrmica rubra, is a high‐quality seed‐disperser in its native range that interacts with myrmecochores (ant‐dispersed plants) and the high‐quality seed disperser Aphaenogaster sp. in its invaded range. We use this system to examine how interactions between two functionally similar mutualist ant species influence the recruitment and community composition of ant‐dispersed plants.
  3. We performed a field mesocosm experiment and a laboratory behavioural experiment to compare discovery and dominance behaviours between ant species, and seed dispersal and seedling recruitment of four myrmecochore species among intraspecific interaction treatments of each ant species and an interspecific interaction treatment.
  4. We found that M. rubra was better at discovering and dispersing seeds, but Aphaenogaster sp. was dominantly aggressive over M. rubra. Interspecific interactions dampened seed dispersal relative to dispersal by the better disperser. Despite this dampening, we found no effect of interspecific interactions on seedling recruitment. However, community composition of seedlings in the interspecific interaction treatment was more similar to composition in the aggressively dominant ant (Aphaenogaster sp.) treatment than in the better discoverer ant M. rubra treatment.
  5. We show that interspecific interactions between mutualist species in the same functional guild affect the outcome of mutualistic interactions with partner species. Despite the native ant dispersing fewer seeds, its dominance over the subordinate (invasive) ant has the potential to allow for some level of biotic resistance against the effects of M. rubra on plant communities when these species coexist.

1 INTRODUCTION

Many mutualisms, such as pollination and seed dispersal, involve generalized multispecies networks of interacting species, where a group of multiple species exchange services with a partner group of multiple species (Bascompte, 2009; Stanton, 2003; Waser, Chittka, Price, Williams, & Ollerton, 1996). Generalized mutualisms are predicted to be robust to alterations in partner community assemblages, buffered by the re‐wiring of interactions and redundancy of links between species (Kaiser‐Bunbury, Muff, Memmott, Müller, & Caflisch, 2010; Memmott, Waser, & Price, 2004; Timóteo, Ramos, Vaughan, & Memmott, 2016). However, we know that not all mutualist species are equal in the quality of services they provide to their partners. Mutualist species vary in traits that affect the fitness of interacting partner species (Janzen, 1975; Oliveira, da Silva, & Martins, 1987; Palmer et al., 2010; Prior, Robinson, Meadley‐Dunphy, & Frederickson, 2015). Thus, even when changes to partner assemblages maintain links among species in networks of generalized mutualists, trait differences in interacting partners can affect mutualist communities in more complex ways by affecting the fitness and population growth of partner species.

In addition to traits of species within mutualist functional guilds (i.e. organisms that provide similar services or receive similar rewards) directly influencing the outcome of generalized mutualisms, interactions among mutualist species within functional guilds can influence interactions with partner species (Afkhami, Rudgers, & Stachowicz, 2014; Stanton, 2003). Mutualist species within a functional guild might engage in direct or indirect interactions that are antagonistic, neutral or positive, enhancing or dampening effects of mutualists on their partners (Afkhami et al., 2014; Spiesman & Gratton, 2016). For example, competitive interactions among mutualists within functional guilds dampen services to partners, such as plants competing for pollinators (Brown, Mitchell, & Graham, 2002). On the other hand, a lack of antagonistic interactions between multiple mutualist species can indirectly facilitate each other by directly benefiting shared partners (Afkhami et al., 2014). For example, coexistence among multiple mutualist ant species increases the fitness of their partner host plant that feeds back to benefit ant partners (Palmer et al., 2010; Palmer, Stanton, & Young, 2003). With the relatively recent appreciation of multispecies mutualisms (Afkhami et al., 2014; Brosi & Briggs, 2013; Palmer et al., 2010), we are now accumulating evidence for how variation in partner traits and interactions among partners affect mutualism outcomes. Understanding the complexities of multispecies mutualisms is pressing given the rate at which species are introduced or removed in mutualistic communities under anthropogenic change (Bascompte, 2009; Kiers, Palmer, Ives, Bruno, & Bronstein, 2010; Tylianakis, Didham, Bascompte, & Wardle, 2008).

Myrmecochory, or seed dispersal by ants, is a generalized mutualism common in several regions of the world, including in North American deciduous forests (Beattie & Culver, 1981; Berg, 1975; Garrido, Rey, Cerdá, & Herrera, 2002; Handel, Fisch, & Schatz, 1981; Lengyel, Gove, Latimer, Majer, & Dunn, 2010). Approximately 11,000 species of plants world‐wide possess lipid‐rich appendages (elaiosomes) that are attractive to foraging ants (Beattie & Culver, 1981; Gorb & Gorb, 2003; Lengyel et al., 2010). While myrmecochory is technically a generalized mutualism, we now know that in many systems, interactions are asymmetrical in that only a few ant species act as high‐quality seed dispersers for several species of plants (Camargo, Rodrigues, Piratelli, Oliveira, & Christianini, 2019; Gove, Majer, & Dunn, 2007; Manzaneda & Rey, 2009; Ness, Morin, & Giladi, 2009; Warren & Giladi, 2014; Zhu, Wang, & Codella, 2017). High‐quality seed‐dispersers are often large scavenging or omnivorous ants that forage individually and discover food quickly. They carry seeds to their nests, remove their elaiosomes and then deposit intact seeds in or near nests in sites conducive to germination (Giladi, 2006; Warren & Giladi, 2014). Poor seed‐dispersers are ant species that interact with myrmecochorous seeds, but fail to move seeds, move them short distances or to inhospitable locations or consume parts of or damage seeds (Giladi, 2006; Warren & Giladi, 2014). In ecosystems where myrmecochory is common, particular ant species or genera that are high‐quality seed‐dispersers serve the role of ‘keystone’ or major dispersers of myrmecochorous plants (Camargo et al., 2019; Gove et al., 2007; Manzaneda & Rey, 2009; Ness et al., 2009; Zhu et al., 2017).

Given the asymmetry and relative specialization of myrmecochory, it is often sensitive to changes in the identity of ant species interacting with seeds (Christian, 2001; Gómez & Oliveras, 2003; Ness et al., 2009; Prior et al., 2015; Rodriguez‐Cabal, Stuble, Guenard, Dunn, & Sanders, 2009). Good examples come from cases of ant species introduced into myrmecochore communities. Invasive ants, often cause a decline in the abundance of native high‐quality seed‐dispersing ants, while acting as poor seed‐dispersers themselves, with cascading effects on plant communities (Christian, 2001; Rodriguez‐Cabal et al., 2009; Stuble, Kirkman, & Carroll, 2009; Warren, McMillan, King, Chick, & Bradford, 2015). In cases where high‐quality seed‐dispersing ants are introduced, the outcome on plant communities is harder to predict as it depends on differences in traits and behaviour of the invasive seed‐dispersing ant compared to native seed‐dispersing ants. Myrmica rubra is a known seed disperser in its native range in Europe (Gorb & Gorb, 1999; Sernander, 1906; Servigne & Detrain, 2008) that has been introduced to North America, where it interacts with myrmecochorous plants and a native keystone disperser (Prior et al., 2015). We leverage this introduction to ask how mutualist ant partner identity directly and indirectly affects seed dispersal mutualisms.

High‐quality seed‐dispersing ant species are embedded within a community of ants, and their interactions with other ant species affect dispersal outcomes (Aranda‐Rickert & Fracchia, 2012; Leal, Neto, Oliveira, Andersen, & Leal, 2014; Tanaka & Tokuda, 2016). Competition among ant species for resources often causes coexisting species to fall along a discovery‐dominance tradeoff, where some species are good at discovering resources quickly before dominant ants arrive, while others are slower to find resources, but aggressively dominate resources once they arrive (Davidson, 1998; Fellers, 1987; Holway, 1999; Holldobler & Wilson, 1990; Savolainen & Vepsäläinen, 1988; except see Parr & Gibb, 2012). High‐quality seed‐dispersing ant species are often good ‘discoverers’ and subordinate to dominant ant species, finding and moving seeds to their nest quickly before dominant ants appear (Aranda‐Rickert & Fracchia, 2012; Fellers, 1987; Giladi, 2006; Stuble et al., 2013; Warren & Giladi, 2014). Seed‐dispersing ants change their behaviour in response to dominant ants to avoid competition, such as by foraging at certain times of the day or the season (Aranda‐Rickert & Fracchia, 2012; Stuble et al., 2013; Tanaka & Suzuki, 2016). However, we know little about how interactions between seed‐dispersing ant species affect seed dispersal and myrmecochore communities.

In its invaded range, M. rubra reduces the abundance of and is aggressively dominant over several ant species (Bertelsmeier et al., 2015; Garnas, Groden, & Drummond, 2014). However, we know little about its interaction with North America's major seed disperser, ants in the unresolved ‘rudis’ complex in the genus Aphaenogaster (except see Warren et al., 2019). Aphaenogaster sp. and M. rubra coexist and interact with myrmecochores in North American eastern deciduous forests (K. M. Prior, pers. obs.), and we use this system to explore how interspecific interactions between species within the same functional guild affect plant partner communities. While belonging to the same behavioural guild, these two ant species vary in several traits that directly affect their interactions with myrmecochores. In particular, in previous work, we found that ant species vary in how quickly they recruit to, remove, process and re‐disperse seeds outside of nests (Prior et al., 2015; Prior, Saxena, & Frederickson, 2014), along with where they deposit seeds in relation to their nests and adult and dispersed plants (Meadley‐Dunphy, Prior, & Frederickson, 2020). Myrmica rubra is better at discovering seeds than Aphaenogaster sp., finding and moving seeds more quickly (Meadley‐Dunphy, Prior, & Frederickson, 2016; Prior et al., 2014, 2015), and to more distant locations (Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2020). This previous work shows that the two seed‐dispersing ants directly impact and cause divergence of myrmecochore communities (Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2020; Prior et al., 2015).

Here we examine how intra‐ and interspecific interactions between Aphaenogaster sp. and M. rubra indirectly affect myrmecochore communities. To this end, we performed a laboratory behavioural experiment and a field mesocosm experiment to ask several questions. First, we seek to uncover the nature and direction of interspecific interactions between seed‐dispersing ant species. Second, we examine how interspecific interactions influence seed dispersal, seedling recruitment and seedling community composition. We predict that the presence, strength and direction of competition will affect outcomes. Specifically, if interactions between ant species are antagonistic and the stronger disperser is also aggressively dominant (M. rubra > Aphaenogaster sp.), then interspecific interactions should have minimal effects on dispersal relative to the stronger disperser. However, if the weaker disperser is dominant (Aphaenogaster sp. > M. rubra), then interspecific interactions should dampen dispersal. If interactions are not antagonistic, we predict no effect, or even an additive or synergistic effect, if different ant species interact with seeds of different plant species in complementary ways. Since the quality of dispersal depends not only on the number of seeds dispersed, but on how ‘well’ seeds are dispersed and how different plant species respond to deposition location (Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2020), the effect of interspecific interactions on dispersal may or may not translate into effects on seedling recruitment and community composition.

2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1 Study system

We collected the species used in the experiments in the Greater Toronto Area and near the Koffler Scientific Reserve (KSR; 44°02′N, 79°32′W), from several patches of deciduous and mixed forest. Our two seed‐dispersing ant species are the most abundant ants in these forests, with Aphaenogaster sp. being more abundant in the well‐drained soils of upland forest patches (Lubertazzi, 2012), and M. rubra in moister, more riparian forest patches (Chen, O'Sullivan, & Adams, 2018). While these two species were often the most abundant ant in their respective microhabitats, there were many locations where they overlapped, and we observed them coexisting within 1 m of each other (K. M. Prior, pers. obs.).

Aphaenogaster sp. occur at high densities in forest ecosystems (~1 colony/m2). Their colonies are monogynous, with colony sizes often in the range of ~200–600 workers, but occasionally reaching ~1,200 workers (Lubertazzi, 2012). Workers are medium‐sized (approx. 4 mm) ants, and colonies nest in rotten wood, under rocks or in the soil (Lubertazzi, 2012). They become active in the spring, and reproduce annually, when queens start new colonies after nuptial flights (Lubertazzi, 2012). There are multiple species of Aphaenogaster that are known to disperse seeds in northeastern North America, including A. rudis and A. picea, which are polyphyletic and challenging to delineate via morphology (DeMarco & Cognato, 2016; Umphrey, 1996). Given that these two putative species are unresolved, we refer to our species as Aphaenogaster sp., with it likely being one of the two named, unresolved species. Myrmica rubra is an introduced ant from Europe that typically forms large (300–10,000 workers), polygynous (1–200 queens), polydomous colonies, also in rotten wood, in the soil, or under rocks (Groden, Drummond, Garnas, & Franceour, 2005). Myrmica rubra is a habitat generalist occurring in fields, but also in riparian deciduous forests (Chen et al., 2018; Warren et al., 2019). Workers are also medium‐sized (approx. 4 mm). Queens start new colonies independently or by budding off from larger colonies with some workers (Walin, Seppa, & Sundstrom, 2001). Both ant species feed on a variety of food sources including insects and elaiosomes; M. rubra also consumes honeydew and extrafloral nectar (Fisher, Olzant, Wanek, & Mayer, 2005; Fokuhl, Heinze, & Poschlod, 2007; Lubertazzi, 2012; Servigne & Detrain, 2008). We have observed both ant species overlapping with several species of woodland myrmecochores, including the species that we use in this study: three native plant species (Asarum canadense, Anemone acutiloba, Sanguinaria canadensis), and the exotic plant species, Chelidonium majus.

2.2 Laboratory interaction experiment

Ant colonies of each species were collected from KSR and other sites in the Greater Toronto area. To test the effect of ant partner identity on myrmecochory, we controlled for colony size by standardizing colonies to one queen, 250 workers and 50 brood. While M. rubra can have larger colonies than Aphaenogaster sp., especially in the invaded range, there is a wide range in M. rubra colony sizes and naturally occurring colonies of both species overlap in colony sizes (see above). Each colony was housed in a plastic nest container (17 × 15 × 12 cm) with sides covered with Fluon (Insect‐a‐Slip [Bioquip]) and a single test tube filled with 1/3 distilled water, plugged with cotton, and covered with aluminum foil that served as a nest. Colonies were fed 0.2 g of standardized, modified Bhatkar–Whitcomb diet (Dussutour & Simpson, 2008) three times a week and housed in an environmental chamber set to 23°C and LD 12:12 hr cycle. To reduce variation in nutritional status among colonies collected from the wild (also see Prior et al., 2014, 2015), colonies were not fed for 1 week prior to trials. While this could affect colony foraging and aggressive behaviour (Grangier & Lester, 2014), it ensured all colonies started the experiment in a similar nutritional state. Seeds of four myrmecochore species (see above) were collected when fruits were naturally dehiscing in the field at KSR (mid‐June 2013; Gordon, Meadley‐Dunphy, Prior, & Frederickson, 2019), and stored at –20°C until use (for approx. 2 months). Previous studies show that seed‐dispersing ants do not prefer fresh over previously frozen seeds (Servigne & Detrain, 2008).

The laboratory experiment was conducted in the fall of 2013. Ant colonies were randomly assigned to one of five treatments: an intraspecific Aphaenogaster sp. treatment (hereafter, ‘ARAR’), an intraspecific M. rubra treatment (‘MRMR’) and an interspecific interaction treatment (‘ARMR’). The other two treatments were single‐colony ‘baseline’ treatments of either one colony of Aphaenogaster sp. (AR) or one colony of M. rubra (MR). We assigned 10 colonies to each treatment. Paired colonies in the MRMR treatment were from different collection sites (<10 km away) to reduce the likelihood that we unwittingly tested interactions between the same or closely related polygynous colonies (Chen et al., 2018). Colony containers were attached to a central foraging arena (43 × 34 × 14 cm) via plastic tubing (19 mm diameter), at first stuffed with cotton to prevent ants from entering the foraging arena. Ten seeds of each plant species were placed on small (6 cm) Petri dishes (‘depots’) in the centre of the arena. Ants were allowed to enter the arena, and we counted the number of ants in the foraging arena, the number of ants in contact with seeds and the number of seeds removed from depots every hour for 5 hr. At the end of 24 hr, we counted the number of seeds removed from depots, the number of dead ants of each species in the arena, and in each colony container and test tube and the number of seeds of each plant species in nest containers and test tubes. Arenas were cleaned with ethanol between trials.

2.3 Mesocosm interaction experiment

In spring of 2012, we set up mesocosms in a red pine plantation at KSR that provided even shading. In each mesocosm, we planted adults of four myrmecochores, added conspecific seeds to the bases of adult plants and introduced one or two colonies of ants following the same combinations for ant treatments as in the laboratory. We measured seed removal by ants, and 1 year later, documented the patterns of resulting seeding recruitment. Mesocosms consisted of a plastic pool (1.2 m in diameter, 45 cm deep) that was dug into the forest floor. This is an appropriate scale to study myrmecochory as dispersal in this system by Aphaenogaster sp. happens within 1 m of the parent plant (Canner, Dunn, Giladi, & Gross, 2012). Dispersal distances for M. rubra have not been directly measured in natural conditions, but other Myrmica sp. move seeds up to 1.4 m (Kjellsson, 1985). Pools had drainage holes covered with fine mesh. Fine mesh was also sewn to thick plastic sheeting (that was painted with Fluon), which was sealed to the bottom of the mesocosm. Pools were filled with soil (native topsoil, compost, and peat (Gro‐max [Arnts, Whitby, Ontario]) that did not contain a seed bank. This was confirmed in our control mesocosms, where myrmecochores only germinated where seeds were placed (see below). Two adults each of native plants (A. acutiloba, A. canadense, and S. canadensis), and two adults of the exotic plant, C. majus, sourced from local nurseries were planted down the middle of each mesocosm (seed pods were removed from these plants). Mesh was sealed at the top, and the mesh and the pools kept the ants and seeds added in (see below) and prevented access to mesocosms by granivores, herbivores and other plants (see Prior et al., 2015).

Mesocosms were 2 m apart in a 6 × 7 array with 42 mesocosms in total. We assigned six treatments in a stratified random design, each with seven replicates. The treatments were the same as the laboratory experiment (AR, MR, ARAR, MRMR and ARMR; see above), but we also included a no ant ‘control’ treatment. Ant treatments reflect the natural density of focal ant species; both can have one to multiple ant colonies within 1 m2 (Groden et al., 2005; Lubertazzi, 2012), and we have observed nests of the two species within 1 m of each other (K. M. Prior, pers. obs.). Ants were collected from KSR and surrounding sites. Colonies were standardized to contain a single queen, 350 workers and up to 100 brood (see above rational). Colonies were transferred to mesocosms on 13 June 2012 under rotting logs that were placed perpendicular to the line of adult plants. Seeds were harvested from plants adjacent to the study site at KSR and added to mesocosms between 14 and 22 June 2012 (within 1 week after fruits dehisced and seeds were collected). We chose to add numbers of seeds to the base of the adult plants to reflect interspecific variation in seed production (see Prior et al., 2015). We added 30 conspecific seeds each of A. acutiloba, A. canadense and S. canadensis within a 10 cm diameter of the base of each adult plant (60 seeds of each species in total). We added 150 seeds of C. majus to the base of each adult plant. Seeds of C. majus are small and dark, making them difficult to see on the soil surface. So, to measure dispersal, we placed 30 of the 150 seeds on white filter paper at the base of each C. majus plant to measure dispersal. After 3 days, we dumped the non‐dispersed seeds onto the soil under the adult plant so they could germinate. Ants were continuously fed once a week with honey water and ant diet. In the fall, toxic ants baits were placed in the mesocosms to kill the ants, which was necessary to overwinter the mesocosms as we lowered mesh to allow for natural snowfall on the soil (see Prior et al., 2015).

After seeds were added to mesocosms, for 3 days, we counted the number of seeds remaining in the 10 cm diameter rings at the base of plants. The majority of removal occurred within 24 hr of putting out the seeds (Prior et al., 2015). One year after seeds were added (June 2013), we counted dispersed and undispersed germinated seedlings. Chelidonium majus started to germinate the year seeds were added (in fall 2012), and in 2013 there were newly germinating seedlings, along with 1‐year‐old seedlings. The three native plant species all germinated the year after the seeds were added to the mesocosms (June 2013). Each plant was marked, and its location in the mesocosm denoted.

2.4 Statistical analysis

For the laboratory experiment, we performed GLMs with negative binomial distributions (to correct for overdispersion) to compare the cumulative number of ants in the foraging arena over 5 hr, the number of dead ants in the arena and nests after the completion of the trials (24 hr), and the number of seeds removed from laboratory depots after 5 and 24 hr among interaction treatments (ARAR, MRMR, ARMR). We fitted a linear model to the average number of ants (log‐transformed) in contact with seeds in laboratory arenas over 5 hr among interaction treatments. As planned comparisons, we ran linear models or GLMs on the difference between different ant species engaging in behaviours that we were able to observe in the ARMR treatment (i.e. number of ants in arena, ants handling seeds and dead ants, seeds dispersed to respective ant nests) with arena replicate as a random factor. For this ARMR analysis, we performed a Bonferroni correction to assess significant differences between ant species.

In the mesocosm experiment, we also conducted GLMs to compare the number of seeds removed from the base of adult plants and the number of seedlings that recruited (both ant‐dispersed and total) among interaction treatments. We used Tukey's post hoc tests to evaluate the statistical significance of differences between treatments.

To assess if intra‐ and interspecific interactions changed behaviours, seed dispersal and seedling recruitment relative to the baselines of each ant species (i.e. in one‐colony treatments), we calculated effect sizes of interaction treatments relative to single‐colony treatments in both experiments. We calculated log response ratios (lnRR) as the natural log of replicates in an interaction treatment/the mean of a single ant colony treatment (either Aphaenogaster or M. rubra). We calculated mean effect sizes for each interaction treatment versus a single colony treatment along with confidence intervals (CI). When CI did not overlap zero, we noted this as a significant effect of the interaction treatment relative to baseline ant treatment. Positive effect sizes on discovery and dispersal responses reveal that interactions increased responses relative to baselines, while a negative effect reveal that intra‐ or interspecific interactions had a dampening effect. For dominance responses (i.e. the number of dead ants), positive effects reveal increased antagonistic interactions relative to baselines. We reported mean effect sizes, CI and if mean effect sizes are significantly higher or lower than zero for the ARAR and ARMR treatments relative to the baseline Aphaenogaster treatment (lnRRAR) and for the MRMR and ARMR treatments relative to the baseline M. rubra treatment (lnRRMR; see Supporting Information). For responses in which we were able to observe which ant species in the ARMR treatment engaged in responses (i.e. in laboratory trials), we calculated effect sizes of each ant species relative to baseline treatments. When comparing the number of seeds found in the nest of each ant species in the ARMR treatment, we calculated effect sizes relative to the average number of seeds found in each ant nest in the intraspecific Aphaenogaster and M. rubra treatments.

Finally, we conducted principal component analyses (PCA) on the number of seeds dispersed (found in ant nests after 24 hr) of each plant species in the laboratory experiment, on the number of total recruited seedlings in mesocosms, and on the number of dispersed recruited seedlings of each plant species in mesocosms. We created biplots using the first two principal components, which in each case explained over 50% of the variation (Table S4). We then conducted linear models and Tukey's post hoc tests to compare PC1 and PC2 among interaction treatments. We report loadings that make up each component (see Supporting Information).

Analyses were conducted in R version 3.3.3 (2017), using packages devtools, vegan, mass, multcomp and ggbiplot2 (Hothorn et al., 2019; Oksanen et al., 2019; Ripley et al., 2019; Wickham, Hester, & Change, 2019).

3 RESULTS

In the laboratory experiment, there were a greater number of ants in the foraging arena in the MRMR treatment than in the ARAR treatment (χ2,27 = 31.68, p = 0.027; Figure 1A; see full statistical tables in Supporting Information, Table S1). We found no difference in the number of ants in arenas between the two ant species in the ARMR treatment; however, there were fewer M. rubra ants in arenas in the ARMR treatment than when M. rubra foraged alone (i.e. relative to the M. rubra baseline treatment; Figure 1B; Table S3). Myrmica rubra spent more time in contact with seeds than Aphaenogaster sp.; more ants were in contact with seeds in both treatments containing M. rubra than in the intraspecific Aphaenogaster treatment (F2,27 = 9.48, p = 0.001; Figure 1C). Myrmica rubra spent less time in contact with seeds in the presence of Aphaenogaster sp. compared to when foraging alone (Figure 1D; Table S3). Interestingly, Aphaenogaster sp. spent more time in contact with seeds when in the presence of M. rubra than when foraging alone (Figure 1D). There was a greater number of dead ants in the interspecific interaction treatment compared to both intraspecific interaction treatments (with more dead ants in the ARAR treatment than in the MRMR treatment; χ2,27 = 30.94, p < 0.001; Figure 1E). There were more dead M. rubra ants than Aphaenogaster ants in the ARMR treatment (Figure 1F).

In the laboratory experiment, seed removal from depots was higher in the MRMR treatment than in the ARAR treatment, with the ARMR treatment being intermediate after 5 hr (χ2,27 = 31.41, p = 0.022; Figure 2A), but there were no differences after 24 hr (χ2,27 = 32.40, p = 0.429; Table S1). In the interspecific treatment, there were a greater number of seeds dispersed to the Aphaenogaster sp. nest containers after 24 hr compared to the M. rubra nests (χ2,18 = 22.29, p = 0.028; Figure 2B), with more seeds ending up in Aphaenogaster sp. nests in the interspecific interaction treatment than in single Aphaenogaster sp. nests in the ARAR treatment and fewer in M. rubra nests than in single M. rubra nests in the MRMR treatment (Table S3; Figure 2B).

In the mesocosm experiment, there were also more seeds removed from the base of adult plants in the MRMR treatment than in the ARAR treatment, with the interspecific treatment being intermediate (χ2,18 = 21.42, p = 0.001; Figure 2C). Seed removal in both the ARAR and ARMR treatments was enhanced relative to the Aphaenogaster baseline treatment, and removal in the MRMR treatment was also enhanced relative to the baseline M. rubra treatment. Removal in the ARMR treatment was lower than when M. rubra was foraging alone, but the decrease was not significant (Table S3; Figure 2C). Recruitment of dispersed seedlings in mesocosms followed similar trends to seed removal, being higher in the MRMR treatment than the ARAR treatment, with the ARMR treatment intermediate (χ2,18 = 21.12, p = 0.001; Figure 3A). Similar to removal, both interaction treatments (ARAR and ARMR) enhanced recruitment of dispersed seedlings relative to the baseline Aphaenogaster treatment, but in this case recruitment of dispersed seedlings was significantly reduced in the ARMR treatment relative to M. rubra only mesocosms (Table S3). Non‐dispersed seedlings also recruited in mesocosms, with no difference in total seedling recruitment among treatments (χ2,18 = 21.37, p = 0.475; Figure 3B).

In the laboratory experiment, PCA of dispersed seeds (i.e. the number of seeds found in nest containers) showed no patterns with respect to which seeds were dispersed under different ant treatments (Table S4; Figure S1a). In the mesocosm experiment, however, we found differences in PC1 (48%) among interaction treatments in community composition of recruited dispersed seedlings (differences between the ARAR and the ARMR treatment, and the ARAR and the MRMR treatment, but not between the ARMR and MRMR treatment). This composition change was driven by recruitment of dispersed seedlings being higher in mesocosms with M. rubra (Table S4; Figure S1c). For total plant recruitment in mesocosms (dispersed and non‐dispersed seedlings), we found no difference among treatments in PC1 (38%; F2,18 = 0.23, p = 0.797), but a difference in PC2 (31%; F2,18 = 5.83, p = 0.011; Figure 4), with the ARAR and ARMR treatments having similar communities with relatively higher recruitment of native plant species (Loadings: A. canadense = 0.54, A. acutiloba = 0.77, S. canadensis = 0.07) and the MRMR treatment having relatively higher recruitment of the exotic plant (C. majus = −0.31; Figure 4, Table S4).

4 DISCUSSION

Our previous work shows that the identity of ant species in the same functional guild directly affects communities of partner plants (Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2020; Prior et al., 2015). Here we show that indirect effects mediated by interactions between ant species in the same functional guild also affect plant communities. Differences in traits and behaviours of seed‐dispersing ant species directly result in differences in seed removal rates, seed deposition location and seedling recruitment (Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2020; Prior et al., 2015). In addition, interspecific interactions between ant species affect foraging and dispersal behaviours with cascading effects on seed dispersal and seedling recruitment patterns. The two seed‐dispersing ant species differed in their discovery‐dominance behaviours. Myrmica rubra was better at discovering and dispersing seeds, but Aphaenogaster sp. was aggressively dominant over M. rubra. Interestingly, when the seed‐dispersing ants interacted, the dominant ant had larger effects on mutualistic outcomes than the better discoverer, by affecting the discoverer's foraging and dispersal behaviours. Seed dispersal by the better discoverer was dampened by the aggressively dominant ant relative to when the better discoverer foraged alone. These interaction effects on dispersal behaviour influenced seedling community composition such that plant communities in mixed ant and dominant ant treatments were more similar to each other than to plant communities in the discoverer ant treatment. In this case, the better discoverer, M. rubra, is an invasive ant, and the aggressively dominant ant, Aphaenogaster sp., is a native ant, suggesting that Aphaenogaster sp. has the potential to provide some level of biotic resistance to the effects of M. rubra on seed dispersal and communities of ant‐dispersed plants in its invaded range.

Although both M. rubra and Aphaenogaster sp. are good at discovering and dispersing seeds relative to the broader ant community (Gorb & Gorb, 1999; Ness et al., 2009; Prior et al., 2015), our results show that M. rubra was better at discovering and dispersing seeds relative to Aphaenogaster sp., supporting previous studies. In previous work, we found that M. rubra dispersed more seeds than Aphaenogaster sp. in natural settings (along with laboratory and mesocosm settings, similar to this study; Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2016; Prior et al., 2014, 2015). Garnas et al. (2014) found that M. rubra recruited to baits faster than native ant species in its invasive range in Maine. Gammans, Drummond, and Groden (2018) found that M. rubra moved more seeds than the native ant species, M. detrinodis and Crematogaster cerasi, also in forests in Maine. In contrast, Warren et al. (2019) did not find that M. rubra recruited more quickly to Drosophila prey than Aphaenogaster sp. However, the weight of evidence indicates that M. rubra is better at discovering food resources, including seeds, more quickly than Aphaenogaster sp.

While both of these ant species (or genera) are known to be subordinate relative to other ant species in communities (Fellers, 1987; Stuble et al., 2013), we found that Aphaenogaster sp. falls higher along the dominance‐discovery continuum than M. rubra. Specifically, the number of dead ants after 24 hr was highest in the ARMR treatment, with 3.7× more deaths of M. rubra compared to Aphaenogaster sp. in this treatment. Warren et al. (2019) conducted laboratory aggression trials between M. rubra and A. picea, and similarly found that the majority of deaths were of M. rubra when paired with A. picea. We also found that more Aphaenogaster sp. ants killed each other than M. rubra ants in intraspecific trials, which is expected given the evidence for reduced intraspecific aggression in M. rubra, especially in invasive populations (Garnas et al., 2014).

Dispersal in mesocosms after 5 hr in laboratory trials was intermediate in the ARMR treatment between the MRMR and the ARAR treatments. This intermediate dispersal could be a result of additive effects of the two ant species (high dispersal by M. rubra + low dispersal by Aphaenogaster sp.) or due to altered dispersal behaviour by either of the ant species in the presence of the other ant species. We found several lines of evidence suggesting that antagonistic interactions between the ant species influenced their foraging and seed dispersal behaviours. Myrmica rubra reduced the time it handled seeds in the presence of Aphaenogaster sp., which was due to ants being directly attacked by Aphaenogaster sp., preventing them from interacting with seeds. Also, fewer M. rubra ants moved into the foraging arena in the presence of the dominant ant. Interestingly, Aphaenogaster sp. spent more time in contact with seeds in the presence of M. rubra, compared to when foraging alone. Several other studies have shown that ants alter their foraging behaviour in the presence of other ant species, both because subordinate ants dampen foraging in the presence of dominant ants, and dominant ants increase foraging in the presence of other ant species (Aranda‐Rickert & Fracchia, 2012; Sanders & Gordon, 2000; Tanaka & Tokuda, 2016). Previous studies also found that ants alter their foraging behaviours by becoming less ‘picky’ for high‐quality resources in the presence of competitors (Chittka, Skorupski, & Raine, 2009; Sanders & Gordon, 2000). In previous work, we found that both Aphaenogaster sp. and M. rubra prefer seeds with high oleic acid concentrations in elaiosomes (C. majus > A. acutiloba > A. canadense > S. canadensis; Prior et al., 2015). Here we found that preferences for seeds of different plant species did not change in the presence of competitors (Figure S1A).

Total seedling recruitment (dispersed and non‐dispersed seedlings) did not differ among interaction treatments, but we found differences in seedling community composition. Seedling composition in the ARMR treatment was more similar to the dominant ant treatment (ARAR) than to the discoverer ant treatment (MRMR). Relatively more native plants (of all three species) established in the ARAR and ARMR treatments than in the MRMR treatment and the exotic plant dominated in the MRMR treatment. The three native species are all shade‐tolerant perennials, and C. majus is a fast‐growing exotic plant. Myrmica rubra moved more seeds further from adult plants and in a more dispersed pattern, which disproportionately favoured recruitment and growth of the fast‐growing C. majus that established well when released from intraspecific competition (Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2020). This high recruitment of the exotic plant in the MRMR treatment negatively affected native plant survivorship and growth, such that the MRMR treatment had relatively higher abundances of the exotic plant compared to the ARAR and ARMR treatments, and the ARAR and ARMR treatments had higher abundances of native plants relative to the MRMR treatment (see also Meadley‐Dunphy et al., 2020; Prior et al., 2015). Differences in both the number of seeds moved and where the ants deposited seeds affected seedling community composition, with the dampening effects by the dominant ant on the subordinate ant causing plant communities in the interspecific ant treatment to be more like plant communities in the presence of the dominant ant than of the superior discoverer.

Interactions at resources among ant species are common, where ants often engage in aggressive interactions or avoid each other when they come in contact (Fellers, 1987; Holway, 1999; Sanders & Gordon, 2000). Ants also avoid contact with other ant species by altering their foraging behaviour, such as foraging at different times or in different locations (Aranda‐Rickert & Fracchia, 2012; Stuble et al., 2013). To our knowledge, no study has yet measured how often Aphaenogaster sp. and M. rubra come in direct contact foraging for seeds in nature. However, we observed that ants altered their foraging behaviour in the presence of one another in laboratory trials and in 1 m‐diameter mesocosms. Even if these ant species do not engage in aggressive interactions where seeds are deposited under adult plants, they may be altering their foraging behaviour to avoid foraging at the same time. In previous work, we found that M. rubra preferred to forage in the morning and Aphaenogaster sp., in the late afternoon, but that both ant species foraged overnight (Prior et al., 2015). Thus, these species seem to have some overlap in their foraging times, meaning that they could engage in aggressive interactions at resources. Observing how these species interact in natural communities would be a good avenue for future work.

Colonies of both ant species range in size, with polygynous colonies of M. rubra reaching larger colony sizes than Aphaenogaster sp.'s monogynous colonies, especially in M. rubra's invaded range (Garnas et al., 2014). These species overlap in colony size at the low end of M. rubra's colony size. Given that these ants seem to have different habitat preferences, where they overlap or interact in nature, it might be over a range of colony sizes. For example, M. rubra seems to form particularly large colonies in more riparian‐forested areas (Chen et al., 2018), with smaller or less dense colonies in drier upland soil forested areas where Aphaenogaster sp. colonies are more common (Lubertazzi, 2012; K. M. Prior, pers. obs.). We chose to standardize colony size in this experiment to examine the effects of inherent differences in ant traits and behaviours on dispersal and dispersal outcomes. Given that invasive ants can break the dominance‐discovery trade‐off by having particularly large colonies in invaded ranges (Holway, 1999), an interesting next step would be to manipulate colony size to examine if M. rubra becomes dominant over Aphaenogaster sp. when it has larger colony sizes.

While it is common for mutualisms to occur in the context of other community interactions (Bascompte, 2009; Stanton, 2003), we are only starting to understand how both direct and indirect effects of multiple mutualists affect mutualistic outcomes (Afkhami et al., 2014). Here we show that in a diffuse mutualism, partners within the same functional guild are not redundant and that partner identity matters. Mutualist species with different traits directly and indirectly, via within functional guild interactions, affect the abundance and distribution of their partner species. Thus, changes in partner identity can affect the fitness of mutualistic partners via both direct and indirect interactions that can scale up to affect mutualistic communities.

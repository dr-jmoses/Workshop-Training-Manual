    Abstract

    1. Herbivorous insects can be classified into several trophic guilds with different levels of specialisation on their host plants, which may influence the topological structure of their trophic networks. The present study tested the hypothesis that the structure of plant–herbivore networks differs between guilds of galling, sucking, and chewing insects.

    2. Six areas of Neotropical savannas were studied in two localities in the North of the state of Minas Gerais, Brazil. In each area, interactions between plant and insect species were used to build networks for different guilds.

    3. In total, 18 plant–herbivore networks were built, comprising 317 insect morphospecies, 50 plant species, and 489 distinct interactions. The networks were characterised using species richness and different network topological measures (connectance, modularity, nestedness, and specialisation).

    4. The results obtained showed no difference in species richness, network size, and connectance between distinct insect herbivore guilds. However, it was found that modularity was higher for exophagous than galling insect networks and nestedness was higher for chewers than for other guilds. On the other hand, galling insect networks showed higher specialisation than exophagous insect networks, and sucking insect networks were more specialised than chewing insect networks.

    5. The findings of the present study indicate that, although species richness did not differ between insect guilds of herbivores in Neotropical savannas, the topological structure of networks is sensitive to biological and ecological differences between these herbivore groups. The present study stands out as the first to systematically compare the network structure of different herbivore guilds in Neotropical savannas.

    Introduction

    In recent years, the investigation of complex ecological networks has enabled a significant advancement in the understanding of interspecific interactions (Poisot et al., 2016; Tylianakis & Morris, 2017; Landi et al., 2018). Network‐based approaches have been used increasingly for the analysis of community structure because they enable simultaneous analysis of questions ranging from the species level to the community level (Delmas et al., 2019). Studies on trophic networks, for example, can include information about richness, frequency, and abundance of trophic interactions, as well as provide a clear functional description of the community structure (Tylianakis et al., 2007; Nobre et al., 2016). A type of well‐studied trophic network involves interactions between herbivorous insects and host plants (Lewinsohn et al., 2006; Sauve et al., 2013); this system shows a great diversity of species in several trophic interactions (Lewinsohn et al., 2006; Novotný et al., 2010). Herbivorous insects have different life‐forms and feeding habits (Lewinsohn et al., 2005; Araújo, 2013) and can be classified into several trophic guilds (Lewinsohn et al., 2005; Novotný et al., 2010). Thus, herbivorous insects of different trophic guilds show different degrees of intimacy with their host plants (Novotný et al., 2010) and form ecological networks with different structures (Forister et al., 2015). Although this is an old and comprehensive theme (Root, 1973), it is still challenging to investigate the different guilds because of their different sampling methods (Novotný & Basset, 2005) and most studies focus on only one specific group (Novotný & Basset, 1999; Morais et al., 2011; Hardy & Otto, 2014).

    Herbivorous insects can be divided into two life‐forms related to both their development and feeding habits: endophages and exophages (Araújo, 2013; Araújo et al., 2015). Endophagous insects are herbivores that, during their immature phase, live inside plant tissues, where they develop and feed, and tend to be highly specialised in their hosts (Novotný et al., 2010; Araújo, 2013; Nobre et al., 2016). Among endophagous guilds are galler insects, which induce galls in plants (Shorthouse et al., 2005). Galls are modifications of plant tissues, such as plant tumors in which immature insects (larvae or nymphs) develop (Stone & Schönrogge, 2003), obtaining food and protection against adverse environmental conditions and natural enemies (Fernandes & Price, 1988; Stone & Schönrogge, 2003). These insects are highly specialised and generally associated with a single or few phylogenetically close plants (Carneiro et al., 2009; Araújo et al., 2017).

    By contrast with endophages, exophagous insects are free‐living herbivores that move within and between plants in search for resources (Neves et al., 2010; Araújo, 2013). They generally have a lower level of host intimacy and specialisation in comparison with endophagous insects (Gaston et al., 1992). However, different groups of exophages may have different levels of specialisation (Novotný et al., 2010). Exophages may constitute different guilds, such as chewing herbivores, with well‐developed jaws specialised in cutting and grinding plant tissue (Raupp, 1985), or sucking herbivores, with a modified oral apparatus or stylet to suck plant sap (Ribeiro et al., 1994). Chewing herbivores generally have a broad diet of host plant species and are considered to comprise the guild with the lowest proportion of specialist species (Novotný et al., 2002; Novotný et al., 2010). In the case of sucking herbivores, the stylet needs to overcome the physical protective barriers of plants, such as waxes and trichomes, to access plant tissues; thus, they tend to be more specialised than chewing herbivores (Novotný et al., 2010).

    Tropical zones provide an optimal environment for studies on specificity patterns of ecological interactions (Novotný & Basset, 2005). The Neotropical savanna (Brazilian Cerrado), for example, is a biome with a very diverse vegetation (Simon et al., 2009), which influences the distribution of phytophagous insects and their guilds (Leal et al., 2016; Ferreira et al., 2017). Despite this variety of resources to be explored, the plant community of Neotropical savannas shows high levels of sclerophylly (Ribeiro & Fernandes, 2000; Silva & Batalha, 2011). Sclerophylly can affect endophagous and exophagous herbivore guilds differently (Raupp, 1985; Fernandes & Price, 1988; Ribeiro & Basset, 2007; Neves et al., 2010). Additionally, other factors may also influence the community structure of different insect guilds in Neotropical savannas; for example, abundance and richness of the plant community (Araújo et al., 2013; Nobre et al., 2016) and the presence, abundance, and composition of natural enemies (Morais et al., 1999). The importance of these factors may vary between different herbivore guilds according to their dependence level on host plants; endophagous insects exhibit a greater dependence on bottom‐up effects (Araújo, 2013), whereas exophagous insects are more influenced by top‐down factors (Morais et al., 1999). The Cerrado has been the scenario of relevant studies aiming at a better understanding of how interspecific interactions shape communities (Del‐Claro & Torezan‐Silingardi, 2009), including studies focusing on the specialisation of herbivorous insects (Prado & Lewinsohn, 2004; Morais et al., 2011; Nobre et al., 2016).

    The present study aimed to characterise plant–herbivore networks composed of different insect guilds in Neotropical savannas. We used different topological descriptors (connectance, modularity, nestedness, and specialisation) to characterise plant–herbivore networks. In this sense, we hypothesised that the network structure differs between galling, sucking, and chewing insect guilds. We expected that galling insect networks would have a higher specialisation, higher modularity, lower connectance, and lower nestedness than networks of exophagous herbivores. Among the exophages, we expected that sucking insect networks would have more specialised, more modular, less connected, and less nested networks than chewing insects.

    Materials and methods

    Study areas

    The present study was carried out in six areas of Neotropical savannas: three in the municipality of Montes Claros and three in the municipality of Jequitaí, northern Minas Gerais State, Brazil (Fig. 1). The areas sampled were approximately 5 km apart. The climate of the region is tropical dry (Aw in the Köppen system), characterised by well‐defined rainy periods, an average temperature of 24.2 °C, and an average annual rainfall of 1000 mm (Alvares et al., 2013). The region is a Cerrado‐Caatinga ecotone, with a prevalence of plant physiognomies and species from the Cerrado (Ribeiro & Walter, 2008). Neotropical savannas have a very diverse flora with a high richness of endemic species (Mendonça et al., 1998; Felfili et al., 2004). All of the studied areas are characterised by similar pristine vegetation classified as typical savanna (cerrado sensu stricto) dominated by sclerophyllous plants that occur in poor soils (Ribeiro & Walter, 2008).

    Plant and insect sampling

    We set up five plots (10 × 10 m), with a total of 30 plots in each of the six study areas. In these plots, we selected all woody plants with a circumference ≥ 10 cm at 1.3 m above ground. All of the selected plants were numbered and identified. Plant sampling was carried out in the rainy (December 2017) and dry seasons (June 2018). Insect collection was carried out in these two seasons for a better characterisation of herbivorous assemblages because seasonality can affect the occurrence of certain insects (Silva et al., 2011).

    Exophagous insects were collected using the beating method with an entomological umbrella (Ribeiro et al., 2005; Neves et al., 2010). We used a modified beat cloth, consisting of a white cloth (100 × 80 cm). During collections, we selected randomly three branches of the plant and beat each branch ten times. The insects that fell into the entomological umbrella were captured with entomological tweezers, killed, and conditioned in plastic pots containing 70% alcohol. Only insects larger than 2 mm were collected. Because galling insects occur inside plant tissues, the collection was performed through active search (Araújo et al., 2013), in which we examined each plant sample for 5 min in search for insect galls. The active search was performed after exophagous collection to avoid interference. The branches containing insect galls were collected manually and stored in plastic bags. All of the insect samples were recorded per plant, plot, and area and sent to the laboratory for sorting and identification. Plants were identified in the field with the help of specialised literature. The plants that could not be identified were collected and taken to the laboratory for identification with the consultation of specialised literature and specialists.

    Exophagous insects were sorted into morphospecies using external morphological characteristics and identified to the lowest possible taxonomic level with identification keys (Triplehorn & Johnson, 2005; Carrano‐Moreira, 2014). After identification, the insects were separated into food guilds, according to their mouthparts and feeding habits (Neves et al., 2010). With this approach, we selected only the insect taxa known as herbivores, according to the specialised literature (Carrano‐Moreira, 2014), in accordance with previously used protocols (Neves et al., 2010). Insect taxa from other non‐herbivorous trophic guilds (e.g. predators, pollinators, fungivores, and parasitoids) were excluded from the study. We considered sucking herbivores, adult and juvenile insects of the orders Hemiptera and Thysanoptera, and chewing herbivores, adult insects of the orders Coleoptera, Orthoptera, and Phasmida, and larvae of the order Lepidoptera (Triplehorn & Johnson, 2005). Galling insects were photographed and morphotyped according to the external morphology of the gall (Gonçalves‐Alvim & Fernandes, 2001).

    Measurements of the plant–herbivore network structure

    We created quantitative adjacency matrices using the data sampled for each guild. In exophagous networks, we quantified interactions by counting the number of insect individuals recorded in each plant species, whereas, in galling insect networks, we counted the number of individuals (i.e. galls) recorded on the branch collected. Thus, we constructed three interaction networks (one for each guild) for each of the six areas sampled, in a total of 18 networks (Fig. 2). In the area 1M (Fig. 1), the galling network was composed by a single plant species and a single galling species (network G1M, Fig. 2) and, for this reason, it was not possible to calculate the topological parameters for this network. We characterised each network according to plant richness, herbivore richness, and network size (plant and herbivore richness), which were quantified using the grouplevel function of the bipartite package (Dormann et al., 2008) in the r environment (R Development Core Team, 2018).

    We used three topological descriptors to describe the structure of networks: connectance (C), modularity (M), nestedness (NODF), and specialisation (H2) (Landi et al., 2018). Connectance is a measure of the relativised average degree of the network, which represents the number of possible interactions that are actually accomplished (Dunne et al., 2002; Bluthgen et al., 2007; Dormann et al., 2009). Nestedness was calculated using the Nestedness metric based on Overlap and Decreasing Fill (NODF) (Almeida‐Neto et al., 2008). The NODF accounts for the paired overlap and the decreasing fill of the matrix representing an interaction network and takes values between 0 (perfectly non‐nested) and 100 (perfectly nested). The NODF was calculated using the nested function of the bipartite package (Dormann et al., 2008). Modularity (M) is a measure of the existence of modules or compartments of densely connected species within the network (Lewinsohn et al., 2006). It can also be used as a measure of specialisation because more modular networks are more specialised (Pinho et al., 2017). We calculated the modularity using the computeModules function of the bipartite package (Dormann et al., 2008).

    We also used the specialisation index H2, which is a quantitative measure of specialisation at the network level and ranges from 0 (total generalisation) to 1 (total specialisation) (Bluthgen et al., 2006). This index measures the degree of specialisation of a network where one species interacts with another in the proportion of its total frequency of occurrence in the network (Jacobsen et al., 2018). In other words, it calculates the difference between observed and expected interactions within the network. The higher the level of selectivity of the species, the greater the H2. This measure was calculated using the H2fun function of the bipartite package (Dormann et al., 2008).

    Statistical analysis

    We constructed generalised linear models (GLMs) to test whether the topological descriptors of networks (connectivity, modularity, and specialisation) differ among herbivorous insect guilds (gallers, chewers, and suckers). Because these topological descriptors are dependent on network size (Dunne et al., 2002; Landi et al., 2018), we also used network size as an explanatory variable in these models. For a better understanding of the characteristics of networks, we used GLMs to test whether plant species richness, insect morphospecies richness, and network size differ among guilds. All of the models had their error distribution assumed to be normal. In addition, we performed post hoc contrast tests to highlight differences between insect guilds. All of the statistical analyses were performed in r, version 3.4.1. (R Development Core Team, 2018).

    Results

    The 18 plant–herbivore networks (Fig. 2) were composed of 1199 insects of 317 morphospecies and 190 woody plants of 50 morphospecies, with a total of 489 distinct interactions (Appendix 1). Among insects, 460 (38.3%) individuals and 263 (82.9%) species represented exophages: 269 (22.4%) chewing insects (151 morphospecies, 47.6%) and 191 (15.9%) sucking insects (112 morphospecies, 37.3%). The galling insects were represented by 739 (61.6%) individuals of 54 (17.1%) morphospecies. Insect morphospecies, host plant species richness, and network size did not differ among different guilds (Table 1).

    The mean ± SD modularity values of plant–herbivore networks were very high; 0.697 ± 0.096 for gallers, 0.779 ± 0.097 for chewers, and 0.827 ± 0.036 for suckers. By contrast with the connectance, there were significant differences in the network modularity among different herbivorous insect guilds (Table 2), with a higher modularity in exophagous networks than in galling insect networks (Fig. 4). The modularity was not affected by the network size, although it was affected by the interaction between the network size and the guild type (Table 2) because only for galling networks the modularity was positively affected by the network size.

    Network nestedness had a pattern contrary with modularity, with very low values ranging from 0.000 to 13.541 (mean ± SD: 2.152 ± 3.484). Network nestedness was higher for chewing insect networks (4.672 ± 4.747) than for sucking (1.243 ± 1.945) and galling insect networks (0.219 ± 0.490) (Fig. 5 and Table 2). Network nestedness was also positively affected by network size, although there was no interaction between size and guild type.

    Network specialisation (index H2) ranged from 0.000 to 1.000. Galling insect networks showed higher specialisation than exophagous networks (Table 2) and sucking insect networks were more specialised than chewing insect networks (Fig. 6), corroborating our expectations. The mean ± SD specialisation for galling insect networks was 0.997 ± 0.004, whereas, for sucking and chewing insect networks, it was 0.352 ± 0.349 and 0.096 ± 0.113, respectively. Network specialisation was also negatively affected by network size (Fig. 7), although there was no interaction between size and guild type.

    Discussion

    We found no difference in insect morphospecies richness, plant species richness, or network size when comparing plant–herbivore networks of different insect guilds. On the other hand, the topological structure of networks varied; guilds of endophages and exophages showed differences in the specialisation and arrangement of their interactions with plants. Galling insect networks were less modular and more specialised than exophagous insect networks, and sucking insect networks were more specialised than chewing insect networks. These results corroborate previous studies, showing that endophagous and exophagous herbivores have different patterns of interactions with their host plants (Gaston et al., 1992; Araújo 2013; Kuchenbecker & Fagundes, 2018). In addition, we also found that network connectance and specialisation depend on network size, as already described in previous studies (Dunne et al., 2002). Our findings indicate that, despite the lack of differences in species diversity between insect guilds of herbivores in Neotropical savannas, the topological structure of networks responds to biological (e.g. degree of intimacy of the herbivore–plant associations) and ecological (e.g. type of trophic interaction and diet breadth) differences among these herbivore groups.

    Plant–herbivore networks of different insect groups showed very high values of modularity. However, exophage networks were more modular than galling insect networks, contradicting our initial expectations. This result can be explained by the defensive and scleromorphic characteristics of the savanna vegetation that imposes different restrictions on the consumption of free‐living herbivores (Ribeiro & Fernandes, 2000). Climatic and edaphic characteristics of Neotropical savannas generate severe filters for plants, resulting in different levels of hardness, thickness, pilosity, and concentration of chemical compounds (e.g. latex, terpenes, and tannins) in leaves (Silva & Batalha, 2011). Therefore, subgroups of exophagous insects tend to use a subset of plant species with similar leaf characteristics. The evidence indicates that the presence of latex in host plants appears to affect the occurrence lepidopteran caterpillars (Diniz et al., 1999) and the density of trichomes reduces the occurrence of some hemipteran species (Ribeiro et al., 1994). Another factor that may have contributed to the modular structure in exophage networks is the large number of singleton species (Novotný & Basset, 2000). The occurrence of rare species is common in communities of tropical insect herbivores (Price et al., 1995; Lewinsohn et al., 2005; Novotný & Basset, 2005), including Neotropical savannas (Kuchenbecker & Fagundes, 2018), and may increase the modularity in plant–herbivore networks (Dormann & Strauss, 2014).

    Unlike modularity, nestedness values extremely low for all types of networks, corroborating previous studies showing low nestedness for trophic networks (Lewinsohn et al., 2006; Pires & Guimarães, 2013). Despite the low nestedness, we found that chewing insects had more nested networks than sucking and galling insects, corroborating our expectations. Nestedness in ecological networks is a pattern where specialised species, which are often very numerous, tend to interact with generalist species, which are generally rare within networks (Lewinsohn et al., 2006). In this sense, networks dominated by specialised species tend to show no nestedness, whereas networks with generalist species tend to form interaction nests (Memmott et al., 2000). Because the level of generalisation shows a positive correlation with nestedness, chewing insect networks were more nested than other networks, likely as a result of the higher occurrence of generalist species in them.

    Confirming our expectations, we found a gradient of specialisation (measured by index H2) when comparing networks of different insect herbivore guilds: gallers > suckers > chewers. Plant–herbivore interactions are often specialised as a result of the co‐evolution of insects and plants (Becerra, 2015; Forister et al., 2015), although the level of species specificity varies within and between different trophic guilds (Novotný & Basset, 2005; Novotný et al., 2010). Despite these idiosyncrasies, the feeding strategy and the level of biological association of herbivores with their host plants may affect the organisation of interactions within the network. We expected galling insects to be more specialised in that they generally have high specificity with their host plants because they live inside plant tissues (Stone & Schönrogge, 2003; Shorthouse et al., 2005; Araújo, 2013). The endophagous life‐form implies that the galler must select, penetrate, and survive in its host plant (Stone & Schönrogge, 2003) and therefore each species of galling insect tends to have a single host or few phylogenetically close hosts (Butterill & Novotný, Butterill & Novotny, 2015; Araújo et al., 2017). Immature galling insects live confined within the plant, whereas exophages have different levels of mobility within the host or between different plants (Novotný et al., 2010; Araújo, 2013). For example, species of adult leaf chewers usually have high mobility and a broad diet (Novotny et al., 2002), such that a single species can consume several plants of different families (Novotný et al., 2010). On the other hand, sucking insects, although not as sessile or specific as endophages, generally have lower mobility or higher selectivity than chewing insects with respect to the use of their host plants (Lewinsohn et al., 2005; Novotný et al., 2010).

    Network connectance was lower than 25% for all of the networks analysed. Low connectance can be a characteristic of trophic networks (Dunne et al., 2002; Villa‐Galaviz et al., 2012). In ecological networks, the level of connectance in interspecific interactions can vary as a function of environmental factors (Villa‐Galaviz et al., 2012; Araújo et al., 2015) and among arthropods with different feeding habits (Araújo & Daud, 2018). In the present study, in contrast with our expectations, we found no differences in the connectance of plant–herbivore networks of gallers, suckers, and chewers. This result suggests that, in Neotropical savannas, the interaction connectivity of herbivorous species with their plants does not depend on the insect guild. Our findings indicate that, because connectance is a binary measure of interactions (Dormann et al., 2009), the use of quantitative measures is more effective for detecting topological differences in ecological networks (Landi et al., 2018), in that herbivorous guilds differed in quantitative modularity and specialisation.

    Network size, measured by the number of plant and insect species, also influenced network topological descriptors. Network size affected the connectance and specialisation of plant–herbivore networks negatively. The negative effect of network size on the connectance occurs because larger networks show many possible interactions within the network (Dunne et al., 2002). The negative effect of the network size on specialisation, however, can result from a large number of specialist species in small networks. Although we did not find significant differences in network size between guilds, there was a tendency for galling and sucking insect networks (which had higher specialisation) to be smaller than chewing insect networks (Table 1). Our results corroborate studies that indicate the importance of controlling the network size to compare the topology of different ecological networks (Dunne et al., 2002; Araújo et al., 2015).

    A closer look at networks of the same guild indicates that the network topology may vary between different locations (Fig. 2). This may result from local factors, such as variations in species composition, which may influence the structure of plant–herbivore interactions (Villa‐Galaviz et al., 2012). On the other hand, despite these differences between networks of the same guild, we found out that differences between guilds are more significant than differences within guilds. For example, even the most generalised network of galling insects tend to be more specialised than the most specialised network of chewing insects. This finding corroborates previous studies indicating that rules related to the type and intimacy of interaction are crucial for structuring networks regardless of local factors (Pires & Guimarães, 2013).

    Studies detailing and differentiating networks of different guilds of herbivorous insects are scarce (Novotný et al., 2010), probably because different guilds require different efforts of sampling and taxonomy (Novotný & Basset, 2005), and so studies usually focus only on one taxon or guild. In this context, the present study stands out as the first to provide a systematic comparison between networks of different guilds of herbivorous insects in Neotropical savannas. Our results show that, although there are no differences in species diversity in networks of gallers, suckers, and chewers, these guilds show structurally distinct interaction networks. This finding indicates that differences in topological structure are more significant between networks of different guilds than within networks of the same guild. Corroborating our expectations, there was a gradient of specialisation in networks; gallers were the most specialised and chewers were the most generalists. On the other hand, exophagous networks were more modular than galling insect networks. Future studies should investigate whether local patterns related to vegetation structure and species composition influence the topology of distinct guild networks differently. Furthermore, because network topology influences the dynamics of species and interactions (Araújo, 2016; Landi et al., 2018), another challenge for future studies is to compare the fragility of co‐extinctions in galling, sucking, and chewing insect networks and how they respond to anthropogenic pressures on natural environments.

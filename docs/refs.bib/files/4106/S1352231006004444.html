Measurements and modelling of marine salt transportation and deposition in a tropical region in Brazil

Author links open overlay panelG.R.MeiraaM.C.AndradebI.J.PadaratzcM.C.AlonsobJ.C.BorbaJr.a
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.atmosenv.2006.04.053Get rights and content

Abstract

This work presents results of marine salt deposition in a marine atmosphere zone in a tropical region in the northeast of Brazil. A general model that represents marine aerosol behaviour in coastal regions is applied. Results show that marine salt deposition data are strongly influenced by wind speeds above the critical value of 3.0 m/s. The effect of the distance from the sea on marine salt data is also observed. This effect is better represented when changes in marine aerosol composition, while being transported inland, are taken into account. Deposition parameters obtained from the model application at a Brazilian site show good agreement with data from other countries. Parametric equations to represent the behaviour of each model parameter are proposed for the studied region and form a specific model that is more accurate than the simple empirical relations previously used.

1. Introduction

Marine aerosol production is the result of surface sea agitation, which is caused by the wind. This mechanism generates air bubbles that explode and produce different sized drops. Marine aerosol production is more effective in the breaking waves zone where drops are more numerous and a higher percentage of those present in the aerosol spectrum are large (Fitzgerald, 1991; O’Dowd et al., 1997). When these drops are generated, they rapidly become equilibrated with the environment and, depending on temperature and relative humidity conditions, they can turn into salt solutions or salt crystals (Zezza and Macri, 1995).

Marine aerosol generation is strongly influenced by wind characteristics. Publications since the 1950s show that salt concentrations exponentially increase with wind speed (Woodcock, 1953; Lovett, 1978; Taylor and Wu, 1992; Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996). This can be explained by the fact that there is an increase in both the number of marine particles generated and the percentage of larger drops in the aerosol spectrum when wind speed increases (McDonald et al., 1982; Fitzgerald, 1991). The influence of the wind on sea-salt concentrations can be observed in Fig. 1, where exponential empirical relationships obtained at different studies around the world are presented (Lovett, 1978; McDonald et al., 1982; Petelski and Chomka, 1996; Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996).

Some studies report that the influence of wind speed on salt concentrations strongly increases after a certain wind speed threshold. There is no agreement as to how much this threshold is, although values of between 3.0 and 7.1 m/s are given (Spiel and De Leew, 1996; Piazzola and Despiau, 1997; Morcillo et al., 2000). As well as this, the residence time of wind speed is another aspect that plays an important role. A study carried out by Feliu et al. (1999), based on previous Russian results (Strekalov and Panchenko, 1994), shows that salt measurements present a stronger relationship with wind power (wind speed multiplied by its residence time) than with wind speed alone. In this way, wind turbulence, with peaks of high intensity, has a stronger influence on salt concentrations as its residence time increases.

When transporting marine aerosol inland, wind also plays an important role. Strong winds enable bigger particles to cover larger distances before settling and also increase salt concentration in generated aerosol (Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996; Morcillo et al., 2000). Thus, higher salt concentrations can be observed at the same distance from the sea when wind speed increases. This effect is not linear and weakens at distances farther from the sea, where large particles are less numerous, due to gravimetric effect. As a consequence, there is a strong tendency for sea-salt concentrations to decrease with the distance from the sea.

Salt measurements when aerosol is transported inland are also affected by land surface roughness, relative humidity and rainfall (Feliu et al., 1999). Cole et al. (2003a) studies illustrate that the influence of ground roughness on salt concentrations is stronger in the first hundred metres from the sea. The influence of relative humidity on salt concentrations is particularly important between 50% and 70%, due to salt wetting. With regard to this influence, the author also observed the washout effect which, due to rainfall, is more effective at higher relative humidity levels. Salt wetting increases the particle sizes and, consequently, rainfall removes a great amount of aerosol salts.

There are few publications focused on the transportation and deposition of marine salts that take into account salt concentrations or chloride deposition on measurement devices at different distances from the sea. The studies that have been carried out used different procedures to capture the salts, as can be seen in Table 1. In this table, one can see that the wet candle method was one of the most used, probably due to its easy operation. The strong decrease in salt concentrations or deposition when reaching distances far from the sea can also be verified. Some of the studies presented in Table 1 also provide information about the wind speed range, which allows one to see the strong influence of this variable on salt presence, regarding the differences of salinity of the different oceans and seas and other environmental conditions.

The modelling of the production and transportation of marine aerosol can be divided into two major groups. The first one refers to parametric models. Based on statistical analysis and empirical relationships, these studies are applicable to those places where they were developed. The second group refers to the process-based models, where modelling is based on phenomena and takes into account general rules that can be applicable to any site, taking into account specific limitations of each model formulation. In this group, Feliu et al. (1999), Meira (2004), and Meira et al. (2005) presented simplified models to represent marine aerosol transportation and deposition, applicable to sites close to the sea. In a more complex approach, Cole et al. (2003a) presented a process-based model regarding the production, transportation and deposition of marine salts.

This work presents climatic and chloride deposition data from a tropical region in Brazil and an application of a sea-salt transportation and deposition model (Meira, 2004; Meira et al., 2005), based on wet candle measurements. It takes into account the strong influence of the wind in marine aerosol behaviour and changes in the aerosol spectrum due to the settlement of salt particles while being transported inland. A parametric approach derived from this application, which is specific to the studied region, is commented on afterwards.

2. Experimental work

Periodic monitoring, for at least 17 months, of climatic parameters and chloride deposition was performed in the city of João Pessoa, which is located in a tropical region in the northeast of Brazil (Fig. 2). In this case, it is important to explain that chloride deposition means the chlorides deposited on a retaining device while marine aerosol is transported inland by wind.

The chloride dry deposition rate is a parameter that has been used to study the presence of salt in marine environments and has supported some studies in atmospheric corrosion (Morcillo et al., 1998). In this work, the chloride dry deposition rate was measured at five monitoring stations placed at sites 10, 100, 200, 500 and 1100 m from the sea (Fig. 2) in order to characterise inland transportation of marine aerosol. The area chosen was as flat as possible, to avoid significant variations in height, and free of obstacles to reduce the effect of ground roughness in the measured data. At each station, a wet candle device (Fig. 3) was installed according to the specifications established in the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) G-140 (1996). Liquid samples from each wet candle device were collected monthly and analysed by potentiometric titration with silver nitrate solution 0.05 M. This procedure was repeated throughout the research period.

With the objective of characterising the local climate, temperature, relative humidity, rainfall and wind speed and direction were measured. The climatic data were collected by a weather station which belongs to the Brazilian Government and which is located at the monitoring station 1100 m from the sea (Fig. 2). Continuous daily anemographic sheets, from a universal anemograph, were used to obtain daily and monthly average wind data. Temperature, relative humidity and rainfall data were also collected at the same weather station, by using a psychrometer and a pluviometer, respectively. The UTC references were followed.

3. Results

Local temperatures show little variation, roughly ranging from 20 to 30 °C (Fig. 4a). Relative humidity usually remained between 60% and 80%, with higher values during longer periods of rain (Fig. 4b). Periods of heavy rainfall occurred from March to June, with a big increase in June (Fig. 4c). All these climatic parameters followed fairly typical behaviour, as can be seen in Table 2, that presents average data for a period of 30 years and for the research period.

The region chosen for the experimental work was characterised by mild winds, with average monthly values between 2.3 and 3.6 m s−1 (Fig. 5a) and predominant winds from the quadrant S–E (Fig. 5b). This is typical behaviour for the northeast of Brazil and favours smaller salinity results.

The results of chloride deposition data are shown in Fig. 6. The data show a clear reduction in salinity in the first metres from the sea, due to the gravimetric settlement of salt particles that occurs simultaneously to the transportation of salt inland. The variability of the results at each site is not large and can be seen as a consequence of the low variability of wind characteristics and other climatic parameters over the research period.

4. Discussion

4.1. Wind speed effect

An increase in wind speed results in the aerosol being composed of more of larger particles, which are transported inland (McDonald et al., 1982; Fitzgerald, 1991; Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996; Piazzola and Despiau, 1997; Morcillo et al., 2000). This effect can be observed in data obtained at monitoring stations that indicate an increase in the presence of chloride when wind speed increases. On the other hand, there is a strong increase in chloride deposition with above average wind speed values near 3.0 m s−1 (Fig. 7).

This behaviour agrees with a study from Spain (Morcillo et al., 2000), which outlined that above 3.0 m s−1 there is a sharp increase in salt concentrations. Other studies (Strekalov and Panchenko, 1994; Spiel and De Leew, 1996; Piazzola and Despiau, 1997) also revealed this increasing tendency. However, there is no agreement about the wind speed threshold, which was reported to be between 3.0 and 7.1 m s−1 in these publications. More research is necessary to clarify if it is possible to set a general wind speed threshold, but it can be outlined that the influence of wind speed is stronger above a certain level of wind speed and this may be taken into account in aerosol studies.

4.2. Effect of distance from the sea

The reduction in the presence of salt with distance from the sea is clearly represented in Fig. 6 by chloride deposition data. Comparing Brazilian data with other previously published in literature, a similar decreasing tendency is observed in the first hundred metres from the sea. The falling off of marine salts is quite dramatic in the first land zones and roughly ranged between 85% and 95% in the first 500 m, in studies carried out in Nigeria (Ambler and Bain, 1955), Australia (Cole et al., 2003b) and Cuba (Corvo et al., 1995).

Some publications propose expressions like D=aX-b (Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996; Meira et al., 2003) or follow exponential relationships like D=ae(-b)X (Feliu et al., 1999) to represent the chloride deposition decay with the distance, where D is the chloride deposition on a testing device and X is the distance from the sea. The exponential relationship was fitted to present data and is represented in Fig. 8. The results show two deviations: data representing monitoring stations at 10 and 100 m from the sea, despite being well distributed around the average value, show high scatter around the fitted curve; the curve fitting shows values very close to zero at monitoring stations at 500 and 1100 m from the sea, which disagrees with experimental values.

In the first case, other variables influence the phenomenon. Among them, wind speed plays an important role, due to its relation with marine aerosol generation. Relative humidity and the washout effect due to rainfall can also be mentioned and probably contributed to this scatter. However, the low month-to-month variation of relative humidity and its high average level (Table 2) did not show variations on wet candle measurements as strong as those observed by Cole et al. (2003a) for relative humidity variations of between 50% and 70%. Rainfall influence was not clear either. The influence of these last variables may be surpassed by the influence of wind speed, which seemed to be clearer in this case.

In the second case, the exponential fitting does not represent the real situation accurately. There are changes in aerosol composition while transported inland due to the settling of larger particles. As larger particles settle before smaller ones, a progressive reduction in the velocity in which aerosol settles takes place while aerosol is being transported inland. This parameter is identified in literature as deposition velocity (Feliu et al., 1999; Petelski and Chomka, 2000). These aspects are not taken into account in the empirical relationships previously presented. Therefore, they have a limited application. In the next section, an analytical model that considers the aspects previously commented on is introduced.

5. Salt transportation and deposition model

Marine aerosol generated along the coastline is transported inland by wind in a region known as marine atmosphere zone. A model to represent aerosol behaviour in this zone was described in detail and presented in other documents (Meira, 2004; Meira et al., 2005). Here, only a brief description is presented.

The model considers the exponential decrease in deposition velocity with time and, thus, the aerosol restructuring while being transported inland, due to the settlement effect. Eq. (1) represents this model, where M is the sea-salt concentration at a distance x from the shoreline, M0 is the sea-salt concentration at the shoreline, vdep0 is the initial deposition velocity representative of marine aerosol at the shoreline, h is the height of the layer considered, α is a coefficient that characterises the distribution of particles in marine aerosol and its influence on deposition velocity decrease, called the coefficient of deposition velocity reduction and v is the wind speed

(1)1MdM=-vdep(x)dxhvvdep(x)=vdep0e(-αx/v)}⇒M=M0e(vdep0/αh)(e(-αx/v)-1).

Considering that the chloride deposition in apparatus like those presented in Table 1 is a consequence of salt particles impacting on device surfaces while aerosol moves inland due to wind, these measurements are directly related to the salt concentration in marine aerosol. Thus, Eq. (1) can be rewritten as Eq. (2), where D is the dry deposition of chlorides on used apparatus at a certain distance from the sea and D0 is the dry deposition of chlorides at the shoreline

(2)D=D0e(vdep0/αh)(e(-αx/v)-1).

Eqs. (1), (2) represent a simplified model. It does not take into account complex effects like resuspension of particles, influence of obstacles on the deposition of salts and the effect of temperature and relative humidity variations during transportation. In the first case, it is important to remember that the model focused on the first hundred metres from the sea, where this kind of phenomenon is less important. In the second case, although the influence of obstacles is not an easy task to be assessed, deposition velocity can indirectly take into consideration the influence of this mechanism increasing its values. In the last case, taking into account that the proposed model starts with an aerosol, which has already been generated, the related influence is less important if there are not large changes on temperature and relative humidity values during the measurements. Thus, for this first approach, these variables are not taken into account.

In the particular case of the measurements taken in this work, wet candle devices were continuously exposed during each 30-day period and the standard deviation for temperature and relative humidity, for the same period, remained at the levels 3% and 10%, respectively. These are not large deviations and, although relative humidity influence is particularly important between 50% and 70% (Cole et al., 2003a), average data remained at high levels, minimising this influence.

6. Model application to the Brazilian site

When applying the proposed model to the region where the experimental work took place, the model parameters were obtained for the research period. These results were compared with data from different places. Some papers, which focused on marine aerosol behaviour, worked with sea-salt concentrations in airborne or sea-salt deposition on testing devices in spite of chloride deposition (Table 1). Taking into account that Eq. (1) represents a general model that refers to sea-salt concentration in marine aerosol at certain distances from the sea and that sodium chloride represents the majority of salts present in sea water, comparisons between model parameters, obtained from chloride deposition data and sea-salt concentrations data were made.

6.1. Behaviour of deposition parameters (vdep0, α)

In Fig. 9a, vdep0 data from the Brazilian site are presented. They show vdep0 values between 0.5 and 0.8 cm s−1 and a slight increase in vdep0 values between August and November 2002. This behaviour agrees with wind speed data that reveal greater values between 3.2 and 3.6 m s−1 in this period. It also agrees with other literature data (McDonald et al., 1982) that reveal deposition velocities between 0.80 and 8.2 cm s−1 when wind speeds vary between 3.4 and 10.6 m s−1 (Fig. 9b).

Data from Poland (Petelski and Chomka, 2000) reveal deposition velocities between 0.92 and 4.3 cm s−1 when wind speeds vary between 4.5 and 7.5 m s−1. Data from Sweden (Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996) ranges between 2.4 and 14.1 cm s−1 when wind speeds vary between 7.3 and 20.7 m s−1. Comparing these results with those obtained in this work, one can see that at low wind speed levels, deposition velocity values are closer to those observed in João Pessoa (Fig. 9b).

In Fig. 10a, α data from the Brazilian site are presented. They show values between 7.5×10−3 and 16×10−3 s−1, including a slight increase around October 2002. This behaviour is similar to vdep0 and can be explained by the relationship with wind speed. An increase in wind speed changes aerosol spectrum, the percentage of large particles grows and, consequently, deposition velocity decrease takes place at a higher rate. Data from Sweden (Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996) and Poland (Petelski and Chomka, 2000) show the same tendency. Nevertheless, their values remained at a higher level (Fig. 10b).

Wind speed may help to explain the differences between the Brazilian data and the Polish and Swedish data (Gustafsson and Franzén, 1996; Petelski and Chomka, 2000), described in the previous paragraphs. At these last two sites, wind speed levels were significantly higher and, therefore, contributed to increase deposition parameter values (vdep0, α). On the other hand, the increasing relationships between deposition parameters and wind speed follow a function that sharply increases after a certain level of wind speed (Morcillo et al., 2000; Meira, 2004).

6.2. Empirical relationships for the specific case study

Data obtained from the experimental programme were analysed observing the parameters of the presented model and their relationships with wind characteristics, particularly with wind speeds higher than 3.0 m s−1, weighted with their residence time (v3t3t−1). This means that wind speeds higher than 3.0 m s−1 (v3) were multiplied by the length of time that it took place (residence time—t3) and divided by the total observation time (t). Wind speeds higher than 3.0 m s−1 are an important threshold for the strong increasing tendency of salt concentrations, as has been previously stated (Morcillo et al., 2000; Meira, 2004). To do this, continuous daily wind data recorded in anemographic sheets were used and vectorial components perpendicular to the seacoast were considered.

D0 behaviour can be seen in Fig. 11. It shows that D0 fits well with v3t3t−1 data and that the influence of the wind speed is stronger when v3t3t−1 rises above 2.0 m s−1. Thus, the best function to represent the relationship between D0 and v3t3t−1 is an exponential growth function (Eq. (4)). The determination coefficient is near 0.80, which seems to be reasonable considering the model's limitations and that the study took place under natural conditions.

Concerning vdep0, results show that, in the same way as D0, there is an exponential growth of vdep0 values as a function of v3t3t−1 (Fig. 12), represented by Eq. (5). Concerning α, that depends on the distribution of particles in aerosol, the best relationship occurs between α and vdep0. This agrees with deposition behaviour, which depends on the initial aerosol structure. This relationship is better represented by an exponential equation (Eq. (6)) and shows that by increasing the percentage of large particles in aerosol and, thus, of the initial deposition velocity, there is an increase in the rate of deposition velocity reduction (Ten Harkel, 1997). Fig. 13 shows this relationship within the range of results observed in the present experimental work.

D0 and vdep0correlated well with the v3t3t−1 parameter. This agrees with other publications that show a strong increase in salt concentrations when wind speed rises above a certain level and also show that the residence time of this particular type of wind plays an important role in determining deposition characteristics. This was first approached by Strekalov and Panchenko (1994) using the wind power concept (wind speed multiplied by its residence time) and was extended by Morcillo et al. (2000), who identified a particular wind speed threshold above which this effect is more critical. In this work, this threshold was assumed to be 3.0 m s−1, as a result of observations made in Fig. 7, which approaches the behaviour verified in Spain (Morcillo et al., 2000).

In this way, the specific relationships concerning D0, vdep0, α can be added to Eq. (2) and be used to propose the specific deposition model to the region where the present research took place

(5)vdep0=0.4558+0.0444e(0.7174v3t3t-1),

These equations, specifically to be used in the Brazilian site studied, were proved with further data (obtained after the research period described in Section 2) that did not take part in the data set that supported the expressions (4), (5), (6). Wind data (v, v3, t3, t) representative of 6 months after the initial research period were obtained according to procedures detailed in Section 2. Through them, D0, Vdep0 and α were obtained according to Eqs. (4), (5), (6). Deposition values were obtained for all distances monitored (10, 100, 200, 500 and 1100 m) by using Eq. (3) and these results were compared with experimental wet candle data in the same period. All results are presented in Fig. 14, and show a good agreement between experimental and model data. This means that the proposed model can be used as an indicator of the presence of chloride at zones not very far from the sea. In the specific case studied, at places around the limit of 1 km from the sea.

In Fig. 14, results from the application of the specific proposed model (Eqs. (3), (4), (5), (6)) and from the empirical model presented in Fig. 8 are also compared. As can be seen, there is an important deviation between both. In the first case, the results are systematically closer to experimental data than those obtained from the empirical relationship, that does not take into account wind influence and results in values very close to zero at sites far from the sea (500 and 1100 m). Only when experimental data are close to average values is the empirical curve quite close to them at sites near to the sea. This means that the proposed model is a more accurate way of representing transportation and deposition of sea-salts phenomenon.

7. Conclusions

The application of the sea-salt model showed that it is possible to represent sea salt presence in a marine atmosphere zone by an analytical model, which includes wind speed as one of the major variables and takes into account aerosol restructuring due to settlement. Process-based models have the advantage of being applicable to different sites. In this paper, the sea-salt model was applied to a specific Brazilian region and compared with data from other environments. The results showed good agreement among their parameters, denoting the validity of the model.

In the specific case of the Brazilian site, the high influence of wind speed on deposition parameters was demonstrated, particularly when it reaches the level of 3.0 m s−1. The presence of this wind speed threshold is in agreement with the literature and remains in the same magnitude of the value established in Spain (Morcillo et al., 2000).

Expressions to represent the behaviour of the parameters of the model at each site can be established to particularise the general model. It was done in the city of João Pessoa in the northeast of Brazil. The set of Eqs. (3), (4), (5), (6) represent a parametric model valid for the specific environment studied, without prejudice to general applications of Eq. (2) to other sites.

Finally, it is important to observe that the analytical model is more accurate than empirical relationships, showing that their expressions, despite being a simplification of reality, represent progress for the mathematical formulation of the phenomenon of sea-salt transportation and deposition in a marine atmosphere zone.

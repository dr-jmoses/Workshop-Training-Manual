    Abstract

    Morphological traits provide the interface between species and their environment. For example, body size affects the fitness of individuals in various ways. Yet especially for ectotherms, the applicability of general rules of interspecific clines of body size and even more so of other morphological traits is still under debate. Here we tested relationships between elevation (as a proxy for temperature) and productivity with four ecologically relevant morphological traits of orthopteran assemblages that are related to fecundity (body size), dispersal (wing length), jumping ability (hind femur length), and predator detection (eye size). We measured traits of 160 orthopteran species that were sampled along an extensive environmental gradient at Mt. Kilimanjaro (Tanzania), spanning elevations from 790 to 4,410 m above sea level (a.s.l.) with different levels of plant productivity. For traits other than body size, we calculated the residuals from a regression on body length to estimate the variation of traits irrespective of body size. Bayesian analyses revealed that mean body size of assemblages, as well as the means of relative wing length, hind femur length, and eye size, decreased with increasing elevation. Body size and relative eye size also decreased with increasing productivity. Both phylogenetic relationships, as well as species‐specific adaptations, contributed to these patterns. Our results suggest that orthopteran assemblages had higher fecundity and better dispersal and escape abilities, as well as better predator detection at higher temperatures (low elevations) than at low temperatures (high elevations). Large body sizes might be advantageous in habitats with low productivity because of a reduced risk of starvation. Likewise, large eye size might be advantageous because of the ability to detect predators in habitats with low vegetation cover, where hiding possibilities are scarce. Our study highlights that changes in temperature and productivity not only lead to interspecific changes in body size but are also related to independent changes of other morphological traits that influence the ecological fit of organisms in their environment.

    Introduction

    Each species is characterized by a unique set of morphological, physiological, and behavioral traits (De Queiroz 2007). These traits influence the fitness of individuals (McGill et al. 2006, Violle et al. 2007, Webb et al. 2010, Nock et al. 2016) and provide the basis for interactions between species and their abiotic and biotic environment. The most important trait influencing animal fitness (LaBarbera 1989) is body size. It influences the rates of physiological processes, interactions with the abiotic environment, and of course with conspecifics or other species (biotic environment; Lawton 1990). For birds and mammals, macroecological patterns of body size have been well documented and the underlying mechanisms are comparatively well known (Bergmann 1847, Ashton et al. 2000, Ashton 2002, Millien et al. 2006). For arthropods, however, the generality of body size clines along latitudinal and elevational gradients is still intensively debated and the underlying mechanisms remain poorly understood (Chown and Gaston 2010, Watt et al. 2010, Shelomi 2012, Zeuss et al. 2017). Furthermore, important interactions have been identified not only between the body size of arthropods, fecundity, and thermoregulation (Whitman 2008) but also between wing length and dispersal ability (Picaud and Petit 2008), hind femur length and escape ability (Queathem 1991), and eye size and predator detection (Land 1997). Both abiotic and biotic factors therefore influence the assembly of species with respect to body sizes and other morphological traits (Bässler et al. 2016, Nock et al. 2016, Schellenberger Costa et al. 2017). Anthropogenic changes of environmental factors can therefore lead to changes in the configuration of morphological traits in assemblages with consequences for species interactions and ecosystem functioning (Millien et al. 2006, Webb et al. 2010, Ohlberger 2013).

    Patterns in average body size of species and other traits depend on the level of organization, e.g., within species or assemblages (Shelomi 2012). Body sizes of bees at Mt. Kilimanjaro, e.g., increase with increasing elevation at the intraspecific but decrease at the interspecific scale (Classen et al. 2017). Intraspecific size clines within arthropod species are often explained by the temperature–size rule (Atkinson 1994, Atkinson and Sibly 1997), the starvation resistance hypothesis (Chown and Gaston 1999, Entling et al. 2010), and metabolic rate (Mousseau 1997; for a recent overview see Horne et al. 2018). Whereas, at, the interspecific level, main drivers of patterns in body size and other morphological traits in assemblages discussed are temperature, via temperature‐driven biochemical processes (Abrams et al. 1996, Berner et al. 2004, Zeuss et al. 2017), resource availability and the resistance against starvation and desiccation (Remmert 1981, Cushman et al. 1993), and predation pressure (Blackburn et al. 1999, Entling et al. 2010).

    Interspecific differences in the composition of morphological traits of insects have been mainly related to changes in abiotic factors along climatic gradients, e.g., elevational gradients (Brehm and Fiedler 2004, Hodkinson 2005, Rodríguez‐Jimenez and Sarmiento 2008, Hoiss et al. 2012, Peters et al. 2016). One of the most striking patterns along elevational gradients is the almost linear decline in temperature (Körner 2007), which is one of the most important factors for ectothermic animals, such as arthropods (Zeuss et al. 2017). At high elevations, low temperatures lead to low food availability, and short growing seasons reduce the period for larval development. The completion of a full life cycle within a single season therefore requires a fast development, which is related to small adult body sizes (Abrams et al. 1996, Berner et al. 2004). By contrast, in habitats with high temperatures and extended growing seasons, e.g., at low elevations, arthropods should have a larger body size, which has positive effects on fecundity, thermoregulatory ability, and desiccation resistance (Whitman 2008, Chown and Gaston 2010, Schellenberger Costa et al. 2017). Indeed, body sizes of spider and bee assemblages are higher in warm compared to cool habitats (Entling et al. 2010, Classen et al. 2017, Schellenberger Costa et al. 2017). However, other studies found increasing body sizes of moth and bee assemblages along elevational gradients (Hoiss et al. 2012, Peters et al. 2016, Schellenberger Costa et al. 2017), emphasizing that relationships between morphological traits and environmental factors depend on the taxon being studied.

    Besides temperature, other abiotic factors also influence morphological traits. For example, the low atmospheric pressure at high elevations, combined with low temperatures, hampers the flight performance of insects (Dillon et al. 2006). Consequently, insects in high elevation habitats often have partly (brachyptery) or completely reduced wings (aptery; Alexander 1951, Sømme 1989, Peters et al. 2016).

    Furthermore, biotic factors, such as resource availability, are important for the composition of assemblages with respect to morphological traits (Lavorel and Garnier 2002, Clapham and Karr 2012). Large morphological traits often demand additional energy for structural reinforcements, e.g., of the muscular system or the exoskeleton (Belovsky 1997, Zera and Denno 1997, Whitman 2008). A useful measure of resource availability for herbivorous species is plant productivity (Wolverton et al. 2009). In accordance with the resource availability hypothesis, areas with high productivity can provide habitat for species with traits that require more resources, e.g., large body size (Wolverton et al. 2009, Huston and Wolverton 2011, Van der Plas et al. 2012). However, habitats with sparse, variable, or unpredictable resources can also favor species with resource‐intensive traits, such as those that increase dispersal ability (Roff 1990, Denno et al. 1991, Zera and Denno 1997). For example, long wings are resource intensive but increase flight ability and are beneficial for tracking resources and colonizing new habitats (Denno et al. 1991, Picaud and Petit 2008). Traits related to mobility are relevant not only for dispersal but also for escaping from predators.

    Predation is a further biotic factor that influences morphological traits of arthropods (Allen et al. 2006). Flying insects escape predators mainly because of the initial acceleration. Acceleration is linked to the length of the hind femur, which contains major muscles of the hind legs (Bennet‐Clark 1990, Queathem 1991). Therefore, long hind femurs positively influence the ability to escape from predators (Picaud and Petit 2007) because they increase the ability to launch the body into flight, to move further away from the predator, and to jump from less stiff substrates (Queathem 1991, Dudley 2002, Burrows and Sutton 2008).

    The ability to reduce predation risk is additionally affected by behavioral and sensory traits (Bucher et al. 2014). Insects use a variety of mechanical, chemical, acoustic, and visual cues to detect predators (Steiner 1981, Dangles et al. 2005). For instance, the visual system of phytophagous insects allows them to differentiate species of host plants (Reeves 2011) and to adapt their escape distance and angle in response to the speed and direction of approaching predators (Cooper 2006). The eye size is thereby positively related to the size of the visual field and to the overall vision acuity and sensitivity, and thus positively affects the ability to visually detect predators (Land 1997, Rutowski 2000). However, other factors, such as the ability to hide, camouflage color, and predator pressure, will also influence anti‐predator traits of insects (Belovsky and Slade 1993, Eterovick et al. 1997, Branson 2005, Whitman 2008).

    Besides abiotic and biotic factors, phylogenetic relationships can also influence large‐scale patterns within interspecific size clines of traits. In phylogenetically conserved traits, the phenotypes of related species are closely correlated. The potential of species to change phylogenetically conserved traits is limited, which can lead to taxonomic sorting along environmental gradients (Hawkins and Lawton 1995, Entling et al. 2010). Especially size clines of body size along environmental gradients have been attributed to such processes of sorting, for example, in bees, butterflies, dragonflies, and spiders (Hawkins 1995, Hawkins and Lawton 1995, Entling et al. 2007, Chown and Gaston 2010, Zeuss et al. 2014). However, the phylogenetic relationships of species per se cannot explain changes in body size and other traits along environmental gradients. For instance, patterns of changes in body size and in hind wing development of carabid beetles have an ecological instead of a phylogenetic origin (Blackburn et al. 1999, Brehm and Fiedler 2004, Homburg et al. 2013). Furthermore, a recent study showed that both the phylogenetic (the legacy of the phylogenetic relationship) as well as the species‐specific component (the deviation of a species from the phylogenetic legacy) of color contribute to the pattern of increasing color lightness in butterfly and dragonfly assemblages toward warm environments across Europe (Zeuss et al. 2014). Thereby the species‐specific component is interpreted as the species‐specific adaption of a species to its environment.

    Previous work showed that body size clines of endothermic species along temperature gradients generally follow the prediction of Bergmann's rule, which states that body size in endotherms (within species or among closely related species) decreases with increasing temperature (Bergmann 1847, Ashton et al. 2000, Rodríguez et al. 2008, Salewski and Watt 2017). However, the applicability of a general rule on interspecific body size clines of ectotherms is still under debate (Atkinson 1994, Blanckenhorn and Demont 2004, Shelomi 2012, Zeuss et al. 2017), despite intensive studies across different environmental gradients (Millien et al. 2006, Shelomi 2012). Even less is known about changes in the composition of assemblages with respect to other morphological traits along environmental gradients. We examined how changes in elevation (as a proxy for temperature) and productivity influence morphological traits of orthopteran assemblages, differentiating between the phylogenetic and the species‐specific component of traits. Orthoptera (grasshoppers, locusts, and bush crickets) is an abundant and species‐rich order that occurs from sea level up to almost 5,000 m above sea level (a.s.l.; Alexander 1951, Bidau 2014). We thereby focused not only on body size, which is related to fecundity, but also on morphological traits that are related to dispersal (wing length), jumping ability (hind femur length), and predator detection (eye size). We used an extensive data set from 440 study plots of 160 orthopteran species collected within 14 yr along a steep elevational gradient ranging from 790 to 4,410 m a.s.l. on the slopes of Mt. Kilimanjaro (Tanzania). Mountains provide ideal conditions to investigate changes in morphological traits. Although elevation per se is not a meaningful predictor of size clines of morphological traits, it is commonly used as a proxy for abiotic factors, particularly temperature (Hodkinson 2005). Furthermore, the rapid change of climatic conditions with elevation allows studies along extensive environmental gradients within feasible amounts of time (Körner 2007, Sanders and Rahbek 2012). We hypothesized that areas at high elevations with low temperatures, short activity periods, and low atmospheric pressure should favor fast development, small body sizes, and short wings of orthopteran assemblages. We also expected that high productivity favors orthopteran assemblages with resource intensive traits that can benefit fecundity, dispersal ability, escape ability, and predator detection. We used simple Bayesian inference to test the following two main predictions: (1) body size, wing length, hind femur length, and eye size of orthopteran assemblages decrease with increasing elevation, i.e., with decreasing temperature, and (2) body size, wing length, hind femur length, and eye size of orthopteran assemblages increase with increasing productivity.

    Methods

    Study area

    We conducted the study along the slopes of Mt. Kilimanjaro in northeastern Tanzania. At low elevations (700–1,000 m a.s.l.), the study area is characterized by savanna woodland and grassland that has almost completely been converted to agricultural lands. The sub‐montane zone between 1,000 and 1,500 m a.s.l. is characterized by complex multi‐cropping agroforestry. The next higher zones consist of lower and middle montane forest (1,500–2,500 m a.s.l.), upper montane forest (2,500–3,500 m a.s.l.), and sub‐alpine forest zone of heathlands and cushion vegetation (~ 3,700 m a.s.l.). Elevations above 4,500 m a.s.l. are practically bare of vegetation. Precipitation follows the intertropical convergence zone modified by elevation (Hemp 2006), with one rainy season between March and May and another between October and November (Maeda and Hurskainen 2014). The mean annual land surface temperature decreases with elevation from 20.2°C at lower elevations (1,500–2,000 m a.s.l.), to 12.6°C at mid‐elevations (2,000–3,000 m a.s.l.), to 8.5°C at high elevations (above 4,000 m a.s.l.; Pepin et al. 2010, Maeda and Hurskainen 2014), with a typical moist adiabatic lapse rate of around −0.56°C per 100 m (Hemp 2006). Anthropogenic disturbance comprises agriculture involving fertilizer and herbicide applications in lowland areas, and fires, occasional timber extraction, and collection of forest products at higher elevations (Hemp and Hemp 2003).

    Sampling of orthopterans

    The abundance and density of orthopteran species on 440 study plots in open and forest habitats along an elevational gradient from 790 to 4,410 m a.s.l. (Fig. 1) were recorded by CH year‐round over 14 yr (1996–2012); most of the plots were located in the lower areas of the gradient between 790 and 2,280 m a.s.l. Each plot (30 × 30 m²) was sampled one to six times, except for four plots, which were sampled 23, 25, 45, and 47 times, respectively, yielding a total of 736 sampling events. The sampling regime allowed a high coverage of the occurring species within the orthopteran assemblages (Hemp 2009, 2013).

    The abundance of orthopteran species was estimated on the basis of sighted individuals and singing males. The observer (CH) strode through each plot on 10 parallel transects of 3 m distance during the day and at night and recorded all species seen and heard. Where present, shrubs and trees were shaken to search for tree‐dwelling species. The abundances were assigned to five categories according to Hemp and Hemp (2003): (1) 1 individual in the plot, (2) <1 individual/m², (3) ca. 1 individual/m², (4) ca. 5 individuals/m², and (5) >5 individuals/m².

    Estimating productivity

    We used the normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) as an estimate of productivity, as often used in ecological studies (Oindo 2002, Kerr and Ostrovsky 2003, Röder et al. 2017, Wallis et al. 2017). NDVI is a measure of greenness calculated from reflectance in the near‐infrared and red portions of the electromagnetic spectrum. The NDVI uses differences in the reflectance to distinguish between full vegetation cover (NDVI = +1) and waterbodies (NDVI = −1). We generated NDVI values from the cloud‐free and terrain‐corrected Landsat GLS 2000 data collection at 30‐m resolution for each plot (data available online).1 NDVI values ranged from −0.33 to 0.58 (0.038 ± 0.21; mean ± SD) and showed a hump‐shaped pattern, with highest values at mid elevations around 2000 m a.s.l. (Appendix S3: Fig. S1C); this pattern resembles the elevational pattern of precipitation, which, on the southern slopes of Mt. Kilimanjaro, also peaks at mid elevations (Hemp 2006).

    Processing of traits

    We measured 10 continuous morphological traits of 657 individuals of 160 orthopteran species (all individuals are in the collection of C. Hemp), namely body length, pronotum length, pronotum width, wing length, length of the femur of the front, middle, and hind legs, length of the tibia of the hind leg, eye diameter, and interocular distance. The 10 traits are important components of the body size and overall morphological shape of insects and are linked to abiotic and biotic factors (e.g., productivity) as described in the introduction. We measured the traits of each species on one to four male and female individuals, respectively depending on the number of individuals sampled (males of eight species and females of two species were not available). For some individuals, we could not obtain measurements of each trait: values were lacking for the interocular distance of 2 individuals, length of front leg femur of 6 individuals, length of middle leg femur of 4 individuals, length of hind leg femur of 7 individuals, and length of hind leg tibia of 10 individuals. In these cases, we replaced the missing value by the mean trait value of the same species and sex. We then calculated the mean trait value of each sex and species and the mean trait value for each species. To estimate whether intraspecific trait variation influenced the species mean trait value we investigated the measured minimum and maximum value of body size for female and male individuals from each species, as well as the average degree of intraspecific variability. Intraspecific variability of body length was small in relation to interspecific variability for all species (interspecific variation of body length, minimum – maximum = 0.98–6.4 cm; intraspecific variation of body length, mean ± SD | maximum = 0.82 ± 0.6 cm | 3.37 cm; Appendix S2: Fig. S2). Intraspecific trait variability should therefore not strongly influence our analysis.

    The four traits body size, wing length, hind femur length, and eye size are related to the functional aspects fecundity, dispersal, jumping ability, and predator detection, respectively (see Introduction). Before we calculated the community‐weighted mean (CWM) values of the four traits, we preprocessed the original trait measures. To obtain a general estimate of total body size, we calculated a multivariate measure across the 10 traits. This method is preferred over univariate measures because it is more likely to accurately reflect the overall size, especially when working with taxa from different families (Green 2001). We first log10‐transformed all trait measures. For wing length, we used square‐root transformation because of the occurrence of zeros (O'Hara and Kotze 2010). We then conducted a principal component analysis based on the covariance matrix of the 10 traits using the function prcomp implemented in the R Stats Package (R Core Team 2017). We extracted the species scores along the first axis and used these as multivariate measures of body size for the species. Wing length, hind femur length, and eye size covary with body length as well as any other general measure of body size. We therefore corrected the measures of the three traits for covariance with body length by using residuals of linear regressions of the trait values plotted against body length (relative traits; Stern and Emlen 1999). We then calculated the CWMs of the multivariate body size and the three relative trait measures with the function functcomp from the R package FD (Laliberté et al. 2014) for each time sample. The CWM values reflect the mean trait value of all species in the assemblage weighted by their relative abundance. Patterns of changes of CWM trait values can be contrary to patterns of unweighted average trait values across species when overall abundances of species with large or small traits increase or decrease (see discussion in Schellenberger Costa et al. 2017). We therefore additionally calculated the unweighted mean of trait values in an assemblage, where we treated the trait values of each species equally (mean of species present). In the following, we used the CWM values of the four preprocessed traits in linear regressions against environmental measures.

    As pointed out by Zelený and Schaffers (2012), co‐occurrence of species can lead to internal statistical relationships of no biological significance, e.g., when trait values are generated at the assemblage level by assigning values to species and averaging them at the plot level. To correct for this potential bias, we used a permutation method with standardized effect sizes proposed by Zelený and Schaffers (2012) and tested whether the CWM trait values differed from values expected under a null‐model. This permutation method corrects for inflated Type I error. Differences between observed patterns and random patterns from null models indicate that ecological processes structured the observed pattern. However, there is still no methodological solution to the problem that co‐occurrence of species and covariation of variables with species richness could still lead to spurious correlations and biased regression coefficients (Hawkins et al. 2017). For the null model, we randomized the rows (species) of the species traits matrix 1000 times, while keeping the trait combinations fixed (random trait combinations for species are not biologically meaningful). This null model leads to orthopteran assemblages with random trait syndromes. Afterward, we calculated standardized effect sizes (ses) for the CWM trait values as ses X = [Xobserved – mean(Xrandom)]/SD(Xrandom), where Xobserved is the observed CWM trait value, mean (Xrandom) is the mean of the randomizations of the null model, and SD(Xrandom) is the standard deviation of the randomly calculated CWM trait value.

    Allometric scaling

    Evolutionary or physical constraints can lead to disproportionate changes in morphological traits (L) with body size B (L ∝ Bb; see Shingleton et al. 2007 for an overview of allometric relationships). Morphological traits scale isometrically when b = 1, hypometrically when b < 1, and hypermetrically when b > 1. Allometry of traits with body size can therefore lead to constrained relationships of trait values with environmental factors if body size correlates with the environmental factor. Hypermetry of wing length with body size (larger orthopterans have proportionally larger wings) could lead, e.g., to a negative relationship of wing length with increasing elevation if body size decreases with elevation. We therefore tested for allometry across the sampled orthopteran species by calculating the scaling exponent (b) of the log10‐transformed traits wing length, hind femur length, and eye size to log10(body length). To do so, we used the function ma from the R package smatr (Warton et al. 2012). The significance of deviations from isometry was evaluated by calculating 95% confidence intervals for the regression slopes. The relationship between wing length and body length was hypermetric because 40 species had very short wings. When we excluded these 40 species from the data set, the relationship was isometric (Appendix S1: Fig. S1A, B, Table S1). For hind femur length and eye size, b did not differ significantly from one, which indicated isometry with body length (Appendix S1: Fig. S1C, D, Table S1). We therefore expected that patterns in trait changes along environmental gradients are not driven by scaling relationships.

    Phylogenetic and specific components of trait values

    Phenotypes are constrained by phylogenetic relationships of species (Ashton 2004, Allen et al. 2006). To test whether and to what extent patterns of morphological traits are phylogenetically independent, we partitioned the trait values into a phylogenetic and a species‐specific component (e.g., Lynch 1991). The phylogenetic component is estimated as the expectation of a trait value of a species from its phylogenetic history (the trait values of its ancestors; Lynch 1991). The species‐specific component measures the deviation of each species from the expectation of the phylogenetic component (Lynch 1991, Zeuss et al. 2014). Sophisticated methods use phylogenies to estimate the phylogenetic as well as the species‐specific component. As such a phylogeny is not available for our species, we followed the approach of Stearns (1992). We implemented the idea of Stearns (1992) using intercept‐only linear mixed‐effect models of the preprocessed species trait values (multivariate measure of body size and relative trait values) with genus nested in subfamily, subfamily nested in family, and family nested in suborder as random factor, using the function lme from the R package nlme (Pinheiro et al. 2018). To obtain the phylogenetic component of the four trait values of each species we used the predict function in R (R Core Team 2017), which estimates the expected trait value of each species based on its nested taxonomy. The specific component of the trait values depicts the residual from the phylogenetic component and was estimated using the residuals function from the R package nlme (Pinheiro et al. 2018). The phylogenetic and specific component values of each species were then used to calculate the CWM values of both components for orthopteran assemblages, following the same approach as for the observed trait values (see Methods: Processing of traits). Furthermore the intercept‐only linear mixed model was used to estimate the variance components on each taxonomic level, which gives an impression on the variability of a trait on the taxonomic levels (see Appendix S6: Fig. S1).

    Effects of abiotic and biotic factors on traits

    We used Bayesian linear mixed models to test the effects of elevation and NDVI on the CWM values, the unweighted mean values of assemblages, the standardized effect sizes of the CWM values, the phylogenetic component, and the species‐specific component of the four preprocessed morphological traits body size, wing length, length of hind femur, and eye size. We used a normal error distribution and fitted all models using Markov‐Chain‐Monte‐Carlo sampling and the Stan program (Carpenter et al. 2017) in the RStanArm library (function stan_lmer; Stan Development Team 2016) in the R statistical environment (R Core Team 2017). To correct for the nested structure of our data (data from different months and years) and for pseudo‐replication (data were collected several times on the same plots), we included sampling month nested in year, as well as the study plot as two random effects in all models. To allow for comparison of the model coefficients of the fixed effects, we applied a z transformation to all predictor variables. All coefficient and variance parameters were given uninformative prior distributions [intercept ~ N(0, 10); coefficients ~ N(0, 2.5)]. To keep the parameters scale‐free, the priors were additionally scaled internally by multiplying the prior by 2 ×  SD (predictor). Mismatches of the scales of the sampler resolution and the target distribution can lead to divergent transitions and biased estimates of the posterior distribution. Owing to the occurrence of divergent transitions, we adjusted the sampler resolution for the models of wing length, hind femur length, and eye size by setting the target acceptance probability of 0.95 to 0.99. This leads to smaller step sizes of the sampler and decreases approximation error. The Markov chain Monte Carlo implementation used four chains of 2,000 iterations; the first 1,000 iterations in each chain were discarded as burn‐in to achieve convergence, which resulted in 4,000 samples for each posterior distribution. Chain convergence and autocorrelation were assessed using trace plots of posterior samples. The values of the Gelman‐Rubin statistic (the potential scale reduction factor from the comparison of within‐chain variance and between‐chain variance) indicated convergence with Ŕ < 1.1 for all parameters in all models.

    The distribution of plots along the studied gradient was unbalanced, with 11 plots (1.5%) distributed across 58% of the elevation gradient (from 2300 to 4410 m a.s.l.) and 437 plots (98.5%) covering the lower part of the gradient (from 790 to 2300 m a.s.l.). We therefore tested whether the results of the original analysis is robust for a reduced data set with a more balanced sampling design. For this, we draw 100 random subsamples of the complete data set (736 observations at 440 study plots). Each subsample consisted of one observation of each plot (440 observations at 440 study plots). We used Bayesian linear mixed models following the same conditions as described above (plot was not included as random factor).

    Results

    In total, we recorded 160 orthopteran species across 448 study plots. These species belonged to 114 genera from nine families (Acrididae, Euschmidtiidae, Gryllacrididae, Lentulidae, Pamphagidae, Pyrgomorphidae, Tetrigidae, Tettigoniidae, and Thericleidae). Species richness ranged from 0 to 27 species (see Appendix S3: Fig. S1 for the variation of species richness along the elevational gradient). The original trait values ranged from 0 (without wings) to 6.4 mm for wing length, from 0.56 to 4.4 mm for hind femur length, and from 0.07 to 0.48 mm for eye diameter (Appendix S2: Table S1; also for the residuals of linear regressions of the trait values plotted against body length, trait means and standard deviations). The first axis of the principal component analysis of the 10 morphological traits explained 75% of the trait variation (Appendix S2: Fig. S1, Table S2). The multivariate body size measure ranged from −1.62 to 1.69 (± 0.75 SD) and was positively correlated with the measured body length (r² = 0.80, P < 0.005; Pearson's product‐moment correlation coefficient).

    Species richness decreased with increasing elevation (Appendix S3: Fig. S1, Table S1). Likewise, CWM values of the four traits body size, wing length, hind femur length, and eye size decreased with increasing elevation (Table 1, Fig. 2). The patterns were also significant for standardized effect sizes of the four CWM trait values (Appendix S4: Fig. S1, Table S1). The CWM values of body size and eye size, as well as their standardized effect sizes, were negatively correlated with NDVI (Table 1, Fig. 2 for CWM values, Appendix S4: Fig. S1 and Table S1 for standardized effect sizes). Wing length and NDVI were negatively related when standardized effect sizes were used (Appendix S4: Fig. S1, Table S1). The relationships between elevation or NDVI and the morphological traits were not significant when we used unweighted mean trait values of assemblages or their standardized effect sizes, except for the relationship between eye size and NDVI (Appendix S5: Figs. S1 and S2). This indicates that the patterns of CWM trait values are driven by changes in the abundance of species and not by changes in the presence of species along the studied gradients.

    Discussion

    Our study revealed a clear negative relationship between elevation and body size of orthopteran assemblages. Independently from the body size pattern, the relative wing length, hind femur length, and eye size also decreased with increasing elevation (traits were corrected for covariance with body length). In addition, productivity (measured as NDVI) was negatively related to body size and eye size.

    Effects of environmental factors on body size

    The body size of orthopteran assemblages decreased with increasing elevation. This finding is in line with our expectation and in line with previous studies, which found decreasing body sizes of ectotherms toward colder areas (Janes 1994, Whitman 2008, Parsons and Joern 2014, but see Zeuss et al. 2017). For instance, body sizes of bees decrease with increasing elevation at Mt. Kilimanjaro (Schellenberger Costa et al. 2017) and likewise, body sizes of grasshopper assemblages decrease with increasing elevation in the Cantabrian Mountains in Spain (Laiolo et al. 2013).

    Decreasing insect body sizes with increasing elevation are often explained by the decreasing season length, which favors fast development (Blanckenhorn and Demont 2004, Parsons and Joern 2014, but see Zeuss et al. 2017). Nevertheless, the growing season at Mt. Kilimanjaro starts to be limiting only at elevations higher than 2,700 m a.s.l. (Hemp 2006) and is therefore not likely to be the reason for the observed pattern in our study area. Instead, we propose that the declining temperature with increasing elevation is the main driver of the observed pattern. Decreasing temperature negatively affects temperature‐dependent biochemical processes and growth rates (van der Have and de Jong 1996), and low temperature shortens the larval growth period via a hormonal pathway that leads to small adult body size (Chown and Gaston 2010). Large orthopteran species, by contrast, have advantages in warm habitats at low elevations, namely reduced desiccation risk (Chown and Gaston 2010). Large size reduces the rate of water loss owing to the smaller surface‐to‐volume ratio and simultaneously increases the capacity to store water (Chown and Gaston 1999 and references within). Furthermore, large size is advantageous for effective thermoregulation; overheating is prevented because a high thermal inertia of high body mass relates to greater temperature homeostasis (Whitman 1987). However, effective thermoregulation would also be beneficial at high elevations where temperatures are low and can be also influenced by cuticle color (Bishop et al. 2016). This might explain why we did not find the smallest orthopteran assemblages on plots at the highest elevations. On the contrary, large body size can also be beneficial under harsh environmental conditions at high elevations. Benefits of efficient energy use of large individuals under harsh environmental conditions at high elevations, high metabolic rates at high temperatures, and the ability to extend generation time over multiple years have been proposed as mechanisms behind negative temperature–size relationships, e.g., among moth, ant, and lepidopteran assemblages (Gibb et al. 2017, Schellenberger Costa et al. 2017, Zeuss et al. 2017). Moreover, changes in coloration can additionally influence body size patterns along temperature gradients, as darker coloration helps to absorb heat (Zeuss et al. 2014, Bishop et al. 2016).

    We used the NDVI as a measure of productivity and as a proxy for resource availability. The NDVI values along our studied environmental gradient indicated the highest productivity at mid‐elevation. At this elevation, the temperature is favorable for plant growth and humidity reaches its maximum, which allows submontane and lower montane forests to grow (Hemp 2006). In line with the resource availability hypothesis, we expected positive relationships between productivity and the four morphological traits because of positive effects of the traits representing fecundity, dispersal ability, jumping ability, and predation risk. The finding of decreasing body sizes of assemblages with increasing productivity therefore contradicts our expectation. However, large body size not only increases fecundity of orthopterans, for example, but also their starvation resistance (Whitman 2008). High resistance to starvation would be of particular advantage in habitats with low productivity. Furthermore, large orthopteran species have a wide dietary breadth and are able to chew tough food, which makes them both intraspecifically and interspecifically competitive (Fielding 2004, Whitman 2008). Large size might therefore be particularly advantageous at low elevations, where starvation risk is high. Moreover, our finding underpins assumptions from other studies that often suggest the greater starvation resistance of larger sized arthropods as an explanation for increasing body sizes with decreasing temperatures or toward more seasonal environments (Cushman et al. 1993, Gibb et al. 2017). This suggests, that it might not be temperature per se that drives the pattern in these studies but the associated declining availability of resources that increases the risk of starvation. The relationship between body size and resource availability can be additionally influenced by seasonal life cycles. Some orthopteran species that occur at low elevation with low resource availability during the hot and dry months, e.g., only occur after the rainy season when resource conditions are favorable (Hemp 2009, 2013). Such species‐specific adaptations could explain the variability in body sizes of orthopteran assemblages in relation to productivity.

    Effects of environmental factors on wing length

    In accordance with our expectation, the relative wing length of orthopteran assemblages decreased along the elevational gradient. We used residuals from linear models of the trait values plotted against body length as trait measures because morphological traits usually scale with body length. The significant negative relationship between (relative) wing length and elevation therefore indicates that wing length decreases more strongly than expected from the decrease in body length. The reduction or loss of wings is a common adaptation of insects at high elevations (Hodkinson 2005). Low air density at high elevation hampers flight performance of insects. Furthermore, low temperatures at high elevation additionally lead to low metabolic rates and negatively affect muscle physiology (Dillon et al. 2006). The maintenance of flight performance at high elevations by means of, e.g., increasing wing length or wing area would therefore be exceptionally resource intensive (Dillon et al. 2006). Furthermore, a larger surface‐to‐volume ratio of insects with long wings can increase the loss of thermal energy and was, e.g., referred to by Peters et al. (2016) to explain decreasing wing length of bee assemblages with increasing elevation.

    Contrary to our expectation, wing length of orthopteran assemblages did not respond to changes in productivity. However, our analysis of the pattern of standardized effect sizes of wing length, as well as results derived from 100 models of randomly subsampled data sets with a more balanced sampling design across the elevation gradient, revealed a significant but negative relationship to productivity. Even though the maintenance of long wings is resource intensive, benefits of long wings, especially in areas with low productivity, might counterbalance the costs. Long wings increase flight ability, which provides advantages for resource tracking, colonization of new habitats, and finding of mates. Short wings and winglessness, by contrast, are a common local adaptation to long‐term stable habitats (Roff 1990, Denno et al. 1991, Zera and Denno 1997, Picaud and Petit 2008). However, the NDVI measure does not incorporate habitat stability, temporal variability, or the distribution of resources in adjacent areas. This might explain why we did not find a significant pattern for the original pattern of wing length with productivity.

    Effects of environmental factors on hind femur length and eye size

    Hind femur length and eye size (corrected for covariance with body length) also decreased with increasing elevation. Both traits influence the ability to avoid predation. Hind femur length is positively related to the ability to jump and therefore affects escape ability, whereas eye size positively affects the quality of the visual system, which influences predator detection (Bennet‐Clark 1990, Rutowski 2000). Our results therefore suggest that orthopteran assemblages have better anti‐predator traits at low elevations than at high elevations. However, note that elevation explained only a relatively small portion of the variation in hind femur length, the pattern was mainly driven by assemblages at the high elevation plots that were characterized by few species with relatively short legs, and we cannot exclude a phylogenetic bias. Changes in predation pressure along the elevation gradient might be a possible explanation for the decrease in hind femur length and eye size with increasing elevation (Roslin et al. 2017). Birds and spiders are the main predators of orthopterans, and their abundances generally decline with increasing elevation, often with mid‐elevation peaks (Rahbek 1995, Branson 2005, Röder et al. 2017). We do not have data on predator abundances on our plots, but we speculate that assemblages of orthopterans in areas with more predators would consist of more species with long hind femurs and large eyes because of the benefits of enhanced escape ability and predator detection. Comparable to the length of wings, also long hind legs can lead to a higher heat loss, which could additionally benefit orthopterans with short legs or hind femur length at high elevations (Peters et al. 2016). Another possible explanation for the decrease in eye size with increasing elevation is the increasing UV radiation. High UV radiation can damage the visual system of insects (Mishra and Meyer‐Rochow 2008) and could make investments into the visual system ineffective at high elevations. Other protective traits such as chemical components and mimicry also influence the predation risk of orthopterans and could have additionally influenced the relationships between hind femur length and eye size with elevation (Whitman et al. 1985, Castner 1995).

    Contrary to our expectation, there was no relationship between productivity and hind femur length of assemblages. Thus and because elevation explained only a small portion of the variability in the data, we assume that additional factors influence hind femur lengths of orthopteran assemblages. Also the pattern of eye size did not comply with our expectation of increasing eye sizes with increasing productivity, based on the high energy needed for the development and maintenance of large eyes (Prokopy 1983). Eye size decreased with increasing plant productivity. Plots with low productivity were dominated by bare ground and low vegetation cover, as indicated by the NDVI values. Open areas provide few possibilities for orthopterans to hide (e.g., between tall grasses or below bushes and trees), which makes it easier for predators to find and catch prey. Consequently, large eyes may be of particular advantage in open areas because they can enhance the early detection of predators. Even though the size of insect eyes is one of the main determinants of visual quality, the total visual performance depends also on structures independent of eye size, e.g., the architecture of the retina and the central nervous system (Land 1997, Rutowski 2000). Furthermore, the ability to detect predators will also be influenced by mechanical and chemical means of predator perception, such as hairs or olfaction (Dangles et al. 2005). Protective coloration and crypsis, however, reduce the risk of being detected by predators (Steiner 1981, Eterovick et al. 1997).

    Other factors that were not considered in our study will additionally affect morphological traits of arthropods (Allen et al. 2006). Sexual selection may, e.g., favor large individuals (Blanckenhorn 2000) and the quality of resources can play significant roles in determining final body size of animals (Chown and Gaston 2010). Furthermore, local microclimatic conditions, which are in turn influenced by e.g., slope, aspect, and humidity, can induce differences between the temperature that individuals experience and the assumed temperature expected from a general linear decline of temperature with increasing elevation.

    Phylogenetic relationships

    Additional to the analysis of the original traits of orthopteran assemblages, we differentiated the total variation in trait values into a phylogenetic and a species‐specific component and analyzed them independently (Lynch 1991, Diniz‐Filho et al. 2007, Ramirez et al. 2007, Zeuss et al. 2014). The phylogenetic component is assumed to measure the phylogenetic legacy of a trait, whereas the species‐specific component estimates the response of a species to its environment. Most of the variation of the morphological traits between species was explained by phylogenetic relationships. This suggests that body size, relative wing length, hind femur length, and eye size are phylogenetically conserved. This is in line with findings of previous studies, which found that morphological traits, especially body size, contain a strong phylogenetic component (Brändle et al. 2002, Freckleton et al. 2002, Blomberg et al. 2003, Diniz‐Filho et al. 2007). The patterns of the phylogenetic trait components resembled the patterns of the original trait values along the elevation and productivity gradient. This is not surprising considering the amount of variation explained by the taxonomic hierarchy. Therefore, our results suggest that the observed patterns in size clines are mainly driven by taxonomic turnover of clades from low to high elevations as well as with respect to changing productivity. Our study therefore underlines the importance of phylogeny as a driver of size clines in morphological traits (Ramirez et al. 2007, Zeuss et al. 2014). Although the phylogenetic component of traits explained most of the variation in body size (> 90%), there were still phylogenetically independent and significant relationships between the species‐specific component of body size, wing length, hind femur length, and eye size with elevation and of eye size with NDVI. Thus, both species‐specific adaptive response to changing environmental conditions as well as phylogenetic conservatism explain the observed patterns of body size, wing length, hind femur length, and eye size within orthopteran assemblages along environmental gradients (Ramirez et al. 2007).

    Conclusions

    Our study revealed that body size as well as wing length, hind femur length, and eye size corrected for covariance with body length of orthopteran assemblages decreases with increasing elevation. Body size and relative eye size also decreased with increasing productivity (measured as NDVI). Thereby, both the evolutionary history as well as recent, species‐specific aspects of changes in morphological traits contributed to the observed patterns. Our results support the importance of temperature as one of the main drivers of interspecific body size clines. Moreover, our findings emphasize the importance of temperature also for interspecific clines of other ecologically relevant morphological traits, independent of changes in body size. Our results suggest that orthopteran assemblages have higher fecundity, dispersal ability, escape ability, and predator detection at low elevations with high temperatures, compared to assemblages at high elevations with low temperatures. Furthermore, our results do not support the resource availability hypothesis in that assemblages were characterized by species with larger body sizes and larger eyes in areas of low productivity. These habitats were characterized by low vegetation cover and few resources, which might be linked with a high risk of starvation and predation. Hence, it seems that the absence of vegetation resources rather than their availability favors orthopteran assemblages with energetically expensive morphological traits. Our study emphasizes that morphological adaptations involve not only adaptations of body size but also independent adaptations of other morphological traits. To better understand the ecological interactions between organisms and their environment, future studies should aim to see beyond patterns of body size and include changes in other morphological traits along environmental gradients.

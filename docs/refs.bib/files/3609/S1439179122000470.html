Positive effects of ants on host trees are critical in years of low reproduction and not influenced by liana presence

Abstract

Tropical tree-climbing lianas form paths that benefit foraging of dominant ants which might protect the host tree against herbivores. In contrast, lianas are often associated with negative effects on growth and reproduction of host trees due to light obstruction, structural stress and other negative effects. It is unclear if dominant ants could mitigate the negative effects of lianas on host plants. We investigated how lianas and carton nest ants (Azteca chartifex) affected herbivory and reproductive structures of the host tree Byrsonima sericea. Considering 68 trees, almost half of them were naturally colonized by A. chartifex nests (32 trees). We removed lianas from half of the trees (34), establishing a factorial sampling design between A. chartifex and liana presence. We sampled ants and leaf herbivory before and after removing lianas, and measured plant fitness in two consecutive years after removing lianas. Liana removal had no effect on A. chartifex foraging, on leaf herbivory and flower-fruit conversion of host plants. However, A. chartifex decreased leaf herbivory and increased B. sericea flower-fruit conversion irrespective of lianas presence. A noticeable positive effect of ants was detected only in the second year of the experiment, consistently on all plants at each experimental level. The reproductive conditions of the first year resulted in most plants with more than 75% flower-to-fruit conversion success, regardless of the presence of A. chartifex, a success sustained only on those ant-colonized plants in the second year. Our results contribute to understanding multi-trophic interactions in tropical forest canopies as we demonstrated i) that dominant arboreal ants can benefit plants even in a non-obligatory interaction and ii) that the influence of lianas on its host tree is context-dependent, presenting even neutral effects depending on habitat type and species involved.

Introduction

Ecological interactions are considered major drivers of plant fitness (Del-Claro et al., 2018; Herrera et al., 2002; Thompson, 2005). Tropical trees may interact with a myriad of species resulting in a large range from positive to negative effects on plant resource acquisition (Dáttilo & Rico-Gray, 2018; Thompson, 2005). Two species assemblages with contrasting effects on tropical trees are 1) the lianas, which can parasite up to 75% of trees in tropical forests, depending on disturbance (Ingwell, Joseph Wright, Becklund, Hubbell & Schnitzer, 2010) and 2) the ants, which make up to 25% of animal biomass in tropical forests (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990). While lianas act as wood climbers and can cause direct negative effects on host plants, such as light obstruction, structural stress, hampering sap conduction and others (Ingwell et al., 2010; Putz, 1984; Putz & Mooney, 1992; Schnitzer, 2018; Schnitzer & Carson, 2010; van der Heijden & Phillips, 2009), ants, by means of an aggressive patrolling behavior, might decrease leaf herbivory and increase plant performance (Rosumek et al., 2009; Trager et al., 2010). Despite being overlooked, to disentangle the contrasting effects of lianas and ants on plant fitness would help to understand how this multi-trophic interaction could influence the tree reproductive success and forest regeneration.

Lianas are especially important for dominant ant species that require abundant resources to maintain large and often polydomous colonies (e.g., belonging to Azteca and Crematogaster genera) (Holldobler & Wilson, 1990). Lianas can be used by ants to expand foraging area and patrolling efficiency (Clay, Bauer, Solis, & Yanoviak, 2010) and as a source of extra-floral nectar (EFN) (Bluthgen & Fiedler, 2002), increasing ant species richness on host plants (Adams, Schnitzer & Yanoviak, 2017). Therefore, lianas might be even more important to dominant ants, which present populous colonies and display aggressive behavior characterized by inter- and intraspecific confrontations to defend resources and territory (e.g., host plants) (Dejean, Corbara, Orivel & Leponce, 2007). Ant protection effectiveness on plants may depend on the resources offered by the host plants or by associated lianas (e.g., nesting sites and food resources such as EFN or trophobionts) which can mediate mutualistic interactions (Fagundes et al., 2017; Ness, Morris & Bronstein, 2006). In ant-plant mutualism, the ants often decrease leaf herbivory and increase the fitness of host plant (Rosumek et al., 2009; Trager et al., 2010). However, for non-obligatory (facultative) ant-plant interactions, which have been neglected in the literature, the effect of ants on hebivory levels is contrasting (Brenes-Arguedas, Coley & Kursar, 2007; Itino & Itioka, 2001; Kaplan, Halitschke, Kessler, Sardanelli & Denno, 2008). Further, how do ants impact the fitness of unattractive plants is not known. As lianas are highly frequent plants living on trees in tropical forests (Ingwell et al., 2010), they likely influence the outcome of ant-host plant interactions.

Here, we studied a multi-trophic system located in the Atlantic Forest in southern Brazil, involving Byrsonima sericea DC. (Malpighiaceae), which is a pioneer tree species that shapes the canopy of forest-lake ecotones. Byrsonima sericea trees present at the same time: i) different species of lianas that naturally climb its crowns; ii) a high diversity of herbivorous insects (Ribeiro, Soares, Campos & Martins, 2008), and iii) a natural mosaic of trees presenting numerous carton nests of Azteca chartifex Emery, 1896 (Dolichoderinae) and other naturally uncolonized trees with low abundance of other ant species. Moreover, by means of its territorialism and aggressive behavior (Longino, 2007), A. chartifex decreases herbivory levels in B. sericea trees (unpublished results).

Thus, based on this natural multi-trophic laboratory, our aim is to experimentally investigate how lianas affect the herbivory and reproduction of B. sericea, comparing trees with and without nests of the ant A. chartifex. Our study addresses three hypotheses: (1) The presence of lianas positively affects A. chartifex foraging on B. sericea; (2) The presence of A. chartifex and lianas negatively influences leaf herbivory on B. sericea; and (3) The presence of A. chartifex and lianas has contrasting effects (positive and negative, respectively) on B. sericea reproduction. However, as the lianas are the main connections between trees in the forest (Odell, Stork & Kitching, 2019; Yanoviak, 2015), A. chartifex should increase foraging by traveling longer distances to protect its territory when lianas are present (Clay et al., 2010). Therefore, we expected the widely described negative effects of lianas (Ingwell et al., 2010; Putz, 1984; Putz & Mooney, 1992; Schnitzer, 2018; Schnitzer & Carson, 2010; van der Heijden & Phillips, 2009) to be mitigated by enhancing the positive effects of ants against herbivores (Rosumek et al., 2009; Trager et al. 2010), consequently favoring the reproductive success of B. sericea.

Materials and methods

Study area

The study was conducted in the Rio Doce State Park (PERD in Portuguese acronym) (19˚48’-19˚29’S and 42˚38’- 42˚28’W), in Minas Gerais State, southeastern Brazil. The climatic regime of the region is tropical seasonal (Aw), according to Köppen's classification (Alvares, Stape, Sentelhas, de Moraes Gonçalves & Sparovek, 2013). The climatic regime is well defined with two seasons: the dry period between May-September and the rainy season between October-April. Average precipitation is around 1,500 mm per year (Alvares et al., 2013). The PERD covers an area of approximately 36,000 ha of Atlantic Forest varying from 200 and 500 m above sea level and it comprises the largest natural neotropical lake system. About 11% of the park is covered by 42 lakes, in spite of the young evolutionary history of the forest (Fonseca-Silva, Carvalho & Ribeiro, 2015, 2019).

The park encompasses a vast ecotone of forest-lake zones with high sun light availability, resulting in the formation of trees with main branches that grow bent towards the lakes at 1-3 meters above the ground. This results in what has been called ‘brought-low canopy’ (Lourenço et al., 2019), which is, strictly speaking, a typical canopy habitat lying close to the ground, on the ecotone shore or over the lakes’ margin. PERD is an International Long-Term Ecological Research site (ILTER) whose tree-arthropod systems have been investigated since 2002 (Campos, Soares, Martins & Ribeiro, 2006; Campos, Vasconcelos, Ribeiro, Neves & Soares, 2006; Coelho & Ribeiro, 2006; Ribeiro et al., 2008; Dolabela et al., 2022). Our group has described that ecotones between forest and natural lakes in the Atlantic Forest keep typical upper canopy traits, due to whole crowns being bent towards the water (Barbosa, 2014). Upper canopy leaves present a high degree of sclerophylly and typical morphological attributes caused by high insolation and lower nutrient quality (Ribeiro & Basset, 2007, 2016; Sanches et al., 2010), as in the lake´s brought-low canopy (Pinto et al., 2021). Additionally, brought-low canopy leaves have rates of leaf respiration similar to those of emergent trees (Sanches et al., 2010) and the crown architecture is typical of a canopy tree (e.g., trunk-leaf biomass ratio, qualitative data on leaf characteristics and trunk ramifications) (Barbosa, 2014; Pinto et al., 2021). Such forests are occupied by typical canopy insects (Lourenço et al., 2019) and fully taken by Azteca chartifex (Campos et al., 2006). Due to this ‘brought-low canopy’, the lake shores in PERD provide easy canopy access, likely to be properly sampled with a ladder and basic security climbing gears, as allow to produce a comprehensive and replicated experiment.

Study system

Azteca chartifex Emery, 1896 (Formicidade: Dolichoderinae) is a territorial, dominant, and polydomic ant that belongs to a strictly neotropical group of arboreal ants (Longino, 2007). The nests of A. chartifex are structurally based on cellulose and processed fibers (i.e., carton nest), and may contain up to a million individuals (Baccaro et al., 2015; Wheeler, 1986). Azteca chartifex has a non-obligatory mutual association with B. sericea, establishing the main nest pending in its trunk and numerous smaller satellite nests distributed along the branches. The satellite nests usually present only workers (Miranda et al., 2021). For instance, on the trees presenting A. chartifex nests in our field area we counted on average 23 (SE = 1.58) satellite nests (4 - 15 cm) per tree (3.45 - 7.99 m height; 5.95 ± 0.20, mean ± SE) in a radius of 8 m around the main nest (n = 32). Considering nest sizes, we specified that the average size was taken from a sample of satellites reachable by climbing. We performed our counting with two independent samplers (always the same ones) using binoculars (one climbing the tree and one in the ground). These two samplers simultaneously counted the satellite nests across the crown in each tree. Thus, we estimated the total number of satellite nests per tree by averaging each sampler blind counting. In the PERD, we observed that when A. chartifex is present, B. sericea trees within its territory are constantly foraged by a large number of A. chartifex workers.

Byrsonima sericea predominates in the vegetation in forest-lake ecotone inside PERD, making it a dominant long-lived leaves species, with sclerophyllous leaves and a complex crown architecture (e.g., height, trunk diameter, number of growth units and number of leaves) (Barbosa, 2014; Pinto et al., 2021). Although of some level of sclerophylly, B. sericea also has leaves with high levels of nitrogen (Pinto et al., 2021). In these forest-lake ecotones, B. sericea crowns grow towards the lakes, positioning at the same height of understory stratum. Flowering and fruiting of B. sericea occur from late October until the end of April (Teixeira & Machado, 2000). This species does not produce EFNs. Byrsonima sericea trees selected in our study site have a mean volume of 6,79 leaves/m3, mean height of 5.68 m (± 0.13 SE), and mean diameter at breast height of 59 cm (± 3 SE) (n = 68). All B. sericea individuals that occur in ecotone areas of PERD host lianas in their crowns (about 10 species of lianas), which spread on the canopy of forest ecotones. An average of 75% of B. sericea crown area (± 30% SE) is occupied by lianas and these are used by A. chartifex as routes for maximizing their foraging.

Experimental design

We arbitrarily selected 68 individuals of Byrsonima sericea, distributed among three distinct forest-lake ecotone sites (Fig. 1). All B. sericea trees were between 3.45 and 7.99 m high and grew on sunny lake borders with similar light availability. All studied tree individuals had lianas in their crowns. Thirty-two trees were naturally colonized by Azteca chartifex and while 36 were not. We considered “colonized trees” individuals that carried the main A. chartifex nest or at least five active satellite nests on its crown (n = 32). Trees without nests were classified as “uncolonized trees” (n = 36). Field experiment was carried out in three phases: phase I in March 2016 (only ant effects), phase II in August 2016 (liana removal experiment), and phase III in March 2017 (complete experimental design). In phase I, we divided all 68 trees into two groups: “colonized trees” and “uncolonized trees”, only to detect A. chartifex effects (see details below). In phase II, we randomly selected half of the B. sericea individuals from phase I (34 of the 68 trees), including colonized and uncolonized trees, and experimentally removed the lianas from their crowns (those plants were then considered as “lianas removed”); the remaining individuals were not manipulated and thus considered as “lianas present” (see Fig. 1). Thus, in phase II we had our complete experimental design as follow: i) colonized trees with lianas present (n = 16), ii) uncolonized trees with lianas present (n = 18), iii) colonized trees with lianas removed (n = 16), and iv) uncolonized trees with lianas removed (n = 18) (Appendix A: “Experimental design details”). We cut-off all lianas from the 34 trees starting at 2 m from ground level using machetes, tree pruner, and pruning shears, and then removed lianas’ aerial parts with a ladder, or climbing if necessary. After liana removal, we visited all patches bi-monthly for the next 18 months to monitor B. sericea individuals and cut-off the re-sprouting liana shoots in all the individuals of the “lianas removed” group. Finally, in phase III we sampled ant diversity and leaf herbivory from all the 68 tree individuals (see details below) in the same experimental design of phase II.

Ant foraging activity and herbivory

In phase III, after lianas removal experiment, we sampled ants and leaves (for herbivory analysis) in crowns of all 68 B. sericea individuals with the aid of an aluminum ladder (max. 5 m high) and basic climbing security gears. We sampled ants using entomological umbrellas and performing the beating technique (Ribeiro et al., 2005). For this, in each tree we randomly selected three branches and performed 10 beats per branch with a beating stick (selected branches had approximately the same number of leaves) in order to dislodge the associated ants onto a tray placed beneath. Ants were identified with the aid of taxonomic keys (Baccaro et al., 2015) and comparing specimens with the collection of the ‘Laboratory of Ecology of Diseases & Forests’ at the Universidade Federal de Ouro Preto, Minas Gerais State, Brazil. In addition, we had assistance of a taxonomist (Dr. Rodrigo Feitosa). On uncolonized trees we only sampled non-Azteca chartifex ants. Overall, as the richness and abundance of non-Azteca chartifex ants were low (in all 68 trees sampled: richness: 1.88 ± 0.22; abundance: 7.08 ± 1.63, mean and SE, N = 68), probably due to the aggressiveness and territorialism of the A. chartifex (see Philpott, Serber & De la Mora, 2018), these data were excluded from the following analyses. Vouchers were deposited at the above-mentioned laboratory. Licenses for the field studies were issued by the state authority ‘Instituto Estadual de Florestas (IEF)’ and the national authority ‘Sistema de Autorização e Informação em Biodiversidade/ Instituto Chico Mendes de Conservação da Biodiversidade (SISBIO/ ICMBio) (SISBIO authorization number 55058-1, IEF authorization 088/2016)’.

We collected the leaves to estimate herbivory level (phase III) with the guidance of a 0.027 m3 wire-framed cube (Appendix A: Fig. 1) positioned at three distinct points inside the crowns (adapted from Ribeiro & Basset, 2007; Shaw, Ernest, Rinker & Lowman, 2006). This methodology aims to guarantee a real random leaf sampling. In order to better represent tree herbivory, we positioned the cube using a ladder and climbing devices and randomly sampled three regions inside crowns. We collected all leaves that were inside the cube (mean = 183 leaves, N = 68 trees), resulting in a final volume of 0.081 m3 and approximately 550.58 ± 28.90 leaves (mean ± SE) per tree. These leaves were digitalized and analyzed in the ImageJ software (Schneider, Rasband & Eliceiri, 2012). The percentage of herbivory per leaf was calculated as (leaf area removed x 100)/(total leaf area) using the software ImageJ. Individual percentages were then averaged for each sampled plant. Finally, in phase III we sampled A. chartifex foraging activity on B. sericea branches. The foraging activity was measured by counting the number of A. chartifex individuals collected by beating sampling.

B. sericea reproductive success

To estimate the possible effect of A. chartifex colonization, the presence of lianas, and the interaction between both, we measured the conversion of flower to fruit (a proxy of plant fitness) in all 68 B. sericea trees. To estimate the conversion of flower to fruit we simply counted (with the aid of two manual counters) all inflorescences and infructescences in each of the 68 trees following the respective B. sericea reproductive seasons of 2017 and 2018. We counted the number of inflorescences at the peak of two flower seasons (January/2017 and December/2017) and the number of infructescences at the peak of two fruit seasons (February/2017 and February/2018). We performed the counting with two independent samplers (always the same ones) using binoculars (one climbing the tree and one on the ground). These two samplers simultaneously counted the inflorescences and infructescences across the crown, and all the growth units presented in each tree. Thus, we estimated the total number of inflorescences and infructescences per tree by averaging across the two counts. There were no inflorescences in one individual of B. sericea in 2017 and in four individuals in 2018, meaning that these individuals were subsequently excluded from analysis of reproductive success. Finally, to estimate the conversion of flower to fruit we divided the final number of infructescences per number of inflorescences produced per tree, in each reproductive season (2017 and 2018).

Data analysis

Using data from the complete experimental design (phase III), we used Generalized Linear Models (GLM) assuming quasi-Poisson error distribution to test whether A. chartifex foraging activity (i.e., number of workers per host plant) is affected by liana presence on colonized trees (hypothesis 1). For this first model, we considered A. chartifex foraging activity as response variable and liana presence (lianas present or removed) as explanatory variables.

Our second model was a Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) assuming beta error distribution (Ferrari & Cribari-Neto, 2004) and it was built to test the effect of lianas and A. chartifex foraging activity on colonized trees (explanatory variables) and their interaction on herbivory levels on B. sericea (response variable) (hypothesis 2). As our third model, we also tested the effect of lianas and A. chartifex presence (colonized and uncolonized trees), and their interaction on herbivory levels of B. sericea using Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) assuming beta error distribution (Ferrari & Cribari-Neto, 2004) (hypothesis 2). For the third model, we considered the presence of lianas and A. chartifex presence as explanatory variables and herbivory as response variable. In these two latter models, we considered the tree patches (see Fig. 1) as a random variable.

Finally, we built a fourth Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM), assuming a beta-binomial error distribution (Brooks et al., 2017) to test whether the presence of A. chartifex, lianas and the year of sampling (2017 and 2018), and their interactions (explanatory variables) affect the reproductive success (flower to fruit conversion) (response variable) of B. sericea pooling together the data from both reproductive seasons (2017 and 2018) (hypothesis 3). For this fourth model we used tree identity nested within treatment as random effect. To measure reproductive success (response variable), we combined the number of successes (representing the maximum number of infructescences recorded in each plant) and number of failures (maximum number of inflorescences recorded minus the maximum number of infructescences) using “cbind” function on software R (version 4.0.0) (R Core Team, 2020) as proportion of successful flower to fruit conversions. The residuals of all models were analyzed to verify the adequacy of the models (Crawley, 2013) using the “DHARMa” package (Hartig, 2017). All analyses were conducted in the software R (version 4.0.0) (R Core Team, 2020) using the “lme4” and “glmmTMB” package (Bates, Mächler, Bolker & Walker, 2015; Brooks et al., 2017).

Results

Effect of ants and lianas on herbivory

There was no effect of liana presence on A. chartifex foraging activity (F1,30 = 1.54, p = 0.22). The trees with lianas had on average 68.81 ± 74.19 individuals of A. chartifex, while the trees without lianas harbored 42.25 ± 37.19 individuals (Appendix A: Fig. 2). Liana removal also had no effect on herbivory, irrespective of ant presence (χ2= 0.40, p = 0.52, N = 68) (Fig. 2A) or ant foraging activity (χ2= 0.37, p = 0.53, N = 32) (Fig. 2B). The presence of A. chartifex had no effect on herbivory (χ2= 1.72, p = 0.18, N = 68). However, the foraging activity of A. chartifex had a negative effect on herbivory (χ2= 4.68, p = 0.03, N = 32) (Fig. 2B). The abundance of A. chartifex was able to reduce herbivory enormously (8.6% to 0.05%, maximum and minimum herbivory level; mean = 2.77%; SE = 0.33%). Finally, there was no interactive effect of lianas presence and i) ant presence or ii) foraging activity on herbivory (χ2= 0.02, p = 0.86, N = 64; χ2= 0.16, p = 0.68, N = 32, respectively).

Effect of lianas and ants on B. sericea reproductive success

Overall, considering all trees (irrespective of liana and A. chartifex presence), there was a reduction in flower/fruit conversion from 2017 (0.89 ± 0.02, N = 67) to 2018 (0.61 ± 0.04, N = 63). This pattern was consistent for plants in all patches (Appendix A: Table 1). We did not find isolated effect of lianas (χ2 = 0.33, p = 0.56, N = 130), A. chartifex (χ2 = 1.21, p = 0.27, N = 130) and year of sampling (χ2 = 2.00, p = 0.15, N = 130) in flower/fruit conversion. However, there was a significant interaction between A. chartifex presence and year of sampling on flower/fruit conversion rate (χ2 = 4.39, p = 0.03, N = 130) (Fig. 3). These results indicate that especially in the year with a reduced reproductive success (2018 compared to 2017), the trees colonized by ants, irrespective of liana presence, suffered a smaller decrease (2 x less) in flower/fruit conversion (19% decrease from 2017 to 2018) than uncolonized trees (45% decrease from 2017 to 2018). Hence, a noticeable positive effect of ants was detected only in the second year of the experiment, consistently to all plants (irrespective of liana presence). The reproductive conditions of the first year resulted that most trees had more than 75% conversion success, regardless of the present of A. chartifex, a success sustained only in the trees with ants in the second year (Fig. 3). Finally, we did not find any significant interaction at third level (Table 1).

Discussion

Our findings revealed that the carton nest ant, Azteca chartifex showed at the same time a negative influence on leaf herbivory and a positive influence on reproductive success of B. sericea tree. These positive effects of ants on plant fitness emerged strongly when the trees experienced a weaker reproductive season. Interestingly and contrary to our expectations liana removal had no effect on A. chartifex foraging, leaf herbivory and plant fitness. Our results contribute to understanding the outcomes of multi-trophic interactions in tropical forest canopies such as tree reproductive success and forest regeneration.

Although lianas often attract a diverse fauna of invertebrates (both herbivores and their natural enemies) (Estrada-Villegas & Schnitzer, 2018), lianas in our system did not enhance foraging of A. chartifex. Many ant species may use lianas as nesting sites and/or to access EFN or other food sources (Bentley, 1981). In our case, as A. chartifex build carton nests, they use more resistant trunks and branches of the host plant to build their nests. Although the use of lianas as trails by ants, promoting connections among tree crowns (Gordon, 2017), their presence did not lead to increased ant foraging. Similar findings from a field experiment considering vine-like vegetation showed that ants discovered baits sooner with lianas present, but this did not imply an increase of ant species richness (Clay et al., 2010). Further, an experimental study in the Brazilian savanna indicates that ant species richness and composition were similar among different levels of canopy connectivity (Powell, Costa, Lopes & Vasconcelos, 2011).

We also found that liana presence did not influence leaf herbivory on trees. This pattern may be because lianas did not attract more herbivores (but see Estrada-Villegas & Schnitzer, 2018) or herbivores have a preference for liana leaves which might represent a high quality food (young stem and leaf tissues) with lower chemical defenses compared to trees (Odegaard, 2000; Odell et al., 2019). Furthermore, this result may have directly influenced the absence of negative effects of lianas on B. sericea reproductive success. On the one hand, there are many other negative impacts of lianas on trees such as light obstruction, structural stress and competition (Ingwell et al., 2010; Putz, 1984; Putz & Mooney, 1992; Schnitzer, 2018; Schnitzer & Carson, 2010; van der Heijden & Phillips, 2009) but unfortunately we have no data to confirm that they were occurring in our study trees. On the other hand, it is possible that 18 months were too short a period of time for those negative effects of lianas to disappear on our liana-removed trees. García León, Martínez Izquierdo, Mello, Powers and Schnitzer (2018), for instance, detected these effects on many canopy tree species two years after liana removal. Here, we have a strongly controlled experiment showing that the ants and their host plant are not affected by the lianas, after 18 months of the removal experiment. Finally, it is also expected that the effects of lianas on the reproduction of host plants can also vary with tree species identity, vegetation structure (surroundings), tree size, abundance of lianas and others (Perez-Salicrup, Sork, & Putz, 2001; Wright, Tobin, Mangan & Schnitzer, 2015).

In contrast to lianas, the abundance of A. chartifex decreases leaf herbivory on B. sericea, regardless this being a plant species not specialized to attract ants (but see Zhang, Zhang & Ma, 2015). Although some trees without ants reached herbivory levels higher than the global average, and also taking into consideration that average herbivory on these plants varies over long periods (Mendes et al., 2021; Pinto et al., 2021), one needs to acknowledge that overall herbivory levels were lower than expected on “unprotected” plants during our study period (Turcotte, Davies, Thomsen & Johnson, 2014). Azteca chartifex forage in groups and recruit workers an alarm pheromone, so that when they discover a prey, they can directly eliminate it in a short time through attacks (Dejean, Grangier, Leroy & Orivel, 2009). For instance, considering Azteca chartifex and three other Azteca species, A. chartifex was the fastest to detect and attack an insect (Dejean et al., 2009). The workers from carton nest ants can even be effective in capturing flying insects on host plants (Dejean et al., 2007).

Trager et al. (2010) in a compreensive review, found that on average the presence of ants in plants increases the reproductive success of host plants by almost 50%. In fact, we observed that plants with A. chatifex nests experienced a higher flower/fruit conversion rate (i.e. fruit set) especially in the less reproductive season. On EFN-plants, for instance, this positive outcome of ant defense on host plant fitness can be a result of reduction of both floral herbivores and/or herbivory on flowers (see Belan, Barônio, Kuster, Oliveira & Vasconcelos, 2020). In addition, we found that B. sericea uncolonized trees by A. chartifex suffered higher leaf damage than colonized ones. Incresead herbivory could influence the allocation of resources to direct defenses (i.e., leaf chemical componds) and/or growth, which tends to decrease energy for investing in reproduction (Vieira da Costa et al., 2016; Huntzinger, Karban, Young & Palmer, 2004). Finally, there is another possible indirect effect of A. chartifex on the reproductive success of B. sericea: nutrient provision by ants (Sagers, Ginger, & Evans, 2000). Azteca trigona, which is a species closed related to A. chartifex, produce a nutrient-rich refuse, which is dropped from the carton nest to the soil below, making it available to the host tree, increasing its seedling growth and the available amount of essential nutrients (Lucas, Clay & Kaspari, 2018). However, future studies investigating this latter issue should be encouraged.

Our study goes beyond the role of dominant ants in structuring the insect community in the canopy (Ribeiro, Espirito Santo, Delabie & Majer, 2013; Sanders & Platner, 2007). As lianas and ants inhabit most plant species in the tropical forest canopies (Dejean et al., 2007; Parthasarathy, 2015; Putz & Mooney, 1992), the outcome of their associations are essential to assess the fitness of host plants and predicting future patterns of multi-trophic interactions in tropical forest canopies. Therefore, our findings add new evidence on how dominant ants in the tree crowns might improve its host plant's performance. Here, we found that the positive effect of dominant and non-myrmecophytic ant on plants can go beyond the classical herbivory defense model to an improvement of the host plant's reproductive success. Besides, we found that those benefits of ants to plants are completely independent of liana presence, at least in a medium term (18 months). Finally, our results showed that the effects of lianas on arthropod foraging and tree fitness can be context-dependent and probably lianas can sometimes act as a commensal species in tropical canopies.

Abstract

enThis link goes to a English sectionfrThis link goes to a French section

Tropical rain forests worldwide are affected by anthropogenic disturbances, and secondary forests that develop afterwards are often dominated by pioneer tree species, but the consequences of different anthropogenic disturbances on nutrient cycling are poorly understood. Because most nutrient cycling in tropical rain forests occurs through litterfall and in the soil organic layer, we measured litterfall of a widespread and dominant pioneer tree, okoume (Aucoumea klaineana, Burseraceae) in Gabon, in one forest previously disturbed by logging and in another by agriculture. Litterfall of okoume trees, measured over 14 months, was 18.2 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 in the formerly logged forest, which was 72.6% greater than in the forest more recently disturbed by agriculture (10.6 Mg haâ1 yearâ1). Litter decomposition rates were more rapid in the formerly logged forest, and this may explain why litter thickness was 32% lower in the formerly logged forest, despite the greater litterfall within it. Our results highlight that two widespread anthropogenic disturbances in Gabonese rain forests have significantly different consequences on litterfall of a dominant tree and therefore are likely to have different effects on nutrient cycling and forest ecosystem function.

1 INTRODUCTION

Litter decomposition plays a crucial role in tropical forest ecosystems whose vegetation depends on the recycling of nutrients contained in the detritus of plants (Arunachalam, Arunachalam, Pandey, & Tripathi, 1998), because of the low concentration of mineral elements in the soil (Sundarapandian & Swamy, 1999). Litter is key to understanding the cycling of mineral elements among forests at different stages of development (Paudel et al., 2015). The life cycle events of tree species including leaffall, flowering, fruiting and seed dissemination could also be seen as a good indicator of litter dynamics on the ground, as reported by phenological studies that have been wildly conducted in tropical forests (Mendoza, Peres, & Morellato, 2017). Phenological events are related to environmental factors such as temperature, precipitation and day length (Brearley, Proctor, Nagy, Dalrymple, & Voysey, 2007; Menzel, Estrella, & Testka, 2005; Sundarapandian, Chandrasekaran, & Swamy, 2005).

Forests of the Congo Basin (Cameroon, Gabon, Equatorial Guinea, Democratic Republic of the Congo and the Congo Republic) have a high diversity of plant species but can still present areas dominated by a single canopy species (Peh, Lewis, & Lloyd, 2011). This monodominance has been defined as >60% of canopyâlevel trees belonging to the same species (Connell & Lowman, 1989; Hart, 1995; Hart, Hart, & Murphy, 1989). Aucoumea klaineana Pierre (Burseraceae), commonly named okoume, is one of those species able to form monodominant stands in the Congo Basin. Singleâspeciesâdominant stands can have lower litter decomposition rate because of slowly released nutrients (Hart, 1995; Henkel, 2003; Nascimento & Proctor, 1997; Peh, SonkÃ©, Lloyd, Quesada, & Lewis, 2011). Loumeto (2002) found that okoume contributed to more than 60% of the total litter production of okoumeâdominated forests. Due to the functions of litter, studies of okoume litter are important for understanding the ecosystem functioning in this monodominant forest. Mondominant species, like okoume, are likely to exert the largest effects on ecosystem function in the forests in which they occur (Grime, 1998); hence, studies of litter in monodominant forests may shed more light on ecosystem functioning than those conducted in mixed forests. Another factor to consider to explain litter dynamics is ecosystem disturbance. There have been many studies on the impacts of anthropogenic disturbance on diversity in forest ecosystems, and particularly habitat degradation and fragmentation (reviewed by Ewers & Didham, 2006; Saunders, Hobbs, & Margules, 1991). Stand structure in tropical rain forests is altered by anthropogenic disturbance (e.g., Hall, Harris, Medjibe, & Ashton, 2003) and stand structure, in addition to climate, species composition, and soil fertility, is a key driver of litterfall (Vitousek & Sanford, 1986).

The management of forests, for all their benefits, should be controlled so that the effects of unnatural disturbance can be contained within the ranges which result from natural disturbance (Attiwill, 1994). However, the consequences of forest disturbances on ecosystem functions, such as litter decomposition and carbon and nutrient cycling, remain poorly understood, despite the important implications for forest conservation and management strategies (e.g., Herbst, Roberts, Rosier, Taylor, & Gowing, 2007; Saunders et al., 1991). Among the main form of disturbances in the Congo basin is agricultural disturbance (Laurance, Sayer, & Cassmann, 2014) that approximately involves 240 million people in the humid tropics (Attiwill, 1994). However, shifting agriculture, together with wind, lightning and fire, is an exogenous disturbance with limited spatial extent that has little effect on soil fertility and on structure and composition of the rainforest which reâestablishes after abandonment (Kleinman, Pimentel, & Bryant, 1995). Logging in central Africa, on the other hand, is an exogenous disturbance with mostly large spatial extent and low intensity that may result in shifts in species composition (Hall et al., 2003).

There have been a few studies of litterfall in the Congo Basin (Cassart, 2018; Cassart, Basia, Titeux, Andivia, & Ponette, 2017; Gautam & Pietsch, 2012) and, to our knowledge, none in forests in Gabon, including okoumeâdominated forests. Moreover, data on litter dynamics with respect to forest disturbance are not available. Therefore, as part of a more comprehensive investigation on the role of disturbances in litter dynamics in tropical forests, this study has two objectives: (a) assess the aboveâground components of litter dynamics (litter production, thickness, decomposition and soil moisture content) over time in two okoumeâdominated forests differing in their degrees of disturbance (b) and test whether litter dynamics differ according to disturbance level. The results will improve understanding of ecosystem functioning of okoumeâdominated forests and enable better management decisions about these forests since they are commercially exploited.

2.2 Litterfall measurement

Litterfall, defined as leaves, twigs, and reproductive parts of plants (Lips & Duivenvoorden, 1996), was quantified at both sites using wooden frame traps 60 cm Ã 40 cm (0.024 mÂ²) lined with 1 mm mesh. Fifty traps were established in each of the two sites on 28 May 2011 and monitored until 15 August 2012 (445 days). Five traps were placed under the crowns of each of 10 okoume trees (all â¥ 70 cm diameter at 1.3 m height) at both sites, with the five traps placed beneath the crowns of the trees centrally and north, south, east and west.

Fallen material was harvested 12 times over the monitoring period, with an average harvesting duration of 37 days (minimum 21 days, maximum 54 days). The contents of litter traps were collected and separated into litter fractions. Category organs were sorted among the collected litter as leaves, wood bodies (branches, bark and twigs) and reproductive organs (fruits, seeds and flowers). The okoume leaves constituted of more than 80% of the fallen material. The litter fractions were conserved in A4 paper envelopes and then sent to the laboratory at Ipassa research station where they were ovenâdried at 80Â°C to constant weight and weighed.

2.3 Litter decomposition

The rate of litter decomposition was determined using litter bags. We quantified the rate of decomposition of okoume leaf litter by placing 10 g of freshly fallen okoume leaf litter in nylon litter bags (20 cm Ã 30 cm, with 2 mm mesh). We placed ten litter bags beneath each of ten okoume trees in each site (i.e., 100 bags per site).

Each month, one bag was removed under each tree to follow the decomposition rate of litter over time. Sample bags collected were put in a cool box to limit biomass loss and then returned immediately to the laboratory to be weighed (fresh weight), then dried at 80Â°C for 48 hr and weighed again (dry weight). The exercise was repeated until all bags were removed from trees in both study sites.

2.4 Litter thickness

The thickness of the accumulated litter on the ground was evaluated using a slice done with machetes. The measurement was obtained using a graduated scale ruler. Five measurements were randomly taken each month from each site within the perimeter of selected trees used for the study of litterfall.

2.5 Soil moisture content

Soils samples were taken using soil cores of 48 mm in diameter. The soil was sampled at a single depth of 0â15 cm to capture the first horizon only. Soil core samples were taken randomly from below five selected okoume trees and combined into a single bulk sample per site with a surface area equivalent to 0.01 m2 for each site. For each site, five samples were taken, and the distance between each sample tree (sampling points) was approximately 15 m. A total of ten soil samples were taken and placed in paper bags and put in a cool box to limit the loss of humidity and then brought back to the laboratory. It takes a maximum of 1.5 hr to reach the research station of Ipassa. Soil samples were sieved and then transferred into coffee cans. 3.2 Temporal changes in litterfall

The production of litter significantly changed across months for all components of the litter and for both sites (Figure 1 and Table 2). The seasonal pattern was more marked for leaves than for woody organs or for reproductive organs. The peaks of leaf litter production were synchronous in both sites (Figure 1a). Leaf litterfall was low from May to August with a minimum in July, and high from February to April and from October to December with a maximum in December. Variation in leaf litterfall therefore follows the pattern of the four climatic seasons, with a small fall during the long dry season and an abundant fall during the two rainy seasons and during the short dry season. For woody organs, the fall is low during the dry season (July and August) with a maximum in February (Figure 1b). The fall of the reproductive organs is higher during the small dry season, that is in December and January for site 1, and from December to February for site 2 (Figure 1c).

4 DISCUSSION

4.1 Litterfall dynamics

Litterfall and its fractions significantly differed according to the disturbance regime, with higher litterfall under the formerly logged site. It has been suggested that lack of disturbance can be an ecological explanation for monodominance which might alter the abiotic conditions of the habitat (Peh, Lewis, et al., 2011). Lewis et al. (2009) found that the aboveâground biomass of monodominant forests is higher than adjacent areas of mixed forest in both Eastern and Western Congo, and this was attributed to the absence of disturbance. Our results also corroborated Ibrahima, Schmidt, Ketner, and Mohren (2002) who found higher litter production in an undisturbed than in a disturbed forest. However, the effect could also be partly explained by the structural composition in the in the logged site having clear open forest on the ground where the litter could easily access the cages, compared to the more disturbed habitat, which had more diverse structural composition with trees and shrubs, grasses and low trees. Litterfall is related also to basal area (NovÃ¡k, DuÅ¡ek, & SlodiÄÃ¡k, 2014). Therefore, lower basal area in the most disturbed regime could also explain part of this effect of lower litterfall dynamics found in more disturbed habitat, which experience lower mean diameter of target tree species.

Moreover, the fraction of leaves in the litter was high in both sites. Leaf litter generally has a much larger contribution to total litter than the rest of litter fractions (BernhardâReversat, 1972; Bray & Gorham, 1964; O'Connell, 1988). In eastern Democratic of the Congo (DRC), Cassart et al. (2017) reported that leaves accounted for 63%â66% of total litterfall. Leaf litter comprised 75%â87% of total litterfall in an undisturbed tropical moist forest in eastern Nepal and 81%â90% in a disturbed forest (Gautam & Mandal, 2016).

However, the total litter production in our study was much higher at both sites than documented elsewhere in tropical forests. Cassart et al. (2017) reported a litter production of 9 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 in Scorodophloeus zenkeri mixed forests and 8.3 Mg haâ1yearâ1 in Gilbertiodendron dewevrei monodominant forests in DRC. In montane forests of the Americas, mean litter production decreased from 8.6 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 to 2.6 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 with increasing altitude (RÃ¶derstein, Hertel, & Leuschner, 2005). In lowland moist forest in Costa Rica, Sanford and Cuevas (1996) measured a leaf litter production of 7.2 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 at a nutrientâpoor site and 4.1 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 at a fertile site. However, it is important to point out that litterfall of okoume trees in two historically disturbed forests in this study is high compared with other forestâwide assessments of lowland tropical rain forests. A sampling bias due to the positioning of litter traps under okoume trees could be put forward to explain the higher litterfall found in our study, even though the high degree of canopy closure of the forest canopy does not support this explanation. This means that the data for litterfall presented here could be an overestimation of litterfall in both areas.

Monthly analyses revealed seasonal variation in litterfall dynamics, with a significant peak of litterfall in the rainy season (SeptemberâDecember) and a minimum in the dry season (JuneâAugust), and more marked seasonal variation for leaves than for other organs. This is in line with heavy precipitation accompanied by strong winds and thunderstorms observed during the rainy seasons (Dawoe, Issac, & QuashieâSam, 2010). Gautam and Pietsch (2012) also observed seasonal variation of litter for two consecutive years, with 2.8 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 litter carbon (end of dry season 2004) and 10.3 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 (rainy season 2005) with an average of 7.0 Mg haâ1 yearâ1. The same patterns were also comparable to 0.9â9.3 Mg haâ1 yearâ1 published for tropical forests (Clark et al., 2001). The study is also in line with the argument of Gautam and Pietsch (2012), who stated that intraâannual variation and interâannual variation of litter over time may complicate reliable estimates of litter carbon from singleâpoint measurements, such as the positioning of litter traps under okoume trees in our study. It remains also to assess whether the climatic pattern from June 2011 to August 2012 was typical of the climate in the study area before concluding on the relationship between litterfall and seasonal variation.

The abundance of the reproductive organs was mostly observed in less pronounced dry season (December to February) and could be associated to the fruiting and blooming of the main dominant tree species (okoume trees) during that period. According to Wright and Cornejo (1990), this peak could be explained by a combination of several environmental factors including a high evaporative demand in the dry season, associated stomatal closure and subsequent leaf overheating together with photoâinhibition which may lead to leaf ageing and subsequent leaf shedding at the end of the drier season. Indeed, in the northeast of Gabon, the small dry season is accompanied by strong sunshine, which may cause high evapotranspiration and high temperatures (Mu et al., 2007).

To avoid water stress, trees lose their organs, and this may explain maximum fall of leaf and wood organs observed during the less pronounced dry season in both study site. Then, the appearance of new okoume leaves occurs after the less pronounced dry season at the end of December, leading to the maximum leaf drop observed in rainy season, with more favourable environmental conditions for leaf renewal in rainy season, such as greater day length, relative humidity and precipitation leading to the maximum leaf drop observed in rainy season, with more favourable environmental conditions for leaf renewal in rainy season, such as greater day length, relative humidity and precipitation (Jackson, 1978; Mapaga, Ingueza, Louppe, & Koumba Zaou, 2002). These results underline the links between climatic factors and the phenological stages of vegetation that influence litterfall dynamics in the Congo Basin forest. Louppe, Hamel, Jaffre, and Roda (2000) have also reported that the fruiting of okoume occurs from the end of December to the beginning of March and that the flowering occurs at the beginning of the rainy season (end of August) and this can last until December.

4.2 Decomposition rate of litter

Soil moisture availability enhances activity of soil microorganisms (BernhardâReversat, 1972; Waring, 2012), increases the amount of soluble substances (Loumeto, 2002), and accelerates litter decomposition (Riutta et al., 2012; XulucâTolosa, Vester, RamÃ­rezâMarcial, CastellanosâAlbores, & Lawrence, 2003). Moisture may not be limiting at either of the sites in our study because litter decomposed more rapidly in the formerly logged forest, which had lower soil moisture content.

However, disturbances can alter litter layers by changing habitat structure, microclimatic conditions, and resource availability for macrofauna, mesofauna, and microbial communities, and, consequently, may significantly affect larger soil invertebrates such as earthworms, millipedes, or isopods, which process large amounts of dead plant material (CÃ¡rcamo, Abe, Prescott, Holl, & Chanway, 2000; David & Gillon, 2002; Seastedt, 1984), and carbon and nutrient cycling, as well as litter decomposition (HÃ¤ttenschwiler, Tiunov, & Scheu, 2005; Sayer, 2006). Gautam and Mandal (2014) found in moist tropical forest in Nepal, that the annual litter turnover rate was faster in undisturbed forest as compared to disturbed forest, and this was associated with the higher microbial biomass and their activities in the soil of undisturbed forest. Studies have showed that the presence of macrofauna accelerated the litter decomposition rate, but there were no effects of moisture treatment on macrofaunal abundance or species richness (Cornejo, Varela, & Wright, 1994) This suggests that the moisture limitation observed in fast decomposition site is not mediated by macrofauna, but is probably driven by fewer changes in invertebrates communities and microbial activity due to the lesser disturbances experienced by the site (Riutta et al., 2012).

4.3 Thickness and accumulated of litter on the ground

The organic layer was thicker in site affected more recently by agriculture than formerly logged site. The secondary character of the site affected recently by agriculture, could explain the thicker litter observed in it. Okoume is described as a pioneer tree in tropical rain forest that often dominates stands after disturbances and forms monodominant stands (e.g., Born et al., 2008; Fuhr, Nasi, & Delegue, 2001). It is also acknowledged that disturbed secondary forests in the Congo Basin often contain pioneer species such as Musanga spp. or Macaranga spp., or Cecropia spp. (e.g., Mesquita, Ickes, Ganade, & Williamson, 2001), which are lightâdemanding, fastâgrowing species with high litter production (e.g., XulucâTolosa et al., 2003). Lower total litterfall and a thicker litter layer in the site affected by agriculture could be a result of shifting agriculture and fire interacting to degrade the structure and composition of soil fauna communities, in turn reducing litter decomposition rates (Powers et al., 2009; Wall et al., 2008).

The larger soil invertebrates such as earthworms, millipedes or isopods, which process large amounts of dead plant material, have been found to strongly control litter decomposition in tropical rainforests (Powers et al., 2009; Wall et al., 2008), and those invertebrates have probably been reduced by agricultural disturbances (CÃ¡rcamo et al., 2000; David & Gillon, 2002; Seastedt, 1984). Thicker litter layers in the site affected by agriculture are likely to be slower litter decomposition rates we quantified in the site, and this could have been caused by a reduced soil fauna.

4.4 Soil moisture content

The soil moisture content was significantly higher in the more disturbed than less disturbed forest; to be related to the thicker litter observed in the more disturbed forest. It has been suggested that thick litter may have improved soil moisture levels by either maintaining higher absolute moisture levels or reducing total moisture loss after rainfall, and increasing pathogen occurrence (Sayer, 2006; Torti, Coley, & Kursar, 2001). This may have contributed to the higher humidity observed in the more disturbed site. Overall, high litter levels improve soil moisture retention (Deutsch, Bork, & Willms, 2010). The low moisture content observed in the logged site is likely to be related to a strong and continuous solar radiation difference, with less tree species having close canopy.

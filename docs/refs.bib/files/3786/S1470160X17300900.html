    Introduction to the Special Issue “Functional monitoring in megadiverse tropical ecosystems”

    Author links open overlay panelNinaFarwigaJörgBendixbErwinBeckc
    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ecolind.2017.02.027Get rights and content

    Abstract

    Land-use and climate change are major threats to biodiversity and ecosystem functions. Most of the current biodiversity monitoring systems are based on periodic records of the populations of a set of threatened or popular ‘flagship’ indicator species. In contrast to the abundance-based monitoring of species, also specific indicators of processes and functional interactions in an ecosystem may become targets of a more functional monitoring which can unveil early responses of an ecosystem to environmental changes at different spatial and temporal scales. The contributions of this Special Issue present such functional indicators for assessing and predicting responses to environmental changes of ecosystem functions in a hotspot of tropical biodiversity.

    The increasing impact of land use and climate changes has lead to modifications in structure and composition of natural ecosystems (Millenium Ecosystem Assessment, 2005, Asner et al., 2010). Particularly, land use intensification results in a dramatic loss of biodiversity, which in turn can affect species interactions, food webs and important ecological functions (Cardinale et al., 2012, Tilman et al., 2012). It is therefore widely accepted that continuous monitoring of biodiversity and ecosystem services on various scales in all ecosystems of the world are urgently needed (Dobson, 2005, Scholes et al., 2008, Butchart et al., 2010). Article 7 (b) of the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) obliges the member countries to “monitor, through sampling and other techniques, all components of (their) biological diversity”. The recently established international science-policy platform IPBES (Intergovernmental Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services) recommends a regular assessment of biodiversity and ecosystem services on various scales (Perrings et al., 2011). National strategies on biodiversity and ecosystem services propose specific indicator systems for monitoring the status of their ecosystems, in particular their biological diversity, with respect to ecosystem stability, conservation or restoration. Guidelines for the evaluation and the analysis of results accompany the measures stipulated by the National Strategies. The Ecuadorian National Strategy on Biodiversity as a prominent example from a country hosting one of the global hotspots of vascular plant diversity (Barthlott et al., 2007) proposes general priority measures for endangered areas, which have been identified on the basis of vegetation cover on a large scale, and on bird diversity. Special strategies for the management and conservation of those endangered areas shall be developed in an action plan and suitable measures involving affected actors shall be implemented. All these measures require research-based action, and they are under considerable pressure of time.

    Most of the current biodiversity monitoring systems simply focus on the recurrent observation of compositional indicators, which frequently are threatened or popular “flagship” indicator species. In a more system-based approach, monitoring should also apply indicator systems unveiling changes in structural and functional biodiversity as well as ecosystem services encompassing multiple levels of organization, mostly related to different spatial scales (Noss, 1990, Geijzendorffer and Roche, 2013). From an environmental perspective a biodiversity monitoring system needs additional abiotic and socio-economic indicators, regarding e.g. climate, water and soil, as well as land use and many others (e.g. Duelli and Obrist, 2003). If the indicators shall particularly act as an early-warning surveillance system for global change impacts, abundant indicators, sensitive to subtle changes and related to ecosystem functions should be used (e.g. van Strien et al., 2009, Palmer and Febria, 2012). Very important is the requirement that the indicators not only reflect a change but also disentangle the hidden functional reason for the change (Bässler et al., 2010). This points to the need of monitoring ecosystem functions by means of change-sensitive indicators where changes in these indicators are expected to reflect also alterations in specific services to which the monitored function belongs (Moonen and Bárberi, 2008). Such type of indicators hardly exists, particularly in developing countries. Hence, the development of monitoring systems targeting ecosystem functions requires extensive research as well as involvement of and knowledge transfer to national stakeholders.

    This special issue presents selected examples of a comprehensive research and knowledge transfer initiative, the “Platform for Biodiversity and Ecosystem Monitoring and Research in South Ecuador” (www.TropicalMountainForest.org) that was launched in 2013 (Bendix and Beck, 2016). One overall aim of the transdisciplinary program is the development of functional indicators that can be used to monitor changes in the state and biodiversity of the ecosystems triggered by environmental changes (climate and land use changes) in the biodiversity hotspot South Ecuador. The system should combine simple (and partly ancillary) as well as functional indicators that reflect changes of ecosystem functions.

    The articles of the Special Issue propose on the one hand the use of selected taxonomic indicator groups and relevant ecosystem functions for monitoring ecological responses to climate or land use changes. This is possible as particularly insects as ectothermic organisms react directly to changes in abiotic conditions such as temperature modifications (Jenkins et al., 2011). A first article therefore addresses the “Long-term equilibrium of a tropical ant community” (Donoso, 2017). As changes in temperature will not only affect species communities but also most likely translate into ant-associated ecosystem functions (Del Toro et al., 2015), a second article focusses on “Ants as indicators of environmental change and ecosystem processes” (Tiede et al., 2017). The quantification of indicator groups and important ecosystem functions is time consuming. Therefore, simple indicator systems for routine monitoring across large areas would be very useful for applications in conservation and the development of management strategies. Particular recent developments in remote sensing technologies offer the possibilities to develop such spatially explicit indicators over large areas and very high spatial resolutions. A third article therefore addresses the possibility of using remotely sensed data: “Remote sensing indicators improve prediction of species diversity but performance differs among taxa” (Wallis et al., 2017).

    On the other hand articles of the Special Issue propose water fluxes as indicators for environmental changes. As plants are active partners in ecosystem water fluxes e.g. via transpiration the rationale for using water as indicator of global change effects is based on the assumption that these fluxes react more readily to small changes in the environmental forcing than patterns of species, communities or the structure of an ecosystem. Water fluxes can therefore be used as indicators of small and subtle changes, which can be quantified from individual trees up to the landscape scale. A fourth article therefore deals with “Tree responses to moisture fluctuations in dry forests as potential indicator for climate change” (Butz et al., 2017). The water use efficiency (WUE) couples carbon acquisition (net primary production, NPP) of a tree/ecosystem with its water consumption by transpiration/evapotranspiration (ET) and thus couples the ecosystem hydrological and carbon cycles (Lu and Zhuang, 2010). A fifths article therefore addresses “Water relations and photosynthetic water use efficiency as indicators of slow climate change effects” (Strobl et al., 2017) as integrating parameter for monitoring changes in water and carbon regulation services of tropical forest ecosystems.

    In summary, the contributions of this Special Issue present a set of simple and functional indicators for assessing and predicting responses of biodiversity and ecosystem services to environmental changes. Within this issue several methods and approaches are presented that might be used to monitor species and functional response to environmental changes as required by the CBD, the IPBES and the Ecuadorian National Strategy on Biodiversity.

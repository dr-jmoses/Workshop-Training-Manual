    Biological Conservation

    Volume 142, Issue 10, October 2009, Pages 2050-2058

    Severe declines of ant-following birds in African rainforest fragments are facilitated by a subtle change in army ant communities

    Author links open overlay panelMarcell K.PetersaBenjaminOkalob
    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biocon.2009.03.035Get rights and content

    Abstract

    Tropical rainforests are disappearing at a rapid rate. Although several studies have revealed the detrimental effects of forest fragmentation on tropical birds, the ecological mechanisms facilitating the decline of populations have been poorly documented. In the tropical rainforests of Africa and America, ant-following birds track the massive swarm raids of army ants and prey on animals which are flushed by the ants. We analyzed the persistence of five species of ant-following birds along a habitat fragmentation gradient in western Kenya and tested if bird populations are limited by the abundance of army ant colonies in forests. Abundances of four of five ant-followers declined by 52–100% in forest fragments ⩽113 ha. Multi-model Bayesian inference suggests that the decline of the three most specialized ant-followers is facilitated by a decrease in the abundance of the army ant Dorylus wilverthi in small forest fragments. Our data suggests that a second, fragmentation-tolerant army ant species, Dorylus molestus, does not functionally compensate for the decline of D. wilverthi because, first, of a higher affinity of birds to D. wilverthi raids (found for one species). Second, because the daytime activity of D. molestus is dependent on high humidity conditions, a pattern which was not found for D. wilverthi. Consequently, specialized ant-followers in small fragments, where D. wilverthi is missing, probably suffer from food scarcity due to a cease of army ant foraging in the dry season. Our results suggest that a subtle alteration of army ant communities caused by habitat fragmentation may have large ecological consequences.

    1. Introduction

    The disappearance of insectivorous birds from fragmented tropical rainforests is a world-wide phenomenon (Newmark, 1991, Beier et al., 2002, Sekercioglu et al., 2002, Sodhi et al., 2004, Barlow et al., 2006). Specialized ant-following birds are among the first species to disappear where tropical rainforest is cleared and fragmented (Harper, 1989, Stouffer and Bierregaard, 1995). These birds regularly follow the massive swarm raids of army ants and feed on invertebrates flushed by the ants (Willis and Oniki, 1978, Peters et al., 2008). Because of this dependence, it has been hypothesized that the decline of ant-following birds is caused by the disappearance of swarm-raiding army ants from fragmented forests (e.g., Sekercioglu, 2007). While several studies indeed point to a high susceptibility of army ants to the loss and fragmentation of forest habitats (Partridge et al., 1996, Terborgh et al., 1997, Boswell et al., 1998; but see Meisel, 2004, Schöning et al., 2006), the mechanism between army ant disappearance and ant-following bird declines remained unresolved.

    Swarm-raiding army ants forage in a fan-shaped swarm of several meters width, which consists of hundred thousands to millions of ants (Gotwald, 1995). The swarm moves at a speed of ∼6–14 m per hour over the forest floor (e.g., Willis, 1986, Willson, 2004, Schöning et al., 2005) whereas the ants search in the leaf-litter and lower vegetation for animal prey. In order to escape predation, animals flee when an ant swarm is approaching. Several bird species regularly follow these swarms, obtain a large proportion of their food from them, show behavioral and morphological adaptations to ant-following, and are therefore considered “specialized” ant-followers (Willis and Oniki, 1978, Keith et al., 1992, Swartz, 2001). The birds depend on, or at least strongly profit from, a high abundance of army ant colonies in their home ranges (Willis and Oniki, 1978, Dowsett-Lemaire, 1983, Willson, 2004).

    By reducing the abundance of ant colonies in forests, habitat fragmentation might lower foraging opportunities for ant-following birds and, consequently, lower their population density. However, ant-following birds may show a closer relationship with a particular ant species (Willson, 2004), such that not only declines in the total abundance of colonies but also changes in the species composition of swarm-raiding army ants may affect populations of ant-following birds.

    In a fragmented rainforest system in western Kenya, Kakamega Forest, we examined the relationship between the abundances of five species of specialized ant-following birds, the size of forest fragments and the abundance of army ants. In a recent study we identified the five bird species as specialized ant-followers because they significantly more often attended swarm raids than was expected from their densities (Peters et al., 2008). In the Kakamega Forest two species of ecologically similar swarm-raiding army ant species coexist and raids of both are attractive to the bird community (Peters et al., 2008). Dorylus (Anomma) molestus is the common swarm-raiding army ant in East Africa and uses habitats ranging from tropical rainforest to savannah and dry bushland (Gotwald, 1995, Schöning et al., 2006), while Dorylus (Anomma) wilverthi has a Central African distribution and is more strongly dependent on forest habitats (Peters et al., 2009).

    We show that most bird species decrease in small forest patches and that the three most specialized ant-followers appear to depend on the abundance of D. wilverthi but not on D. molestus army ants. Concerning an explanation for the reliance of specialized ant-followers on D. wilverthi, we evaluated two different non-exclusive hypotheses: first, that bird species preferentially follow raids of D. wilverthi. Second, that the two army ant species show differences in their activity in relationship to humidity which may affect their ecological value for ant-following birds. African army ant species have been reported to cease foraging when conditions become too dry (Willis, 1985, Willis, 1986). However, the relationship between ant activity and humidity has not been empirically tested and it is unknown if all African army ant species show the same response to decreases in humidity. We expect that ant-following birds more strongly rely on an army ant species showing a continuous diurnal activity through times of drought (as described for the Neotropical Eciton burchellii, Willis and Oniki, 1978) than on a species whose foraging activity ceases when humidity conditions become unfavorable. We also tested whether habitat fragmentation alters the ant-following behavior of birds, i.e., if bird species less often follow army ant raids in small than in large fragments.

    2. Methods

    2.1. Study site and study organisms

    The study was conducted from April 2004 to December 2005 in the Kakamega Forest, western Kenya, which is regarded the easternmost remnant of the Congo–Guinean rainforests (e.g., Wagner et al., 2008). The forest is located at an altitude of about 1650 m a.s.l. and receives an annual precipitation of about 2000 mm. In most years a pronounced dry season occurs, which typically lasts from December to February. From an estimated pre-colonial forest area of 25,000 ha about 13,000 ha remain (Brooks et al., 1999), which are distributed over a central forest fragment of 8245 ha, here referred to as the main forest, three medium-sized forest fragments (420–1370 ha), and three small forest fragments (65–113 ha). Two of the medium-sized fragments were connected by a narrow corridor of degraded forest. Because these fragments were additionally interrupted by a wide road, which we assumed to be a barrier for army ants and ant-following birds (see e.g., Laurance et al., 2004), we regarded them as separate forest fragments. The main forest and the forest fragments were in most areas surrounded by agricultural land, with maize, sugar cane and tea being the dominant crops.

    We established one study site in each of the six forest fragments and five control sites in the main forest (distance between control sites: mean = 6.04 km, SE = 1.68 km). In each study site three transects of 500 m were established (in one study site one of the three transects had a length of only 350 m) with point count stations in 100 m intervals (6 points × 3 transects = 18 point count stations per study site). The three transects of one study site had a minimum distance of 100 m to each other.

    The studied birds differ in the degree of specialization on ant-following (Peters et al., 2008), with the most specialized species being the White-tailed Ant Thrush (Neocossyphus poensis, Turdidae, mean weight: ca. 51.8 g), the Brown-chested Alethe (Alethe poliocephala, Turdidae, ca. 26.6 g) and the Red-tailed Bristlebill (Bleda syndactyla, Pycnonotidae, ca. 46 g), followed by the less specialized Gray-winged Robin (Cossypha polioptera, Turdidae, ca. 17.3 g) and Equatorial Akalat (Sheppardia aequatorialis, Turdidae, ca. 14.7 g). All bird species mainly feed on invertebrates but the larger ones may also include small vertebrates in their diet (Willis, 1985, Willis, 1986, Keith et al., 1992).

    2.2. Ant and bird monitoring

    Army ant abundances were measured over a total of 16 months (April–August 2004, January–April 2005, June–December 2005) by transect monitoring (Franks, 1982a, O’Donnell et al., 2007, Vidal-Riggs and Chaves-Campos, 2008). Study sites were visited successively within these time periods, each site about once or twice in 14 days, for a total of 37–41 surveys per site. Abundance of swarm-raiding army ants was measured for each study site as the mean number of encountered colonies per transect kilometer (for details see Peters et al., 2009). Transect monitoring was exclusively conducted during daytime hours, i.e., when ant-following birds forage. Mean army ant abundance as measured by transect monitoring is highly correlated to the forest area raided by army ants per week (Peters et al., 2009) and is an indicator of the mean availability of swarm raids to ant-following birds over long time periods.

    Parallel to most army ant monitoring surveys, we measured relative humidity in 50 m or 100 m intervals for a total of 18–36 measurements per survey. To measure relative humidity the observer directed himself towards the undergrowth at a right angle to the transect line. A thermo-hygrometer (Kestrel 3000) was waved at breast height for the duration of about 1–2 min. When the relative humidity value stayed at a ∼constant level the measurement was taken. In case of unstable humidity conditions, e.g., caused by winds coming from the direction of the canopy, we waved the thermo-hygrometer for another 30 s and then took the reading. For each survey we calculated, first, the simple mean relative humidity. Second, by modeling humidity as a fourth-order polynomial function on daytime we controlled for the variation in humidity which could be explained by daytime. From the residuals of this function we calculated the daytime-corrected mean relative humidity for each transect monitoring survey.

    From May 2004 to July 2004 and from February 2005 to April 2005 abundances of bird species were monitored by point counts. Birds were monitored on the same transects as army ants. Point count monitoring has already successfully been used for studying birds of Congo–Guinean rainforests (e.g., Waltert et al., 2005, Farwig et al., 2008). We preferred point count monitoring over other bird census techniques (e.g., mist-netting) as it appeared to be more feasible than other techniques for repetitively monitoring birds over the whole area spanned by the 33 transects.

    At each point, bird activity was observed for ten minutes. All bird species seen or heard within a radius of 25 m were recorded. Point count surveys were conducted in a standardized non-biased way in the morning hours between 7 am and 11 am. In total, each point was visited six times, thrice in 2004 and thrice in 2005. Study sites and transects within study sites were visited successively and we ensured that there appeared no systematic differences in starting times among transects or study sites which could have influenced bird abundance patterns. In total 108 point counts were conducted per study site (at one site, i.e., in the smallest forest fragment, only 96 point counts could be conducted). For each study site and bird species, we calculated bird abundance as the mean probability of a species to be recorded during a 10 min point count (presence/point count).

    From April 2004 to August 2005 the eleven study sites were repeatedly searched for ant swarms and a total of n = 75 were located (32 D. wilverthi raids, 43 D. molestus raids). In comparison to data presented in Peters et al. (2008), only ant swarms which were observed in primary forest were used, while swarms observed in secondary forests were not considered. This was done because in the current study we focused on the effects of habitat fragmentation on birds but not on the effects of habitat degradation. At each swarm raid, bird activities were observed for 1 h. All bird species seen foraging at the raid were recorded. For each raid we noted the size of the forest fragment in which the observation took place, the time when the observation started, differing between morning (8:00 am–11:00 am), midday (11:01 am–02:00 pm) and afternoon hours (02:01 pm–05:00 pm), and the species of army ant which conducted the swarm raid.

    2.3. Statistical analyses

    Bird abundance in forest fragments: to test if the mean abundances of specialized ant-following birds and the abundance of army ants differed among forest fragment size classes we calculated one-way ANOVA. In case of significant differences in the ANOVA we used TukeyHSD post-hoc tests to determine the significance of pairwise comparisons of means.

    Effects of army ant abundance and fragment size on birds: we analyzed the effect of fragment size (log-transformed), the abundance of D. wilverthi colonies, and the abundance of D. molestus colonies on the abundances of ant-following birds with multiple linear regression models assuming additive effects of explanatory variables. We did not include interaction terms in models because our sample size was not appropriate to effectively deal with interaction effects (Crawley, 2005). The abundance of the Brown-chested Alethe was log-transformed to improve linearity and normality of residuals. Pearson correlations of all explanatory variable combinations (for all combinations |r| < 0.63) and variance inflation factors (for all variables VIF < 1.89) indicated that multicollinearity was not high. VIF values of more than 5 or 10 (depending on the author) are considered to indicate high levels multicollinearity (e.g., Belsley et al., 1980). Models and explanatory variables were evaluated using Bayesian model averaging (BMA, function bicreg in the R package BMA). In the BMA procedure, first, all eight models which can be composed of the three explanatory variables (including the null model), were evaluated according to model fit (to the data) and complexity, measured by the Bayesian information criterion, and derived posterior model probabilities (a measure comparable to the Akaike weights). Second, for each explanatory variable the posterior effect probability (PEP) was calculated (a measure comparable to the summed Akaike weights of all models in which an explanatory variable is included) which can be regarded as a measure of the relative importance of an explanatory variable (see Johnson and Omland, 2003). The posterior effect probability of an explanatory variable is calculated by summing up the posterior model probabilities of each model in which an explanatory variable is included. Third, the average mean and standard deviation of each regression coefficient was calculated by weighted averaging of coefficients under each separate model, with weights given by the posterior model probabilities. We exclusively used non-informative priors for Bayesian analyses in order not to bias any analysis towards an expected pattern. In contrast to other techniques often used in similar analytical contexts, like e.g., stepwise regression procedures or other ‘one best model selection techniques’, BMA not only accounts for uncertainty in parameter estimates but also for uncertainty in model selection (Raftery et al., 1997, Wintle et al., 2003, Ellison, 2004). This is particularly relevant (but often neglected) in ecological models, in which uncertainty about model structure is usually high (Conroy et al., 1995, Wintle et al., 2003).

    In the analysis described above we found that abundances of the three most specialized ant-followers were positively correlated to the abundance of D. wilverthi army ants (for all birds an effect of D. wilverthi was supported by a PEP of >75%). We used a randomization test to calculate the probability that this result was found because of co-correlation of the abundance of D. wilverthi with another (untested) environmental variable, which may influence the abundance of birds. If this would be the case we would expect to find a positive correlation between the abundance of D. wilverthi and bird abundance not only for the three most specialized ant-followers but also for other insectivorous birds. We randomly sampled three bird species from a matrix containing abundance estimates of 96 insectivorous bird species and calculated the BMA analyses as described above. This procedure (random sampling of three species plus subsequent BMA analyses) was repeated for 1000 times and we calculated how often (%) three bird species showed a positive relationship with the abundance of D. wilverthi, supported by a PEP of ⩾75%.

    Effects of fragment size on ant-following behavior: to obtain measures of ant-following behavior for each species in each study site we compared the attendance rates of birds at swarm raids with bird species’ overall abundances in study sites. First, we linearly regressed swarm raid attendances of bird species (mean probability of presence/swarm raid) on their overall abundances in study sites. We fixed the intercept in the model to zero as a bird abundance of zero was expected to correspond to a swarm attendance rate of zero. We used the number of observed swarm raids in each study sites as weights in the linear model. Please note that for this calculation not only data of the five specialized ant-following birds were used but data of all bird species which had been observed following army ant raids during the study time were included (n = 56 species, see Peters et al., 2008). A positive deviation from the regression line (b = 0.72, R2 = 0.47, F1,406 = 358.9, p < 0.001) indicated that a bird species followed swarm raids in a study site more often than predicted by its overall abundance. These residuals therefore represent species-specific measures of ant-following behavior in study sites and we hereafter term them simply ‘ant-following behavior’. With few exceptions (some opportunistic birds showed in some study sites moderately high residuals), the 51 opportunistic ant-followers showed ant-following behavior values (residuals) of ∼0 or lower (Fig. A1 in Supplementary material). We compared the ant-following behavior of specialized ant-followers among fragment size classes using weighted linear models (weights = the number of swarm raid observations conducted in each study site).

    Preferences of ant-followers for swarms of specific army ants: we analyzed if the species of army ants (categorical) influenced the probability of ant-following bird species to be present at swarm raids (categorical) by logistic regression assuming additive effects of explanatory variables (for each bird species one analysis was conducted). We included fragment size (log-transformed, continuous) and daytime (categorical) as explanatory variables in models as we assumed that they may affect the attendance of birds at swarm raids. Models and explanatory variables were evaluated using BMA (function bic.glm in the R package BMA). Pearson correlation of all explanatory variable combinations (for all combinations |r| < 0.52) and variance inflation factors (for all variables VIF < 1.37) indicated that multicollinearity was not high.

    Army ant activity in relationship to humidity: we analyzed the response of D. wilverthi and D. molestus to increasing dryness by performing generalized linear mixed-effect models (GLMM) with a poisson distribution of errors (family = quasipoisson, link function = log) with army ant abundance (mean number of colonies/km, one value calculated for each survey) as the response variable and humidity (uncorrected) or standardized humidity (corrected for differences in humidity which could be explained by daytime) as explanatory variables. Study site was used as a random variable in the GLMM. GLMM were calculated with the function glmmPQL in the R package MASS. In addition, we used general additive mixed models (GAMM) to model the relationship between army ant abundance and relative humidity. In contrast to generalized linear models, in general additive models the relationship between response and predictor is not specified by some explicit functional form, so that e.g., non-linear relationships can be identified without prior (and often subjectively chosen) model specifications (Crawley, 2005, Wood, 2006). Instead, non-parametric smoothers are used to describe the relationship. We calculated GAMM with the abundance of army ants as the response, relative humidity as a smoothed term and study site as a random factor using the gamm function (family = quasipoisson, link function = log) of the R package mgcv. The gamm function in this package by default automatically estimates the degree of smoothing of model terms as part of the fitting process. Smooth terms are represented using penalized regression splines with smoothing parameters selected by minimizing the Un-biased Risk Estimator Criterion (UBRE).

    We tested for differences in mean army ant abundance and relative humidity between forest fragments ⩾420 ha and those ⩽113 ha in the peak of the dry season (end of January plus the whole February 2005) and, for comparison, in time periods of similar length in the rainy season using Welch- t-tests. Welch- t-tests rather than t-tests were used because of unequal variances.

    We used R 2.6.1 (R Development Core Team, 2006), and the R packages BMA (Raftery et al., 2005), version 3.03, mgcv (Wood, 2006), version 1.3-17, and MASS (Venables and Ripley, 2002), version 7.2-27.1, for statistical analyses.

    3. Results

    3.1. Bird abundances in forest fragments

    Abundances of four specialized ant-following bird species were similarly high in the 8245 ha-sized main forest and medium-sized forest fragments (420–1370 ha) but dropped in small forest fragments (65–113 ha, Table 1). The most specialized ant-follower, the White-tailed Ant Thrush, was totally absent from small fragments. In contrast, the abundance of one species, the Gray-winged Robin, was higher in small forest fragments than in medium fragments or the main forest. The total abundance of ant-following birds (all species combined) was ∼60% lower in small forest fragments than in medium-sized fragments or the main forest.

    3.2. Effects of army ant abundance and fragment size on birds

    The mean abundance of swarm-raiding army ants was nearly constant among fragment size classes, but the species composition differed strongly (for details see Peters et al., 2009). While D. wilverthi was the dominant army ant in the main forest and in medium-sized forest fragments, it was nearly absent from small forest fragments. In contrast, the mean abundance of D. molestus tended to increase with decreasing size of forest fragments, even though this effect was not significant (Table 1).

    The abundances of the three most specialized ant-followers (Table 1) were positively correlated with the abundance of D. wilverthi, even after controlling for effects of fragment size, but the same pattern was not found for D. molestus (Fig. 1, Table A1 inSupplementary material). Models including the abundance of D. wilverthi as an explanatory variable performed much better in predicting the abundances of the three bird species than models including only fragment size as an explanatory variable, resulting in high posterior effect probabilities for effects of both fragment size and the abundance of D. wilverthi (in case of the White-tailed Ant Thrush and the Red-tailed Bristlebill) or only for an effect of the abundance of D. wilverthi (in case of the Brown-chested Alethe) on bird abundances. An influence of the abundance of D. molestus on bird abundances was generally poorly supported. Using randomization tests, we found that it is highly unlikely that the positive correlation of the abundances of these birds and the abundance of D. wilverthi was gained by co-correlation of abundances with an untested environmental variable, to which both the army ants and birds may have responded: the probability that three bird species, randomly sampled from a matrix containing 96 insectivorous birds, showed a positive relationship to the abundance of D. wilverthi, supported by a posterior probability of at least 75% (the lowest posterior probability for an effect of the abundance of D. wilverthi found among the three birds), was as low as ∼1%.

    In contrast to the three most specialized ant-followers, army ant abundance appeared not to be an important explanatory variable for the abundance of the two less specialized ant-followers (Fig. 1, Table A1 inSupplementary material). In case of the Equatorial Akalat, the only explanatory variable receiving high support was fragment size. In case of the Gray-winged Robin, model uncertainty was generally very high and, consequently, no explanatory variable received overwhelming support. The abundance of this bird was nearly equally well explained by fragment size, by the abundance of D. molestus or by the abundance of D. wilverthi, suggesting that our data were inappropriate for identifying the true determinant of the spatial distribution of this Robin.

    3.3. Effects of fragment size on ant-following behavior

    The attendance of specialized ant-followers at swarm raids was not constant among study sites but varied as a function of fragment size, even after controlling for differences in bird abundances among study sites. While the Brown-chested Alethe was found much more often at swarm raids in the main forest and medium-sized forest fragments than expected from its overall abundance, thus showing a pronounced ant-following behavior, its attendance at swarm raids in small forest fragments was much lower (Fig. 2, F2,7 = 12.88, p = 0.005) and on a level similar to that of opportunistic ant-followers (which show an ant-following behavior around zero or lower). Similarly, the ant-following behavior of the Red-tailed Bristlebill (F2,7 = 7.62, p = 0.017) and the Equatorial Akalat (F2,7 = 5.98, p = 0.031) was much lower in small compared to medium-sized fragments and the main forest. Differences in ant-following behavior among fragment size classes could not be tested for the White-tailed Ant Thrush and the Gray-winged Robin, since they were missing in small fragments and in most forest fragments ⩾420 ha, respectively.

    3.4. Preferences of ant-followers for swarms of specific army ants

    The White-tailed Ant Thrush, the Brown-chested Alethe, and the Red-tailed Bristlebill were more often recorded at swarms of D. wilverthi than at swarms of D. molestus, even after controlling for effects of fragment size (influencing the probability of bird attendance at swarm raids). However, the effect of ant species on the presence probability of birds at swarm raids was statistically well supported only in case of the White-tailed Ant Thrush (PEP = 70.4%; e.g., in large forest fragments the White-tailed Ant Thrush followed 77% of all D. wilverthi raids but only 44% of all D. molestus raids), while in case of the Brown-chested Alethe and the Red-tailed Bristlebill posterior effect probabilities for an effect of ant species were rather low (PEP < 28.9%, see Table A2 in Supplementary material). The two lesser specialized ant-followers equally followed raids of both army ant species.

    3.5. Army ant activity in relationship to humidity

    Within study sites, the abundance of D. molestus decreased with decreasing humidity (GLMM, df = 1289, t = 4.63, p < 0.001) while the abundance of D. wilverthi was not significantly related to humidity (GLMM, df = 1289, t = 0.21, p = 0.83). Generalized additive mixed models indicated a non-linear, strong decline in the abundance of D. molestus once the mean relative humidity dropped below ca. 80% (F = 4.75, p < 0.001), while, again, no significant relationship between the abundance and humidity was found for D. wilverthi (Fig. 3, F = 0.045, p = 0.831). Also when daytime-corrected humidity measures were used or the analysis was restricted to forest fragments ⩾420 ha (where both species frequently occur) patterns were very similar (Fig. 3).

    In the peak of the dry season (end of January and February 2005) all study sites in forest fragments ⩾420 ha showed army ant abundances of at least 0.13 colonies per km (Fig. 4, mean ± SE = 0.65 ± 0.17). Most of the colonies were D. wilverthi. In contrast, in the same time period the abundance of army ants in small fragments (⩽113 ha) was very low (mean ± SE = 0.04 ± 0.03, in two out of three study sites no colonies were found). For comparison, in most wet periods, no differences in the abundance of army ants were found between small and large fragments (in eleven wet periods, each of ∼1 month with moderate to high relative humidity conditions no significant differences in mean ant abundance were found; in one month with moderate humidity conditions army ant abundance was twice as high in fragments ⩾420 ha than in fragments ⩽113 ha). The activity of army ants in the dry season was significantly and positively correlated with the proportion of D. wilverthi on colonies of both army ant species (measured over the whole study time) in study sites (rs = 0.82, p = 0.002). Differences in army ant abundances might have been exaggerated by a generally drier microclimate in small forest fragments, particularly in the dry season, which probably further reduced the activity of the humidity-sensitive D. molestus.

    4. Discussion

    Several studies documented a high sensitivity of ant-following birds to habitat fragmentation (Willis and Oniki, 1978, Harper, 1989, Stouffer and Bierregaard, 1995, Sekercioglu et al., 2002, Kumar and O’Donnell, 2007). Our study is the first to show that the disappearance of these birds is correlated to the decline of a swarm-raiding army ant species in fragmented rainforests.

    Four out of five African ant-followers showed significant declines in abundance in small forest fragments. These declines were observed in forests of quite large extent (even our small forest fragments had sizes of 65–113 ha), suggesting that significant areas of tropical rainforest have to be conserved to sustain ‘natural’ population densities of ant-following birds. The disappearance of ant-following birds in small forest fragments may have large ecological consequences, since they are among the most abundant avian insectivores in continuous rainforests (MKP, unpublished data), and probably of high functional importance (Sekercioglu, 2006, Gaston and Fuller, 2008).

    Specialist ant-followers obtain most of their food by capturing arthropods and small vertebrates attempting to escape from ant swarms and therefore these birds show a high dependency on army ants (Willis and Oniki, 1978). As raids of both army ant species appeared to be attractive to ant-following birds (Peters et al., 2008) we expected that increases in the colony abundance of both army ant species would positively affect the population densities of ant-following birds. However, we found that the abundances of the three most specialized ant-followers were positively correlated only to the abundance of D. wilverthi (for the two less specialized ant-followers the abundance of neither army ant species appeared to be a limiting factor). An effect of the abundance of D. molestus on bird abundance was generally poorly supported, and in addition, for the most specialized ant-follower even a negative correlation between bird abundance and the abundance of D. molestus was found. This suggests that even though D. molestus compensates for the decline of D. wilverthi in small forest fragments in terms of mean abundance (Peters et al., 2009), it can not compensate for D. wilverthi’s functional importance for specialized ant-following birds.

    We evaluated two different non-exclusive hypotheses to explain the dependency of these birds on D. wilverthi army ants: (i) Preferences of ant-following birds for swarms of one ant species, i.e., D. wilverthi, may exist (Willson, 2004). Even though the three most specialized ant-followers all tended to follow swarms of D. wilverthi more regularly than those of D. molestus, high support for this hypothesis was only found for the White-tailed Ant Thrush. D. wilverthi foraged more regularly in the undergrowth, i.e., climbed on plants, than D. molestus (at 81% and 52% of swarms of D. wilverthi and D. molestus workers were observed to climb plants, respectively (χ2 = 7.56, df = 1, p < 0.01)). Certain animal taxa of high nutritional value which are predominately found on plants, e.g., many orthopteroid species, may be flushed in higher quantities by D. wilverthi, possibly making its raids more attractive for ant-following birds. (ii) We found good support for the hypothesis that the two ant species differ in their response to dryness which may affect their ecological value for ant-following birds. The strong dependency of most Neotropical ant-following birds on the army ant E. burchellii has been explained by the strict diurnal foraging activity of this ant and the independency of its activity (in forest habitat) from relative humidity conditions (Willis and Oniki, 1978). The ants forage even in times of drought. E. burchellii is therefore considered a reliable source for food for ant-following birds throughout the whole year (Willis and Oniki, 1978). In the Kakamega Forest, only the army ant D. wilverthi showed stability in activity similar to E. burchellii: the abundance of D. wilverthi was not affected by relative humidity conditions and the ant showed high activity levels even in the dry season. Similar to E. burchellii in the Neotropics, Dorylus wilverthi may therefore be considered a reliable source for food for ant-following birds throughout the whole year. In contrast, the abundance of D. molestus significantly declined with decreasing relative humidity and its daytime activity nearly ceased in the dry season. This is concordant with our observation that the proportion of swarm raids of D. molestus was much lower at midday (27%) and afternoon hours (17%) than in morning hours (44%, values are based on the observation of n = 64 swarms in forest fragments ⩾420 ha where both species regularly occur). In areas where only D. molestus is abundant, during periods with little rainfall, birds might be forced to restrict ant-following to early morning and possibly evening hours. However, during morning hours army ants tend to move rather slowly (because of coldness) and flush smaller amounts of invertebrates. Consequently, morning raids are less attractive for ant-following birds (Willis, 1985, Willis, 1986).

    Due to the absence of D. wilverthi and much higher dry season humidity deficits, small forest fragments showed no or reduced daytime activity of army ants in the peak of the dry season. In contrast, in larger forest fragments army ant activity was maintained at fairly high levels during times of drought. For insectivorous birds in the tropics the availability of food during dry seasons is considered an important factor limiting populations (Strong and Sherry, 2000, Brown and Sherry, 2006, Johnson et al., 2006). We assume that in periods of strong dryness an ongoing activity of army ants is of extraordinary significance for specialized ant-followers. In times of drought, invertebrates are scarce in the leaf-litter and lower vegetation (Levings and Windsor, 1982, Levings and Windsor, 1984, Frith and Frith, 1990, Strong and Sherry, 2000) and regular ant-following birds may, possibly to a larger extent than in other parts of the year, rely on army ants – driving the remaining animals out of the soil and leaf-litter – to obtain sufficient quantities of food to cover metabolic requirements. In areas where D. wilverthi is uncommon, foraging opportunities and food resources for ant-following birds in the dry season are probably much lower than in areas where this army ant is abundant, which possibly limits populations.

    Abundances of three bird species were positively correlated to the size of forest fragments, even after controlling for the effect of army ant abundance. Our finding that specialized ant-followers followed army ant raids less frequently in small than in medium fragments may explain parts of the fragment size effect. What may be the reason for these changes in ant-following behavior? Possibly the more frequent presence of humans, sometimes accompanied by dogs or cattle, and their direct and indirect disturbance (Bleher et al., 2006, Mammides et al., 2008), hindered birds from following ant swarms in small forest fragments. Alternatively, since ant-following birds use each other to find army ant swarms (Willis and Oniki, 1978, Chaves-Campos, 2003, Chaves-Campos, 2008), the decline of the overall abundance of specialized ant-followers may lower the overall capability of the ant-following bird community to locate army ant swarms, leading to lower attendance rates of individual bird species. However, our data did not allow testing any of these hypotheses. Irrespective of the exact causes for the change in ant-following behavior, since specialized ant-followers certainly profit from following army ants due to increased prey availability in front of raids (Harper, 1989), and consequently, have higher foraging efficiencies in the presence than in the absence of army ant raids (Meisel, 2004), a reduction in swarm raid attendance probably affects the foraging success, and the survival or reproduction probability of individual birds in small rainforest fragments.

    Another mechanism how fragment size may influence the abundance of ant-following birds is by limiting the total number of army ant colonies (Stouffer and Bierregaard, 1995). As individual army ant colonies do not forage every day (Franks, 1982b), obligate ant-followers need to track more than one colony to assure daily foraging (Stouffer and Bierregaard, 1995, Willson, 2004). For example, Leroux (1982) reported that colonies of the African army ant Dorylus nigricans do not forage in about 16% of all days, so that 2 and 3 colonies are necessary to have a 97% and 99% probability that at least one colony is foraging on a given day, respectively. Rainforest fragments below a particular size threshold might be too small to contain enough army ant colonies to support these birds (Harper, 1989, Stouffer and Bierregaard, 1995). Even for non-obligate ant-following birds, a lack of enough army ant colonies would be detrimental. In the absence of army ant raids birds would be forced to forage away from ants, where they forage much less efficiently (Meisel, 2004). However, the forest fragments studied by us were quite large (⩾65 ha) and it was estimated that even the smallest fragments may hold on average more than nine army ant colonies (see Peters et al., 2009). This large number of colonies makes it rather unlikely that the total number of army ant colonies in forest fragments limited the abundance of the studied ant-following birds.

    Most Neotropical ant-followers have extensive area requirements (Willson, 2004) and this appears to be an important reason for their high vulnerability to habitat fragmentation (Ferraz et al., 2007, Van Houtan et al., 2007). Home range sizes of African ant-followers are unknown. Unfulfilled area requirements could also be an important determinant of the susceptibility of African ant-followers to habitat fragmentation, especially for populations in very small fragments (e.g., <50 ha). Behavioral studies (e.g., using radio-tracking methods) are highly demanded to better understand the ecology of ant-following birds in Africa and to derive further recommendation for their conservation.

    Abstract

    A growing number of species are being transported and introduced by humans to new locations and are establishing self‐sustaining populations outside their native ranges. Since ants play many ecological roles, introduction of an invasive ant species, and subsequent disruption of Australia's rich and abundant native ant fauna, has the potential for numerous adverse consequences. Over 6700 ants representing 105 species from 73 genera have been intercepted at Australian ports of entry in the last 20 years and all six of the world's most destructive invasive ants have become established in the country. Here we review the current and potential consequences of these ants on Australia's natural and agricultural environments. To date, several studies, most involving the big‐headed ant, Pheidole megacephala, and the Argentine ant, Linepithema humile, have documented a decline in native ant species richness. The displacement of native ants by these invaders could have multiple consequences for the native flora and fauna. Since few of these have been investigated in Australia, we combine knowledge from invasions elsewhere, the ecology of the interactions, and data on current and predicted geographic ranges of introduced ants to hypothesise about likely indirect effects of invasive ants in Australia. Further investigations that are aimed at testing these predictions will also aid in justifying and prioritising national prevention and control efforts, and will contribute to some of the long‐standing questions about ant invasions globally.

    INTRODUCTION

    Ants are the dominant terrestrial invertebrates in Australia (Shattuck 1999). They occur in all terrestrial habitats acting as predators and prey, scavengers, herbivores, granivores, plant mutualists and soil aerators. Their functional importance in the Australian ecosystem is reflected in their use as bioindicators for environmental assessment in relation to disturbance (Andersen & Majer 2004). Australia currently has representatives of about 15% (approximately 1300 described species) of the world's described ant species (Majer et al. 2004), most of which are endemic. Despite the abundance, richness and ubiquity of the Australian ant fauna, it remains poorly understood, with perhaps half of its species still undescribed, and little known about the biology and ecology of many described species (Shattuck 1999).

    Underscoring the need to understand the Australian native ant fauna is the potential for ants from elsewhere to invade and disrupt native ant assemblages. Invasion by non‐native species is one of the most significant threats to native biodiversity, natural ecosystems and ecosystem functions worldwide, and several ant species are among the most destructive invaders (Lowe et al. 2000). The rich and abundant native ant fauna in Australia suggests a wealth of potential interactions that introduced ants may disrupt or usurp. The best‐documented effect is the displacement of native invertebrate fauna, primarily ants, through direct competition or predation (Holway et al. 2002 and references therein). However, less apparent are the indirect effects this displacement can have. For example, invasive ants may fail to fulfil the ecological roles of the native ants they displace (Lach 2003 and references therein), or they may form mutualistic interactions with either native or introduced species, to the detriment of the native species (e.g. O'Dowd et al. 2003). Given the abundance and diversity of the Australian ant fauna, there is significant potential for adverse consequences of ant invasions via one or more of these mechanisms.

    Worldwide, there are approximately 150 species of ants that have been recorded outside their native ranges (McGlynn 1999). In Australia, over 6700 ants representing 105 species from 73 genera have been intercepted at ports of entry in the last 20 years (Commonwealth of Australia 2006). Although not all of these species have established in Australia, the presence of six species that have been identified globally as being the most detrimental (Holway et al. 2002) is of significant concern (Table 1). These species are well known for affecting biodiversity and ecological communities around the world (Holway et al. 2002) and all but one of these species (Solenopsis geminata (Fabr.)) are currently listed among the world's 100 worst invaders (Lowe et al. 2000). In Australia, these six species are at various stages of the invasion process (Table 1). Consequently, there is great disparity in the nature and scale of their impact on native fauna and flora.

    In addition to these six species, a number of other non‐native ant species are also established in Australia, including Paratrechina longicornis (Latreille), Tapinoma melanocephalum (Fabr.), Tetramorium bicarinatum (Nylander), Te. simillimum (Smith), Monomorium destructor (Jerdon), M. floricola (Jerdon), M. pharaonis (Linnaeus) and Hypoponera punctatissima (Roger) (McGlynn 1999; Shattuck 1999; May & Heterick 2000; Invasive Species Specialist Group 2006). The biological impacts of these species remain largely uninvestigated throughout their introduced ranges. We do not consider Technomyrmex albipes (Smith) here, despite it being reported as occurring in Australia (Shattuck 1999), because the genus is currently undergoing revision, and previous identifications in Australia may be incorrect (B Fisher pers. comm. 2007).

    In this review we synthesise the current knowledge of the direct and indirect impacts of introduced ants on the native flora and fauna of Australia. In the many cases where the effects of introduced ants in Australia have not yet been investigated in detail, we postulate on the potential impact based on studies undertaken in other regions of the world, taking into consideration the characteristics of the Australian environment. We consider Christmas Island to be a special case because while it is geographically and ecologically closer to Indonesia than mainland Australia, politically it is part of Australia and has yielded one of the best‐documented examples of invasive ant effects (O'Dowd et al. 2003). This review differs from other recent reviews on invasive ants (e.g. Holway et al. 2002; Krushelnycky et al. 2005; Commonwealth of Australia 2006) by focusing on consequences of ant invasions in the Australian environment and exploring the ecological interactions that ants have with other members of the community.

    EFFECTS OF INVASIVE ANTS ON AUSTRALIA'S NATIVE FAUNA

    Displacement of ants

    Globally, the displacement of native ants and other ground‐dwelling invertebrates is the best‐documented effect of ant invasions (Holway et al. 2002). In Australia, ant invasions are associated with a decline in ant species richness, with most studies focusing on the big‐headed ant, Pheidole megacephala Fabr., or the Argentine ant, Linepithema humile (Mayr) (Table 2). Both of these species have been associated with lower abundance and species richness of native ants in urban regions (Majer & Flugge 1984; Heterick et al. 2000; May & Heterick 2000; Walters & Mackay 2003b; Walters 2006), as well as less modified environments (Heterick 1997; Hoffmann et al. 1999; and Vanderwoude et al. 2000 for big‐headed ants; Rowles & O'Dowd in press for Argentine ants). Although more recently introduced and therefore less well studied in Australia, the red imported fire ant, Solenopsis invicta Buren, has been associated with lower ant species richness in a preliminary study undertaken near Brisbane (Natrass & Vanderwoude 2001). However, this study was confounded by the presence of P. megacephala at the ‘uninfested’ site. On Christmas Island, ant species richness is known to decline with increasing forager densities of the yellow crazy ant, Anoplolepis gracilipes (Smith) (Abbott 2006), but most of the ant species on Christmas Island are non‐native (Thomas & Framenau 2006).

    Although there is evidence of an association between low ant species richness and the presence of invasive ants, it is not clear if this association is the cause or consequence of invasion. In the absence of pre‐invasion data of the affected communities, studies tend to compare native ant species diversity of invaded sites with uninvaded ‘control’ sites. However, studies that use this methodology cannot discriminate whether low ant species richness in invaded sites is due to the displacement of native ants by the invading species or due to a low native ant species diversity prior to ant invasion. There is, however, one recent study that takes a longitudinal approach to this question (Hoffmann & Parr 2008). This study re‐surveys a known infestation of P. megacephala in 2005, 9 years after an initial survey (Hoffmann et al. 1999). Results indicate that sites in which P. megacephala biomass had increased over those 9 years had a corresponding decrease in native ant species richness and abundance. These data suggest that this invasive ant does in fact displace native ants during the invasion process, at least in this habitat type. More longitudinal studies like this are required in order to determine if this pattern is consistent across habitat types and invasive ant species.

    Most invasive ants have a social structure known as unicoloniality in which aggression among nests is rare even across vast spatial scales. In California and Europe Argentine ants form expansive supercolonies that can extend up to 6000 km2 (Giraud et al. 2002; Thomas et al. 2006) and the success of invasive ants in displacing native ants in these countries has been attributed to the higher abundance of Argentine ants relative to native ants (Holway et al. 2002). Recent research on the Argentine ant in Victoria, Australia, indicates that it also forms supercolonies in this country (Björkman‐Chiswell et al. 2008). In fact, a disparity in worker abundance between Argentine ants and native Iridomyrmex species in invaded South Australian sites has been shown to increase competition success of Argentine ants (Walters & Mackay 2003b). However, unlike sites invaded in California (Holway 1998), the abundance of Argentine ants in invaded sites in South Australia tends to be similar to the abundance of native ants in non‐invaded sites (Walters 2006). The big‐headed ant also has been found to have a numerical advantage over native ants in Perth gardens (Heterick et al. 2000), and some rainforests (Hoffmann et al. 1999) but not in dry sclerophyllous forests (Heterick 1997). On Christmas Island, the yellow crazy ant occurs in the highest densities ever recorded for ants (Abbott 2005), and this infestation has had one of the most dramatic effects on the native flora and fauna ever recorded for an invasive ant population (O'Dowd et al. 2003).

    Dominant dolichoderines

    The effect of invasive ants on species in the Dolichoderinae subfamily is a topic of debate. In Australia, dolichoderines, particularly those in the genus Iridomyrmex, are well known for their aggressive behaviour and almost always dominate the native ant community. The level of behavioural dominance achieved by Iridomyrmex species in the Australian ant fauna is far higher than in matched habitats elsewhere in the world. It is because of this strong competitive ability of Iridomyrmex in Australia that this group has been hypothesised to limit the range expansion of many invasive ants (Majer 1994; Andersen 1997; Hoffmann et al. 1999; Walters & Mackay 2003b; Thomas & Holway 2005). However, to date, there is little evidence to support or refute this hypothesis. That Iridomyrmex species are rarely found in areas heavily infested by L. humile (Heterick et al. 2000; May & Heterick 2000; Walters 2006) or P. megacephala (Heterick et al. 2000; Vanderwoude et al. 2000) suggests either that they are displaced by these invasive species or that these invasive species are incapable of invading areas dominated by Iridomyrmex. Studies with pre‐ and post‐invasion sampling would enable us to more accurately discriminate between these two possibilities.

    To date, direct competitive interactions between native Iridomyrmex and non‐native ants have only been investigated with the Argentine ant at a local scale, and only over short periods. These studies show that Argentine ants are able to dominate food resources in the presence of Iridomyrmex in highly disturbed habitats (Thomas & Holway 2005; Rowles & O'Dowd 2007). However, direct competitive interactions between Argentine ants and Iridomyrmex species indicate that at least some Iridomyrmex species are able to resist displacement from food baits by this invader (Thomas & Holway 2005; Walters & Mackay 2005), especially when abiotic conditions are not well suited to the Argentine ant (Thomas & Holway 2005). Relative to native Iridomyrmex species, Argentine ants are more vulnerable to desiccation and have reduced survival and activity levels at high temperatures (Walters & Mackay 2003a, 2004). This ultimately depresses their competitive ability, with Argentine ants dominating fewer baits against native Iridomyrmex species when conditions are too hot (Thomas & Holway 2005). Likewise, ‘hot climate specialists’ such as species from the endemic Australian genus Melophorus (Thomas & Holway 2005) and native ‘cold climate specialists’ such as Prolasius and Notoncus (Walters 2006) may inhabit areas invaded by Argentine ants by exploiting daily foraging rhythms linked to thermal preferences that differ from these ants.

    Displacement of other fauna

    In Australia, there is inconsistent evidence on the impact of invasive ants on other arthropods. Pheidole megacephala has been reported to decrease the abundance of native invertebrates by 42–85% in monsoonal Australia (Hoffmann et al. 1999; Hoffmann & Parr 2008), and may adversely affect collembolans and mites in dry sclerophyll forest (Heterick 1997). In urban reserves in South Australia, areas invaded by Argentine ants have more isopods, amphipods and millipedes (Walters & Mackay 2003b), and fewer earwigs, spiders and wasps than nearby non‐invaded areas (Walters 2006); however, these findings are inconsistent across seasons. In peri‐urban bush in Victoria, sites invaded by Argentine ants had a different invertebrate composition, but similar invertebrate abundance as nearby non‐invaded sites (Rowles & O'Dowd in press). Solenopsis invicta does not appear to affect the diversity of other invertebrates in remnant forests near Brisbane (Natrass & Vanderwoude 2001), but comparison with a site invaded by P. megacephala makes conclusions from this study questionable. Anoplolepis gracilipes is having a dramatic impact on a keystone species, the endemic red land crab Gecarcoidea natalis of Christmas Island, resulting in a dramatic change to the composition and structure of the island's rainforest (O'Dowd et al. 2003).

    Vertebrates may also be susceptible to introduced ants, primarily because of a lack of co‐evolved defences to these intruders. Introduced ants can have formidable defences such as the stinger and venom of S. invicta and the formic acid spray of A. gracilipes, and as mentioned, are generally higher in abundance than native ants, enabling them to overcome larger fauna more easily. As such, vertebrates can be more affected by invasive than native ants (Holway et al. 2002). Yellow crazy ants may be responsible for the declines of the endemic Christmas Island Pipistrelle, Pipistrellus murrayi (Lumsden et al. 2000), and the endemic emerald dove (Chalcophaps indica), and a reduction in the nesting success of the endemic Christmas Island Thrush (Turdus poliocephalus erythropleurus) (Abbott 2006; Davis et al. in press). Red imported fire ants may already be displacing scincid lizards (Natrass & Vanderwoude 2001), and at least two species of crocodiles, two genera of frogs, the Australian loggerhead turtle, egg‐laying terrestrial lizards and terrestrial nesting birds, including quail, parrots, wading birds and songbirds (Moloney & Vanderwoude 2003). The Argentine ant is known to have a similar impact on the native fauna in other parts of its introduced ranges. For example, it has been associated with declines of the grey shrew in California and nest failure of the California gnatcatcher, possibly through predation (Holway et al. 2002). However, as yet, no effects of this ant on birds or mammals have been reported in Australia.

    Native ants also serve as prey for many Australian vertebrates. Ants are a large part of the diets of native birds including Eastern bristlebirds, little wattlebirds, splendid fairy‐wrens, treecreepers and honeyeaters (Noske 1979; Tullis et al. 1982; Gibson & Baker 2004). Marsupial myrmecophages include echidnas, striped possums, numbats and long‐nosed bandicoots (Abensperg‐Traun 1991; Abensperg‐Traun & Steven 1997; Thums et al. 2005). Most terrestrial skinks also depend heavily on ants as food (Abensperg‐Traun & Steven 1997). Whether these birds, reptiles and marsupials would find the smaller invasive ants palatable is unknown. Juvenile coastal horned lizards in California will not eat Argentine ants that have displaced the larger native ants (Suarez et al. 2000) and fail to grow when offered only L. humile (Suarez & Case 2002).

    Mutualistic interactions

    Lycaenid butterflies

    While associations with ants are globally common among members of the butterfly family, Lycaenidae, Australia may contain the highest proportion of obligatory ant‐associated lycaenid butterflies of any zoogeographical region, about 40% (Eastwood & Fraser 1999). Another 40% of the lycaenid fauna have a facultative relationship with ants (Eastwood & Fraser 1999). Larvae that have facultative relationships may associate with several different species of ants, whereas obligate associations are species specific, possibly mediated by the chemistry of the nectar reward (Eastwood & Fraser 1999). The localised distribution of many lycaenids may be limited by their ant hosts (Smiley et al. 1988) because of the protection they afford the caterpillars from predators and parasitoids (Pierce et al. 1987), and/or because adult females preferentially oviposit in the presence of the ant hosts (Pierce & Elgar 1985; Sands 1999; Fraser et al. 2002). Larvae also may benefit by being able to feed more frequently (Cushman et al. 1994) or, in myrmecophagous associations, by eating ant brood (Eastwood & Fraser 1999).

    Representatives of five subfamilies of ants in Australia are known to tend lycaenid larvae, with dolichoderines being most common (Eastwood & Fraser 1999). Ants are attracted by the secretion of the larvae's nectary organs, and foraging on plants and imbibing nectar appear to be key traits of tending ants. Most invasive ants have a proclivity for carbohydrate‐rich resources (Holway et al. 2002) and so it is not unlikely that they would be attracted to secretions of lycaenid larvae that are facultatively associated with ants. Although butterfly distributions can be highly patchy and localised, the range of many facultative ant‐associated lycaenid butterflies (Commons & Waterhouse 1981) overlaps with the current ranges of P. megacephala and the potential ranges (Commonwealth of Australia 2006) of S. invicta, and Wasmannia auropunctata. Seven Australian lycaenid species are known to have facultative associations with P. megacephala, and one with L. humile (Eastwood & Fraser 1999; Schmidt 2002), although it is not known whether they benefit the larvae to the same extent as the displaced native ants. Solenopsis invicta, S. geminata, L. humile, P. megacephala and A. gracilipes are all known to prey upon Lepidoptera larvae in other regions of their introduced ranges (Bernays & Cornelius 1989; Bach 1991; Holway et al. 2002).

    Obligately associated larvae are less amenable to substitution of their ant partner (Eastwood & Fraser 1999) mandating that the ecology of the host ant be included in conservation plans (New & Britton 1997). At least 39 of the 56 obligately associated lycaenids (Eastwood & Fraser 1999) have geographic distributions (Commons & Waterhouse 1981) that overlap partly or entirely with coastal areas already invaded by P. megacephala, L. humile or A. gracilipes, but it is not known whether any are tended or preyed upon by these non‐native ants.

    Hemiptera

    Ants are attracted to sap‐sucking hemipterans by the honeydew they excrete and, in exchange, protect the bugs from their natural enemies and the growth of sooty mould. Invasive ants are thought to be especially adept at tending hemipterans because of their predilection for carbohydrate resources, their aggression and their abundance (Holway et al. 2002). Moreover, A. gracilipes and L. humile have physiological modifications that enable them to consume large amounts of liquid food (Davidson 1998), and P. megacephala soldiers are able to carry droplets (Dejean et al. 2005). The abundance of invasive ants may be linked to the acquisition of carbohydrate resources, especially honeydew (Helms & Vinson 2002; Holway et al. 2002). Likewise, invasive ants are often associated with a high abundance of honeydew‐producing hemipterans, frequently to the detriment of native and crop plants.

    In Australia, P. megacephala tends pseudococcids on custard apple (Murray 1982) and, along with the tramp ant Te. bicarinatum, on sugarcane (Carver et al. 1987; De Barro 1990), with unreported consequences. Pheidole megacephala has also been associated with an outbreak of the scale insect Pulvinaria urbicola leading to the death of Pisonia grandis trees on Tryon Island, within the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park (Hoffmann et al. 2004). On Christmas Island, the formation of supercolonies of A. gracilipes has been associated with an outbreak of the scale insects Tachardina aurantica (Kerridae) and Coccus celatus (Coccidae), which has resulted in mass dieback of the forest (O'Dowd et al. 2003). Although the effects of invasive ants on host plants in Australia appear to be limited to islands and agricultural systems, this is potentially a bias in research rather than a true reflection of their impact. Elsewhere in the world, L. humile, A. gracilipes, P. megacephala, S. invicta and W. auropunctata have all demonstrated their ability to tend hemipterans in a variety of ecosystems (Holway et al. 2002). However, a recent review suggests that fire ants are unlikely to be strong mutualists of hemipterans due to their food preferences for protein and oils (Ness & Bronstein 2004, but see Helms & Vinson 2002).

    There are significant possibilities for invasive ants to usurp hemipteran mutualisms from native ants in Australia. Like some invasive ants, native Australian ants in the Iridomyrmex, Camponotus and Crematogaster genera also have physiological modifications for liquid food consumption (Davidson 1998). Species in these genera and others are well known for tending phloem feeders in a variety of settings in Australia including on Eucalyptus (Buckley 1992; Rozario et al. 1993; Shcherbakov et al. 2000; Eastwood 2004), and other native and non‐native plants (Collins & Scott 1982; Dolva & Scott 1982; Buckley et al. 1990), in rainforests (Blüthgen & Fiedler 2004), and in agriculture, including citrus (James et al. 1999), sugarcane (Carver et al. 1987) and rambutan (Lim 1992). Although most of these reports fail to experimentally test the effects of the ant‐hemipteran mutualism on the host plant, it appears that native ant tending of sap‐sucking bugs has not led to the catastrophic dieback that invasive ant tending can. In fact, a recent global review of ant‐hemipteran effects primarily involving native ants contends that in most cases there is a net positive effect on the host plant because ants interfere with more harmful herbivores (Styrsky & Eubanks 2007). If invasive ants in Australia fail to emulate any positive effects of native ants, or have a more beneficial effect on the hemipterans, host plants could suffer greater herbivory. To date there are no studies that directly compare the repercussions of native with invasive ant tending in Australia, for either the plant or the bugs, and few worldwide (Holway et al. 2002; Ness & Bronstein 2004). Likewise, whether invasive or native ants benefit more from the honeydew they obtain from the mutualism is completely unknown, although it may shed light on invasion dynamics and supercolony formation.

    Other invertebrates

    Bugs and butterfly larvae are not the only insects to have close associations with ants. Some species of carabid (Erwin 1981), and staphylinid beetles (Ashe 2003), eurymelid bugs (Day & Pullen 1999) and termites (Higashi & Ito 1989) have symbiotic relationships with Australian ants. Since these relationships are specialised to the extent that even other closely related native ant species are not suitable substitutes, it is unlikely that invasive ants would fulfil the mutualist role if they displace the native ant.

    EFFECTS OF INVASIVE ANTS ON AUSTRALIA'S NATIVE FLORA

    Ant relationships with plants may be obligate or facultative, direct or indirect, and harmful or beneficial to plants. When introduced ants displace native ants, they may assume their roles and potentially alter the interactions, or fail to emulate the functional roles of native ants thereby disrupting relationships with the plants (Lach 2003). Here we outline the known and potential impacts of introduced ants on plants in Australia.

    Extrafloral nectaries

    Extrafloral nectaries (EFNs) are glands that produce nectar but are not associated with flowers. Ants are known to defend plants that produce EFNs in exchange for the food rewards they produce (Keeler 1989). At least 94 plant families have species with EFNs (Koptur 1992; Blüthgen & Reifenrath 2003) and 76 of these families are present in Australia (Australian Biological Resources Study 2006). Acacia spp. in particular are extremely common in Australia and most of the 900+ species on the continent have EFNs (Simmons 1987). Acacia are most diverse along the interface between the arid zone and the South West Botanical Province near Perth, and along the tablelands of the Great Dividing Range between Canberra and Sydney (World Wide Wattle 2006); areas that already overlap with the distribution of Argentine and big‐headed ants. Acacia are least diverse in rainforests (Simmons 1987), but Australian rainforests may be rich in other EFN plants. One Queensland rainforest study discovered 34 EFN plant species, with 17% of large trees and 21% of climbing plant species having EFNs (Blüthgen & Reifenrath 2003).

    Although EFN‐bearing plants are relatively common and widespread in several parts of Australia, only a few studies have investigated their interactions with native ants (e.g. Majer 1979; Buckley 1983; O'Dowd & Catchpole 1983; Mackay and Whalen 1996; Blüthgen et al. 2004b) and these studies show little consistency in the benefits of these interactions. Some ants, including both native and invasive species, protect plants more than others, and the same ant species can have different effects on different plants (Koptur 1992; Ness & Bronstein 2004). To date, research on invasive ant–EFN interactions in Australia is limited to monsoonal rainforests in the Northern Territory where P. megacephala visits two EFN‐bearing shrubs, one native and one invasive, to the apparent benefit of both plants (Hoffmann et al. 1999). Pheidole megacephala and L. humile are both frequent visitors to EFNs on Acacia saligna in Western Australia (L Lach pers. obs. 2006), and research is currently underway to determine their effects relative to native ants.

    The abundance and aggression associated with invasive ants suggests that they might be more effective at displacing herbivores than native ants, but in other invaded regions interactions between invasive ants and EFNs have shown similar inconsistencies to that of interactions between native ants and EFNs. For example, L. humile and S. invicta have been shown to decrease herbivore abundance, herbivory, and/or increase plant growth and reproduction on some EFN‐bearing plants (Koptur 1979; McLain 1983; Freitas et al. 2000; Stiles & Jones 2001), even if they do not visit the EFNs (Ness 2003), but they have neutral or negative effects on others (Agnew et al. 1982; Stiles & Jones 2001; Lach 2008a). The apparent context‐dependent nature of EFN–ant interactions makes predicting the outcome of invasive ants on Australia's EFN plants virtually impossible. Certainly this is an area that requires significantly more research both within Australia and internationally.

    Ant–plants

    Ants associated with true myrmecophytes nest in the plants' hollow structures such as thorns, often in exchange for defending the plant against insects, mammals or other plants. Worldwide, myrmecophytes (ant–plants) are restricted to the tropics (Keeler 1989). However, the ant species complex Philidris cordatus (previously known as Iridomyrmex cordatus) associated with the best‐known myrmecophytic genera in Australia (Myrmecodia and Hydnophytum (Rubiaceae)) plays more of a nutrient‐providing role, rather than a defensive role. As a result of this symbiosis, the epiphytes are able to survive in the open canopies of swamps and savannas (Huxley 1982). Pheidole megacephala has been observed in Myrmecodia beccarii (Commons & Waterhouse 1981), although whether it displaces P. cordatus, and benefits the plant in a similar way, has not been investigated.

    Ant defence of plants without rewards

    Ants may also defend plants in the absence of a plant‐offered reward, although these types of interactions are reported far less than those mentioned above (Beattie 1985). The highly territorial and aggressive Oecophylla smaragdina may be the best example in Australia. In northern Australia, they protect cashew (Peng et al. 1997) and mango crops (Peng & Christian 2004) from several pest insects; however, these ants may also protect pest sap‐sucking bugs from their natural enemies (Peng & Christian 2005). Native Pheidole spp. and, to a lesser extent, Iridomyrmex spp. are predators of the cotton bollworm Helicoverpa armigera, but agricultural practices limit their effectiveness as controls (Mansfield et al. 2003). Iridomyrmex spp. are major predators of cabbage moth larvae, Pieris rapae (Jones 1987). However, native predaceous ants sometimes protect weed species against introduced biocontrol agents (van Klinken et al. 2003).

    As with other ant–plant interactions, the relationships between invasive ants and plants without explicit rewards have not yet been investigated in Australia. Yellow crazy ants can displace native O. smaragdina from trees (Young et al. 2001), although big‐headed ants cannot (Hölldobler 1983), but even the expense of territory defence against other ants diminishes the effectiveness of the weaver ant as a biocontrol agent (Peng et al. 1999). Hypothesising about the potential effects of invasive ants on Australian plants after they have displaced native ants is hampered by the complexity and context dependency of interactions observed elsewhere. For example, although S. invicta has been shown to be a significant predator of insect pests in many crop systems (Holway et al. 2002), and has been associated with decreased folivory of some plants (Stiles & Jones 2001), it can also be a major intraguild predator (Eubanks et al. 2002), making the measurement of net effects difficult (Eubanks 2001). Anoplolepis gracilipes sometimes protects coconut palms from bug damage in the Solomon Islands, but is never an effective predator of coconut bugs in Zanzibar (Lach 2003 and references therein). Linepithema humile, P. megacephala, W. auropunctata and S. geminata have all been reported to prey upon plant‐associated arthropods in various areas they have invaded, but direct comparisons of their effects relative to displaced native ants are rare (Holway et al. 2002) and have to be considered in the context of the potential for mutualistic relationships with honeydew‐producing Hemiptera.

    Ants and flowers

    Nectar thieving

    Ants are often considered floral antagonists because they may steal nectar at the expense of pollinators, or may mechanically or chemically damage floral structures (Beattie et al. 1984; Galen & Butchart 2003). To defend themselves against ants, plants can have chemical or mechanical deterrents in their flowers (Kerner 1878; Ghazoul 2001), although it is unclear how widespread and specific these deterrents are. In Australian tropical rainforests, only 3 out of 18 floral nectars studied show evidence of containing ant repellents and many flowers are visited by ants (Blüthgen et al. 2004a). Published reports of the effects of native ants on flowers are few and range from consuming nectar (Abbott 1985; Horskins & Turner 1999), deterring pollinators (Anderson et al. 1982), damaging flowers and providing protection from floral antagonists (Scott 1979; Kirk 1984).

    While invasive ant–floral interactions have not been studied in Australia, it will be interesting to establish whether invasive ants have a greater or different effect on floral interactions than native ants. Given the strong competitive ability of most introduced ant species, and their attraction to floral nectar (Koptur & Truong 1998; Lach 2005), it follows that they would be capable of disrupting important mutualistic interactions between plants and pollinators. In fact, some invasive ant species are well known for disrupting such interactions in other regions of their introduced ranges. For example, in Hawai'i, native bees avoid flowerheads of Metrosideros polymorpha (Myrtaceae) that have P. megacephala (Lach 2008b), while in South Africa, Argentine ants exploit nectar from Eucalyptus sideroxlyon (Myrtaceae) at the expense of honeybees (Buys 1987) and deter honeybees and other insect pollinators from at least two protea species (Visser et al. 1996; Lach 2007, 2008a). These studies suggest that Australia's eucalypts and other Myrtaceae as well as native Proteaceae, including banksias, may be the first place to look for effects of invasive ants associated with flowers. Indeed, P. megacephala can be found in abundance in Banksia attenuata conflorescences, Grevillia spp., and as with L. humile, in Calothamnus sp., and Calistemon sp. as well (L Lach pers. obs. 2006), but the effect of their presence remains to be examined. It is also possible, however, that as with native ants, invasive ants may be inadvertent floral mutualists if they deter harmful floral antagonists.

    Pollination

    There are few well‐documented cases of ants acting as pollinators, perhaps because they often fail to transfer pollen and forage over smaller distances than flying insects, and their antifungal metapleural gland secretions destroy pollen grains (Beattie et al. 1984; Keeler 1989). In Australia, the endemic terrestrial orchid species, Leporella fimbriata, is pollinated exclusively by native winged male ants of Myrmecia urens when they attempt to copulate with the flowers (Peakall 1989). The orchid occurs in the south of Western Australia, and coastal South Australia and Victoria (Peakall 1989), and hence may overlap with the current or future range of Argentine ants. Since pseudocopulation relies on sexual mimicry, pollination is unlikely to continue should the native ant be displaced, especially since most invasive ant species, including L. humile, lack mating flights (Passera 1994). Pollination of another terrestrial orchid, Microtis parviflora, near Sydney and Melbourne, can be effected by flightless native Iridomyrmex gracilis workers but another local native ant, Myrmecia gulosa, is not able to pollinate these orchids (Jones 1975; Peakall & Beattie 1989) and it follows that an introduced ant species would most likely also fail as a pollinator.

    Seed dispersing and harvesting

    Hundreds of studies have investigated relationships between ants and seeds in Australia. Although there can be overlap, these can be broadly categorised as those in which the seed has a lipid‐rich appendage that attracts ants that then disperse the seeds (myrmecochory), and those in which the seeds are not adapted for ant dispersal but are collected by seed harvesters or other granivorous ants (Buckley 1982; Keeler 1989). Both seed‐dispersing and seed‐harvesting ants can be important factors in plant community structure (Keeler 1989; Christian 2001).

    Seed dispersing

    With at least 1500 myrmecochorous plants, Australia may be ‘the world's main centre’ of ant‐dispersed plants (Berg 1975). Most myrmecochores occur in the temperate sclerophyll forest and heath/shrubland in the south (Berg 1975) where they comprise 30–50% of the species in some communities (Willson et al. 1990). Benefits to the seeds can include increased dispersal distance (Andersen 1988; Whitney 2002), scarification (Plummer et al. 1995) or protection from fire (Auld 1986). The fate of seeds is largely dependent on the attributes of the dispersing ants (Hughes & Westoby 1992). For example, larger ants with bigger foraging areas such as Iridomyrmex sanguineus disperse Acacia holosericea seeds an average of 7.25 m, whereas, smaller Pheidole spp. move them less than 0.5 m, and other species move them only a few centimetres (Andersen & Morrison 1998). Burial depth also varies with species and affects seed viability (Hughes & Westoby 1992).

    Invasive ants that carry seeds may disperse them shorter distances than native species, may not bury them, may eat them or may collect fewer per unit time than native ants (Ness & Bronstein 2004). Solenopsis geminata, W. auropunctata and P. megacephala are known for their attraction to seeds (Holway et al. 2002), but experiments to test their dispersal capabilities in their invaded ranges are lacking. In the USA, S. invicta destroys some myrmecochorous herbaceous seeds, but has little effect on others (Zettler et al. 2001) and it is unclear what ramifications it has on myrmecochorous plants relative to the seed‐attracted S. geminata (Horvitz 1981), which it displaces in the USA (Holway et al. 2002). Linepithema humile is known to be a poor disperser of seeds in South Africa, Corsica and California (Holway et al. 2002 and references therein; Carney et al. 2003).

    In Australia, the extent to which invasive ants emulate native ants in terms of seed dispersal distance and optimum burial depth has not been examined. Although P. megacephala has been recorded collecting eliaosome‐bearing seeds of Acacia concurrens on rehabilitated sand mines in Queensland (Majer 1985), it is not known how their dispersal patterns compare to native ants. Given the general intolerance of invasive ants to arid regions, myrmecochorous relationships in the arid interior (e.g. Davidson & Morton 1981) may escape challenge by invasive ants. However, seed‐dispersing ants in the woodlands and open forests of the north (e.g. Andersen & Morrison 1998) may have to compete with A. gracilipes and those in the Banksia woodlands of south‐western Australia (e.g. Schatral et al. 1994) with L. humile or P. megacephala. The richly myrmecochorous areas of the southeast also may be susceptible to invasion by L. humile, while big‐headed ants may invade coastal dunes with myrmecochorous species in both the east (e.g. Majer 1985) and west (e.g. Gill 1985). In all of these areas, the native seed dispersing ant fauna comprises several species that vary in the level of benefits provided to myrmecochores; the simplification of these ant assemblages alone will likely have an impact on the plant community. Additionally, the effect of invasive ants will depend on whether they act as mutualists or ‘cheaters’, i.e. eating the eliaosome but not dispersing the seed. In the case of several weed species in Australia that have ant‐dispersed seeds (Piggin 1982; Brown & Brooks 2003; Bebawi & Campbell 2004), cheaters would be preferable to an ant assemblage that facilitates seed germination.

    Seed harvesting

    Seed harvester assemblages in Australia, as elsewhere, may be highly structured, with niche partitioning occurring diurnally, seasonally and/or spatially (Briese & Macauley 1980; Buckley 1982; Gross et al. 1991; Andersen et al. 2000). Unlike the rest of the world, in Australia some members of the formicine and ponerine subfamilies are important seed harvesters (Andersen 1991). Specialised granivores, including those in the genera Melophorus, Meranoplus and Tetramorium, are largely restricted to the arid zone, whereas opportunistic granivores, such as those in Prolasius and Rhytidoponera, are found in the cool‐temperate regions (Andersen 1991). A predominant problem is that of seed‐collecting native ants interfering with regeneration or restoration of eucalypt and other woodlands, especially in the absence of fire (e.g. Ashton 1979; O'Dowd & Gill 1984; Andersen 1987; Yates et al. 1995; Hosking and Turner 1997; Li et al. 2003; Scott and Gross 2004). Additionally, granivorous ants can disrupt dune revegetation (Ashton & van Gameren 2002), land reclamation following mining (Majer 1990), pasture establishment, and tobacco, sorghum and sunflower crops (Andersen 1990), although they may also cause seed loss of weeds (Panetta 1988). Australian ants more readily take smaller lighter seeds, with grasses being preferred over legumes (Mott & McKeon 1977; Gross et al. 1991; Andersen et al. 2000).

    Given the low likelihood of spread to the arid interior of the continent (Commonwealth of Australia 2006), none of the invasive ant species currently present in Australia is likely to affect the harvester ant assemblages in desert communities (e.g. Morton & Davidson 1988). However, in other regions where seed predation by ants is an important factor in plant community dynamics, including the temperate east and south‐east, the monsoonal north and the Mediterranean south‐west of Australia (Andersen 1990), ant invasions may have significant consequences. These consequences will likely depend on the behaviour of the invader. Invasion by ants that are not granivores (e.g. L. humile, A. gracilipes) but are able to displace part or all of a native ant harvester assemblage (e.g. Carney et al. 2003) may result in a markedly increased propagule pool, for grasses, in particular. In restoration or other projects in which native harvester ants are problematic, such a substitution may be perceived as beneficial. Invasion by granivorous invasive ants (e.g. S. geminata, S. invicta, P. megacephala, W. auropunctata) and subsequent displacement of native harvester ants also has the potential to change the composition of the seed bank, reflecting the invaders' preferences for some seeds over others. More disruption may occur where granivorous invasive ants invade communities not adapted to seed harvesting by ants. In Australian agriculture, big‐headed ants are already considered a pest because of the amount of seeds they take (Campbell 1966). In the USA, S. invicta is a seed pest of many crops (Morrison et al. 1997), while S. geminata is a seed predator of crops in Asia (Veeresh 1990), and of weeds in Mexico (Carroll & Risch 1984), but has not been reported as an invader of agricultural systems in Australia.

    CONCLUSIONS AND DIRECTIONS FOR FUTURE RESEARCH

    Although this review reveals the paucity of documented effects of invasive ants in Australia, it should not be used as evidence that ant invasions in Australia are without ecological consequence. Using the ecological roles of native ants as a guide, we have summarised mechanisms through which invasive ants may affect native flora and fauna, and their potential for doing so based on their current and predicted geographic ranges. Clearly, the many types of potential interactions merit further careful exploration and research. Here we stress several reasons why we believe this further exploration is critical, and needs to be performed in Australia.

    Australia has a unique flora and fauna

    Relying on the assessment of ant invasions in other parts of the world disregards intercontinental differences between native ant functional groups, community structures and ecological processes. For example, there is considerable structural and functional variation between the native ant fauna of Australia and North America. Australian ant communities are almost always dominated by dolichoderines and are far richer in taxa with physiological, morphological and behavioural specialisations (Andersen 1997).

    The distinctive Australian flora should also be considered. The unparalleled native ant species richness in arid, semi‐arid and seasonally arid areas of Australia is possibly driven by carbohydrate and moisture‐rich plant and hemipteran exudates, associated with, for example, the rich Acacia flora (Andersen 2003). Pheidole megacephala, A. gracilipes and L. humile are already established in some of the semi‐ and seasonally arid areas presenting a prime opportunity to test whether these exudates can facilitate invasion success (e.g. Helms & Vinson 2002; Abbott 2005).

    Invasive ants are widespread, and are predicted to increase their distributions

    Argentine and big‐headed ants currently have the greatest distributions in Australia, but models predict that at least 20% of the continent may have suitable climatic conditions for each of the six species (Table 1). Almost all coastal areas are predicted to be climatically suitable for more than one species (Commonwealth of Australia 2006). The red imported fire ant could cover over 2 000 000 km2 within Australia by 2028 if no long‐term control measures are in place (Scanlan & Vanderwoude 2006). Ever increasing domestic and international travel and trade will facilitate further introductions and dispersal of new species and species that are already present. Climate change may make much of the northern part of the country too hot and dry for some of the current invaders to spread, but may make the southern part of the continent more suitable for all but L. humile, given this ant's thermal preferences (Walters & Mackay 2003a, 2004)

    There are many unanswered questions regarding the consequences of ant invasions in relation to time since invasion

    Recent research on the effect of S. invicta several years after initial invasion has called into question whether the ants have any long‐term adverse effect on native ant diversity (Morrison 2002; King & Tschinkel 2006). Yet on Christmas Island several decades passed from the time of first introduction to the first observations of adverse effects of A. gracilipes (O'Dowd et al. 2003). These contrasting examples highlight our ignorance about invasion timelines and their connection to impacts. Super‐abundance may be a key correlate (Holway et al. 2002;O'Dowd et al. 2003), but we know little about how different species achieve or sustain this, or whether it is necessary for the continuation of initially observed displacement of native fauna.

    The role of disturbance in the success of invasive ants is poorly understood

    The successful establishment of invasive ants is often attributed to a disturbed habitat (Holway et al. 2002). In a disturbed habitat, there may be more opportunities for exotic species to become established because the original community members may be less well adapted to these new conditions and may perish. Without a universally applicable graded measure of disturbance this argument is difficult to support or disprove. Certainly, much of the research on invasive ants in Australia has been conducted in human‐modified environments (e.g. Majer & Flugge 1984; Majer 1985; Heterick et al. 2000; Walters 2006). However, several species including A. gracilipes, L. humile, P. megacephala and W. auropunctata have been able to invade areas in Australia or elsewhere that are relatively undisturbed (Hoffmann et al. 1999; Young et al. 2001; Le Breton et al. 2003; Lach 2007, 2008a). Even if ant invasions were limited to disturbed habitats, investigating their consequences remains valuable. Many sites affected by current or past human activities (e.g. restored urban bushlands, coastal dunes surrounded by suburban development, partially logged forests, rehabilitated mine sites, degraded rangelands) have substantial conservation value, even if they do not have the full complement of native flora and fauna that their more pristine counterparts do.

    Several species have not been studied at all

    There is a long list of ant species that have been intercepted at Australian ports of entry (Commonwealth of Australia 2006), and with increasing global travel and trade, the list of exotic species breaching Australian territories will only grow. Even so, few introduced species have been studied in detail, even those that are among the most renowned invaders (Table 1).

    Findings may enable prioritisation for management and further justify prevention and control efforts

    As ants spread, land managers will need to know the relative risks associated with each species, how to prioritise any control efforts, and the ecological consequences of controlling one species instead of another if only limited control is feasible. Attempts to control or eradicate invasive ants in Australia and elsewhere have been characterised by high costs, tremendous effort and few successes (Commonwealth of Australia 2006). Infestation size notwithstanding, the huge expense and likelihood of failure associated with eradication attempts translate to a need for clear and compelling evidence that inaction will have irreparable negative consequences for local ecosystems. With their current limited distributions, evidence from overseas and Christmas Island has been sufficient to justify the attempted eradication of S. invicta and W. auropunctata in Queensland, and A. gracilipes in Queensland and Arnhem Land. However, recent attempts at control for species that are already widespread in Australia are almost unheard of (but see Hoffmann & O'Connor 2004). Identifying native species or processes that may be particularly vulnerable may facilitate development of more targeted and feasible control options. For example, eradicating an invasive ant from a vegetation fragment utilised by myrmecophilous butterflies may be more realistic and worthwhile than removing it from an entire metropolitan area.

    Even where there is a desire for control or eradication, the methods to do so often have significant non‐target effects, are unavailable or are too costly. The multidecade, multimillion dollar attempt to eradicate Argentine ants from greater Perth (Commonwealth of Australia 2006) was discontinued when heptachlor was banned, and the paste bait subsequently developed by the WA Department of Agriculture has not been registered for use in Australia (K Winn, Flybusters pers. comm. 2006), although it has been used with success in New Zealand (Harris 2002). Amdro®, the most effective toxic bait for the control of fire ants and big‐headed ants, is stocked by few suppliers in commercial quantities in Australia, and is four times more expensive than in the USA. Presto(tm), the most effective bait currently available for the control of yellow crazy ants, is not registered or commercially available in Australia (K Abbott pers. comm. 2006). Until novel means are developed (e.g. biological, genetic) an effective, available, inexpensive and easy to administer toxic bait is a fundamental ingredient for any ant control program. Companies that own active bait ingredients can impede development of effective baits if they do not have the incentive of a large, perennial market, while bureaucratic hurdles hamper the registration of new toxins in Australia (K Abbott pers. comm. 2006). Further documentation of impacts leading to greater justification for management may provide impetus for reducing these commercial and political barriers.

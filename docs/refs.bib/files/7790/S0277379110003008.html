    Natural and human impacts in a 35 000-year vegetation history in central New Britain, Papua New Guinea

    Author links open overlay panelCarolLentferaChristinaPavlidesbJimSpechtc
    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.quascirev.2010.08.009Get rights and content

    Abstract

    Phytoliths and micro-charcoal from the Yombon Airstrip archaeological site in central New Britain, Papua New Guinea, provide the longest vegetation history record yet available for the New Guinea islands. The record begins about 35 kya with the first evidence for human presence at the site and, with the exception of the Last Glacial Maximum period, is continuous to the present. Three other sites provide supplementary evidence, including plant macro-remains, from the early Holocene onwards. The record is punctuated by a series of volcanic events, which are reflected in the vegetation record by alternating frequencies of closed forest and regrowth elements. Micro-charcoal is present from the oldest levels and fluctuates in frequency throughout the sequence, increasing substantially from the terminal Pleistocene-early Holocene onwards. This coincides with the first appearance of panicoid grasses and a range of potential cultivars including bananas and Saccharum. Increased levels of burning coinciding with the appearance of potential plant cultivars may indicate shifts in plant food production leading to cultivation from the early Holocene onwards. This compares favourably with previously reported evidence from Garua Island off the north coast of New Britain. The combination of trends in burning, vegetation clearance and appearance of potential cultivars on New Britain appears to parallel changes in the Papua New Guinea highlands at a similar time, and suggests regional similarities in subsistence and vegetation management practices from before the LGM onwards. Further studies are needed to clarify the timing and extent of these shifts across the region, and to provide a vegetation picture for the period before human colonisation of New Britain.

    1. Introduction

    Modern humans entered the New Guinea-Australia region during the last glacial period about 50 000 years ago, when lower sea levels united the two landmasses as the palaeo-continent of Sahul (Roberts et al., 1994, Turney et al., 2001, Oâ€™Connell and Allen, 2004). By 45 000â€“40 000 years ago these people had spread across much of this continent and were colonising the islands to its east (Leavesley and Chappell, 2004, Torrence et al., 2004). This process focussed initially on the coastal regions of these eastern outliers of Sahul, now part of Papua New Guinea (Fig. 1), and by 36 000â€“34 000 years ago people were exploiting the chert resources of the interior of New Britain (Pavlides and Gosden, 1994, Pavlides, 2004).

    Little is known, however, about the history of land use and plant food subsistence strategies in these outlier islands through this long period, and even less about their environmental and climatic histories. Four pollen records cover only the last 4â€“5000 years or less (Southern, n.d., Hope et al., 1999, p. 399; Jago and Boyd, 2005). Evidence from plant macro-remains before the late Holocene is discontinuous, scattered in time and space, and restricted to a small range of nut-bearing taxa (Spriggs, 1996, Specht, 2005). Microscopic plant residues on stone and shell tools indicate exploitation of tropical root crops from the family Araceae on Buka Island before the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM) (Loy et al., 1992), and a range of tubers in the post-LGM period on New Ireland (Barton and White, 1993). Studies of phytoliths, micro-charcoal and starch residues from archaeological sites on Garua Island and Willaumez Peninsula, north New Britain, reveal a picture of repeated vegetation disturbance and recovery resulting from volcanic eruptions coupled with various human interventions throughout the Holocene (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007, Parr et al., 2009). At these locations, however, the record prior to the middle Holocene is problematic on account of the lack of reliable dates for the basal levels, though they are most likely of terminal Pleistocene age or older. Human intervention in the forest cover through burning and clearance and the introduction and expansion of plant management practices was underway by the start of the middle Holocene, and probably earlier (Boyd et al., 2005, Lentfer and Torrence, 2007, p. 102; Parr et al., 2009; cf Torrence et al., 2009, p. 526). Thereafter, human impacts, coupled with the effects of successive volcanic events, continued to affect vegetation cover, with â€˜a very slow increase in the intensification of landscape clearanceâ€™ until after the last major volcanic eruption of 1200â€“1350 years ago (cf Neall et al., 2008, Torrence et al., 2009, p. 526).

    Non-plant evidence of human intervention in the landscape occurs only in the late Holocene. On Lavongai, New Ireland, mounds and ditches associated with plant food production are at least 2000-years old (Leavesley and Troitzsch, 2007), but comparable earthworks appear much later in New Britain at 950â€“350 cal BP (Swadling, 1991). The formation of clay deposits behind beach ridges at several Lapita pottery sites (ca 3350â€“2750 cal BP) has been used to propose human-induced vegetation disturbance associated with cultivation of plants (Gosden et al., 1989, p. 573; Gosden and Webb, 1994, p. 47). These deposits, however, do not necessarily indicate initial vegetation disturbance. Sea level was slightly higher than today prior to and during at least part of the Lapita pottery period (Gosden and Webb, 1994, Kirch, 2001; cf Dickinson, 2001), and sediments resulting from vegetation disturbance pre-dating Lapita would probably not have accumulated in shallow water.

    In this paper we extend this picture with new data on vegetation history and land use using microscopic and macroscopic plant fossils and charcoal from additional localities in New Britain that span the late Pleistocene to the present. These sites include two open-air localities and two caves that constitute â€˜closedâ€™ environments. In Section 7 we introduce archaeological evidence from New Britain that bears on vegetation history, including inferences drawn from studies of flaked stone technologies.

    2. Regional setting

    2.1. The physical setting

    The study region is located within the wet, equatorial tropical lowlands of West New Britain Province, Papua New Guinea (Fig. 1). The inland sites (Yombon Airstrip/FIF, Auwa/FGT, Misisil/FHC) are located within the â€˜Passismanuaâ€™ area (Chowning and Goodale, 1965), between the Palicks and Andru Rivers, about 30â€“35 km from the administrative centre of Kandrian on the south coast.1 The regional geology has been summarised by Ryburn, 1975, Ryburn, 1976, whose terminology we follow here. The airstrip and Auwa stand at ca 450â€“500 m above sea level on a heavily dissected and tilted limestone plateau of Miocene age (the Yalam Limestone) (Fig. 2). Misisil cave is situated at about the same altitude in a pinnacle of limestone, about 4 km northeast of Auwa in the southern foothills of the central Whiteman Range (Pain and Specht, 1984/1985). This range is formed by Yalam Limestone and the Kapuluk Volcanics of Oligocene age, and rises to ca 1800 m in the uninhabited centre of the island.

    The limestone plateau displays moderately well developed polygonal karst topography with low relief and many dolines. Its irregular surface slopes from ca 800 to 1000 m on its inland margin to ca 200 m at its southern edge 5â€“10 km from the Kandrian coast. Here it abuts the gently sloping platform of the Johanna Beds of Pliocene age marine sediments, which extend almost to the south coast, where they meet uplifted reef limestone of Pleistocene age. This coastal limestone rises locally to 150 m above a narrow coastal plain that was partly formed by an uplift event in the late Holocene (Boyd et al., 1999a). The coastal site Alanglongromo/FLF is located in the base of this limestone.

    In addition to the Palicks and Andru Rivers, there are many small streams on the Yalam Limestone plateau, but surface water is scarce on the Johanna Beds and Pleistocene limestone, where drainage is almost entirely underground. Rainfall on the south side of the Whiteman Range occurs mainly between May and August, but with marked differences between the coast and interior. Precipitation at Kandrian on the coast is about 3850 mm per annum (McAlpine et al., 1983, p. 158), whereas limited records for the Passismanua area are between 6350 and 7500 mm per annum (Chowning, 1980, p. 9; P. Ahern, pers. comm., 1991). Within both areas there are marked monthly variations. Kandrian has a mean annual maximum temperature of 29.6 Â°C and a mean annual minimum of 22.7 Â°C (McAlpine et al., 1975, table 10). Temperature data are not available for the Passismanua area.

    The regionâ€™s soils were formed by weathering of the underlying limestone and lagoonal sediments and airfall tephras from volcanic centres on the north side of the Whiteman Range. These centres form part of the Bismarck Volcanic Arc (Johnson and Blake, 1972, Johnson, 1976). Tephras from Witori and Dakataua volcanoes, ca 90â€“100 km to the northeast and north of the study area, have repeatedly blanketed parts of central New Britain from the Pleistocene onwards (Machida et al., 1996, table 1; Torrence et al., 2004, Neall et al., 2008). The magnitude and impact of these eruptions ranged from minor events with restricted local impacts, to cataclysmic events with significant airfall and flow deposits that affected large areas and caused extensive landscape devastation close to the volcanic centres (Torrence and Doelman, 2007, Torrence, 2008, Torrence et al., 2009). At least eight eruptions are evidenced at archaeological sites in the study area, but the oldest four cannot be related to the older series recognised on Willaumez Peninsula (Neall et al., 2008, Torrence et al., 2009, table 1). Four later tephras can be confidently identified with major Holocene eruptions of Witori (the W-K series) (Table 1; Jackson, 1999). This series provides a distinctive sequence of tephras and associated palaeosols that allows stratigraphic correlation of localities over a wide area (Pavlides, 2004, Pavlides, 2006, Torrence, 2008). These tephras decrease in thickness towards the south coast, where their usefulness for correlation is reduced by their absence or expression as thin (ca 10 mm), discontinuous bands and patches.

    2.2. Regional vegetation

    The vegetation is characterised by lowland wet-tropical communities including lowland hill forest, coastal regrowth forest, mangroves, and coastal strandline vegetation (Paijmans, 1976, PNG Forest Research Institute, 1991, p. 24; Mueller-Dombois and Fosberg, 1998). Within the temporal framework of this study, these vegetation communities were probably always present in the region, with the extent of each vegetation type varying through time in response to variables such as changes in sea level, climate, hydrology, volcanism and human activity.

    Within the LGM period, when sea levels were ca 120 m lower than at present (Fleming et al., 1998, Lambeck and Chappell, 2001), conditions in the most elevated areas of the study region might have favoured lower montane forest vegetation, or elements thereof (cf Hope and Haberle, 2005). However, given the current vegetation distribution and with the altitudinal limits of montane forests variously proposed as 900 m (Whitmore, 1984) or 300â€“1000 m (Johns, 1981), depending on several factors influencing temperature, rainfall patterns and cloudiness, it is unlikely, given the proximity to the coast that montane forests were ever widespread within the study area.

    Prior to large scale logging operations commencing in the 1990s, the vegetation was dominated by a mosaic of lowland hill forests interspersed with regrowth forest in various stages of regeneration, and small clearings for settlements and gardens (Chowning, 1980, PNG Forest Research Institute, 1991). Typically, the mature forests consist of medium to large-crowned trees forming a closed canopy that restricts light penetration into the underlying strata. Density of vegetation in the shrub and herb layers is inversely related to canopy cover and light penetration. Under a closed canopy these layers have sparse undergrowth, comprised mainly of palms, tall gingers, mosses and ferns. Greater exposure of the understorey to solar radiation as a result of small-scale disturbance (e.g., tree-fall, minor landslips and defoliation due to tephra falls) favours denser growth and tree seedling germination (cf Bayliss-Smith et al., 2003). Compared with the mature forests, the floristic composition and structure of regrowth forests resulting from disturbance are more variable, influenced by the extent and nature of the disturbance to the original forest vegetation, introductions of new species and length of time involved in regeneration. Light-demanding grasses such as Themeda, Imperata and Saccharum species invade larger clearings with sufficient light penetration, and can persist in the understorey layers along with bamboos, gingers, Musaceae, Marantaceae, ferns and palms. Light-demanding trees including Euphorbiaceae, Moraceae and Ulmaceae species commonly occur in the upper storeys. Cultivars and other useful plants are often present in areas regenerated from old gardens and plantation sites.

    Along the coast, mangroves and beach strand vegetation dominate. Rhizophoraceae species (Rhizophora, Bruguiera and Ceriops spp.) form the predominant mangrove vegetation, and Callophyllum, Cordia, Barringtonia, Premna, Hibiscus and Terminalia species are common on the beach strand along with isolated stands of Casuarina equisitifolia. Sand-binding herbs, grasses, sedges and creepers including Canavalia maritima and Ipomoea pes-caprae dominate ground cover.

    2.3. Regional populations: inland

    Yombon Airstrip, Auwa hamlet and Misisil cave lie within the territory of the Sengseng people who occupy land between the Andru and the Palicks Rivers, and from Auwa southwards to the coast (Chowning and Goodale, 1965, p. 265). The Sengseng speak an Austronesian language (Ross, 1988, p. 161, map 6; Chowning, 1996, pp. 33â€“37), and share several cultural traits with other groups in southern New Britain (Goodale, 1966, Chowning, 1980, p. 8). In the 1960s the Sengseng numbered about 750â€“1000 persons, about half that recorded in the 1990s (Chowning and Goodale, 1965, p. 266; Chowning, 1980, p. 9; Bourke et al., 1996, p. 37). On the basis of these figures, population density would have been about 1 person/km2 in the 1960s. Traditionally, the Sengseng lived in small, dispersed hamlets with a communal house for men and one or more houses for women and children; in the 1960s the distance between menâ€™s houses was 5 km or more (Chowning, 1980, p. 11). Up to 1975, the Australian colonial administration attempted to create centralised settlements to facilitate administration, but this was unsuccessful (cf Goodale, 1995, p. 34 for the neighbouring Kaulong speakers). Establishment of a New Tribes Mission station and airstrip at Auwa in the 1980s later provided incentives to adopt a more centralised settlement pattern around the Mission station (Fig. 3).

    Subsistence is currently based on cultivation in small gardens with taro as the staple crop. These gardens are used for a single cropping cycle, after which a long fallow period of 15 years or more allows the development of tall (10+ m) woody regrowth (Bourke et al., 1996, p. 37). Pig husbandry forms a minor part in subsistence activity. Foraging in the rainforest for wild and managed plant foods, including a range of fruits, nuts, palms and wild yams provides the Sengseng with about 50% of their plant food intake (Chowning, 1980, p. 10). This is supplemented by river fishing, hunting of feral pigs, birds and small game, and the opportunistic capture of various insects (Chowning and Goodale, 1965, p. 266; Goodale, 1995, pp. 67â€“73; Bourke et al., 1996, pp. 37â€“39).

    2.4. Regional populations: coastal

    The Alanglongromo site is located on the south coast near Iumielo village to the west of Kandrian. The people of this area and the neighbouring islands speak Agerlep, an Austronesian language (Ross, 1988, p. 161, map 6), and share cultural features with the Sengseng (cf Gosden and Knowles, 2001). The main differences between them reflect the coastal location of the Agerlep, particularly their extensive use of coconuts and the animal and plant products of coastal swamps and the sea (Chinnery, 1928, Todd, 1934-1935, pp. 193â€“198; Kocher Schmidt, 2001). Their gardens produce a single harvest of taro and other crops followed by 5â€“15 years of fallow, which permits the development of tall woody regrowth (Bourke et al., 1996, pp. 31â€“32). Garden foods are supplemented by a range of fruits, nuts and palms, such as Canarium sp. (Wissink, 1996) and breadfruit (Artocarpus spp.). Traditionally settlements in the Kandrian area were situated on sheltered beaches within the harbour (Chinnery, 1928; Kocher Schmidt, 2001), or on cliff tops. The latter locations were selected partly for defence and partly because of the limited size of the coastal plain. Today, following coastal progradation and drainage of swamps, settlements cluster along the beaches next to the coastal road. The 1990s population density of 19 persons/km2 partly reflects the attraction of administrative and other services concentrated in the Kandrian area (Bourke et al., 1996, p. 31).

    3.1. Dating

    Dates younger than 26 000 radiocarbon years are calibrated using the CALIB 5.0.1 programs (Stuiver and Reimer, 1993, Hughen et al., 2004, Reimer et al., 2004). Calibrated dates are cited as years â€˜BPâ€™, whereas uncalibrated results are given as years â€˜bpâ€™. Date ranges are rounded to the nearest 10-years, and represent the highest probability (p = >0.95) at 2-sigma. Dates for the inland sites were on charcoal or charcoal and nutshell fragments. Results on marine shell samples from the coastal Alanglongromo site were calibrated using the default Î”R value of 0 Â± 0 years, as there is no locality-specific value. Dates cited for the W-K tephra series (Table 1) are based on a Bayesian analysis of dates for the various tephras (Petrie and Torrence, 2008, tables 5 and 7).

    3.2. Yombon Airstrip/FIF

    The Yombon Airstrip site, hereafter FIF, was excavated in 1991 and 1993 (Pavlides, 1993, Pavlides, 1999, Pavlides, 2004), and provides the main body of evidence. It extends as a series of lithic scatters exposed by surface grading over the entire area of Yombon Airstrip (Fig. 3). Analysis of three trenches totalling 5 m2 reveals that the area has been periodically occupied since the late Pleistocene, but there are no deposits clearly attributable to the period of the LGM. Since the terminal Pleistocene, human activity in the area has been persistent but intermittent (Pavlides, 2004, fig. 2). The artefactual materials are restricted to flaked tools in chert and, from the early Holocene onwards, obsidian imported from sources on the north side of New Britain.

    In the contiguous excavation squares FIF/2-3-4, there are eleven stratigraphic units. Excavation of the square selected for this study, FIF/4, ended at 200 cm below ground surface without reaching bedrock (Fig. 4, Table 2). The basal sticky, pink clay (Layer 1) was derived primarily from the weathering of the limestone bedrock, but above this clay the sediments comprise at least four, possibly eight tephras and their associated palaeosols. Layer 2 is mottled brown clay that may be a heavily weathered tephra (A), which grades up-profile into sticky dark brown clay containing flaked and fire-cracked chert artefacts dated to 26 350â€“34 270 bp (Pavlides and Gosden, 1994, Pavlides, 2004, fig. 2). This is sealed by a heavily weathered, compacted yellowish-brown tephra (Layer 3, tephra B) of unknown origin that is dated at other Passismanua sites to between 17 300â€“17 010 and 12 735â€“11 660 cal BP (Pavlides, 2004, table 2). Above tephra B is 30â€“60 cm of sticky brown clay (Layer 4), the lower part of which includes two possible tephras (C and D) of unknown origin. As these occur between tephra B dated to the terminal Pleistocene and a date of 6720â€“6315 cal BP in the upper part of Layer 4, they probably belong to the early Holocene. Layer 4 is sealed by the W-K1 tephra (Layer 5, 6160â€“5750 cal BP) and its dark brown palaeosol (Layer 6), which contains chert and obsidian artefacts dated to 4400â€“3900 cal BP. These are sealed by the W-K2 tephra (Layer 7, 3480â€“3150 cal BP) and its brown palaeosol (Layer 8) with a date for human activity of 2760â€“2360 cal BP. Layer 9 is a dull yellow-orange mixture of tephra from the W-K3 and W-K4 events (1740â€“1540 and 1310â€“1170 cal BP respectively), with a yellowish-brown palaeosol (Layer 10). The dark brown topsoil (Layer 11) contains traces of more recent tephra falls.

    3.3. Auwa hamlet/FGT

    The Auwa hamlet site is included for information on plant use from macrofossils. It is situated on a ridge-top about 1 km from Yombon airstrip (Fig. 3). The site was excavated in 1980â€“1981 (Specht et al., 1981) and 1991 (Pavlides, 1993, Pavlides, 1999, Pavlides, 2006). It covers the same period as the upper part of Layer 4 and Layers 5â€“11 in FIF, and has the same tephra sequence (Fig. 5a). Most of the pre-W-K1 sediments have eroded from the ridge-top, and the oldest chert and obsidian artefacts in the basal clay here date to 5290â€“4450 cal BP (Specht et al., 1981, Specht et al., 1983). Evidence from a trench below the ridge-top extends the date of human presence here to 7000â€“6600 cal BP, with extensive evidence for chert quarrying (Pavlides, 1993, Pavlides, 2006).

    3.4. Misisil cave/FHC

    Misisil cave has a floor area of about 300 m2. It was excavated in 1980â€“1981 (Specht et al., 1981, Pain and Specht, 1984/1985), and was revisited in 1992 to recover a column sample for microfossil and other studies. The sediments form two major depositional units. The upper unit (Layers 5â€“8) consists of dense fire ash, charcoal lenses and artefacts interleaved with bands of inwashed clays beginning around 4350â€“3100 cal BP (Table 3, Fig. 5b).     3.5. Alanglongromo/FLF

    Alanglongromo is a small cave near Iumielo village, about 3.5 km west of Kandrian.     4. Materials and methods

    4.1. Microfossil samples, extraction and analytical procedures

    Twenty-six sediment samples were taken from Layers 2 to 11 at FIF/4. Phytoliths and micro-charcoal were extracted from 5 g sub-samples using the standard heavy liquid flotation procedure recommended for clayey sediments (Lentfer and Boyd, 1998). These sub-samples, which contained no macro-charcoal, were lightly ground, deflocculated in 5% Calgon solution, and sieved through a 300 Î¼m mesh. Residues were mounted in benzyl benzoate. Estimates of phytolith abundance were recorded for each sample and a minimum of 100 morphotypes were counted for all samples with the exception of two with very low phytolith yield. All phytolith morphotypes in each field of view examined were included in the counts. Levels of weathering as well as numbers of charcoal particles >5 Î¼m were also recorded (cf Piperno and Becker, 1996). After counting, slides were fully scanned for phytolith morphotypes not previously encountered.

    Seventeen sediment samples were taken from the 1992 column of Misisil. This was removed from the north end of trench I (Pain and Specht, 1984/1985, fig. 2; left of the drip hole), and did not include the unconsolidated materials of Layers 1 and 2 and the lower part of Layer 3. Eleven samples (0.5 g) were taken from the excavation units in square FLF/IB at Alanglongromo. Phytoliths were extracted from the Misisil and Alanglongromo samples by microwave digestion (Parr, 2002). Samples were lightly ground but not sieved prior to extraction. Slides were fully scanned in consecutive fields and the presence of phytolith morphotypes and their levels of abundance were recorded. Presence of micro-charcoal >5 Î¼m was also recorded.

    All phytolith residues were examined under polarized light with an optical transmitted light microscope at 400Ã— and 600Ã— magnification and digital images were taken of all different phytolith morphotypes encountered. Diagnostic morphotypes were identified using modern comparative reference collections (Kealhofer and Piperno, 1998, Lentfer, 2003a, Lentfer, 2009); non-diagnostic phytoliths were given descriptive labels and categorized into morphotype groups. Frequencies are expressed as percentages of total phytoliths counted in each sample. For FIF, micro-charcoal particles were counted, and their frequency is expressed as a ratio of charcoal-to-phytoliths for each sample.

    4.2. Macrofossil remains

    Macroscopic organic remains are scarce at open sites, as they do not survive well in the constantly damp tephra-derived soils. None were available from FIF/2-3-4, and only a few were recovered from the uppermost level of Auwa. The late Holocene contexts at Misisil and the latest levels at Alanglongromo yielded a range of unburnt and carbonised nutshells and fragments. D.E. Yen (formerly Australian National University) identified those from Misisil and Auwa.

    5. Results

    5.1. The open sites: Yombon Airstrip/FIF and Auwa/FGT

    Micro-charcoal particles were present in all samples and phytoliths were abundant to very abundant in all samples except in tephra horizons B, W-K2 and W-K3/W-K4, and the possible tephras A and C. Phytoliths were very abundant in the W-K1 tephra, and very common in D (Fig. 6a and b). We interpret this patterning to confirm the identification of A, C and D as tephras, with D perhaps representing an accretionary phase rather than a separate eruptive event.

    Similar to the FAO site on Garua Island (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007), the preservation of phytoliths was excellent in samples above the W-K1 tephra layer, but in most of the samples below this they were highly weathered. The phytolith record provides evidence for a mixed grass/forest assemblage typical of lowland vegetation, with very distinct changes through time. No montane forest elements were recorded in the entire sequence. Bamboo was present in Layer 2 at the base of the sequence, prior to the LGM, indicating forest with an open canopy that allowed light penetration (Fig. 7a). During this period and until the early Holocene (Layer 4), palms including closed forest Calyptrocalyx species and gingers were significant components of the understorey vegetation (Fig. 7b).

    There is a distinct change in the vegetation sequence in the early Holocene, signalled by the first appearance of light-demanding panicoid grasses in Layer 4. The majority of phytoliths are morphotypes common to Imperata and Saccharum spp. (Fig. 6aâ€“c). Consistent increases in grasses occur higher in the sequence, with a major pulse just before the W-K1 tephra (Layer 5). Following a decline associated with the W-K1 tephra, grasses gradually increase until another, larger pulse immediately before the W-K2 tephra (Layer 7). A similar, but more intensive cycle is repeated after this. Unlike previous tephra layers that show a marked decline in grass composition, however, panicoid grasses feature as a substantial component of the W-K3/W-K4 tephra horizon (Layer 9), signifying sustained disturbance within this deposition period. Subsequently, there is a slight decline in grasses, followed by another significant increase.

    Approximating the panicoid grass sequence is that of elements suggestive of regrowth forest elements, represented particularly by Euphorbiaceae and Zingiberaceae morphotypes. The Euphorbiaceae morphotypes show consistent increases from the early Holocene, including tephra horizons C and D in the lower half of Layer 4, and thereafter following each eruption event from the W-K1 horizon onwards. The initial emergence of Euphorbiaceae immediately above tephra horizon B and before the appearance of panicoid grasses in tephra horizon C shows that the canopy was sufficiently open to encourage growth of pioneer trees, but not open enough for more light-demanding grasses. The Zingiberaceae sequence follows a similar pattern, except in the period immediately prior to the W-K1 eruption (upper Layer 4). Here there is a significant component of grass and ginger morphotypes, but no record for diagnostic Euphorbiaceae morphotypes.

    Micro-charcoal occurs at the base of the FIF sequence. From Layer 4 onwards, each major increase in charcoal was followed by an increase in panicoid grasses and secondary forest elements (Fig. 6b). There are large proportions of closed forest morphotypes directly above the W-K1 and W-K2 tephra horizons, followed by peaks in micro-charcoal. Another peak coincides with the W-K3/W-K4 tephra layer, and again in the topsoil. Closed forest elements, however, dominate the entire upper sequence, albeit with substantial regrowth elements and shade tolerant panicoid grasses Oplismenus sp. and Scrotochloa sp. Further discussion of specific plant groups from phytoliths and macroscopic organic remains is presented in the following sections.

    5.1.1. Grasses

    Phytolith morphotypes found in Saccharum and Imperata species are the major component of the panicoid grass assemblage. These species have similar epidermal short cell (ESC) morphotypes. However, unlike Imperata, large trichomes are very common in Saccharum (Fig. 7c and d). Therefore, ESC morphotypes and trichomes occurring together are a likely indicator of Saccharum, especially where high numbers of trichomes occur relative to ESC morphotypes. Using this criterion, Saccharum has been tentatively identified in several samples, including one in the early Holocene before the W-K1 eruption (Fig. 6c). Other edible grasses identified from diagnostic ESC morphotypes are also present. Coix lachryma-jobi (Jobâ€™s tears), which has large corn-like edible seeds, appears before the W-K1 eruption, and Setaria palmifolia-like ESC morphotypes occur just before the W-K2 eruption. However, since Setaria morphotypes are more difficult to differentiate from the other panicoid grass morphotypes, this identification is not certain.

    5.1.2. Bananas

    Musaceae is represented by seed and leaf morphotypes that are uncommon to rare in the assemblages. Diagnostic seed morphotypes allow the identification of Ensete, Musa acuminata ssp. banksii and Australimusa bananas (Fig. 7eâ€“h). All three taxa first appear with panicoid grasses in the sample from tephra D.

    5.1.3. Palms

    The Caryota rumphiana fishtail palm has distinctive phytoliths that were recovered in FIF/4 from the W-K2 tephra sample and in two more samples above this (Fig. 6a). In addition to Caryota, other globular palm phytolith morphotypes are common throughout the sequence. These forms occur in a range of species, including Licuala spp. and Calamus spp. and, at the small end of the size range, Cocos nucifera and Areca catechu. More detailed morphometric analysis of the assemblages is required before specific identifications can be made (cf Fenwick et al., in preparation).

    5.1.4. Plant macrofossils

    The macrofossil record for the open sites is restricted to late Holocene contexts at Auwa, where Yen tentatively identified Canarium indicum and Aleurites sp., and a nutshell of a Helicia or Myristica species in the latest Holocene levels.

    5.2. The closed sites: Misisil/FHC

    Misisil shows a predominance of closed forest throughout the sequence, with regrowth forest elements present from the base of the column sample, at around the early to middle Holocene boundary (Table 3). Phytoliths from a range of useful plants including grasses, tall herbs and fruit and nut-bearing trees are present. Palms occur throughout. Panicoid grasses first appear in Layer 4, around the time of or soon after the W-K1 eruption and, although they are present throughout the sequence, they are always rare. C. lachryma-jobi occurs only in the latest levels, but morphotypes derived from Canarium sp., Ficus sp., Ulmaceae (cf Celtis sp. among the macro-remains, Section 5.2.2) are present from Layers 5 to 6 onwards. The Canarium phytoliths in Misisil are polyhedral morphotypes found only in leaves and wood, and these are clearly different from the echinate, globular nutshell morphotypes (Lentfer, 2003a).

    5.2.1. Plant macro-remains

    Uncarbonised seed cases of Celtis sp. appeared in the period following the W-K2 eruption (Layer 5) and continued through to the latest level. Carbonised nutshells occur within the last 1500 or so years in Layers 6â€“8. They include C. indicum and Terminalia sp., as well as Aleurites and Pangium edule, which appear in the very latest levels.

    5.3. The closed sites: Alanglongromo/FLF

    The Alanglongromo sequence starts in the middle Holocene between the W-K1 and W-K2 eruptions, slightly before the Misisil record (Table 4). With the exception of the Layer 1, phytoliths are very common to very abundant throughout. Diagnostic phytoliths are rare in Layer 1 and only Musaceae has been tentatively identified. The record is dominated by arboreal elements, but with a substantial component of regrowth elements including Euphorbiaceae, palms and gingers. Grass phytoliths are present in one sample only (Layer 4), and postdate the W-K2 eruption. Arboreal elements dominate the upper part of the sequence after the time of the W-K3/W-K4 eruptions, the presence of which is not confirmed here.

    Similar to Misisil, several useful plant species and families occur in the phytolith record. Musaceae may be present in the middle Holocene (Layer 1). Artocarpus sp. (probably the seeded variety, Artocarpus camansi, commonly called breadnut: Zerega et al., 2004, Zerega et al., 2006) and probably Musaceae occur with palms and dentate-stamped Lapita pottery in Layer 3 (3490â€“3070 cal BP). Ulmaceae phytoliths and small globular echinate phytoliths closely matching C. nucifera (Lentfer, 2003a) are present in the post-pottery period ca 2280â€“1990 cal BP (Layer 4). The first definite evidence for C. nucifera, however, comes from nutshell fragments found in the topsoil of trench II on the coastal plain below the cave. Within the cave, Canarium sp. nutshell was only found in the top level of square FLF/I, but Canarium phytoliths are present in all samples from Layers 2 to 7 (Table 4, Fig. 9).

    6. Discussion

    The present study provides a vegetation sequence covering about 35 000 years from the late Pleistocene through to the recent past. This constitutes the longest record in the lowland region of the New Guinea islands, and is comparable in length to those of the montane regions of mainland New Guinea. Singularly absent from this record is the period of the LGM, perhaps because that time was one of extremely slow deposition. Some loss of sediment from erosion is also possible, as the FIF site is on a slight slope, though this seems less likely as there is no evidence later in the sequence for such a process. Although the vegetation pattern in the LGM cannot be identified from the present analysis, the absence of montane elements from the phytolith record and the high level of continuity between the assemblages from before and after the LGM suggest that the vegetation was relatively stable throughout this period.

    Comparison between the phytolith and micro-charcoal records in relation to the tephra horizons is important for understanding how changes in the vegetation sequence were influenced by human or natural causes. Torrence et al. (2009) provide an interpretation for study localities close to the volcanic centres that caused periodic destruction of parts of northern New Britain during the Holocene. In particular, they noted: â€˜it is quite difficult to extract the signal of human modifications from those of an ecosystem in the process of recovering from a volcanic eruptionâ€™ (Torrence et al., 2009, p. 526). As our study sites are more distant from the epicentres of the catastrophic events discussed by Torrence et al., they provide an opportunity to place the north coast situation in a wider perspective.

    We have noted (Section 5.1) the co-occurrence of micro-charcoal and bamboo in the lowest samples from Layer 2, before the LGM. The provenance of the micro-charcoal cannot be determined as local or extra-local. However, as bamboo requires openings in the forest canopy to allow light penetration, its presence with micro-charcoal suggests that burning within the local area may have contributed to canopy modification. Panicoid grasses, including Imperata and Saccharum spp., did not appear until later in tephra horizon D. This suggests that the opening of the canopy cover during the late Pleistocene was sufficient for bamboo growth, but not for more light-demanding grasses.

    Throughout the sequence tephra falls would have undoubtedly caused degrees of defoliation and other vegetation damage. Modelling of the impact zones of the four W-K eruptions indicates that their effects on the Passismanua area were â€˜moderateâ€™ (W-K1 and W-K2) to â€˜minimumâ€™ (W-K3 and W-K4) (Boyd et al., 1999b, table 1, figs 2â€“5); no data are available for older events. The W-K1 and W-K2 tephras range from 20 cm to 50 cm in thickness, according to location and aspect. Given the distance from the source volcano (90 km), it is unlikely that these tephra falls totally destroyed the forest canopy, which would have recovered relatively quickly from defoliation and minor limb damage if left to regenerate without further disturbance (Boyd et al., 1999b; cf Lentfer and Boyd, 2001). The large proportion of closed forest morphotypes directly above these tephra horizons supports the likelihood of such periods of regeneration. This contrasts with the evidence from Garua Island, where grasses dominate the record immediately after these two eruptions (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007). The faster forest recovery at FIF could indicate periods of abandonment by the human populations following the tephra falls (Table 1). The W-K3 and W-K4 tephras are weakly represented in the study area and are likely to have had only minor impacts on the vegetation.

    Persistent presence of burning throughout the sequence calls for explanation. The possibilities include fires from lightning strikes, especially during periods of reduced precipitation, and burning via human agency. Palaeoclimate proxies for the broader New Guinea region suggest a prevalence of cool, wet conditions in the pre-LGM and shifts towards wetter conditions in the early-middle Holocene (Gagan et al., 2004, Denham and Haberle, 2008, p. 485, fig. 3; Hope, 2009). Therefore, if local conditions were similar across the region, including WNB, the vegetation is likely to have been subjected to less naturally induced fire than expected for drier conditions. This would make an anthropogenic origin during the purported wetter periods more likely, with people using fire to modify the vegetation. Before the LGM burning was not so extensive that it created clearings sufficiently large to encourage light-demanding panicoid grasses, but towards the end of the early Holocene, the increased presence of charcoal suggests that the amount of fire in the landscape increased. This indicates a major shift in land management practices.

    The small scale of impacts during the late Pleistocene could reflect opportunistic promotion of plant growth for subsistence or other activities in patches where natural causes had partly opened the canopy, along the lines suggested for prehistoric hunter-gathers on the New Guinea mainland (Groube, 1989). The larger scale of burning and vegetation disturbance evident from the early Holocene onwards implies more deliberate forest clearance and change in land use, mirroring the evidence from Garua Island (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007). This is supported by the appearance of various plants of subsistence value before the W-K1 tephra, and may reflect the development of managed plant food production well before the W-K2 event and the appearance of Lapita pottery in the New Britain area. Furthermore, there is no marked difference in patterns of forest clearance before and after the W-K2 event. The most marked change occurred much later in the W-K3/W-K4 period of tephra fall. Thereafter, a land use system causing equivalent or lower levels of forest disturbance prevailed throughout the remainder of the late Holocene.

    Although the pattern of increased vegetation manipulation and land use continued after the W-K1 eruption, closed forest elements dominate the upper sequence, albeit with substantial regrowth elements. The presence of the shade tolerant panicoid grasses Oplismenus sp. and Scrotochloa sp. is further testimony to this (Fig. 6c), and suggests that forest clearings were always on a relatively small scale, perhaps forming a mosaic pattern similar to the recent past (Fig. 8).

    The phytolith record includes a range of grasses, palms, gingers, bananas and trees with edible fruit, nuts or leaves that are today cultivated or managed for subsistence or other purposes (Fig. 9; Kennedy and Clark, 2004). Some of these useful plants are present from the start of the sequence, while others appear from the terminal Pleistocene and early Holocene onwards. Bamboos and gingers are present in the basal levels, where they have similar distributions. Their increase through the early Holocene tracks the rise of panicoid grasses and bananas, and could represent planting of selected species, deliberate preservation of bamboo stands or simply natural regrowth in forest clearings.

    The vegetation sequence during the early Holocene implies low intensity land use with small clearings and long periods of recovery. In contrast, the increase in panicoid grasses in later levels, possibly including edible Saccharum, suggests more intensive land use with larger clearings and/or shorter recovery periods favouring the persistence of pioneers including Euphorbiaceae and Urticaceae species. This could also reflect the introduction, deliberate planting and/or selective preservation of some of the useful tree species such as Aleurites moluccana, Macaranga and Codiaeum, although the lack of diagnostic phytoliths for these taxa prevents specific identifications.

    Musaceae appear in an early Holocene context at FIF/4, and possibly in the middle Holocene at Alanglongromo. Whether Musaceae were naturally dispersed or were introduced by people cannot be determined. At FIF there are no physical features such as those found at Kuk Swamp in the New Guinea highlands (Denham et al., 2003, Denham et al., 2004) that might point to deliberate management, though there was a burning episode immediately prior to the first appearance of Musaceae, indicative perhaps of intentional vegetation clearance. In their natural state, wild seeded bananas colonise disturbed areas caused by landslips, forest clearings resulting from storm damage, fires and defoliation by volcanic ash fall (MacDaniels, 1947, Argent, 1976, Lentfer, 2003b). It is possible that seeds were introduced to the Passismanua area in the droppings of birds and bats. However, the absence of the wild genus Heliconia, which has relatively large and easily distinguished diagnostic morphotypes (Lentfer, 2003a, Lentfer, 2009), is notable. Heliconia is a close relative of Musa and Ensete. It grows in similar habitats and commonly occurs in association with bananas and gingers. Its absence could reflect deliberate selection of more favourable Musaceae species for planting and/or the intentional preservation of stands of edible bananas when clearing other vegetation.

    Caryota palm first occurs in the W-K2 tephra. Several species of this genus are suitable for sago production and provide an edible â€˜palm cabbageâ€™ (Ruddle et al., 1978, Table 1, Table 3; Riffle and Craft, 2003, pp. 289â€“292). These include C. rumphiana on New Britain (Powell, 1976, table 3.1). Goodale (1995, pp. 68, 83) reports that in the 1960s the Kaulong, the Sengsengâ€™s neighbours, ate the â€˜roasted trunkâ€™ of a â€˜wild palmâ€™ during food shortages, though it was usually treated as pig fodder. She describes this palm in Tok Pisin as limbum, a term referring to a species of Caryota known as â€˜black palmâ€™. The wood is hard, and in recent times has been used for spear tips, bows and house construction (Powell, 1976, pp. 154, 160, tables 3.15, 3.17). Yen (1990, p. 260) has suggested that Caryota was introduced to the New Guinea region as a cultivar from the west, where it occurs in cultivated or managed stands from eastern India to island southeast Asia (Ruddle et al., 1978, p. 7, table 1). If Yen is correct, then the evidence from Garua Island and FIF, and also Watom Island (Lentfer and Green, 2004, table 2), places this at the time of Lapita pottery, though we cannot discount dispersal into the New Britain area by non-human agents such as birds and bats, that eat Caryota fruit.

    Of the other palms, the natural distributions of Licuala and Calamus and their prevalence in the region make it likely that they were always present at FIF as wild species, their growth possibly encouraged by forest clearance. Intentional cultivation of either species is unlikely. Confirmation of the presence of C. nucifera and A. catechu at FIF/4 would be of particular interest as they would be outside their natural range, and this would point to their deliberate introduction. C. nucifera has a long history in the New Guinea islands, where it occurs as macro-remains from about 10 000â€“7500 cal BP onwards, though an earlier presence is possible (Specht, 2005, p. 266). At the coastal Alanglongromo site, it appears at 2300â€“2000 cal BP.

    Among other trees with edible fruits or seeds, Celtis sp. (Ulmaceae) and Canarium sp. (Burseraceae) are extremely rare in the phytolith record at FIF, where their only occurrence is in the palaeosols on the D and W-K2 tephras; they are totally absent from later contexts. This absence contrasts with the phytolith record and macro-remains at Misisil, which show that Celtis sp. and Canarium sp. were both common from about 2000 BP onwards.

    Unburnt seed cases of Celtis are common finds in late Pleistocene and early Holocene levels of cave sites in the New Guinea region (summarised in Specht, 2005, p. 266, footnote 7), but they are absent from open site assemblages of probable or definite human origin (Yen, in Kirch, 1989, Swadling et al., 1991, Matthews and Gosden, 1997). Latinis (2000, p. 51) treats Celtis as a potential human dietary item, but its presence inside Misisil in an uncarbonised state may indicate that it was introduced by bats rather than by humans.

    The presence of Canarium nutshells and phytoliths at Misisil implies that they were derived from the same source. The phytolith morphotypes are derived from leaves and wood, and it is likely that nuts were harvested by breaking off fruit-laden branches, and these were taken into the cave. This method of harvest is common practice today in the region. In 1979â€“1981 a C. indicum tree grew near the entrance to Misisil and was probably the source of some macro-remains recovered from recent levels. Canarium exploitation increased substantially after the W-K2 eruption, and similar increases are indicated for Celtis, Ficus, Terminalia, palms and bamboos. However, it was only within the last 1000 years or so that another suite of plants including Aleurites sp. (probably A. moluccana), P. edule and C. lachryma-jobi appears at the site for the first time (Table 3, Fig. 9).

    The occurrence of phytoliths from other plants with arboricultural and horticultural value is sporadic and not helpful for tracing the history of plant management in this region. Furthermore, many useful plants, including staples such as taros, yams, pandanus and a range of edible ferns, do not produce diagnostic morphotypes and cannot be detected in the phytolith record (Lentfer, 2003a). Nonetheless, the records clearly show that several plant families with good potential for fruit and nut production as well as leafy greens were present, and these would have provided a healthy and varied foraging resource, and possibly a basis from which to select potential cultivars.

    The presence of Helicia or Myristica sp. in the topsoil of Auwa reminds us that not all plant remains had sustenance value. Peekel (1984, p. 158) records that in recent times Helicia atramenti has been used as a tattooing dye in New Britain. While there is no evidence in the Passismanua or Alanglongromo lithic assemblages for tattooing implements, tools possibly used for this purpose have been reported from middle and late Holocene contexts on Garua Island (Kononenko and Torrence, 2009).

    7. Trends and implications

    7.1. New Britain

    The broad trends presented here are similar to those on Garua Island and Willaumez Peninsula on the north side of New Britain (Boyd et al., 2005, Lentfer and Torrence, 2007, Parr et al., 2009). There, the impacts of volcanic events recorded in the Passismanua sequence were somewhat more severe, though the overall increase in vegetation disturbance through time is seen as a reflection of human activity. The interpretation of the Passismanua data follows a similar pattern, but greatly extends the time depth. The micro-charcoal record from pre-LGM contexts indicates burning activity from the time of earliest human presence. There is no reason to reject human activity as the cause of burning, possibly in the course of creating or maintaining gaps in the rainforest canopy caused initially by tephra falls or other natural agencies. Thereafter, burning continued during the early Holocene with the appearance of several potential cultivar taxa, including both the Australimusa and Eumusa sections of Musaceae, possibly Saccharum sp., Canarium sp., and several other nut and fruit trees of subsistence value. This apparent coincidence of forest clearance, burning and appearance of potential cultivars may constitute the earliest evidence for organised plant food production in New Britain, thus extending the Garua Island evidence beyond the middle Holocene.

    As plant macrofossil remains survive poorly in the acidic soils of the region, the phytolith record at FIF extends the presence of Canarium beyond the ca 2000-year record of macro-remains at Misisil back to the end of the early Holocene, which aligns with dates for unidentified â€˜nutshellâ€™ samples from Garua Island and Willaumez Peninsula (Petrie and Torrence, 2008, p. 732). Elsewhere in the New Guinea region Canarium has a history of human use extending into the Pleistocene (Yen, 1995, Yen, 1996). On the other hand, macrofossils in late Holocene levels of Misisil and Auwa provide evidence for several taxa (e.g., Aleurites sp., P. edule, Terminalia sp., Helicia sp. or Myristica sp.) that cannot be positively identified in the microfossil record due to the lack of diagnostic phytoliths. This suite of plants seems to occur for the first time in the late Holocene and might signify extension of the subsistence base, possibly within the last 1000 or so years following the W-K4 eruption. Interestingly, the current data suggest that the degree of change at this time was greater than that following the earlier, more severe W-K2 eruption, when Caryota was possibly the only new plant introduction, along with the first appearance of Lapita pottery at coastal sites.

    In addition to the preservation of macro-remains, Misisil cave shows a slightly different picture of human activity from that of the open site FIF, and this emphasises the importance of understanding the recovery contexts of microfossil samples. The predominance of arboreal elements and scarcity of panicoid grasses in the phytolith assemblages of Misisil show persistence of closed rainforest vegetation around the cave, which is situated in a closed valley and steep ridge environment. The steep slopes in the immediate vicinity of the cave preclude extensive clearance of forest for either gardens or house sites. At both Misisil and Alanglongromo the phytolith record is the product of both natural processes through inwash of sediments and periodic human activity, during which people introduced to the caves plant foods, firewood (expressed mainly as charcoal), and wood, bamboo and other materials for construction of beds and perhaps craft items. This contrasts with the FIF open site, which is on a gently sloping ridge within the dissected plateau system that is more suitable for the establishment of permanent houses and other structures, and the pursuit of domestic activities. The FIF phytolith evidence shows sustained vegetation clearance and regrowth, with more intensive land use through time, perhaps resulting from clearance of larger living spaces, cultivation of more or larger garden plots and/or a reduction in fallow length. The question arises whether this change in land use intensity reflects an increase in the human population of the area or merely more intensive use of this particular location.

    The spike in panicoid grasses and micro-charcoal in Layer 9 at FIF/4, the period of the W-K3 to W-K4 eruptions, contrasts with the record from the north side of New Britain, where forest regrowth elements dominated at that time. The FIF situation might be explained by one of several possible mechanisms, such as population growth, or a forcing effect resulting from volcanic activity that caused population displacement from the north side of the island into southern refuge zones (cf Boyd et al., 1999b, Torrence and Doelman, 2007). Allowing for a very low population density at the time, possibly similar to that of the 1960s at about one per/km2 (Section 2.3), even a small influx of people into the fringes of the Passismanua area might have had a significant impact on land use practices.

    Analyses of lithic assemblages from the Passismanua area, Garua Island and the Willaumez Peninsula suggest changes in mobility, land use and subsistence practices through the early and middle Holocene (Torrence, 1992, Pavlides, 1999, Pavlides, 2006, Torrence et al., 2000). In both areas, these periods are characterised by the production of a variety of notched, stemmed and/or waisted tools that required complex production sequences (Torrence, 1992, Pavlides, 1999). Through the middle Holocene there were shifts towards less retouch and the production of expedient tools considered to reflect a reduction in mobility related to intensification of land use practices (Torrence, 1992, Pavlides, 2006). On Garua Island and Willaumez Peninsula, changes in artefact discard patterns between the middle and late Holocene, from widespread and relatively even distribution across the landscape to more spatially concentrated discard in fewer localities, probably signify the reduction in settlement mobility (Torrence, 2002, Torrence, 2008).

    This shift was underway on Garua Island with the re-occupation of the area after the W-K1 eruption, when vegetation clearance and burning practices had significant impacts on forest cover, presumably signalling forms of food plant production. The scale of landscape modification following the W-K2 eruption and the appearance of Lapita pottery was â€˜not significantly different to the previous patternâ€™, though there was a â€˜quantum shift in the degree of human interference through clearance and burningâ€™ following the later Dk eruption (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007, p. 102). In the periods after the W-K1 and Dk eruptions there were significant burning episodes and peaks of grasses that are likely to have been anthropogenic in origin, rather than related to natural causes; this is reinforced by the peaks in artefact quantities at these times (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007, p. 100).

    The Passismanua results indicate impacts on the vegetation through clearance and burning from the early Holocene onwards, somewhat earlier than indicated by the interpretation of lithic assemblages, but probably equivalent to the â€˜moderateâ€™ burning events and increase of grasses noted in the undated Layers 2 and 4 at FAO on Garua Island (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007, p. 100). The Passismanua lithic evidence reflects changes linked to reduced residential mobility, and shifts in land use and subsistence activities associated with plant food production (Pavlides, 1999, Pavlides, 2006).

    7.2. The broader region: New Guinea and island southeast Asia

    A significant outcome of the FIF analyses is the recognition of consistent burning and opening of the forest canopy from at least 33 600 Â± 670 bp, during a cool period of relatively high precipitation before the onset of the LGM (Gagan et al., 2004). This temporal context fits comfortably with biomass burning patterns within the broader region of Sahul and island southeast Asia, which are thought to be influenced by human foraging and hunting activities (Haberle et al., 2001, Kershaw et al., 2003, Thevenon et al., 2004, Hunt et al., 2007; see summary by Denham et al., 2009). Furthermore, there are marked similarities between FIF and the montane region of New Guinea at Kosipe in the Owen Stanley Ranges (Fairbairn et al., 2006, Hope, 2009) and further west in the highlands of Papua New Guinea (Haberle, 1998), and the Baliem Valley of the Indonesian Province of Papua (Hope, 1998) where there were sharp increases in burning after ca 35 000 bp. These are ascribed in part, if not largely, to human activities (Haberle, 1998, Hope, 2009). The combined evidence, therefore, suggests that Pleistocene foragers quickly and successfully adapted to a range of different environments in the New Guinea region, from cold montane habitats to tropical lowland rainforests. Paralleling earlier evidence from Niah Cave in Kalimantan (Hunt et al., 2007, p. 1967), biomass burning might have been employed widely to cope with this range of different environments, creating, intentionally or unintentionally, new ecotones with increased diversity and enriched foraging resources.

    The early Holocene trends in burning, vegetation disturbance patterns and appearance of potential cultivars witnessed in New Britain may mirror regional changes in vegetation management, land use and settlement patterns on the New Guinea mainland (Denham and Haberle, 2008). Musa species appear at FIF in the early Holocene, at about the same time as Kuk in the New Guinea highlands, where there is substantial evidence favouring intentional cultivation (Denham et al., 2003, Denham et al., 2004). This timing is in keeping with arguments for the lowland derivation of the deliberate selection and management of Musa spp. and crops such as taro, and their later transfer into the New Guinea highlands (Yen, 1995, Hope and Golson, 1995, Denham et al., 2003, Denham and Haberle, 2008). The plausibility of the development of cultivation practices in the coastal and lowland regions of New Guinea and its islands earlier than in the Highlands is strengthened by the presence on New Britain of a suite of potential cultivars and evidence for vegetation disturbance. Certainly, lowland cultivation of Musa at this time bides well with evidence for the dispersal of Eumusa section bananas from coastal regions in New Guinea across southeast Asia and into Africa by the middle Holocene (Lejju et al., 2006, Denham and Donohue, 2009). It also fits comfortably with the time frame required for the development and dispersal of banana cultivars within the New Guinea archipelago, and the later dispersal of possibly fully domesticated bananas into the southeast Solomon Islands (Lentfer, Crowther, and Green, work in progress.) and Vanuatu (Horrocks et al., 2009) by ca 3000 BP.

    The intensification in burning and forest clearance at FIF during the middle and late Holocene parallels the evidence from the Wahgi Valley in the Western Highlands Province of New Guinea (Powell, 1982, Denham et al., 2003, Denham et al., 2004, Denham and Haberle, 2008, fig. 4), and from several other montane and lowland locations in New Guinea and island southeast Asia (Haberle et al., 2001, pp. 261â€“263). This widespread pattern of burning has been attributed, in part, to climatic variability due to high ENSO frequency (Haberle et al., 2001), and in the Passismanua area this may have been exacerbated by vegetation disturbance from tephra falls. In both the Passismanua and Wahgi Valley areas, however, the intensified patterns of burning appear to correlate with archaeological indicators for regional shifts towards systematic plant management and reduced settlement mobility that suggest widespread and increasing dependence on plant food production (Denham and Haberle, 2008). In New Britain, this preceded the W-K2 eruption and the appearance of Lapita pottery at ca 3350 cal BP (Lentfer and Torrence, 2007). The palaeoenvironmental records show no major shift indicating a sudden increase in intensification after this time. The only change in possible cultivars is the appearance of Caryota in post-W-K2 tephra contexts. This suggests an overriding continuity across the W-K2 boundary of plant management practices, which were not substantially influenced by new technologies or subsistence strategies associated with the introduction of Lapita pottery.

    8. Conclusions

    The New Britain evidence parallels region-wide trends in palaeoenvironmental impacts associated with human settlement and subsistence behaviour. For the Pleistocene prior to the LGM, it adds to a small but growing body of data linking human presence with increased levels of vegetation disturbance. Most significantly, it provides supportive, although still tentative, evidence for biomass burning being a widespread strategy adopted by Pleistocene foragers to cope with different environments including wet-tropical rainforests in both montane and lowland contexts on New Guinea and New Britain, and more broadly across island southeast Asia. The reasons underlying this burning could be the development of plant food production which intensified from the early Holocene, in the highlands of New Guinea as well as the lowlands, where such a shift is much earlier than otherwise accepted (Bellwood, 2005).

    The localised nature of phytolith and micro-charcoal records and the lack of comparative data from the LGM and the period before human occupation call for further investigations in the Passismanua and surrounding regions to determine spatial and temporal variability across the landscape, and to provide a broader framework for our interpretations. Since the study found no evidence linking environmental change to climatic influences, additional studies including pollen records and other climate proxies are needed to extend the understanding of the impacts of regional climate fluctuation on lowland rainforests in this tropical island environment. These will contribute to the broader regional perspective on vegetation and climatic histories, and the history of human activities and their impacts on equatorial environments.

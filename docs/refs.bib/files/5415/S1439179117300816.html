    Basic and Applied Ecology

    PERSPECTIVES
    Bridging science and practice in conservation: Deficits and challenges from a research perspective

    Abstract

    Biodiversity is being lost at alarming rates in spite of efforts to conserve ecosystems, species and genetic diversity. There is broad consensus that biodiversity conservation needs to become more effective, and this requires an analysis of the causes that have prevented previous efforts from reaching their goal. A lack of knowledge exchange and cooperation between science and practice has been identified as a key issue in this context. Scientific knowledge frequently does not find its way into conservation practice, and information requirements by practitioners are often not considered appropriately by scientists. Here, we summarize deficits and challenges in the relationship between science and practice in conservation and outline approaches to achieving more effective knowledge exchange and collaboration at the interface between both. We propose that existing platforms for communication need to be complemented by independent institutions in particular at national and sub-national levels to facilitate successful co-production of knowledge as a prerequisite for effective conservation measures.

    Rationale

    Recent biodiversity assessments have documented an ongoing decline in population sizes of most wild species and accelerating rates of local extinctions as well as losses in the extent, quality and connectivity of many habitats (Butchart et al. 2010; WWF Living Planet Report 2016). Ethical reasons and utilitarian considerations related to the role of biodiversity in providing ecosystem services have been put forward to argue that further loss of biodiversity should be prevented (Hooper et al. 2012).

    Numerous efforts such as the Biodiversity Convention Countdown Target have aimed at significantly reducing the rate of biodiversity loss by 2010 (Rands et al. 2010). Despite some local successes and increasing societal responses to global change drivers, this target was not achieved (Global Biodiversity Outlook 4; www.cbd.int/GBO4). The conclusions drawn from these observations are that political, economic and societal interests interfere with internationally agreed goals of biodiversity conservation (e.g. Perrings et al. 2011; Pouzols et al. 2014), increasing the risk that e.g. the Aichi Targets may not be met by 2020 unless progress can be significantly sped up (Tittensor et al. 2014).

    A lack of exchange between science and practice in conservation has been identified as a key issue that hampers the effectiveness of measures taken to conserve and restore biodiversity (e.g. Fazey et al. 2013; Laurance et al., 2012, Young et al., 2014). Different definitions have been put forward to clarify the types of relevant knowledge in a biodiversity conservation context (see Fazey et al. 2013), sometimes including the distinction between “knowledge producers” and “knowledge users” (Mitton, Adair, McKenzie, Patten, & Perry 2007), although these roles become less pronounced in transdisciplinary approaches (Tomich et al. 2010). Insufficient exchange of relevant knowledge has been observed between academics and practitioners in the field of conservation as well as between the groups with a traditionally strong interest in biodiversity issues (ecologists in academic institutions, administrative offices and environmental NGOs) and parts of the society that do not share the same level of concern for biodiversity (stakeholders who tend to focus more on opportunities for exploiting natural resources; Cvitanovic et al. 2015).

    The aim of this ‘perspectives’ article is to outline how knowledge exchange and collaboration across the divide between science and practice in conservation can be improved in order to make joint efforts for the conservation of biodiversity more successful. We summarize key deficits and challenges that have been identified in the relationship between conservation science and practice (see also Box 1) before moving on to the discussion of possible solutions. We argue that new institutions are needed to enable different stakeholder groups to interact successfully, ensuring that research can generate the knowledge which is necessary to implement measures that have a high probability of delivering conservation goals. The views presented here are primarily based on a research perspective.

    Box 1

    Science and its implementation in conservation.

    Conservation science comprises a continuum from basic to applied research (Simberloff 1988) and from a researcher’s perspective all approaches are valid. From a societal point of view, however, there may be disparities in the evaluation of these approaches given that those approaches with direct benefits for human well-being may be seen most valuable such as applied research on ecosystem services. While the potential for implementation of basic research may frequently not be immediately apparent, the combination of basic and applied approaches often enhances scientific progress. In particular, many findings from basic research have been vital for the technological progress of society.

    Measures and actions that ensure the unconfined knowledge exchange is a relevant part of the practical implementation process in conservation management. Successful knowledge exchange requires continuous dialogue between scientists, decision-makers and practitioners in nature conservation management in order to develop assessment reports, handbooks and guidelines, as well as incorporating such recommendations into legal regulations and practical action.

    Deficits and challenges

    Currently, most conservation activities are not based on the full range of evidence available but rather on expert knowledge, individual experience and traditional land management practices (Pullin, Knight, Stone, & Charman 2004; Cvitanovic et al., 2015, Russell-Smith et al., 2015; Toomey, Knight, & Barlow 2016). The need for integrating scientific evidence concerning biodiversity and its role for ecosystem functioning (Hooper et al. 2012) into decision-making processes is increasingly recognized (reviewed in Fazey et al. 2013; Cvitanovic et al. 2015). Conservation scientists and practitioners alike are striving to develop sustainable forms of biodiversity conservation and ecosystem management (Millar, Stephenson, & Stephens 2007) and often have similar research interests (Cvitanovic et al. 2013). Still, numerous studies have identified barriers that prevent knowledge exchange between science and practice (Fleishman et al. 1999; Knight et al., 2008, Arlettaz et al., 2010; Braunisch, Home, Pellet, & Arlettaz 2012; Laurance et al. 2012; Turnhout, Bloomfield, Hulme, Vogel, & Wynne 2012; Cvitanovic, McDonald, & Hobday 2016).

    Among others, cultural differences between conservation scientists and practitioners play a key role for this lack of exchange between “knowledge producers” and “knowledge users” and science-based decision-making (Roux, Rogers, Biggs, Ashton, & Sergeant 2006; Young et al., 2014, Cvitanovic et al., 2015). For instance, the aims of conservation science are to increase knowledge and to carry out innovative, well-designed studies to obtain generalizable results. This is often driven by scientists’ intrinsic curiosity, previous achievements or availability of funding. The number of publications in peer-reviewed journals and success in acquiring third party funding determine scientific merit and the overall recognition by the scientific community as well as public perception. Many scientific questions therefore deal with conceptual questions and do not match the needs of practitioners, as they do not aim at implementation (Sutherland, Pullin, Dolman, & Knight 2004; Knight et al. 2008). Furthermore, dissemination activities are not always carried out effectively even though they are a requirement for conservation-oriented research projects.

    The needs of conservation practice, however, comprise trustworthy and reliable contacts, information that is easy to access and understand such as handbooks, guidelines or internet-based decision tools, and ad hoc and local solutions in real-world conservation contexts (Fleishman et al., 1999, Laurance et al., 2012). For many conservation topics, scientific knowledge alone is not sufficient; instead practical and local knowledge is needed to provide solutions for stakeholder-specific problems (Tengö, Malmer, Brodizio, Elmqvist, & Spierenburg 2013). Consequently, to provide rapid management solutions, practitioners often use specific information that supports particular political, economic and social issues rather than using all available evidence.

    These rapid and often stakeholder-specific solutions frequently prevent considering successful foreign experiences or management adaptations that could improve the implementation. Moreover, personal perceptions can undermine effective knowledge exchange given that information is interpreted inherently personal (e.g. Fazey, Fazey, Salisbury, Lindenmayer, & Dovers 2006;Cvitanovic et al. 2015). This can be particularly problematic when practitioners are dealing with highly popularized conservation issues such as new forest management approaches for which preconceived perceptions may prevent evidence-based conservation management (e.g. Vítková, Ní Dhubáin, & Upton 2014; Schall et al. 2017). For instance, despite weak evidence for positive effects of fine-grained forest management on biodiversity (e.g. Angers, Messier, Beaudet, & Leduc 2005; Schall et al. 2017), all over temperate Europe, practitioners favour fine-grained over more traditional coarse-grained management assuming that local heterogeneity enhances biodiversity (Pro Silva 2012: https://prosilvaeurope.wordpress.com/prosilva-forestry-principles-2012). Recent findings show, however, that for forest biodiversity a mosaic of different management regimes (Mönkkönen et al. 2014) and age-classes at the landscape scale is more important than local heterogeneity (Schall et al. 2017).

    These cultural differences between conservation scientists and practitioners often result in restricted interactions between them (Laurance et al. 2012). Scientists may even be concerned that engaging with practitioners may be detrimental for their scientific reputation and thus prefer not to initiate a science-practice dialogue (Young et al. 2014). Furthermore, low academic recognition for scientists involved in implementation-oriented projects by universities or research institutions, insufficient time to conduct engagement activities or the lack of financial support for joint projects between conservation science and practice definitely limit interactions between them (Cvitanovic et al. 2015). In addition, there is currently no academic credit for contributions to actual implementation as well as only limited credit for scientific articles in languages other than English (Chapron and Arlettaz, 2008, Arlettaz et al., 2010, Cvitanovic et al., 2016).

    Practitioners, on the other hand, usually have only limited access to the international scientific literature due to financial constraints. Furthermore do language barriers and scientific vocabulary make it difficult to extract relevant scientific findings for conservation activities from this literature (Nooteboom 2001; Fazey, Fischer, & Lindenmayer 2005; Amano, Gonzalez-Varo, & Sutherland 2016; Böcher & Krott 2016). Additionally, for many practitioners it is very challenging to gain a complete picture of the current scientific knowledge in relevant fields as results are often published in small pieces and widely spread in different international journals and may even be contradicting in some cases. Also the chance of a successful application of research findings may be difficult to predict because the necessary research quality can be cumbersome to assess even for scientists (e.g. see Yoccoz, Nichols, & Boulinier 2001 and Legg & Nagy 2006 for potential problems of monitoring studies, especially regarding the design and its impact on the drawn conclusions). A targeted summary, focusing on the needs of the practitioners would be very helpful in this regard but, in most cases, is not available. As a consequence, evidence-based conservation research does not reach all relevant stakeholders and only a negligible proportion of relevant results find their way into practice (Pullin et al., 2004, Sutherland et al., 2004).

    Another aspect hindering knowledge exchange is that the majority of funding schemes only finances short-duration scientific projects and thereby does not include implementation and evaluation of conservation practices. Notable exceptions are e.g. the EU LIFE project-scheme and the German Federal Program for the implementation of the National Strategy on Biological Diversity. On the one hand, short-term projects often do not allow studying the outcomes of specific landscape interventions as the affected ecological processes and responses can be too slow. On the other hand, funding for conservation management often does not include research on the effectiveness or efficiency of the measures taken. The potential to derive scientific knowledge from the success or failure of conservation and restoration projects therefore remains insufficiently utilized. For instance, selected umbrella or flagship species often fail to serve as suitable conservation surrogates for other organisms as the selection of surrogate species or indicators remains largely untested (Andelman & Fagan 2000). Similarly, studies that tested surrogate indicators such as bird diversity for regional biodiversity mostly found limited utility (Andelman & Fagan 2000; Wolters, Bengtsson, & Zaitsev 2006; Billeter et al. 2008).

    While Cvitanovic et al. (2015) provide an extensive review of all these deficits and challenges that prevent knowledge exchange and science-based decision-making, an integrating environment to stimulate interactions between “knowledge producers” and “knowledge users” is still lacking.

    Perspectives

    To improve knowledge exchange and cooperation between conservation science and practice, many authors have suggested systematic literature compilation (Fazey, Salisbury, Lindenmayer, Maindonald, & Douglas 2004; Sutherland et al. 2004). As not all research findings will be practice-relevant, a first step in knowledge exchange can be realized through target-group oriented processing and presenting of practice-relevant information in handbooks, guidelines, e-bulletins or web sites for evidence-based conservation (Sutherland et al. 2004). Still, results of conservation science that require statutory invention are often difficult to integrate into conservation management. For instance, a study on water management schemes has revealed that small-scale local restoration such as riparian buffer strips may fail to improve benthic invertebrate assemblages because of intensive upstream land use (Leps, Tonkin, Dahm, Haase, & Sundermann 2015). The authors therefore recommend that stream managers need to adapt their conservation practice to the adequate spatial scales to effectively protect and restore running waters requiring changes in land use practices (Leps et al. 2015). Such changes in land use, however, cannot be achieved through conservation measures but concern the agricultural sector. To this end these changes may even require adjustments of statutory regulations in order to improve overall land use practices emphasizing the significance of science-policy interactions.

    The likelihood of implementing scientific findings increases if knowledge is gained in participatory approaches (e.g. Fleishman et al., 1999, Cash et al., 2003). A suitable tool for successful cooperation between conservation science and practice may be so-called Transfer or Testing and Development Projects. Such projects transfer existing knowledge to the practitioners as well as help bring out information on the most urgent questions of practitioners (also see Durham, Baker, Smith, Moore, & Morgan 2014), which may also serve as a starting point for further research. In such a scheme, funding agencies demand an adequate interaction between scientists and practitioners. This ensures both that conservation science is implementation-orientated and that the relevant results find their way into practice. Owing to active participation of practitioners in research projects from the design to the analysis, practitioners develop a strong understanding of the findings and identification with the research, which improves the chance of implementation (Cvitanovic et al. 2015). Consequently, co-production of knowledge between science and practice should be expanded in funding schemes to cover both, knowledge exchange as well as its implementation. For instance, several cooperation projects on preventing the spread of invasive species have developed successful management and prevention programs (see examples in Box 2). Certainly, the implementation and success of such cooperation projects should be monitored. Therefore, funding schemes should include flexible funding pools for the evaluation of implemented conservation measures. However, even the results of successful transdisciplinarily designed studies often remain of local importance, recognized by the local stakeholders but not transferred to comparable conditions elsewhere.

    Box 2

    Conservation management of invasive species.

    1) Black cherry (Prunus serotina Ehrh.) is native to eastern North America and was introduced to Europe for producing high value timber, for improving the quality of nutrient-poor soils, and for fire prevention (Annighöfer et al. 2015). However, the species spreads rapidly and in many cases control measures were unsuccessful. For example in an Italian biosphere reserve, where P. serotina had been suppressing the regeneration of native tree species, different measures to control the invasive species, applied by the local park management, failed. In a German-Italian research project, in cooperation with the biosphere reserve, various treatments to control P. serotina were tested. The project resulted in a management recommendation that combined planting of native shade-tolerant tree species under P. serotina and only felling dominant P. serotina trees (diameter at breast height >20 cm). Each step of the project, i.e. study design, treatments, preliminary results etc., was discussed with the park management and the final findings were disseminated to park rangers and neighbouring woodland owners. Scientific and regional papers were published and served to transfer knowledge to both the scientific community as well as the practitioners (e.g., Annighöfer et al., 2012, Terwei et al., 2013, Kawaletz et al., 2014, Terwei et al., 2015).

    2) Garden waste deposits in natural and semi-natural habitats including forests are a source for the spread of invasive species. In Switzerland, legislation does not allow any dumping of garden waste in public forests and along streams and rivers. Yet, illegal garden waste deposits can frequently be observed in public forests of highly populated areas. A study conducted in North-western Switzerland demonstrated that numerous non-native horticultural plant species escape from dumping sites and colonize forests (Rusterholz, Wirz, & Baur 2012). The findings indicated an urgent need to increase awareness of garden owners concerning problems associated with illegal garden waste dumping. The implementation of these research findings comprises an improved organisation of public garden waste disposal by local communities. In most villages, garden waste is nowadays collected for a moderate fee in the quarter streets at intervals of 2–4 weeks. Furthermore, information on the potential risk of garden waste disposal in natural habitats is provided to the people at regular intervals. Finally, the foresters remove any newly established waste deposits and growing ornamental plants in their forests several times per year.

    Despite an increasing number of joint projects, the problem of insufficient knowledge exchange remains acute. This has been largely attributed to unequal cooperation between partners from science and practice (Fazey et al. 2013). For instance, some projects fail to develop a strategy for implementation because conservation scientists were merely requested as regulatory formalities (Fleishman et al. 1999). Similarly, conservation ecological research sometimes serves to green-wash projects with mainly economic targets (Sauvé, Bernard, & Solan 2016). The fact that research projects are often not jointly developed calls for a dialogue to adjust applied research questions to conservation practice (Laurence et al. 2012; Kruess, Riecken, & Sukopp 2016) and vice versa. This could be achieved through the establishment of forums for efficient communication between science and practice (for a model which still can be improved see the Biodiversity Information Service “IBS” (e-newsletter) and the magazine “Hotspot” produced by the Swiss Biodiversity Forum; www.biodiversity.ch).

    A feasible approach to improve effective communication between science and practice is the current development of platforms for synthesizing and disseminating scientific information. These platforms aim to make relevant information available to practitioners in various decision-making contexts. Some platforms also collect information requirements of decision-makers to communicate them to the scientific community. In the case of the International Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services, information requests by policy-makers are even a prerequisite for assessments to be initiated (Larigauderie & Mooney 2010). The integrated content, which relies on contributions from different stakeholders, should then be made publicly available or disseminated to target audiences. Traditional distinctions between scientists and practitioners become less pronounced in this process, because practitioners take an active role in defining research questions and gathering data to answer them (for a model see the Mistra Council for Evidence-based Environmental Management in Sweden; www.eviem.se). At the same time, scientists become more involved in supporting decision-making concerning practical management issues. At international levels, first successful linkages between scientists and practitioners have been demonstrated in the context of the Convention on Biological Diversity and the Stakeholder Engagement by the Intergovernmental Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (Petersen and Huntley, 2005, Hotes and Opgenoorth, 2014). For instance, current developments concerning the uptake and use of findings of the first IPBES assessments by policy-makers and administrators provide some positive outlook (Opgenoorth & Hotes 2016).

    The use of knowledge brokers has been proposed as another approach to improve equal collaboration and knowledge exchange between partners from science and practice (Cvitanovic et al. 2015). Knowledge brokers, often embedded within research teams or institutions, usually act as intermediaries that develop networks among and between “knowledge producers” and “knowledge users”, thereby ultimately facilitating the exchange of knowledge between partners from different fields such as science and practice (Michaels, 2009, Meyer, 2010). The implementation of knowledge brokers has the potential to improve evidence-based decision-making in conservation by giving consideration to the full range of available evidence for conservation activities (Dobbins et al. 2009). Furthermore, these brokered networks between scientist and practitioners have been shown to improve the spread of knowledge and information (Crona & Bodin 2009). These new networks are assumed to build long-lasting bridges between science and practice (Dobbins et al. 2009), which potentially promote academic credit for engagement activities of scientists in actual implementation (Young et al. 2014). They also relieve scientists from a task which they are not trained for and which they may be reluctant to do.

    A further approach to improve collaboration and knowledge exchange between science and practice is to develop an independent boundary institution that oversees the dissemination of information and the fostering of mutual understanding between different stakeholders (Cook, Mascia, Schwartz, Possingham, & Fuller 2013; Cvitanovic et al. 2015). In contrast to synthesizing platforms and knowledge brokers, boundary institutions are established as separate entities to guarantee the representation of different stakeholders, i.e. science and practice, as well as credibility, i.e. the accuracy, quality and validity of knowledge, through independence (Guston, 2001, Cvitanovic et al., 2015). In addition to their credibility, independent boundary institutions unite different groups of stakeholders and thus disseminate relevant information for decision makers (Cash et al. 2003). Furthermore, the spread of knowledge must be considered legitimate, i.e. transparent, free of any bias and with a balance of perspectives, for the success and impact of boundary institutions (Cash et al., 2003, Cvitanovic et al., 2015). Such a boundary institution will in particular have to achieve a more effective linkage between existing synthesizing platforms and knowledge brokers, and they will have to consider and integrate knowledge across sectors (Hulme, Mahony, Beck, Goerg, & Hansjuergens 2011). To achieve this, boundary institutions need to promote interaction with scientists and practitioners, providing simultaneously information that the respective other group offers. This exchange needs to be enhanced particularly at the local scale where biodiversity is affected by decisions taken in specific sectors such as agriculture, forestry, fisheries and urban planning; on this scale boundary institutions prove to be particularly effective (Osmond et al. 2010). Nevertheless, supervision and quality control should regularly target brokers and boundary institutions alike and periodic assessments should provide options to respond to conservational and societal needs.

    Conclusions

    There is a strong need to account for the specific requirements rising from everyday work in conservation while formulating research objectives in conservation science. Frequently, however, knowledge exchange between conservation science and practice is difficult, as they may not be corresponding to current knowledge needs or societal issues. Knowledge co-production, i.e. collaborative identification of relevant questions by scientists and practitioners, should be a requirement for projects aiming at improvement in conservation. Existing platforms for communication need to be complemented by an independent boundary institution that enables better structured interactions to improve the knowledge exchange between science and practice in an integrative approach.

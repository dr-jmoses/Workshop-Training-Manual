    Abstract

    Terrestrial Laser Scanning (TLS) is an active technology that can acquire the finest characteristics of canopy structure and plays an increasing role in estimating Leaf Area Index (LAI) in forest canopies. However, 3D information is not directly used in conventional TLS-based methods using the gap fraction theory. In addition, quantifying clumping effect within canopies is still a difficult task. In this paper, we presented a method to reduce clumping effect and estimate LAI using TLS data. Our recently proposed path length distribution model was applied to TLS data. Instead of converting 3D points to 2D image, the path length distribution can be extracted using the TLS-recorded 3D data and the crown models built with the alpha shapes algorithm. Two simulated scenes and one actual forest plot were utilized for validation. The results of the proposed method agree well with both the true LAI (in the simulated scenes) and the extracted PAI by the digital hemispherical photography (in the actual plot). This LAI estimation method using TLS and the path length distribution model provides a novel way for ground-based LAI measurements and shows its great potential.

    1. Introduction

    Leaf area index (LAI), defined as one half of the total leaf area per unit ground surface area, is a key vegetation structural parameter for modeling carbon balance, and the exchange of mass and energy between vegetation and atmosphere (Asner et al., 2003; Chen and Black, 1991; Chen, 1996; Chen and Black, 1992; Hyde et al., 2006; Jonckheere et al., 2004; Monteith and Unsworth, 2013; Parker et al., 1995). Fast and accurate extraction of LAI in vegetated environments is of great importance to physiological, climatological, and biogeoscience studies.

    Ground-based measurements of LAI are divided into two categories: direct measurements and indirect measurements (Bréda, 2003; Jonckheere et al., 2004). The direct methods mainly include destructive sampling, allometry and litterfall collection (Bréda, 2003). Accurate LAI can be measured using these methods. However, they are costly, time-consuming and labor-intensive, especially in forest plots (Weiss et al., 2004). Therefore, indirect ground LAI measurements are widely used. Indirect methods can be grouped into indirect contact measurements (inclined point quadrats and plumb lines) and indirect non-contact measurements (Wilson, 1963). Over the last two decades, indirect non-contact measurements have been commonly utilized. Most of these methods are based on the measurement of light passing through forest canopies and many passive optical instruments were developed. By measuring the gap fraction distribution or gap size distribution, LAI can be estimated using these instruments (e.g., TRAC, LAI-2000, Hemispherical photography). However, these passive optical methods are sensitive to variable sky illumination conditions (Srinivasan et al., 2015).

    More recently, Terrestrial Laser Scanning (TLS) provides a novel indirect ground-based measurement for the estimation of LAI. It is an active technology that describes the finest characteristics of forest structure by acquiring 3D point clouds with high accuracy and efficiency (Hosoi and Omasa, 2006). TLS has been widely used for measuring numerous forest structural parameters, including stem diameters, canopy height profiles, stem density and forest biomass (Bienert et al., 2007; Brolly and Király, 2009; Greaves et al., 2015; Jupp et al., 2009; Lovell et al., 2011, 2003; Pueschel et al., 2013; Srinivasan et al., 2015).

    For the estimation of LAI using TLS data, voxel-based methods and gap fraction methods are commonly used. The voxel-based methods divide the whole space into numerous 3D cubes (voxels). The location of the center point of each voxel cell is determined in a Cartesian coordinate system. For TLS data, each 3D point can be assigned to a voxel cell after transforming the whole point cloud into a grid structure. Then LAI is estimated by counting the number of voxels that contain canopy elements (Béland et al., 2014; Greaves et al., 2015; Hosoi and Omasa, 2006; Moskal and Zheng, 2011). These methods directly calculate the LAI using the geometrical information from the point cloud. However, the extracted LAI is mainly affected by the size of the voxel. In addition, occlusion within canopies will cause the underestimation of LAI. Further analyses based on 3D voxels are also demonstrated to acquire vegetation density, gap fraction and leaf area density profiles (Cifuentes et al., 2014; García et al., 2015; Hosoi and Omasa, 2006; Zheng and Moskal, 2012).

    The gap fraction methods introduce the Beer’s law and the gap fraction theory into TLS data to retrieve LAI (Tang et al., 2014; Zhao et al., 2011). Beer’s law was originally proposed in atmospheric science to describe the radiation attenuation of light traveling through a turbid medium (Beer, 1852). Assuming the random spatial distribution of leaves within canopy, it was applied in vegetation canopies to state the exponential relationship between the transmission of light and LAI (Chen and Leblanc, 1997). As the transmission of light at each location can hardly be measured directly, the gap probability, defined as the probability of a ray beam which has no contact with vegetation elements during its transmission, was introduced as a replacement within canopies. Thus, the effective LAI can be calculated using the exponential relationship between gap probability and LAI (de Wit, 1965; Monsi and Saeki, 2005; Monteith and Unsworth, 2013). Based on the collected TLS data, two main approaches are used for the calculation of gap fraction. The first approach uses the apparent reflectance transformed from the returned-power to derive the gap fraction (Jupp et al., 2009; Jupp and Lovell, 2007; Yang et al., 2013; Zhao et al., 2011). However, uncertainty on the true reflectance of partial hits and the calibration of the reflectance directly affect the calculation of gap fraction (Béland et al., 2014). In the other approach 3D points are converted into 2D images to extract gap fraction and effective LAI like the hemispherical photography (Danson et al., 2007; Ramirez et al., 2013; Zheng et al., 2013). Obviously, the 3D information of TLS data is lost in this approach (Pueschel et al., 2014). In addition, the gap fraction theory is based on the random distribution assumption of foliage elements. However, in actual canopies, leaves are generally clumped within canopies. Nilson (1999) used a canopy architecture model to extract the actual LAI. However, some canopy parameters, such as the tree density and crown size, were difficult to be accurately measured (Weiss et al., 2004). Thus, the clumping index is widely proposed for accurate LAI extraction (Chen and Black, 1991). It is a variable parameter which changes with observing direction, location, scale, and season, even in the same forest (Chen and Black, 1991; Pisek et al., 2013; Yan et al., 2016; Zou et al., 2015). However, the spatial heterogeneity caused by various path lengths within canopies has not been considered using a simple clumping index. It is found to be another important and non-negligible cause for LAI underestimation (Hu et al., 2014).

    The recently proposed path length distribution model (Hu et al., 2014) provides a novel solution for quantifying clumping effect. Path length is directly related to the gap probability in Beer’s law. Therefore, a physical model can be established to connect average gap probability with LAI using path length distribution. By extracting the distribution of the inconsistent path lengths, this path length distribution model accurately characterizes the non-randomness inside canopies. Researchers have successfully applied this model on the gap data extracted by many ground instruments such as TRAC, LAI-2000, digital photography and multispectral canopy imager (MCI) (Hu et al., 2015, 2014; Yan et al., 2016; Zeng et al., 2015).

    In comparison to these ground instruments, TLS can provide 3D information (direction and the traveling distance of each laser) directly for extracting the path length distribution. Therefore, the path length distribution model offers an effective way for LAI estimation using TLS data.

    In this paper, we implemented the path length distribution on TLS data for estimating LAI. Firstly, the 3D crown model of each individual tree in the plot was built using alpha shapes algorithm (Edelsbrunner and Mücke, 1994). Secondly, by simulating the emitted pulses and first return signals of TLS from all these crown models, the path length distribution was extracted using the traveling distance through crowns of each simulated laser. The within-crown and between-crown gap probability were extracted with the number of emitted lasers, actual first returns, and simulated first returns from all 3D crown models. Thirdly, the plot LAI was estimated using the path length distribution model, with the inputs of gap probability and path length distribution. The performance of this LAI estimation method was tested in both simulated and actual plots.

    2. Material

    2.1. Study plot

    The study area in this paper is a forest site located in Fangshan District, Beijing, China (39°46′N, 116°13′E). The forest is mainly composed of Robinia pseudoacaciawith different stand ages. The elevation of this forest area is about 50 m and the slope is less than 5°. In this study, a 30 m × 30 m rectangular plot with 33 mature stems and sparse undergrowth vegetation was selected as our study plot (Fig. 1).

    2.2. Data acquisition

    2.2.1. Field hemispherical photography

    In this study, the field hemispherical photos were acquired in October 2016. They were taken at the resolution of 3680 × 2456 pixels with a Nikon D800E equipped with the SIGMA EX DG fisheye lens. In the study plot, we captured these photos every 5 m along the two diagonals across the plot, including one photo taken at the center of the plot (18 photographs in total) (Fig. 1). Like the TLS system, hemispherical photography can provide a coverage of the upper hemisphere, which makes it suitable to evaluate the TLS-extracted LAI directly.

    2.2.2. Terrestrial LiDAR data

    The point cloud data were collected using Riegl VZ-1000 (RIEGL, Austria) after the field photography. The specifications of this LiDAR system are shown in Table 1. In this study, the angle stepwidth was set to 0.03° in both the horizontal and vertical directions to get a detailed description of the plot. When the scanner is vertically mounted, the zenith angle of the scanner ranges from 30°to 130° and the azimuth angle ranges from 0° to 360°. The scanner can also be tilted from 0 to 90° in the vertical direction with a tilt mount. Thus, three views of scanning were acquired at each scan station to provide a full coverage in the upper hemisphere. The relative positions of the scanner in acquiring these three scans at one scan station are shown in Fig. 2. Red, green and blue arrows represent the X, Y and Z axes in the scanner’s own coordinate system respectively in each view of scanning. One view of scanning was acquired with the scanner standing vertically and marked as scan A. The other two views, marked as scan B and C, were acquired with the scanner laid down (tilted at an angle of 90°) and pointed to different orientations. In our experiment, the angle around the vertical axis between two lying down positions was 100° in order to get the hemispherical coverage of the plot. Taking the coordinate system of the vertical-mounted scan as a reference, the data collected at the tilt positions were then transformed using the tilt mount calibration provided by the RiSCAN PRO software.

    2.3. Point cloud preprocessing

    The preprocessing of the TLS point cloud includes registration, noise removal and ground filtering. Once the point clouds were collected from different stations in the plot, the registration process was needed for the alignment of these scans. Our earlier work proposed a coarse-to-fine registration approach for registering TLS point clouds from multiple scans (Zhang et al., 2016a). The coarse registration part took advantage of the backsighting orientation theory (Lichti and Gordon, 2004) and decreased the number of corresponding reflectors to only one between every two adjacent scans. In the fine registration part, corresponding stem center locations at the breast height were calculated to perform a rigid-body transformation. Results showed that the registration error was approximately 1.5 cm.

    In this study, we took the center scan of this plot as the reference scan. The other four corner scans were then well aligned after registration. As an example, Fig. 3 shows the registration results between the Northeast scan (Green) and the Center scan (Red).

    Spurious returns from the sun and the sky can cause noises in the scans. Therefore, a noise removal procedure was applied with the help of Point Cloud Library (Rusu and Cousins, 2011) and Cloudcompare (Girardeau-Montaut, 2011). Given the voxel size and a number threshold of neighbor points, the spurious returns from the background environment were removed.

    In addition, a ground filtering procedure was used to identify ground and non-ground points. Then understory vegetation and grass can be removed from the non-ground points with a relative height above the ground. In this study, the CSF ground filtering algorithm proposed by our group before was used (Zhang et al., 2016b). It simulated a piece of cloth to cover the whole point cloud and modified the physical process of the simulation to be adapted to point filtering. Compared to other filtering algorithms, the parameters of this method are less numerous and much easier to set. Finally, the threshold of relative height was chosen as 1 m above the ground to remove low vegetation in the study plot.

    3. Method

    In this study, we applied the path length distribution model to TLS data for LAI estimation. This model was proposed to improve the LAI estimation by characterizing non-randomness within canopies and correcting for the clumping effect for crop and tree canopies in particular (Hu et al., 2014). As the path length distribution can provide more detailed information within canopies, it is more capable of describing the heterogeneity of a canopy than using a simple clumping index. For the TLS data, the path length distribution also provides a novel way to use 3D information (direction and travelling distance of each laser) directly without converting 3D points to 2D image. In addition, this method improves the underestimation of LAI obtained by voxel counting methods caused by the occlusions in the point cloud.

    The workflow of this proposed TLS-based LAI estimation method is shown in Fig. 4. Firstly, the 3D crown model was built for each tree crown using the alpha shapes algorithm and the TLS points. Secondly, by simulating the intersection between each emitted laser and the crown models, the path length distribution was calculated. The within-crown and between-crown gap probability were then extracted separately with the number of emitted lasers, actual first returns recorded in the plot and the number of simulated first returns from all 3D crown model. Thirdly, the LAI within canopy was then calculated using the path length distribution model with the inputs of path length distribution and within-crown gap probability. Finally, taking the between-crown gap probability into consideration, the LAI in the whole plot was calculated.

    3.1. Path length distribution model

    The path length distribution model is an extension of Beer’s Law with constant path length. In forest canopies, the path length l is affected by the crown shapes and no longer constant. Therefore, a relative path length lr is introduced as the ratio of the absolute path length at current location of the transect to the max path length along the transect:

    (1)lr=l/lmax
    where l is the path length at a specific location of transect and lmax is the max path length along the transect.

    Then the probability density function of all relative path lengths is defined as:

    (2)plr(lr)=plr∧(lr)∫01plr∧(lr)d(lr)
    where lr is the relative path length ranging from 0 to 1. plr∧(lr) is the frequency of lr falling within the interval [lr,lr+d(lr)].

    Thus, the total gap probability P and LAI within canopies (LAIcanopy) and can be expressed as:

    (3)P=∫01e−G⋅(FAVD⋅lmax)⋅lr⋅plr(lr)d(lr)
    (4)LAIcanopy=∫01(FAVD⋅lmax)⋅cosθ⋅lr⋅plr(lr)d(lr)
    where FAVD is the foliage area volume density. G is the leaf projection coefficient. θ stands for the zenith angle.

    Combining these two equations, FAVD⋅lmax were eliminated and LAI can be calculated with only two input parameters: average gap probability and path length distribution function. As the TLS system records the direction and traveling distance of each emitted laser directly, the path length distribution model fits the TLS data better than other 2D models.

    3.2. 3D tree crown modeling

    As shown in the workflow, a 3D crown model was built for each individual tree crown to extract gap probability and path length distribution. Convex and concave hulls are two widely used modeling approaches. A convex hull can be built using the Quickhull algorithm with the help of Matlab software (Barber et al., 1996). It provides a fast shape estimation of the crown with hundreds of triangles. However, the convex hull was incapable of fully reflecting the characteristics of a tree crown in this study. The concave hull is a more advanced approach used to capture the exact shape of the crown points. In this paper, the alpha shapes algorithm was utilized to build the concave hull for modeling each tree crown. This approach is derived from the Delaunay triangulation of the point cloud with a parameter alpha (α) used to control the level of detail (Edelsbrunner and Mücke, 1994). In the computation step, each simplex of the Delaunay triangulation is compared with the specified alpha value. Removing the simplices which have an empty circumsphere with a radius larger than the defined alpha value, a concave hull is generated to represent the 3D tree crown.

    Taking one White Oak crown as an example, Fig. 5 shows the triangulation mesh of the convex-hull and alpha shapes hulls with different alpha values (0.5 and 1.0) respectively for comparison between convex and concave hulls visually. An Alpha shapes hull can provide much more details than a convex-hull. Furthermore, as the alpha value gets smaller, the alpha shapes hull contains more detailed features by providing more triangles. As a result, the modeling efficiency drops. Therefore, we choose the alpha value as 1.0 in this study to provide detailed crown features with acceptable efficiency for modeling and the further calculation of path length.

    3.3. Path length distribution extraction

    For each emitted laser, the path length was defined as the distance traveling through crowns. In this paper, it was calculated in three steps. Firstly, based on the scan configuration in the center station (scan location, zenith angle, azimuth angle and angle stepwidth), we simulated the spatial distribution of each emitted laser. Secondly, the intersections between each laser and all the crown models were calculated. The path length of an emitted laser was then extracted as the within-crown traveling distance.

    Unlike the near-vertical zenith angle in Airborne Laser Scanning (ALS), TLS instruments provide a much larger range of zenith angle. As a result, one single laser may pass through (intersect with) more than one crown. Thus, the path length of each laser was corrected as the accumulation of the traverse distances within all the intersected crowns. Using Eq. (1), the relative path length of each laser was calculated. Finally, the path length distribution was extracted as the probability density of all the relative path lengths. For further comparison with the in-situ digital hemispherical photography (DHP), this path length distribution was calculated every 5° zenith angle band from 40° to 70°.

    3.4. Gap probability calculation

    According to Eqs. (3)–(4), within-canopy LAI (LAIcanopy) can be estimated by using the path length distribution and gap probability as inputs. In a forest plot, the input gap probability is the within-crown gap probability (Pcrown) after removing large between-crown gaps. Fig. 6 shows the distribution of the between-crown gaps and the crowns with alpha shapes hulls in a plot from the top view (a) and side view (b).

    In order to convert the within-canopy LAI (LAIcanopy) to the plot LAI (LAIplot), the between-crown gap probability (Plarge), defined as the proportion of large between-crown gaps to the plot, was calculated. Then the plot LAI can be estimated as:

    (5)LAIplot=LAIcanopy⋅(1−Plarge)

    For the calculation of Pcrown and Plarge in the TLS data, three related metrics were needed: Nemit stands for the number of emitted lasers from the scanner. Nreturns represents the number of actual first returns recorded by the scanner. Nhull is the number of the simulated first returns from 3D crown models. In this study, Nemit was calculated using the range of zenith angle and azimuth angle in TLS scanning. Assuming the zenith angle ranges from Zemin to Zemax, the azimuth angle ranges from Azmin to Azmax and the angle stepwidth was represented as Stepwidth, Nemit can be calculated as:

    (6)Nemit=((Zemax−Zemin)/Stepwidth)*((Azmax−Azmin)/Stepwidth)
    Nhull was then regarded as the amount of the emit pluses entering 3D crown models built with the alpha shapes algorithm. This can be done by calculating the intersection between each emit pulse and all crown models. As these crowns were modeled as alpha shape hulls, within-crown gaps were ignored. Therefore, the difference between Nreturns and Nhull was caused by the within-crown gaps. Then Pcrown can be calculated as:
    (7)Pcrown=1−Nreturns/Nhull

    Furthermore, the difference between Nhull and Nemit was the number of non-return pulses caused by the between-crown gaps. Thus, the between-crown gap probability Plarge was expressed as:

    (8)Plarge=1−Nhull/Nemit

    The TLS data provided wide zenith angle ranges to cover the upper hemispherical field of view. Therefore, the within-crown and between-crown gap probability were both calculated in 5° zenith angle bands from 40° to 70°, like the path length distribution extraction process. Within-canopy LAI (LAIcanopy) was then calculated and converted into LAI in each zenith angle band using Eq. (5). Finally, the plot LAI (LAIplot) was estimated by taking the sine of the center angle in each zenith angle band as the weighting function for accumulation.

    3.5. Experimental design

    Before applying the path length distribution model on the TLS data acquired in the actual forest plot, we simulated two forest scenes to test the feasibility of this method. In both scenes, the geometrical structure and the leaf area of each tree crown are known. True LAI can be calculated directly and accurately based on its definition to provide the ground truth for validating the TLS-extracted LAI.

    The first forest scene is a cylindrical canopy plot as shown in Fig. 7. It is a 200 m × 200 m rectangular plot with 220 non-overlapped cylindrical crowns in different heights with the same radius of 2 m. Within each crown, leaves were randomly distributed. The true LAI of this simulated plot is 2.765.

    There are two main assumptions (cylindrical crown and randomly distributed leaves within crown) in the first simulated plot. In order to get a more realistic scene, three simulated White Oak crowns after eliminating the wood structure were generated with OnyxTREE BROADLEAF 7.0 (Bosanac and Zanchi, 2011) (Fig. 8). The leaf areas of these three models were 282 m2, 147 m2 and 138 m2 respectively. The shapes of the simulated crowns were similar to actual crowns. In addition, leaves were not strictly randomly distributed within each crown.

    This simulated plot is a circular plot with a radius of 50 m. Every 40 crowns were simulated in the plot using each White Oak model. In other words, there are 120 simulated crowns in this plot in total and the true LAI is 2.89. The overview of the simulated plot is shown in Fig. 9.

    In addition to these two simulated plots, an actual forest plot was also used to evaluate TLS-extracted LAI. As mentioned above, we conducted terrestrial LiDAR scanning in a 30 m × 30 m plot with Robinia pseudoacacia trees. The TLS-extracted LAI was then estimated with the path length distribution model. Unlike the simulated data, true LAI of the actual plot was difficult to measure as the direct field measurement was destructive and labor-intensive. Instead, field digital hemispherical photos were taken and processed with the CAN-EYE software (Baret and Weiss, 2004). In our experiment, this software processed 18 photos in total and calculated the gap fraction from the RGB images after a supervised classification. Then the LAI was estimated from the gap fraction in every 5° zenith angle band for the comparison with the TLS-extracted LAI.

    3.6. Leaf angle distribution and G function

    Leaf projection coefficient, G, describes the mean projection of a unit foliage area in a particular direction. It can generally be calculated with the leaf angle distribution. In the estimation of LAI, the projection coefficient G(θ) is usually taken as 0.5 for the assumption of the spherical leaf angle distribution (Chen et al., 2012; Hu et al., 2016, 2014; Zeng et al., 2015). In order to quantify the effect of this assumption on the estimated LAI, we calculated leaf angle distribution (LAD) and G function based on the TLS point cloud of one representative individual tree in the study plot (Fig. 10).

    The leaf points were then segmented manually. In this study, we selected 468 leaves from the point cloud and calculated the normal directions after plane fitting. Fig. 11 shows the distribution of leaf inclination angles. As the azimuth angle were assumed to be randomly distributed within the crown, the LAD can be represented by this leaf inclination angle distribution. In this study, the leaf area of each leaf within the crown is approximately the same. Then the G function can be calculated based on the TLS-extracted LAD (Fig. 12).

    3.7. Wood structure

    The wood structure is also an important factor that affects the estimation of gap fraction and LAI. For the simulated plot, it was easy to separate the wood structure from the foliage element. In order to quantify this effect of wood structure, we used the original Oak Tree model without eliminating wood structure to build another simulated plot. All the other parameters were the same as those used in the simulated Oak tree plot as mentioned in Section 3.5. The true plant area index (PAI) was acquired based on its definition without distinguishing between the wood structure and the foliage elements. In this simulated plot with Oak tree models, results showed that the wood structure led to a 13.6% overestimation of the extracted-LAI.

    For the actual plot data, it was difficult to segment the wood structure directly from the TLS point cloud. Therefore, no distinction was made between woody and foliage elements. Thus, the extracted metric (LAI) was PAI. As the plot specific woody-to-total plant area was constant for all methods, it would not affect method-to-method comparisons. Therefore, this wood-to-total plant area factor was not derived nor utilized in this study.

    4. Experimental results and discussion

    4.1. Results

    In this study, we tested the LAI estimation method in both simulated and actual plots. According to the G function calculated with the leaf angle distribution in the actual plot (Fig. 12), this G value ranges from 0.40 to 0.74. Considering the available zenith angle range of 40° to 70° in this study, the error of the plot LAI was less than 10% when assuming the G function to be 0.5.

    4.1.1. Simulated plots

    The objective of the two simulated plots was to provide the ground truth for validating the TLS-extracted LAI. To get the TLS-extracted LAI in the two plots with the path length distribution model, we simulated the TLS point cloud at the center of each plot with a height of 1 m above the ground. The angle stepwidth was set as 0.05° to improve the simulating efficiency. Other parameters of the scanner were the same as those used in the actual forest plot as shown in Table 1. The point clouds of the two simulated plots are shown in Fig. 13.

    For the cylindrical canopy plot, the true LAI and the TLS-extracted LAI is 2.765 and 2.75 respectively. The absolute error is 0.015 and the relative error is 0.5%. For the White Oak plot, the true LAI is 2.887 while the TLS-extracted LAI is 2.60. The absolute error is 0.287 and the relative error is 9.9%. In addition, Fig. 14 shows the estimated LAI in each zenith angle band in both simulated plots.

    Results showed that the TLS-extracted LAI with the path length distribution model in both plots agreed well with the true LAI with an underestimation of less than 10%. In the cylindrical canopy plot, the estimated LAI in every zenith angle band changed more smoothly than in the other plot. The underestimation of the TLS-extracted LAI was smaller in the cylindrical canopy plot. This was mainly caused by the different assumptions used in these two plots. In the first plot, the crowns were simulated as regular geometry models (cylinders) and the leaves were randomly distributed within each crown. However, the White Oak models used in the second plot were more realistic. As a result, the estimated LAI in each band changed more sharply as the zenith angle grew. This phenomenon was mainly caused by the following two reasons. First, the shape of the crown was tapering from bottom to top. Second, unlike the first simulated plot, the clumping effect exists in this plot. Therefore, the amount of leaves and the LAI in each zenith band were different.

    4.1.2. Actual forest plot

    In the actual forest plot, the extracted metric based on the path length distribution model was PAI as no distinction was made between woody and foliage elements. The TLS-extracted PAI is 1.14 using our proposed method. As the direct field measurement is destructive and labor-intensive, we did not measure the true PAI. Instead, upward digital hemispherical photos were taken in the plot for relative validation. With the help of CAN-EYE software, all the pixels in each photo were well-classified into sky pixels or vegetation pixels. Then the gap fraction was computed and used for the estimation of PAI. In this plot, this DHP-extracted PAI is 1.06. Therefore, the TLS-extracted PAI is comparable to the PAI extracted from hemispherical photos with the absolute error of 0.08 and the relative error of 7.5%. As the plot specific woody-to-total plant area was constant for all methods, it would not affect method-to-method comparisons.

    We also compared the TLS-extracted PAI with the DHP-extracted PAI in every zenith angle band (Fig. 15). In this study, the effective zenith angle of the hemispherical photos ranged from 0° to 60°. However, the TLS-extracted PAI was calculated from 40° to 70°. Therefore, the comparison of PAI was taken in every zenith angle band from 40° to 60°. Results showed that the TLS-extracted PAI agreed well with the DHP-extracted PAI. The largest relative error in all bands is less than 9.2%.

    4.2. Discussion

    4.2.1. Laser beam size

    Another important factor that would affect the estimation of gap probability was the laser beam size. Previous findings indicated that the partial hits of laser pulses can cause a significant underestimation of gap probability (Danson et al., 2007). Therefore, in simulating the TLS point cloud of the virtual plots, the laser beam size needs to be considered carefully.

    Béland et al. (2011) provided the minimum necessary portion of the beam power to trigger a return when a laser beam hitting a leaf. For a leaf having a reflectance of 0.28, the minimum fraction of the power required was determined to be 0.219. In this paper, we set up four Spectralon reference panels of reflectance of 20%, 30%, 50% and 99% in laboratory. All these panels were scanned using our Riegl VZ-1000 instrument from distances of 10, 15, 20, 25 and 30 m respectively. Then the recorded amplitude (DN) value was expressed as a function of the target reflectance and distance from the scanner to the target. For the Riegl VZ-1000 instrument, the reflectively value (given in dB) gives the ratio of the actual optical amplitude versus the optical amplitude of a diffuse white target at the same range. Therefore, the I/I0 ratio (I: received energy, I0: emitted energy) proposed in Béland’s paper can be replaced by this reflectivity value. Fig. 16 shows the reflectivity plotted against the recorded DNs during our laboratory experiment with four panels. Considering the plot size in our field experiment, we chose the DN value at the distance of 15 m. The solid curve is a second order polygonal equation relating the reflectivity to DN.<-- >

    In our study, we acquired the reflectance spectra of Robinia pseudoacacia leaves with a spectroradiometer and integrating sphere before TLS scanning. The leaf reflectance (αLeaf) is 0.214 at the wavelength of our TLS instrument (1550 nm). Based on the above relationship, the corresponding reflectivity (REFLeaf) is -8.80 dB. In the point cloud of our plot, the minimum reflectivity value (REFMin) was approximately -14 dB. Therefore, the minimum required fraction of the power was found to be 0.302 and used in the simulation process.

    Besides, in simulating the energy distribution of each pulse, we divided the laser beam into 24 parts (Fig. 17). Along the direction of each red radius shown in this figure, 6 laser rays were simulated. Therefore, there are 145 rays (including one laser ray at the center) in total for each pulse. Considering each ray has the same energy, we rearrange the relative locations of every 6 laser rays along the direction of each radius to match the Gaussian distribution of power in each laser beam.

    4.2.2. Applicability to denser forests

    In this study, the proposed LAI retrieval method using TLS data and the path length distribution model was applied in open-canopy forests. When two or more crowns are slightly touching in a denser forest plot, this method would still work by combining the two crowns as a whole in building the 3D concave hull. The alpha shapes hull could provide both the exterior and interior enveloping surface of a crown for calculating the path length of each laser beam correctly. However, this method may fail when using it in closed-canopy forests. Therefore, future studies are needed for applying this method to all forest conditions.

    4.2.3. Method-to-method comparison

    In forest plots, actual LAI is hard to acquire in field measurements. Therefore, many TLS-based LAI retrieval methods use the DHP-extracted LAI as a comparison. In this study, we chose the comparison results from two typical TLS-based LAI estimation methods for further comparison. In the first method, the authors compared the effective LAI derived from Echidna Validation Instrument (EVI) with DHP-extracted LAI (Zhao et al., 2011). The EVI-extracted LAI showed a mean overestimation of 13.0% in the three broadleaf (Harwood) plots. In the second method, the TLS-extracted LAI showed a mean overestimation of 30.1% among 12 broadleaf plots. In this study, the overestimation of PAI was 7.5% in comparison to the DHP method. Therefore, our proposed TLS-based PAI retrieval method using the path length distribution model is comparable to other existing methods.

    5. Conclusion

    The path length distribution model was proposed to reduce the clumping effect occurs within plots and estimate LAI in forest plots. It has been successfully implemented on the gap data measured by many indirect methods using optical sensors, such as the TRAC, MCI and DHP. In this study, we applied this path length distribution model to TLS data to improve indirect LAI estimation. Instead of converting 3D points to 2D image, the traveling distance of each emitted pulse from the TLS scanner was used for the LAI estimation. Thus, this path length distribution model shows its superiority in directly using the 3D information recorded by the TLS.

    Two simulated plots with exact true LAI were built in this study for validation purpose. Results showed that the TLS-extracted LAI using the path length distribution model agreed well with the true LAI during each simulation. We also compared the estimated PAI with DHP-extracted PAI in an actual forest plot. Results showed that the TLS-extracted PAI was comparable to the field hemispherical photography.

    In comparison to current indirect ground-based LAI measurement with passive optical instruments, such as TRAC, LAI-2000 and hemispherical photography, LAI estimation using TLS technology is less dependent on environmental conditions. TLS technology also provides a direct way for measuring 3D crown structure. Moreover, TLS-extracted LAI could be used as an accurate validation for airborne and spaceborne earth observing systems.

    The proposed LAI estimation approach based on the path length distribution model shows great potential in estimating LAI with TLS data. However, this method follows the assumption of random distribution within crowns. The clumping within crowns is not considered. In future studies, we will continue to quantify and correct the within-crown clumping effect. Future studies will also focus on quantifying the influence of the level of details in 3D crown modeling. Furthermore, the application of this path length distribution model to airborne laser scanning data should be studied.

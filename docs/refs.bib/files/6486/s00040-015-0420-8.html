Progressive vegetation succession of fen habitats promotes the lack of habitat specialist ants

Abstract

Vegetation succession, drainage, and climate change are causes of peatland habitat loss in the Western Balkans. Ants (Hymenoptera: Formicidae) are one of the most ecologically important invertebrate taxa in terrestrial ecosystems. To explore whether, and to what extent, fen ant communities are specialized, we sampled an alkaline fen, adjacent habitats undergoing vegetation succession, and the surrounding beech forest in west-central Croatia. Here, we show how a fen and its surrounding habitats differ in ant community structure, and which of the environmental variables (soil temperature, moisture, and pH) have the strongest impact in shaping those communities. In the two growing seasons of sampling we recorded 16 ant species, none of which were peatland specialists. While species richness did not differ between the sites, species diversity was the highest at a succession site. Specific environmental conditions of the fen limit colony establishment and allow survival of a small subset of ant species from the surrounding habitats. Consequently, only two ant species (Myrmica rubra and Lasius platythorax) were abundant at the fen site. Their presence at other study sites indicates that the fen fauna is composed of generalist species. The fen and forest community composition differed from the successional sites, and it was most strongly affected by soil pH. The fauna of these habitat islands completely lacks peatland-specific ant species. Knowing more about communities of these disappearing habitats will help us predict future biodiversity losses in similar habitats threatened by vegetation succession.

Introduction

Peatland habitats are widespread in North and Central Europe, whereas in the Western Balkans they comprise small and exceedingly isolated areas (Horvat 1939; Montanarella et al. 2006; Lazarević 2013). Throughout Europe peatlands are increasingly endangered habitat types (Spitzer and Danks 2006; Minayeva et al. 2009). In the Western Balkans they are critically endangered due to abandonment of traditional human activities, drainage, climate change, and vegetation succession (Topić and Stančić 2006).

Peatlands in Croatia are disjunct patches of peatland habitats formed during, or immediately after, the last period of glaciation when low temperatures and high humidity enabled dispersal of peatland vegetation further south (Horvat 1950). We know that they were once widespread in the Western Balkans because peat deposits were found beneath agricultural fields (Topić and Stančić 2006).

Due to their special hydrology and soil properties, peatlands usually divide two distinct types: alkaline fens and acidic peat bogs (Spitzer and Danks 2006). Fens are in general minerotrophic peatlands which are supplied with water and nutrients from the ground water, and their soil pH is basic or at least neutral (Rydin and Jeglum 2006). Peat bogs are, in contrast, characterized by low pH (below 5.0) and vegetation dominated by Sphagnum spp. L. mosses.

Rapid vegetation succession is the biggest threat for both fens and bogs in the Western Balkans due to abandonment of traditional management practices and climate change (Hršak 1996; Minayeva et al. 2009; Šoštarić et al. 2012). In the past, bogs and fens were mowed and grazed by livestock that successfully suppressed spreading of woody vegetation into the peatlands (Alegro and Šegota 2008). Generally, vegetation succession leads to changes in plant species composition and increases habitat heterogeneity (Clements 1936; Huston and Smith 1987). Soil properties such as availability of nutrients, pH and mineralization, change with vegetation succession (Bautista-Cruz and del Castillo 2005). Furthermore, changes of the habitat structure affect the composition of animal communities (Tews et al. 2004). Usually, increase of habitat structural complexity corresponds to increase in animal diversity (Karr 1971; Hurlbert 2004; Tews et al. 2004). At the same time, fen encroachment by surrounding vegetation often results in loss of peatland-specific flora and fauna (Topić and Stančić 2006; Buchholz et al. 2009).

Specialization of peatland biota is a result of peatland-specific habitat conditions: soil saturated with water, lack of oxygen in the soil, reduced decomposition and nutrient deficiency, and large fluctuations in daily and annual temperatures (Främbs et al. 2002; Rydin and Jeglum 2006). Therefore, these specific abiotic conditions of peatlands lead to specialization not only of associated floras (Rydin and Jeglum 2006), but also of arthropods such as spiders (Scott et al. 2006), carabid beetles (Peus 1932; Roubal 1934; Spitzer et al. 1999), butterflies and moths (Spitzer et al. 1999), caddisflies (van Kleef et al. 2012), and ants (Vepsäläinen et al. 2000; Ellison et al. 2002). Species inhabiting peatlands can be classified into four distinct categories: tyrphobiontic species—present exclusively at peatlands; tyrphophilous species—characteristic of peatland habitats but not completely confined to them; tyrphoneutral species, with no preferences towards peatlands, can be found at various habitat types; and tyrphoxenous species—accidental species which cannot survive in peatlands (sensu lato Peus 1928; Roubal 1934; Spitzer et al. 1999).

Ants (Hymenoptera: Formicidae) are one of the most ecologically important invertebrates omnipresent in terrestrial habitats worldwide (Hölldobler and Wilson 1990). Furthermore, ants are sensitive to habitat disturbance (Andersen 1991; Folgarait 1998; Hoffmann and Andersen 2003), and they are increasingly used in biodiversity assessments and conservation studies (Andersen 1997; King et al. 1998; Kaspari and Majer 2000). Additionally, in large and continuous peatlands in Northern Europe, tyrphobiontic ant species are considered good indicators of habitat quality and change (Peus 1932; Vepsäläinen et al. 2000).

Among the abiotic factors governing the structure of ant communities, temperature is the most important predictor of their activity, behavioral dominance (Cerdá et al. 1998; Hurlbert et al. 2008), and diversity (Kaspari et al. 2003; Dunn et al. 2009). Temperature alone, however, is not enough to explain ant diversity (Jenkins et al. 2011). High soil moisture and high water table affect ants by reducing nest site availability (Lesica and Kannowski 1998), thus decreasing opportunities for colonization. Because vegetation succession is known to increase ant diversity (Boomsma and Van Loom 1982; Gallé et al. 1998; Vepsäläinen et al. 2000), we wanted to test in the consequences that successional habitats might have on peatland ant communities.

To our knowledge, there has not been a systematic ecological survey of peatland ant communities in the Western Balkans, and current knowledge of ant diversity relies on purely taxonomic data (Agosti and Collingwood 1987; Petrov 2004; Bračko 2006, 2007; Karaman 2009). This is the first study to provide ecological data of an ant community from a peatland habitat of the Western Balkans with respect to its biggest threat—succession. Vegetation succession replaces peatland biotas of this region, causing irreversible biodiversity losses (Topić and Stančić 2006). Specifically, our objectives were to determine: (1) the occurrence of ants that are peatland specialists in the Western Balkans; (2) the impact of vegetation succession on ant community composition and structure; (3) the specific abiotic factors governing the composition and structure of ant communities in fen habitats threatened by succession.

Materials and methods

Study sites

Research was carried out in a Croatian fen, Jarak, and three of its surrounding habitats of different vegetation associations. Jarak fen is an alkaline fen habitat located along the Jarak stream in the Žumberak-Samoborsko gorje Nature Park, situated in west-central Croatia at an elevation of 690 m above sea level (N 45°45′46.44″, E 15°22′1.2″). The climate is temperate with mean annual temperature of 6.5 °C and mean annual precipitation of 1450 mm (Zaninović et al. 2008). Habitats differed in vegetation composition, diversity, and height (see below), as well as their pedological properties. To determine vegetation composition we used phytosociological releves, in accordance with standard Central European methodology (Braun-Blanquet 1964). Plant nomenclature follows Flora Europaea (Tutin et al. 1964, 1965, 1966, 1967, 1968, 1969, 1970, 1971, 1972, 1973, 1974, 1975, 1976, 1977, 1978, 1979, 1980; Tutin et al. 1993).

The first site was located in the center of the fen, close to a small stream inside an Eriophoro latifolio-Caricetum panicae HT ex Trinajstić association (Trinajstić 2002) with broad-leaved cottongrass (Eriophorum latifolium Hoppe) dominating the site. The fen encompasses an area of 1 ha which is encroached upon by common reed [Phragmites australis (Cav.) Trin. ex Steud] (Šoštarić et al. 2012). We found fen-specific plant species E. angustifolium Honck. and four Carex spp. (C. flacca Schreb., C. caryophyllea Latourr., C. hostiana DC. and C. paniculata L.) in great abundance. Average vegetation height was 90 cm.

The 2nd site was located inside a 1st successional phase of common reed and willow (Salix sp.) with average vegetation height of 130 cm. The area is dominated by common reed, willow and common butterbur [Petasites hybridus (L.) P.Gaertn., B.Mey. and Schreb.], which formed dense stands.

The third site was situated inside a 2nd successional phase of young forest—a mixture of willows and different woody and herbaceous species characteristic of the surrounding beech forest. The average vegetation height was 150 cm. In order to prevent its spread into the fen, all woody vegetation from this site was removed 1 year before the beginning of the research. In addition to common reed and common butterbur, purple willow [Salix purpurea L. (G)] and common aspen [Populus tremula L. (G)] were highly abundant. Specific fen plant species were sporadic at both successional habitats. These two sampling sites were 60 m apart. A stream runs through both of them, and they are each located approximately 100 m from the fen site.

The fourth site was located in a mature beech forest (Luzulo luzuloidi—Fagetum Meusel, 1937) with estimated plant height of 30 m. The forest is situated on a slope west of the fen with an easterly aspect and inclination of 30°–50°. As a result of the steep inclination and high precipitation, erosion is rapid at this site causing development of an acidophilous association with a very poor herbaceous layer but a deep layer of leaf litter around the trees.

Collecting methods and identification

We sampled ants using pitfall traps (d = 7.5 cm, V = 200 mL) partially filled with saturated sodium chloride solution. A drop of scentless detergent was added to break the surface tension so animals could not escape from the trap. Each trap had a protective top made of Styrofoam to prevent rain and leaves from falling into the trap. Sampling at the fen was challenging due to the high water table. The traps, however, did not flood during our sampling and trapping was equally successful on the fen as on the other sites. Since the area is rather small and critically endangered we minimized our impact by restricting the number of pitfall traps to five: one in each corner of a 5 × 5 m quadrat, and one in the center. Consequently, we applied the same sampling effort to the rest of the sampling sites. We sampled on a monthly basis during the growing season from May to December of 2008 and 2009, and traps were left open continuously for 30 days. We preserved all pitfall samples in 70 % ethanol in the field.

For identification of ant specimens we used keys from Kutter (1977), Collingwood (1979), Agosti and Collingwood (1987), Czechowski et al. (2002) and Seifert (2007). Identified, labeled, and mounted specimens were deposited at the laboratory of the Croatian Myrmecological Society, Zagreb.

Soil analysis

At all study sites we measured soil temperature and soil moisture monthly in the vicinity of each pitfall trap. Soil temperature was measured at a depth of 7 cm using P300 Dostmann electronic thermometer. Soil moisture was measured monthly from the core samples (d = 10 cm, h = 2 cm) collected at each of the habitats. We determined water content from soil samples using the gravimetric method. In addition, we used the same soil sample to measure pH in water with a ratio of 1:2.5 (10 g substrate/25 mL H2O) using WTW pH 330i meter.

Data analysis

We used the inverse Simpson diversity index to calculate ant diversity across the sampled sites. We estimated if sampling had gone to completion using Chao2 species richness estimate, after 100 randomizations, calculated with the program EstimateS 9.1.0 (Colwell 2013). We tested the differences in Chao2 estimates for every sampled site and examining the overlap of the confidence intervals of sites. No overlap in confidence intervals suggests a significant difference in species richness amongst the plots.

The differences in ant abundance between the 2 years (monthly catch per site) were tested with a Mann–Whitney U test. We used pooled presence—absence data for each of the 2 years of sampling in our canonical correlation analysis (CCA). In our CCA we included only environmental variables which were found to differ significantly between the sites to see the effect they have on community composition. We used the RStudio (R Development Core Team 2011) to produce figures and to run our statistical analysis, using the Vegan package in R (Oksanen et al. 2011).

Normality of the environmental variables data was tested using Shapiro–Wilk W tests. To compare environmental variables between the sites, we used one-way ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis tests, followed by post hoc Tukey HSD or multiple comparison tests, respectively. Use of parametric or non-parametric methods depended on data distribution. These tests were performed using the Statistica 10.0 software package (StatSoft Inc 2010).

Results

Ants

All together we collected 1291 individuals belonging to 16 species from 2 subfamilies: Myrmicinae Lepeletier de Saint-Fargeau, 1835 and Formicinae Latreille, 1809 (Table 1). Lasius platythorax Seifert, 1991 and Myrmica rubra (Linnaeus, 1758) were numerically dominant in both fen and successional habitats. They were also the only species present at all four habitat types. Five species: Formica gagates Latreille, 1798, Lasius paralienus Seifert, 1992, Lasius umbratus (Nylander, 1846), Tetramorium cf. caespitum (Linnaeus, 1758), and Temnothorax sp. were considered rare since we recorded them only as singletons in 2 years of sampling. During our study we did not find any tyrphobiontic or tyrphophilous species. Therefore, all collected species can be classified as tyrphoneutral.

Species diversity was the highest at the 1st successional site (Estimate of Simpson Inverse index, D S1 = 2.05 ± 0.21), while forest, fen and 2nd successional site, had lower diversity (D Forest = 1.83 ± 0.26, D Fen = 1.77 ± 0.05, D S2 = 1.66 ± 0.05). These diversity estimates, ran after 100 randomizations show a significant difference [Kruskal–Wallis test, H (3, N = 70) = 106.63, p ≪ 0.001]. Multiple comparisons test revealed differences in diversity between all the sites, but fen and forest (Mann–Whitney U test, p = 0.76). Based on the vegetation analysis conducted in this study, plant communities showed a similar pattern of species richness and diversity—the highest species richness and diversity were recorded at the successional sites (H S1 = 1.27 bits/ind, H S2 = 0.97 bits/ind).

Chao2 estimate of species richness at the forest and 1st succession site reached an asymptote, while there is still some unrecorded species richness at the fen and 2nd succession site (Fig. 1). The latter sites have the lowest relationship between observed species richness and Chao2 estimate: 41.5 % at the fen habitat, 77.0 % at the 2nd succession site, while observed species richness of 1st succession habitat has a 78.0 % and forest site 89.6 % similarity with the Chao2 richness estimate. Differences in species richness based on Chao2 estimate did not vary significantly between the plots, as their confidence intervals overlapped.

Environmental variables

Mean soil moisture [Kruskal–Wallis test, H (3, N = 64) = 30.13, p < 0.001] and soil pH [Kruskal–Wallis test, H (3, N = 40) = 32.22, p < 0.001] differed among the habitats, whereas mean soil temperature did not [Kruskal–Wallis test, H (3, N = 64) = 0.56, p > 0.05].

According to multiple comparisons post hoc tests, soil moisture was the highest at the fen where the soil was strongly saturated with water 82.1 ± 9.5 %, and this difference was significantly higher when compared to three other study sites—1st successional site 54.2 ± 11.7 % (p < 0.001), 2nd successional site mean of 52.5 ± 8.6 % (p < 0.001), and the forest with a mean of 56.8 ± 11.9 (p < 0.01). Additionally, the same post hoc test revealed that soil moisture was higher in 1st successional habitat compared to the forest (p < 0.05).

Fen soil had on average lower pH values (7.01 ± 0.10) when compared to both successional sites (S1 = 7.42 ± 0.15; S2 = 7.31 ± 0.18), but the difference was significant only between the fen and 1st successional site (multiple comparisons post hoc test, p < 0.05), while the differences between other habitats were not significant (p > 0.05). Forest soil had lower pH values (5.64 ± 0.51) compared to both successional sites (p < 0.001). Since there was no difference in mean soil temperature among the studied sites, we excluded it from our CCA analysis, to increase the strength of our model.

CCA analysis shows a distinct separation of the forest and fen ant community from the succession habitats (Fig. 2). Forest includes drier habitat with a low pH, and its ant community groups in a separate space than the other sites. Fen community occupies high moisture space which is lower in the second sampling year. Both succession sites are clustered tightly together in both years of sampling suggesting the highest similarity of those communities, which are being impacted by the same environmental factors. Our ANOVA analysis revealed that increase in soil pH is the most important factor causing this community separation (p = 0.035).

Discussion

Our study shows that tyrphobiontic species, such as Formica picea Nylander, 1846, Formica uralensis Ruzsky, 1895 and Myrmica sulcinodis Nylander, 1846, are not present at the Croatian fen. There have been previous records of some tyrphobiontic species occurring in the Western Balkans and adjacent countries [e.g., M. sulcinodis in Slovenia (Bračko 2007) and F. picea in Italy (Poldi et al. 1995)]. Myrmica sulcinodis is the only tyrphobiontic species reported for Croatia thus far, but those records are questionable (Bračko 2006). Furthermore, no tyrphobiontic ant species were recorded for Romanian fens (Markó et al. 2004) which, together with our results, could suggest a biogeographical limitation for their dispersal into the Western Balkans and southern Europe.

The tyrphophilous species Myrmica scabrinodis Nylander, 1846 has not been recorded at the Jarak fen, although its presence has been confirmed at other Croatian peatlands (Bujan 2010). This species can often be found on peatland habitats due to its high tolerance to saturation of soil with water (Seifert 1988; Vepsäläinen et al. 2000), but it is not a peatland specialist (Markó et al. 2004). Highly specialized peatland ant species might be relicts of a once widely spread community that inhabited these habitats across Europe. They currently seem confined exclusively to peatlands of an area large enough to maintain the specific set of environmental conditions to which peatland fauna is adapted. Larger habitat areas, higher elevations, and lower temperatures might be some of the reasons why tyrphobiontic species have maintained populations at some peatlands in the Western Balkans, but are absent in other smaller sites threatened by successional encroachment.

The majority of the species found at the Jarak fen are generalists, collected at all other sampled habitats. During the second year of sampling we recorded only two ant species (L. platythorax and M. rubra) at the fen site. Since both species are present in the surrounding habitats it is highly plausible that the fen is being colonized from the successional habitats. Even though these species are not fen specific, they tolerate habitats with high soil moisture (Collingwood 1979; Seifert 1991) and can nest in the fen. Nevertheless, during the first year of sampling, several other ant species were recorded at the fen site, mostly as singletons or doubletons (Table 1). Such accidental species occurrence during 7 months of sampling is probably due to stray foragers of species which do not nest in the fen. We did not record these species in the second year of our sampling. Consequently, the species richness estimate for the fen site did not reach an asymptote, and shows an unstable ant community. Species diversity at the fen site was low which is common for fen habitats across the world (Lesica and Kannowski 1998; Ratchford et al. 2005; Markó et al. 2004). High water level and constant stream flow prevent most ant species from forming colonies in fen habitats. Since the fen site has sparse vegetation without a well-developed root system, there are not many adequate nesting sites. Ants on frequently inundated sites will nest within turfs, and well aerated grass mounds (Boomsma and Isaaks 1982), or in Sphagnum spp. mounds (Markó et al. 2004). Within the succession phase, on the other hand, young forest growth provides patches of drier habitat. Fen vegetation, unlike bog vegetation, is not composed of thick Sphagnum spp. mats, so ants cannot use moss interstices for nesting. As a result of scarce nesting sites and food availability at the fen, species richness and abundance of ants is reduced.

Ant diversity peaked at the habitats with the highest plant diversity, in accordance with other ant studies in successional habitats (Boomsma and Van Loom 1982; Gallé et al. 1998; Vepsäläinen et al. 2000). Higher vegetation complexity enhances structural heterogeneity of the habitat and leads to an increase in the number and availability of nesting sites and food resources. Moreover, succession sites are situated between two different habitat types (fen and forest), which allows generalist species from those habitats to forage and disperse into the more diverse habitats resulting in higher ant diversity in successional habitats.

Our findings suggest that fen ant communities in Croatia are lacking tyrphobiontic species, which is most likely caused by fen area reduction. Composition of ant species at the fen is in large part determined by species composition of the neighboring habitats. Analysis of abiotic factors showed that soil moisture and pH are the most important drivers of ant community structure. Surprisingly, we found that soil pH had a strong influence on species composition in the fen and succession sites. Ellison et al. (2002) did not find any correlation of ant species richness with soil pH of the peatland habitat they sampled; however, their study explored a narrow range of pH. Studies of other animal taxa showed a similar pattern: there was no change in diversity of nematodes (Mulder and Zwart 2003) and only low impact of pH change on presence of certain ground beetle species (Holopainen et al. 1995). Our results might be due to the range of soil pH at our sites, from weak acidic to weak alkali (4.63–7.71), a much wider range than usually examined in invertebrate studies. Changes in pH in this study correspond to the changes in plant communities. Thus, the relationship between ant communities and pH is, in this case, confounded by the changes in vegetation structure and habitat heterogeneity.

To minimize the negative impact our disturbance might have on the protected fen habitat, we sampled with 5 pitfall traps. We used the standardized sampling effort across the habitats. While we clearly undersampled some richness at the fen and 2nd succession site, more pitfalls on the fen, vegetationally homogeneous habitat, would not drastically change our results. Particularly, since our second year of fen sampling resulted in only 2 recorded ant species, also the most abundant species of the surrounding habitats. Pitfall traps are known to undersample ants with a small foraging range (Bestelmeyer et al. 2000), and higher number of pitfalls could help capturing their presence. However, the tyrphobiontic species we were aiming to detect are species with large colonies, and wide foraging range.

Future research should investigate whether all peatlands in the Western Balkans indeed lack specialized ant communities, and if they are colonized exclusively by species from the surrounding habitats that are easily adaptable to specific peatland conditions. This would allow us to determine whether biogeographic barriers limit the dispersal of peatland species, and to predict their future distribution. Moreover, including other arthropod groups in the sampling would provide a wider scope of faunal response to habitat reduction and allow us to estimate the importance of peatland habitats as reservoirs of biodiversity. Thus far, it appears that the ant fauna of these paleorefugial habitats lacks peatland-specific species. Knowing more about these habitat islands will help us predict the future of similar habitats which are declining due to vegetation succession.

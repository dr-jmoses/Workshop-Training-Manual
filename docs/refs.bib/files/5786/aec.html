Abstract

Tropical rainforest canopies are renowned for their high invertebrate diversity and abundance. The canopy comprises a range of microhabitats representing very different food resources (including photosynthetic, reproductive, and structural tissues). As these resources vary considerably in temporal and spatial availability, nutritional quality, chemical protection and other attributes, we hypothesized that microhabitats support structurally different invertebrate communities. To test this we used the Australian Canopy Crane to sample invertebrates from mature leaves, flush leaves, flowers, fruit and suspended dead wood from 23 plant species. Invertebrate faunas on different microhabitats varied in taxonomic composition and feeding guild structure in support of the microhabitat differentiation hypothesis. Herbivores were found predominantly on new leaves (Hemiptera, Lepidoptera) and especially flowers (Coleoptera, Thysanoptera), but were relatively uncommon on mature leaves. Instead, the mature foliage community was dominated by predators, especially spiders and ants, and supported high abundances of saprophages. Ripe fruit and dead wood were scarce canopy resources that were utilized by a relatively small number of invertebrates, mostly saprophages and fungivores. Flowers supported a more heterogeneous fauna than the leaves in terms of proportional abundances of taxonomic groups and feeding guilds, both within tree species (evenness) and between tree species (non‐uniformity). These results demonstrate microhabitat differentiation in a rainforest canopy and are the first to quantify differences in taxonomic composition, guild structure and abundance patterns between such diverse invertebrate assemblages within host trees. We conclude that studies based only on sampling one microhabitat, and leaves in particular, may provide a distorted picture of invertebrate community structure.

Introduction

Understanding how biodiversity influences ecological processes requires an understanding of how species are distributed (Taylor 1984) and how they utilize available resources (Novotny et al. 2010). Both the spatial and temporal distribution of organisms determines food web dynamics, as well as rates and extents of nutrient cycles and energy flows (Montoya et al. 2006). For example, the canopies of tropical rainforests are important sites of essential ecosystem processes such as pollination and herbivory (Ozanne et al. 2003) and attempts to quantify and understand these processes, and to link diversity and ecosystem function has led to many studies of invertebrate biodiversity in rainforest canopies over the last 30 years (e.g. see papers presented in Stork et al. 1997; Basset et al. 2003; Lowman & Rinker 2004).

After woody structures, mature leaves make up most of the biomass of tree crowns (Chave et al. 2003; Liddell et al. 2007) and most rainforest canopy biodiversity and/or ecological studies have focused on insects inhabiting the foliage (Novotny & Basset 2005). Concentration of sampling within this most conspicuous element of tree canopies has meant that the taxonomic composition and (feeding) guild structure of invertebrate communities that exploit other microhabitats remain largely unknown. The possible exception to this is the invertebrate community inhabiting epiphytes (Nadkarni & Longino 1990; Ellwood & Foster 2004), although epiphytes could be considered a different plant growth form rather than an alternative microhabitat in the sense of plant structures. While there have been a few studies that have compared invertebrate communities between various forest components (i.e. between soil, leaf litter, tree trunks, ground vegetation and the forest canopy (Stork 1988; Nadkarni & Longino 1990; Stork & Blackburn 1993; Basset 2001; Kitching et al. 2001; Beaulieu et al. 2010)) even fewer have compared different microhabitats within the canopy (Walter et al. 1998; Ødegaard 2000, 2004; Wardhaugh et al. 2012a, 2013). Furthermore, these latter studies were taxonomically restricted to mites (Acari) and beetles (Coleoptera) respectively. It therefore remains uncertain whether analyses based on samples taken from mature foliage accurately reflect the abundance, diversity and guild structure of the invertebrate community in the canopy as a whole. Thus, assessing differences between tree species and between microhabitats is vital to understanding spatial variation in predation pressure, herbivory and decomposition that all underpin food web dynamics and nutrient and energy cycling.

While studies designed to result in strict comparison between canopy microhabitats are lacking, evidence is emerging that there is great variability in invertebrate abundance (Wardhaugh et al. 2012b) and species composition (Ødegaard 2004; Wardhaugh et al. 2013) across different canopy resources. For example, feeding trials have shown that many canopy feeding herbivores are restricted to feeding on new leaves, and are unable to consume fully expanded mature foliage (Bernays & Janzen 1988; Basset 2001). Herbivore densities are also often much higher on flush foliage (Basset 1991, 1999; Steinbauer et al. 1998; Barone 2000; Marquis et al. 2001; Itioka & Yamauti 2004; Wardhaugh et al. 2012a; Stork & Hammond 2013) and most lifetime herbivore damage on many plant species occurs during leaf expansion (Coley 1983; Aide 1993). Because resources such as flowers, new leaves and dead wood in the canopy vary considerably in quality, composition and spatial and temporal availability, we hypothesized that the structure of the invertebrate communities associated with these microhabitats will show greater variability than those associated with mature leaves. Elsewhere we have shown that the guild and taxonomic compositions of the beetle community (Wardhaugh et al. 2012a, 2013) vary considerably between canopy microhabitats, reflecting their various food preferences. In particular, when corrected for microhabitat availability, herbivorous beetles were significantly more abundant on flowers and new leaves, and significantly less abundant on mature leaves. Here, we compare the relative abundance, taxonomic and guild composition of all invertebrates collected from those same canopy microhabitats (mature leaves, new leaves, flowers, fruit and suspended dead wood). Our aim is to test the generality of this finding across all invertebrate groups.

Methods

Study site

All fieldwork was conducted using the Australian Canopy Crane at the Daintree Rainforest Observatory (a long‐term ecological research site (http://www.jcu.edu.au/canopycrane/)), near Cape Tribulation (16°17′S, 145°29′E) Queensland, Australia (Stork 2007). The crane is situated approximately 40 m a.s.l. and >300 m from the forest edge in complex mesophyll vine forest (Tracey 1982) that is contiguous with the extensive lowland and upland rainforests of the Daintree National Park and Wet Tropics World Heritage Area (0 m a.s.l. → 1300 m a.s.l.). Approximately 1 ha of rainforest containing 745 individual trees (>10 cm d.b.h.) from 82 species and 34 families is accessible from the crane gondola (based on a recent (2009) survey at the crane site which updates plant species data reported in Laidlaw et al. 2007). The canopy is noticeably uneven in height, varying from 10 to 35 m. Although some rain does fall each month (the lowest average rainfall occurs in August; 80 mm), there is a distinctive wet season from November to April (the highest average rainfall occurs in March; 550 mm) (see also Grimbacher & Stork 2009). The 50‐year average annual precipitation at Cape Tribulation is 3996 mm (Hopkins et al. 1996). However, recent records (2000–2002) suggest that the mean precipitation at the field site may be slightly higher than this.

Sampling

Invertebrates were sampled from five microhabitats; mature leaves, new leaves, flowers, fruit and suspended dead wood from 23 canopy plant species. The host tree species selected represent a broad range of taxonomic relatedness, growth pattern, phenology, distribution, size, and abundance. In addition to woody trees (19 species), two species of palms and two species of lianas were sampled (Table 1). For the sake of clarity, all host plant species are hereafter referred to as trees when referred to collectively. These species comprise roughly 25% of the species, 58% of the individuals (435/745) and >70% of the basal area of all trees >10 cm d.b.h. in the 1 ha area of forest under the crane (Laidlaw et al. 2007). One to three individuals of each host species were sampled each month for one year (May 2008–May 2009). Sampling did not occur in October 2008 because of the temporary unavailability of the crane.

Invertebrate sampling was carried out by beating the microhabitat over a small beating sheet (0.23 m2) suspended in a rigid frame. This technique was chosen over the use of passive traps as it provided certainty that the collected invertebrates were associated with the microhabitat in question. It should be noted though, that while the beating techniques we employed are very good at catching flightless or weak‐flying species (e.g. Coleoptera, Formicidae, Araneae and Hemiptera), they are relatively poor at catching some strong‐flying species (e.g. Diptera, Lepidoptera and Hymenoptera). Our results therefore, should be interpreted with this issue in mind. Each microhabitat on each replicate tree was sampled for 10 min, during which time the beating sheet was typically moved to several different positions on the host tree. In general, trees that were flowering, fruiting and/or leaf flushing were selected wherever possible in order to maximize the number and temporal distribution of samples from these more ephemeral microhabitats. New leaves were distinguished from mature leaves by texture and colour. Suspended dead wood was characterized as any dead branches hanging in the canopy and may or may not have included attached dead leaves. Cross contamination between microhabitat samples was kept to a minimum by only sampling microhabitats that were discretely partitioned on host trees. The small size of the beating sheet also allowed for precise targeting of particular microhabitats, which is difficult using a standard larger beating sheet (which are typically 1 m2).

Invertebrate sorting

All invertebrates were sorted to at least the level of order and then to feeding guild (Moran & Southwood 1982; Stork 1987). Several groups were more finely sorted because of known intra‐order variation in feeding mode. For example, the Hemiptera were divided into phloem feeders, mesophyll feeders, xylem feeders, Cicadellidae, and predators, to reflect the different feeding modes within this order (Carver et al. 1991). Cicadellidae was a separate category because of the variety of feeding modes (phloem, mesophyll and xylem feeding species) within this family. Each hemipteran group was also divided between adults and nymphs, as the distribution of winged adults and flightless nymphs may differ. The Hymenoptera were divided into ants, parasitoids (hereafter referred to as Hymenoptera), and Symphyta (represented by a single individual). We categorized ants as an unspecified herbivore/predator guild, as did Moran and Southwood (1982) and Stork (1987), as we are uncertain as to how much of their food is derived from plant sources (extra‐floral nectaries or tending sap‐sucking Hemiptera) or from predation (see Blüthgen et al. 2003).

The ecologically diverse Coleoptera were sorted to family or subfamily level. Each family or subfamily was then assigned to a feeding guild following Lawrence and Britton (1991) and Lawrence et al. (2000) (see Wardhaugh et al. 2012a for details). Species‐level examinations of the distribution of beetles among canopy microhabitats have been published elsewhere (Wardhaugh et al. 2012a, 2013). Beetles were chosen for species‐level analyses because of their ecological diversity, while sorting all invertebrates to species was deemed unfeasible because of the magnitude of the task in relation to the resources and expertise at our disposal. Larvae were sorted separately from adults for all holometabolous orders because of the often large differences in feeding biology and habitat choice between each life history stage (Stork 1987; Novotny & Basset 2005; Pokon et al. 2005).

A summary of the taxa included in each feeding guild is: Ants (Formicidae adults and larvae), Predators (Acari, Hymenoptera, Araneae, various Coleoptera, predatory Hemiptera, Mantodea, Neuroptera, Pseudoscorpiones, Scorpiones), Saprophages (Blattodea, Collembola, various Coleoptera, Dermaptera, Diplopoda, Isopoda), Herbivores (sap‐sucking Hemiptera, Lepidoptera, various Coleoptera, Gastropoda, Orthoptera, Thysanoptera), Fungivores (Psocoptera, various Coleoptera), Xylophages (various Coleoptera), Tourists (Diptera, Ephemeroptera, Trichoptera), Unknown (various adult and larval Coleoptera, larval Diptera, Symphyla, unidentified larvae) (Stork 1987). For information on the identities of Coleoptera families included in each feeding guild, see Wardhaugh et al. (2012a).

Statistical analyses

Prior to the analyses, all temporal samples were pooled with respect to tree species and microhabitat. As a first approach we began by testing the overall hypothesis that community structure (i.e. abundances of each invertebrate taxonomic group in the analysis, see Table 2) differed among microhabitats and host tree species via permutational multivariate analysis of variance (also called permanova (Anderson 2001)) implemented in the Vegan library in R (Oksanen et al. 2010; R Development Core Team 2010). This procedure tests the hypothesis of between‐group differences by portioning variation into within‐ and between‐group effects. The benefit of the approach is that significance tests use a pseudo‐F statistic, and an R2 value describing the proportion of the variation explained by the treatment effects is returned as in standard anova. Unfortunately, at present there is no recourse to post‐hoc pair‐wise comparisons, and while permanova (Anderson 2005) can implement post‐hoc comparisons, the design must be balanced. This was not the case in our dataset. We performed the overall analyses including both main effects (tree species and microhabitat) as well as their interaction using a dissimilarity matrix based on Bray Curtis distances.

While permanova can provide a general answer to the question of between‐group differences, we were also interested in identifying taxa and feeding guilds as being over‐ or under‐represented on different microhabitats. To answer this question we employed a simple randomization procedure that took the entire invertebrate dataset (40 374 individuals) and randomly assigned each taxa or feeding guild to microhabitat types in the exact proportions as they existed in the original data set. The procedure was iterated 1000 times. After each iteration the number of individuals in each taxa and feeding guild was recorded to generate the frequency distributions of expected numbers under the hypothesis of randomness. To test over‐ or under‐representation we compared the observed (true) number of individuals in each taxa or feeding guild against the 25th and 975th values in the ordered data set to ensure α = 0.05 for all conclusions regarding significance. Invertebrate groups or feeding guilds with observed abundances under the 25th value in the ordered randomization data set on particular microhabitats were considered to be significantly under‐represented, while observed abundances above the 975th value were over‐represented. Analyses were restricted to those taxonomic groups and feeding guilds where the total abundance was >40, chosen as a compromise between including a maximum number of groups while excluding those with insufficient abundances to detect significant differences, if they exist.

Variation in mean evenness between microhabitats was explored with anova. Where significant differences were detected, post‐hoc pair‐wise comparisons between microhabitats were performed using Tukey tests. Significant outcomes in these tests would reveal between‐microhabitat variation in invertebrate community evenness. Conversely, non‐significant outcomes are expected when proportional abundances of component taxa are similar on different microhabitats.

While evenness can assess the variation in abundance on each microhabitat within each tree species (i.e. within a single community), it cannot be used to measure variation in the relative abundances of individual taxonomic groups or feeding guilds across tree species (i.e. across multiple communities). To measure the uniformity of taxonomic groups and feeding guilds across tree species we used 2 × k contingency tables (where k is the number of tree species, = 14) (Moran & Southwood 1982; Stork 1987). Although invertebrates were sampled from 23 plant species in total, mature leaves, new leaves and flowers were sampled from only the 14 species used in this analysis (see Table 1). Analyses were restricted to those taxonomic groups (17/41) and feeding guilds (7/8) where n  ≥ 14 as this was the number of tree species used in the analysis (i.e. this threshold is the minimum possible for perfect uniformity). Bonferroni corrections were made to significance levels (adjusted P = 0.0029 for taxonomic groups, and P  = 0.0071 for feeding guilds) prior to pair‐wise analyses. Rejection of the null hypothesis in these tests indicates variation in the relative abundances of taxonomic groups or feeding guilds across tree species on each microhabitat.

Results

Across all tree species and microhabitats we collected a total of 40 374 invertebrates (Table 2). Most (73.7%) of these were insects (29 764 individuals), but arachnids also made up a large proportion (25.4%) of total abundance (10 240 individuals). The most abundant insect groups were Coleoptera (10 335 individuals, or 34.7% of all insects), Thysanoptera (5746, 19.3%), Hemiptera (4628, 15.5%), Formicidae (3292, 11.1%) and Blattodea (1244, 4.2%). Among the arachnids, most individuals belonged to the two most abundant groups; Araneae (5393, or 52.7% of all arachnids), and Acari (4794, 46.8%). Total sample sizes and numbers of individuals sampled from different microhabitats were variable. The number of samples (one sample is equal to one microhabitat on one tree at one sampling point) and the total number of invertebrates collected from each microhabitat were; mature leaves, 363 samples and 15 274 invertebrates; new leaves, 78 and 2682; flowers, 82 and 21 320; fruit, 26 and 475; suspended dead wood, 23 and 623.

Overall, the permanova returned a significant interaction term (tree species X microhabitat, F42,265 = 1.67, P  < 0.01) indicating that community composition (i.e. the relative abundances of taxonomic groups) was not similar on the same microhabitat in different tree species. There were also significant between‐group differences in abundance among categories for both main effects (tree species, F22,265 = 3.63, P < 0.001; microhabitat, F4,265 = 6.06, P < 0.001).

The randomization procedure identified all 25 invertebrate groups represented by >40 individuals and all eight feeding guilds to be non‐randomly distributed among the five focal microhabitats (Tables 2 and 3). Mature leaf invertebrate communities included higher than expected abundances of Blattodea, Psocoptera, Araneae, and Formicidae, and lower abundances of Thysanoptera, Coleoptera and Lepidoptera caterpillars. Samples from new leaves had higher than expected abundances of herbivorous Hemiptera, Formicidae and Araneae, and lower than expected abundances of Psocoptera and Acari. Samples from flowers revealed high abundances of Coleoptera, Thysanoptera, larval Coleoptera and Diptera, and Acari, and were notable for low abundances of Formicidae, Blattodea and Psocoptera. The decomposer community was abundant on dead wood, with higher than expected abundances of Psocoptera, Blattodea and Pseudoscorpiones. Dead wood also showed significantly fewer than expected Formicidae, Coleoptera and Hemiptera. Fruit was utilized by very few invertebrates in the canopy, but there were higher than expected abundances of Psocoptera and Lepidoptera caterpillars, and lower than expected abundances of Coleoptera and Thysanoptera. The high abundance of Formicidae larvae on fruit was because of the presence of a single ant nest (Echinopla australis: Formicinae) within a Cardwellia sublimis seed case, and is therefore not reflective of the fruit community in general.

Differences between feeding guilds inhabiting each microhabitat were similar to the differences observed in the analyses based on taxonomic composition (Tables 2 and 3). This similarity reflects the inclusion of whole orders in single feeding guilds, because of a lack in real or assumed variation in feeding biology at the ordinal or family level for most groups (Stork 1987). Herbivores were significantly more abundant than expected on new leaves and flowers, reflecting higher abundances of Hemiptera, Coleoptera, and Thysanoptera on these microhabitats. Fungivorous Psocoptera, in contrast, were significantly less abundant than expected on new leaves, and higher than expected on dead wood and fruit. The ant guild was of course similar to the taxonomic analysis above, with slight differences because of the inclusion of Formicidae larvae. The results remained similar though, with higher than expected abundances of ants on the foliage and lower than expected abundances on flowers and dead wood. Predators, saprophages and tourists were all over‐represented on mature foliage. Dead wood and fruit also supported higher than expected abundances of saprophagous invertebrates. Over‐representation of invertebrates in the unknown feeding guild category on flowers was because of the relatively high numbers of beetle and fly larvae of unspecified guilds collected from this microhabitat compared with the other microhabitats. Xylophages were rare, but were significantly more abundant on fruit, because of several scolytine weevils (Curculionidae: Scolytinae) collected from Cryptocarya mackinnoniana fruit.

Evenness across microhabitats

Evenness varied significantly between microhabitats for taxonomic groups (F4, 68 = 21.45, P  < 0.0001). Pair‐wise comparisons revealed that the flower‐visiting invertebrate community was significantly less even than the mature leaf, new leaf, fruit and dead wood communities (Fig. 1a). The mature leaf inhabiting community was also significantly less even than the dead wood community. There was no difference in the evenness of invertebrate taxonomic group abundances on any other combinations of microhabitats. At the level of feeding guild, evenness also varied significantly between microhabitats (F4, 68 = 7.56, P  < 0.0001). As with the taxonomic groups, the flower‐visiting invertebrate community was significantly less even in guild composition than the mature leaf, new leaf, fruit and dead wood invertebrate communities (Fig. 1b). There were no significant differences in the evenness of feeding guild abundances between any other combinations of microhabitats.

Uniformity across tree species

All 17 taxonomic groups analysed from flowers were non‐uniformly distributed across tree species (Table 4). On mature leaves, only the Diptera and Hymenoptera were uniformly distributed across the 14 tree species examined. For the new leaf invertebrate fauna, lepidopteran caterpillars, Collembola, Diptera, Orthoptera and mesophyll feeding hemipteran nymphs were the only groups that were uniformly distributed across tree species (Table 4). Among feeding guilds, ants, fungivores, herbivores, predators and saprophages were all non‐uniformly distributed across tree species for the mature leaf, new leaf, and flower‐visiting communities (Table 5). Tourists were uniformly distributed between tree species on mature and new leaves, but displayed a non‐uniform distribution between tree species on flowers. Invertebrates within the unknown feeding guild were uniformly distributed across tree species on new leaves, but not on mature leaves or flowers.

Discussion

Our results support the hypothesis that the structure and abundance of invertebrate communities inhabiting different canopy microhabitats are not similar (e.g. Walter et al. 1998; Ødegaard 2000, 2004; Condon et al. 2008), corroborating previous findings for the beetle community at the same site (Wardhaugh et al. 2012a, 2013). The significant differences in guild structure and taxonomic composition between microhabitats, between tree species, and within microhabitats between tree species that we report indicate that resource differentiation potentially plays a leading role in determining the composition and diversity of the canopy invertebrate community. Our data show that mature and new leaves support a more homogenous invertebrate community than flowers. Indeed, the low density (Wardhaugh et al. 2012b) and broad range of invertebrates found on mature leaves suggest that many species may be transient (Basset 1999) and use the foliage, because of its ubiquity, as little more than a substrate on which to rest or hide. On the basis of our results, the relatively coarse approach of investigating invertebrate community structure using samples from mature leaves alone (Basset & Arthington 1992; Basset 1999, 2001; Wagner 2000; Novotny et al. 2002, 2004, 2006) must be questioned with respect to its ability to reflect the full complement, relative abundance, taxonomic composition or guild structure of the canopy invertebrate community as a whole. The different composition of the invertebrate communities on different microhabitats suggests that biodiversity studies that focus on single microhabitats are sampling only a subset of the canopy assemblage.

Invertebrate assemblages on different microhabitats

The results support our hypothesis that variability in the composition of the invertebrate communities on different microhabitats likely reflects variation in resource availability and/or resource quality. For example, dead wood communities were characterized by high numbers of saprophagic and fungivorous invertebrates, while new leaves and flowers harboured high numbers of herbivores. The greater abundance of herbivores on new leaves compared with mature leaves is consistent with the results of many previous studies on the distribution of herbivorous insects (Basset 1991, 1999, 2001; Steinbauer et al. 1998; Barone 2000; Marquis et al. 2001; Itioka & Yamauti 2004; Wardhaugh et al. 2012a, 2013). This is likely because young leaves are not as tough (Coley 1983; Aide & Londoño 1989) and typically contain higher concentrations of available nitrogen (Mattson 1980; Aide & Londoño 1989; Basset 1991) than older leaves.

Each respective assemblage reflects the concentration of particular taxa on specialized food resources (Basset 2001; Grove 2002), which is consistent with Wardhaugh et al.'s (2012a) analysis of the distribution of different guilds of beetles across the same microhabitats. Flowers especially attracted a specialized fauna, with particular taxonomic groups (Coleoptera, Thysanoptera, Acari) dominating the assemblages on this microhabitat. Equally, the identities of the taxa that are under‐represented on particular resources also indicate differences in abundance based on the availability of food resources. For example, fungivorous Psocoptera were under‐represented on flowers and new leaves, which, because of their rapid growth and short temporal availability, are unlikely to support significant fungal growth. In general, our results for the invertebrate community are very similar to those of the ecologically diverse Coleoptera (Wardhaugh et al. 2012a), where the distribution and relative abundances of different feeding guilds on each canopy microhabitat reflect the distribution of preferred food sources.

Although the distributions of each feeding guild largely followed that of beetle feeding guilds, taxonomic groups within each guild often varied in their preferred microhabitat. For example, herbivores were more abundant on flowers, but some herbivorous taxonomic groups (Orthoptera, Cicadellidae, phloem‐feeding nymphs) were under‐represented on flowers. These differences reflect variation between taxonomic groups in their preferred food source (e.g. most Orthoptera are leaf chewers), or perhaps in the case of cicadellid and phloem‐feeding nymphs, a lack of mobility to take advantage of ephemeral floral resources. These results re‐emphasize the need to target microhabitats according to the taxonomic groups of interest, as it is now clear that not all taxa within a broad feeding guild follow the general trend.

A particularly notable finding was that Formicidae were predominantly a foliage‐inhabiting group, with few individuals collected from flowers or dead wood. Ants are a ubiquitous part of the canopy community in tropical rainforests, often dominating all other invertebrate groups in terms of abundance and biomass, and playing a significant role is setting community structure (Stork 1987; Basset et al. 1992; Krüger & McGavin 1998; Floren et al. 2002; Dial et al. 2006). However, the lack of ants, especially smaller species, attending high concentrations of potential invertebrate prey on flowers lends further support to the hypothesis that tropical arboreal ants are predominantly herbivorous, feeding on hemipteran honeydew or extra‐floral nectar (Blüthgen et al. 2003; Davidson et al. 2003). Most ants on flowers were green weaver ants, Oecophylla smaragdina (Fabricius). Predation by O. smaragdina was rarely observed on flowers, perhaps because at this site much of their nourishment is derived from honeydew and extra‐floral nectar sources (Blüthgen et al. 2003).

Variation in abundance between microhabitats and tree species

Heterogeneity in abundance patterns was significantly higher among the flower‐visiting community compared with the foliage communities, both within and between tree species. Flowers attracted very high abundances of particular taxa (e.g. Coleoptera, Thysanoptera) and relatively low abundances of most other groups, which may explain why flower‐visiting invertebrate communities were distinctly uneven in proportional abundances. Foliage samples in contrast, were not dominated to the same extent by high abundances of any particular taxa, but generally consisted of relatively low abundances of most taxonomic groups, reducing the differences in proportional abundance between each taxon. The greater heterogeneity in the abundances of invertebrate communities visiting flowers compared with leaves shows that flowers are intensively utilized by a subset of the canopy fauna, while leaves are utilized by a broader spectrum of less‐abundant visitors, many of which are unlikely to use leaves directly as a food source.

Moran and Southwood (1982) and Stork (1987) showed that at the level of species, most arthropod guilds sampled by insecticide fogging were uniformly distributed across tree species and that these proportions were remarkably similar between temperate and tropical trees. In contrast, they found that for individuals, no invertebrate feeding guilds were uniformly distributed between host plants. A species‐level analysis of the beetle community corroborated these previous findings by showing remarkable uniformity in the breakdown of numbers of species within each guild between trees, while the relative abundances were distinctly random (Wardhaugh et al. 2012a). Our results were similar, where the proportional abundances of most feeding guilds and taxonomic groups were also non‐uniformly distributed. Both Moran and Southwood (1982) and Stork (1987) sampled entire trees using insecticide knockdown, which precluded comparisons between discrete canopy microhabitats. In our discrete samples, just 2/17 taxonomic groups and the tourist feeding guild on mature leaves were uniformly distributed between host tree species. On new leaves, 5/17 taxonomic groups and the tourist and unknown feeding guilds were uniformly distributed. It is not unexpected that tourists should display a uniform distribution, as they are not associated with the host tree, but are simply collected by chance. In contrast, no taxonomic groups or feeding guilds on flowers were uniformly distributed across tree species. Non‐uniformity on flowers was largely the result of particular host plant species harbouring very high numbers of individuals, while other host trees supported very low abundances. For example, Argyrodendron peralatum flowers supported a very high abundance of a specialist species of phloem feeding Psyllidae, resulting in high heterogeneity in abundance across all of the sampled trees.

This study represents a rare quantification of microhabitat differentiation in invertebrate community structure in a tropical rainforest canopy. The variation between microhabitats in invertebrate guild composition and abundance patterns we report should be considered by all researchers working in hyper‐diverse, complex tropical rainforests. In particular, studies on the structure of invertebrate communities based only on samples taken from the leaves may contain substantial errors because the mature leaf community does not fully represent the structure and composition of the wider canopy community. We did show, however, that the guild structure of the entire invertebrate community was very similar to that of the ecologically diverse Coleoptera on different microhabitats, where invertebrate distributions largely reflect the distribution of the resources they feed upon. The Coleoptera therefore, are potentially an ideal group to be used as a proxy for determining the distribution and abundance of the wider invertebrate community in the rainforest canopy. The incorporation of multiple microhabitats in future canopy invertebrate biodiversity studies is likely to reveal a more accurate description of canopy invertebrate community structure and dynamics.

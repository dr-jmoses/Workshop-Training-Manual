    Abstract

    Worldwide, the most ecologically damaging invasive ants are associated with introduced honeydew‐producing insects, yet the mechanisms by which this mutualism may support invasive ant populations and drive impacts remain unclear. On Christmas Island, the invasive yellow crazy ant (Anoplolepis gracilipes; YCA), found in association with introduced honeydew‐producing scale insects, can reach extraordinary densities, triggering an ecological meltdown. In this study, we utilized stable isotope analysis to determine resource use of YCAs in naturally occurring low‐ and high‐density populations, and we conducted laboratory experiments mimicking availability of honeydew to measure effects of carbohydrate subsidies on colony growth and worker behavior. High‐density YCA populations in nature incorporated a larger proportion of plant‐based resources in their diet than low‐density populations, and laboratory colonies with access to greater sugar resources had larger colony sizes, higher reproduction rates and worker survival, and higher per capita activity levels and showed higher aggression and potency toward opponents. These results support the positive feedback mechanism between honeydew‐producing insects and YCAs that underlies the ecological meltdown on Christmas Island, and add to the growing body of literature illustrating the importance of mutualistic interactions in the success of invasive species.

    Introduction

    How invasive species successfully establish and impact their environment is a critical question in understanding the ecology and management of invasive species. Generally, the ability of an organism to successfully invade is thought to be determined by propagule pressure, the attributes of the recipient community, the traits of the invading organisms, and the impacts of other invaders (Lockwood et al. 2007, Catford et al. 2009, O'Loughlin and Green 2017). Invaders with broad niches may be more successful than those with narrow niches because they are more likely to encounter necessary resources and environmental conditions (niche breadth‐invasion success hypothesis; Vazquez 2005). Support for this hypothesis can be found in successful plant (Higgins and Richardson 2014), arthropod (Snyder and Evans 2006), and vertebrate (Sol 2007) invasions. In animals, flexibility in foraging behavior (Sol et al. 2002) and dietary switching (Caut et al. 2008) is associated with invasion success, allowing invaders to track fluctuating resources (Caut et al. 2008) and to switch to alternative resources in the presence of competitors (Olsson et al. 2009, Zhang et al. 2010, Jackson and Britton 2014).

    As generalist omnivores, dietary flexibility of invasive ants may contribute to their extraordinary abundance in their introduced range and their ability to impact native biodiversity and disrupt ecosystem function (Holway et al. 2002, Tillberg et al. 2007). In particular, dietary switching to more plant‐derived resources (e.g., extrafloral nectar, hemipteran‐produced honeydew) during an ant invasion is linked with elevated abundance (Tillberg et al. 2007, Wilder et al. 2011a) and decreased native ant diversity (Tillberg et al. 2007). Indeed, all five of the world's most damaging invasive ants are associated with honeydew‐producing insects in their introduced range (Helms 2013).

    The mechanistic role of carbohydrate‐based plant resources fueling invasive ant success has been studied primarily in terms of benefits to colony growth. Carbohydrates, specifically simple sugars, appear to be the key macronutrient in plant‐based resources that drive ant colony numbers (Wilder et al. 2011b), and laboratory studies demonstrate the benefit from carbohydrate resources to increase colony size in both the Argentine ant, Linepithema humile (Grover et al. 2007, Kay et al. 2010), and the red imported fire ant, Solenopsis invicta (Helms and Vinson 2008, Wilder et al. 2013, Wills et al. 2015).

    Less clear is the role of carbohydrate resources on invasive ant behavior. The resource imbalance hypothesis predicts that excess carbohydrates may be used as fuel to increase activity levels and shunted to the production of carbon‐based, nitrogen‐free defensive compounds (Davidson 1997). However, evidence for increased individual‐level activity and use of defensive compounds under increasing levels of carbohydrates has not been found in invasive ants. Per capita foraging rates may increase under moderate, but not high carbohydrate availability (Grover et al. 2007, Shik and Silverman 2013), or may not be linked to the relative amount of carbohydrates in the diet (Kay et al. 2010, Horn et al. 2013). Individual worker aggression may be similar under low and high carbohydrate levels (Grover et al. 2007) or may be not detected with an increasingly rich carbohydrate diet (Kay et al. 2010). When nutritional effects of carbohydrates on worker behavior are found, they appear to be due to sugar deprivation (Grover et al. 2007), with experimental designs often employing categorical dietary treatments (e.g., zero‐, low‐, and high‐concentration sugar treatments). Interpretation of carbohydrate effects in these experimental studies may be difficult to apply to conditions experienced by ants in nature, as the availability of plant‐based resource to a particular ant colony is more likely to exist on continuum, changing with seasonality, abundance of honeydew‐producing insects, or density of competitors.

    The yellow crazy ant (Anoplolepis gracilipes, YCA hereafter) is one of world's most destructive introduced species (Lowe et al. 2000), perhaps best known for its impacts on Christmas Island, Indian Ocean (O'Dowd et al. 2003). Across the island, YCA densities vary widely, from YCA‐free areas to areas where it can reach record‐breaking densities (Abbott 2005). In these high‐density areas, YCA extirpate an endemic land crab (the Christmas Island red crab Gecarcoidea natalis), the dominant keystone consumer on the forest floor. Without G. natalis, seedling recruitment and litter decomposition are deregulated (Green et al. 1997, 1999, 2008, O'Dowd et al. 2003), the abundance and species density of other non‐native ants and land snails increase (O'Dowd and Green 2009, O'Loughlin and Green 2015), and secondary invasion by the giant African land snail is facilitated (Green et al. 2011). YCA invasion is also associated with growth of sooty mold and canopy dieback (O'Dowd et al. 2003), as well as disruption of foraging, reproduction, and frugivory in endemic land birds (Davis et al. 2008, 2010).

    Field observations suggest that YCA abundance, and thus ecological impact, may be driven by plant‐based carbohydrate resources from honeydew‐producing insects. Wherever YCAs are found at high densities, they are found to tend extremely high densities of introduced honeydew‐producing scale insects (i.e., Tachardina aurantiaca (Kerriidae), Coccus spp. (Coccidae), and Saissetia sp. (Coccidae) [O'Dowd et al. 2003, Abbott 2004, Abbott and Green 2007, Neumann et al. 2016]). Experimental removal of YCAs has shown that honeydew‐producing scale insects rely upon YCAs for survival (Abbott and Green 2007), likely due to YCA removal of honeydew that prevents scale insect asphyxiation, husbandry of the scale insect mobile crawler stage, and protection of scale insects against natural enemies (Abbott 2004, Ness and Bronstein 2004).

    However, it is still unknown how YCAs may specifically benefit from this putative mutualism, and whether variation in the association with honeydew‐producing insects is also associated with the population density and impact (Helms 2013) of this invasive ant across the landscape.

    Here, we test the prediction that increased availability of carbohydrate resources drives the ecological dominance and impacts of YCAs. Specifically, we examine: (1) Do YCA colonies assimilate a greater proportion of plant‐based carbohydrates in their diet where they occur in densities known to have cascading impacts? and (2) Do carbohydrates drive YCA colony growth and individual behavior?

    Methods

    Resource use in low‐density and high‐density populations

    To determine whether resource use differs between YCA colonies occurring at different densities, we first located pairs of sites across Christmas Island at which YCAs naturally occurred in low vs. high densities. In May through September 2011, YCA activity densities were measured at ~1000 sites across Christmas Island during an island‐wide survey conducted by Christmas Island National Park. At each site, 11 white plastic cards were placed every 5 m along a 50‐m transect, and the number of YCAs crossing a 10 cm × 10 cm area in 30 s was recorded (Abbott 2005). Eight paired sites, all sampled in June, were identified that comprised one high‐density and one low‐density site such that, on average, there was a 20‐fold difference in ant activity between them. Sites within a pair were adjacent waypoints, separated by ~366 m, the distance between waypoints used in the survey (Green and O'Dowd 2009). The high‐ and low‐density sites fell either side of ~37 ants/30 s, the threshold at which YCAs kill Gecarcoidea natalis (Green and O'Dowd 2009), triggering an ecological meltdown (see Introduction).

    To determine whether resource use differs between YCAs occurring at low and high densities, we analyzed the ratio of stable isotopes of nitrogen (15N/14N, formulated as δ15N [per mil ‰]) in YCA tissue. The value of δ15N generally increases 3–4‰ per trophic level in insects (McCutchan et al. 2003), and in omnivorous insects increases as they assimilate a greater proportion of animal‐derived vs. plant‐derived resources (e.g., honeydew) in their diet (Davidson et al. 2003, Tillberg et al. 2007). Stable isotope analysis was also run on a plant common in all sites (Pandanus christmatensis) to ensure baseline δ 15N values (due to different decomposition rates, seabird guano deposition, etc.) do not differ among high‐density and low‐density YCA sites, potentially confounding YCA δ 15N values.

    At the end of each site survey, YCAs and Pandanus were hand‐collected, frozen, and dried to a constant weight. To determine nitrogen isotopic ratios, 1.0–1.5 mg of ant tissue and 2.5–3.0 mg of plant tissue were ground and weighed into tin capsules. Ant tissue (~5–10 workers) consisted of heads and altitrunks only. Gasters were removed to ensure stable isotope values represent dietary resources assimilated into body tissue, unbiased by recently ingested material stored in the crop.

    Samples were analyzed with continuous flow isotope ratio mass spectrometry at Natural Isotopes, Parkwood, Western Australia (20‐20 IRMS; Sercon, Crewe, UK), and the University of Western Australia, Perth (Delta V Plus with a Thermo Flush 1112 via ConFlo IV; Thermo‐Finnigan, Bremen Germany).

    Carbohydrate effects on colony growth and individual behavior

    We conducted a laboratory dietary experiment to determine the effects of carbohydrate availability on YCA colony growth and individual worker behavior. Colonies of YCAs were collected from a single rainforest site in Christmas Island National Park in June 2011. Queens and workers were extracted from the litter using a soil‐free extraction technique (Rowles and O'Dowd 2007) and transferred to 2.4‐L plastic containers with sides coated with Fluon. Colonies were housed indoors and exposed to ambient temperature and photoperiod. Each colony contained two queens and 200 workers. During the initial two‐week acclimation period, all colonies were fed unlimited sugar, water, and crickets to establish and stabilize colony numbers. Sugar availability was administered in a regression design, with levels ranging from 0, 10, 20, 40, 80, 160, 320, 640, and 1280 μL of a honeywater (Capilano Natural Australian Honey; Capilano Honey Limited, Richlands, QLD, Australia) solution provided to colonies during each feeding day. The honeywater solution was prepared at a 13% sugar concentration, the same concentration as honeydew naturally collected by YCAs in the Christmas Island rainforest (S. Wittman, unpublished data). Thus, the experiment was designed specifically to mimic a range of scale insect densities and their honeydew production. A pair of colonies was randomly assigned to each sugar level (n = 18 nests total). During the experiment, YCAs had unlimited access to water and freeze‐killed crickets. Honeywater was delivered every 3–4 d over a total of 53 d (=15 feeding days). The number of YCAs feeding on the honeywater solution ten minutes after delivery was recorded every week, and dead YCAs were removed and counted every five days. In the last week of the experiment, a novel, non‐food object (pyramid structure made of four bamboo skewers; modified from Grover et al. 2007) was carefully placed into nests to track YCA exploratory behavior. The number of YCA workers walking on the object was counted 1, 5, 10, and 15 min after its placement in the nest. This measures the passive patrolling of the environment by YCAs, not active recruitment to a food resource. Finally, on the last day of the experiment, we tested whether carbohydrate availability affected the propensity of YCAs to attack (spray formic acid) and the efficacy of those attacks by conducting aggression assays between YCAs and Pheidole megacephala (minor workers). Pheidole megacephala workers were collected from a single rainforest site into laboratory nests and allowed to establish for 24 h. Three YCA and three P. megacephala workers were placed in a 6 cm diameter vial with Fluon‐coated sides, and all interactions between YCA and P. megacephala were tracked immediately and video recorded for a total of ten minutes. Although three ants of each species were located in one vial, all behavioral interactions occurred on a one‐on‐one basis. The time of each YCA's first spray, the total number of sprays by each YCA, and the total number of P. megacephala dead at the end of the ten minutes were recorded for each laboratory colony. No YCAs died during the aggression assays. At the end of the experiment, all colonies were sacrificed and all workers and brood were counted. To determine whether colonies administered a greater volume of honeywater solution incorporated more of this resource into their diet, stable isotope analysis was also run on workers from each experimental colony. If colonies with greater carbohydrate availability fed upon this resource at higher rates, then workers should show lower δ15N values. Sample preparation and nitrogen isotopic ratio analysis occurred in the same manner described above.

    Analysis

    YCA activity densities and YCA and Pandanus δ 15N values from low‐density and high‐density sites were analyzed with a matched‐pairs analysis in JMP, version 11.0.0 (SAS Institute, Cary, North Carolina, USA).

    The response variables of the dietary experiment include colony size (eggs + larvae + pupae + adults) at the end of experiment, per capita number of dead workers, per capita number of workers patrolling novel object, and δ15N of workers at the end of the experiment. All per capita values are calculated based on the total number of adults alive at the end of experiment.

    In six of 18 trials, YCAs did not interact (made no contact) with P. megacephala. Thus, behavioral interactions are analyzed using all 18 trials (all trials) and only the 12 trials in which YCA and P. megacephala interacted (interaction trials).

    The total mg of carbohydrates delivered during the 15 feeding days of the experiment was calculated for each volume of honeywater solution administered. The relationship between total carbohydrates delivered and all response variables was evaluated with the Standard Regression Test module in EcoSim (Gotelli and Entsminger 2006), a randomization test analogous to a conventional regression test relieved of its parametric assumptions (Edgington 1995). EcoSim's Standard Regression compares the observed slope to the distribution of slopes from repeatedly reshuffled data.

    Results

    Resource use in low‐density and high‐density populations

    YCA activity densities were on average 20 times greater in designated high‐density sites (239.38 ants/30s ± 65.56 vs. 11.75 ants/30s ± 6.79; t = −8.75, P < 0.001; Fig. 1a). YCA occurring at high densities had lower δ15N values than YCAs occurring at low‐densities (10.13‰ ± 0.56 vs. 10.73‰ ± 0.50; t = −2.56, P = 0.019; Fig. 1b), indicating naturally occurring high‐density YCA populations have a greater proportion of plant‐derived resources in their diet than low‐density YCA populations. Pandanus δ15N values did not differ in high‐ vs. low‐density sites (t = −1.37, P = 0.107), indicating underlying δ15N pools were similar between sites.

    Carbohydrate effects on colony growth and individual behavior

    Both YCA behavior and stable isotope analysis indicate the dietary experiment successfully delivered a full functional range of carbohydrates to YCA colonies. Colonies that received the two highest sugar volumes often did not fully collect all of the solution between delivery dates, suggesting these quantities were in excess of colony needs. Lower recruitment also occurred at higher sugar levels (S. Wittman, unpublished results), likely a product of colony satiation in which sugar had becomes a less valued resource (Howard and Tschinkel 1980). Stable isotope analysis indicated that when colonies had access to a greater quantity of honeywater solution, workers assimilated a greater proportion of plant‐based resources in their body tissue. Colonies fed a greater volume of the honeywater solution contained workers with lower δ15N values at the end of the experiment (observed slope = −0.271, mean of simulated slopes = −0.005, P = 0.033; Fig. 2a). Final YCA δ15N values had both increased and decreased from the initial values (~9.8‰) of colonies at the beginning of the experiment (Fig. 2a). Thus, not only did foraging behavior differ across the different sugar levels, but the intake of this resource differed as well.

    Carbohydrate availability increased total colony size (observed slope = 131.29, mean of simulated slopes = −1.17, P = 0.001; Fig. 2b), with 2.5× greater number of adults in the highest vs. lowest sugar treatment (Fig. 2c). This was a product of both greater reproductive rates (observed slop = 64.20, mean of simulated slopes = −1.63, P = 0.001; Fig. 2b, eggs) and lower morality in high‐sugar treatment groups (observed slope = −0.0737, mean of simulated slopes = −0.0018, P = 0.026; Fig. 2d). A greater proportion of the colony explored the novel, non‐resource object in the high‐sugar treatments (observed slope = 0.00517, mean of simulated slopes = 0.00011, P = 0.018; Fig. 2e).

    In staged laboratory behavioral assays against Pheidole megacephala, YCAs with access to more carbohydrates sprayed sooner (all trials: observed slope = −43.17, mean of simulated slopes = 4.01, P = 0.200; interaction trials: observed slope = −89.17, mean of simulated slopes = −0.18, P = 0.006; Fig. 3a), sprayed more often (all trials: observed slope = 0.541, mean of simulated slopes  = 0.011, P = 0.018; interaction trials: observed slope = 0.962, mean of simulated slopes = 0.019, P < 0.001; Fig. 3b), and killed more of their opponents (all trials: observed slope = 0.388, mean of simulated slopes = −0.005, P = 0.077; interaction trials: observed slope = 0.628, mean of simulated slopes = −0.003, P = 0.003; Fig. 3c). YCAs that sprayed more often resulted in a greater number of P. megacephala dead at the end of the trial (observed slope = 0.852, mean of simulated = 0.0005, P < 0.001; Fig. 3d).

    Discussion

    Identifying how invasive ants achieve their extraordinary abundance is critical to understanding how invasive ants become deleterious to their environment (Wittman 2014). Previous work investigating the trophic ecology of invasive ants demonstrated that both Argentine ants (Tillberg et al. 2007) and red imported fire ants (Wilder et al. 2011a) occupy lower trophic positions in their invaded vs. their native range. On Christmas Island, comparing across naturally occurring high‐density and low‐density field sites, we also detect lower δ15N values of YCA workers, supporting the view that carbohydrate resources are sufficiently plentiful to alter the trophic ecology of YCAs (Fig. 1) and that dietary shifts occur at YCA densities known to cause cascading ecological impacts within this ecosystem.

    Results from the laboratory experiment support the stable isotope results from the field: High‐density YCA populations consumed a greater proportion of plant‐based resources (Fig. 1), and increased carbohydrate availability in the laboratory supported larger colonies (Fig. 2b) due to increased reproductive rates (Fig. 2b) and lower worker mortality (Fig. 2d). The mechanistic link between lower trophic positions and the success and impacts of invasive ants has generally focused on the role of honeydew subsidies on increased ant colony growth (Helms 2013 and references therein). While protein is needed for colony growth, growing evidence indicates that carbohydrate availability can boost colony size in insects (Murphy et al. 1983, Kay et al. 2006, Grover et al. 2007, Helms and Vinson 2008, Wilder et al. 2011b) and may be due to the combined positive effects of sugar at multiple stages. Oogenesis in insects is typically a nutrient‐limited process, triggered only with sufficient nourishment, and sugar availability is linked to egg production in butterflies and moths (Wheeler 1996). Greater carbohydrate availability may also benefit both adult and larval ant survival. Adult ants can only utilize liquid diets (Eisner and Happ 1962), and larvae are the only stage that can process solid prey resources, which are then redistributed through the colony via trophallaxis. When carbohydrate resources are available, adult energy supplies are fulfilled and will rely less upon larval‐processed resources. Thus, when adults are supplied with an abundant energy source, not only may adult survival increase, but more resources may also be available for larval growth. Finally, worker survival may suffer under relatively high‐protein diets (Maklakov et al. 2008, Dussutour and Simpson 2009), potentially due to costs associated with eliminating nitrogenous waste products (Lee et al. 2008).

    Invasive ants with access to larger quantities of carbohydrates may benefit from larger colony sizes simply due to a greater number of active ants. In Argentine ants, higher colony‐level activity levels occurred under conditions of increased carbohydrate resources (Kay et al. 2010, Shik and Silverman 2013). Carbohydrate impacts on individual‐level behavior, however, are less clear. Carbohydrate scarcity has been shown to impact per capita activity levels and aggression (Grover et al. 2007, Shik and Silverman 2013), with the strongest response found in low (not high)‐sugar treatments. The regression design employed in this study provided sugar along a continuum of zero to excess, and we found increasing per capita patrolling activity (Fig. 2e) and propensity and lethality of individual attacks (Fig. 3) with increasing sugar availability. These findings have an important distinction: Individual behavior in YCA is not linked to prolonged scarcity of carbohydrates (as in Grover et al. 2007), but rather shows an increasing ability of YCAs to shunt carbohydrates into per capita activity and chemical defense.

    The experimental results provide support of the resource imbalance model (Davidson 1997), which predicts both activity levels and production and use of chemical defenses increase with excess carbohydrates. Because adult ants rely primarily upon carbohydrates as fuel, excess carbohydrates may be used directly to increase worker activity levels and produce high tempo, frenzied behavior (Davidson 1997) often seen in (and basis of nomenclature for) YCA. Increased worker activity rates allow the colony to cover extreme foraging distances, especially relevant when transporting resources from tropical rainforest canopy treetops to the ground (Room 1975, O'Dowd et al. 2003, Abbott and Green 2007).

    As formicine ants, YCAs are able to produce and store large volumes of highly concentrated formic acid (Blum 1981), and a fully stocked poison gland of formic acid may provide a proximate cue to influence individual worker behavior (Davidson 1997). Because formic acid is a nitrogen‐free compound made of only carbon, oxygen, and hydrogen, surplus carbohydrates may be used for its production. As carbohydrates become more and more plentiful, individual YCAs may be able to produce larger and larger volumes of formic acid, resulting in a workforce that is more likely to act aggressively and effectively within its environment.

    Taken together, these data provide a mechanistic link to indicate how carbohydrate subsidies from honeydew‐producing insects may drive the abundance and potency of YCAs across the landscape and establish a positive feedback mechanism that underlies the ecological meltdown on Christmas Island. When YCAs have access to honeydew‐producing scale insects, YCA colonies are able to grow more quickly, patrol more efficiently, and tend more effectively those scale insects, which increase the volume and stability of their carbohydrate resources, further increasing the abundance and dominance of YCA across the landscape.

    This work adds to the growing body of literature illustrating the importance of mutualistic interactions in the success of invasive species, generally (Simberloff and Von Holle 1999, Simberloff 2006), and ant invasions, specifically (Helms 2013). Through greater understanding of how invasive ants can reach and sustain deleterious densities, we can better inform management programs. Current evidence suggests efforts that sever ant access to abundant carbohydrate resources or reduce honeydew‐producing insect abundance may help to control invasive ant densities and impacts.

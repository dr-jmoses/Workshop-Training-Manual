    Abstract

    Species distribution models for Amazonian trees have mostly been produced at scales and resolutions that are too broad and coarse for practical use in either conservation or forestry. On the other hand, several studies have shown that elevation and the medium‐resolution remote sensing data available via Landsat imagery can be successfully used to detect differences in plant species composition in Amazonia. Therefore, it seems likely that the same data can also be used to predict geographical distributions of individual taxa. Here we use remotely sensed data and a maximum entropy algorithm (MaxEnt) to generate landscape‐scale distribution models at 30‐m‐resolution for five economically important timber tree genera (Apuleia, Amburana, Crepidospermum, Dipteryx, and Manilkara). Individual Landsat Thematic Mapper bands and normalized difference vegetation index yielded acceptable model performance, and the use of averaging filters (3 × 3 and 5 × 5 pixel low‐pass filters) improved model performance further. Including elevation as a predictor also improved model performance for all the genera. Our results suggest that it is possible to use Landsat bands and elevation as predictors for modeling the potential distribution of tree species in lowland Amazonia at a fine enough resolution to facilitate the practical management of forest resources.

    Species distribution modeling (SDM) is used to predict species occurrences in areas for which field data are unavailable, and to deliver predictive maps of species distribution (Franklin & Miller 2010). These modeling approaches have become increasingly popular (Barbosa & Schneck 2015) and have benefitted from improved availability of open source information and the development of advanced statistical techniques as well as integration with geographic information systems (Guisan & Zimmermann 2000, Guisan & Thuiller 2005).

    Typically, the predictive variables in SDM are climatic factors or bioclimatic parameters of coarse spatial resolution applied over global to regional extents (Guisan & Thuiller 2005, Franklin & Miller 2010). Less research has been performed on modeling the distribution of species at smaller extents using predictors of higher spatial resolution (Thuiller et al. 2003, Franklin & Miller 2010, Barbosa & Schneck 2015, Oke & Thompson 2015). Nevertheless, this would be needed in many practical applications of SDM, such as the planning of land use, identification of important areas for conservation, and the management, monitoring, and protection of natural forest resources, such as timber species.

    An obvious source of medium resolution (tens or at most hundreds of meters) predictor data for SDM is remotely sensed information. It has been shown that remotely sensed continuous information of surface characteristics can be superior to classified land cover maps (Cord et al. 2013) and can contribute more to model performance than the more traditional climatic and topographic variables (Wen et al. 2015). Despite its potential in SDM, remote sensing remains underexplored (He et al. 2015), and medium‐resolution remotely sensed information has not been commonly used in the tropics. Some studies have documented correspondence between patterns in Landsat TM pixel values and floristic composition of Amazonian forests (Tuomisto et al. 2003a,b, Salovaara et al. 2005, Higgins et al. 2011, 2012). Prates‐Clark et al. (2008) used derived remote sensing data and bioclimatic variables at 5‐km spatial resolution to predict potential distribution of tree species in lowland Amazonia. Figueiredo et al. (2015) used remote sensing variables to predict the distribution of commercial trees in Amazonia at a finer 90‐m resolution.

    In many cases, it is not possible to model species distributions at high resolution, because accurate enough species occurrence and environmental data are not available. This is especially the case in remote areas with difficult access, such as much of Amazonia. Nevertheless, some Amazonian countries, such as Brazil and Peru, require all commercially valuable trees to be inventoried and individually georeferenced before logging can be carried out in forest concessions. Such high‐resolution occurrence data in combination with remotely sensed information on environmental variation could potentially provide the background data needed for SDM at medium spatial resolution. At least two studies in western Brazil (Figueiredo et al. 2015, 2016) have taken advantage of this possibility. Figueiredo et al. (2015) found that the normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) derived from Landsat Thematic Mapper (TM) imagery and elevation above sea level were useful environmental variables in predicting timber species distributions.

    Because landscape heterogeneity does not follow pixel limits, mixed pixels are common in satellite images, which affects the use of remotely sensed data in image classification (Fisher 1997, Cracknell 1998). It could be assumed that source data of a finer spatial resolution, and hence with a smaller proportion of mixed pixels, would yield more accurate distribution models than coarser‐resolution data. However, with diminishing size of the individual pixels, the focus of the variation in pixel values shifts toward smaller details of the landscape. Pixel size in Landsat TM data is comparable to that of the crown of a single large canopy tree, and much of the variation in pixel values is due to gap dynamics, that is, to what degree the signal is reflected from canopy trees vs. tree fall gaps in various stages of regrowth. When modeling habitat suitability for long‐living sedentary organisms like trees, such local variation due to gap dynamics, is mainly noise. Another problem is that matching field observations with the correct pixel become more difficult as the pixel size becomes smaller, especially inside closed‐canopy tropical forests where the GPS signal may be both weakened by the canopy and only received from a few satellites at a time.

    The effects of both local noise and georeferencing error can be diminished by applying averaging (smoothing) filters on the satellite image data (Jones & Vaughan 2010). Such filters suppress the high‐frequency variation (noise) between neighboring pixels but retain the background component (landscape variation), the latter being the component that is relevant in SDM. These filters assign to each pixel the average pixel value from its neighborhood, which both moderates extreme pixel values and increases the likelihood that the field observation comes from the area covered by the radiometric data. Earlier studies have found filtering to improve the correspondence between pixel data and floristic data (Tottrup 2004, Salovaara et al. 2005, Thessler et al. 2008). Although using a coarser‐resolution image would provide some averaging benefits as well, filtering gives more accurate results because it retains the original spatial resolution, and each filtered value is obtained from a window that is centered on (and thereby optimized for) the pixel it is assigned to.

    In this study, we model the distributions of tree species in southern Peruvian Amazonia by combining occurrence data from a timber concession's inventory with reflectance values from Landsat imagery and elevation data from the Aster satellite. We follow the same basic approach as Figueiredo et al. (2015) but with some modifications, aimed to further improve the performance of SDMs for Amazonian tree species at fine spatial resolution. Specifically, we ask: (1) if the combination of Landsat TM data and tree species occurrences recorded in forestry operations can be used to derive acceptable SDM in Amazonia; (2) if applying averaging filters to the Landsat images yields better modeling results; and (3) if individual Landsat TM bands add useful information for SDM or if NDVI alone is sufficient.

    Methods

    Study area and species occurrence data

    The study area covers about 2600 km2 in the Iberia district, in Madre de Dios, Southern Peruvian Amazonia (Fig. 1). Climate in the area is tropical and humid with monthly average temperatures between 22 and 25°C and average annual rainfall of 1860 mm (as extracted from WorldClim; Hijmans et al. 2005). The terrain is flat to undulating with elevation ranging between 200 and 400 m above sea level. The most extensive geological formation is Madre de Dios Formation (>90% of the study area) which was formed between the Neogene and Quaternary periods. More than 80% of the study area is covered by forests in which rubber trees and bamboo are abundant. Forestry and selective logging have been the main economic activities in the area for the past 25 years. Recent satellite images show that small‐scale agriculture is expanding in a part of the area, but our study was focused on the areas that still have a continuous forest cover.

    According to the Peruvian Forestry and Wildlife Law N° 29763, forest concessions have to make a forest census for each annual management area before initiating any logging activities. In this study, we use the forest census data produced by the forestry concession Consolidado Otorongo within their management area of the year 2012, which consists of two rectangular areas that together cover approximately 4000 ha. Commercial trees with diameter at breast height (dbh) at least 30 cm were tallied and their local name, height, dbh, volume, and position within the inventory line were recorded. Trees were inventoried using straight transects evenly distributed across the management area. The beginning and end of each transect were georeferenced using GPS devices. Individual trees were georeferenced in the field using a relative system of X and Y coordinates referring to the long axis of the transect and lateral displacement from it. Geographical coordinates were later assigned to each tree by combining their field‐measured within‐transect locations with the transect GPS coordinates. We used the tree position data to define presence and absence of each focal taxon in each Landsat pixel.

    We selected five commercial trees from the census data for modeling. The primary selection criterion was that the tree was abundant enough for robust modeling. We arbitrarily chose a minimum of 185 presence records as the lower limit, which is well above the recommended minimum of 20–50 observations (Guisan et al. 2007, Merow et al. 2014a, van Proosdij et al. 2016). The second selection criterion was that the taxonomic identification was considered sufficiently reliable at the genus level. Identification of tropical trees to biological species is difficult (Köhl et al. 2006), especially without voucher specimens. The census was performed for commercial rather than scientific purposes, so the trees were identified with local names only. Fortunately, many of these consistently refer to a single genus, so we searched the literature for publications that have matched local names with biological species (Aróstegui & Sato 1970, Reynel 2003), and also consulted the concessions’ general forest management plans and the annual operating plans delivered by Consolidado Otorongo.

    The five tree genera that fulfilled both selection criteria were used in the modeling part of the study: Apuleia, Amburana, Crepidospermum, Dipteryx, and Manilkara (Table 1). According to the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (consulted on 08/03/2018) and The Plant List (2013), Apuleia only has one species in Peruvian Amazonia, Amburana and Manilkara have two, Crepidospermum has four and Dipteryx five. However, not more than three species have been reported from the vicinity of our study area for any of the genera. Species of all five genera are shade tolerant and have slow growth rates (Reynel 2003).

    Remote sensing data

    We used a cloud‐free Landsat 5 TM image covering the study area (Path: 003, Row: 068) taken on 3 September 2011, that is, during the same year when the forest census was carried out. The image was downloaded from the US Geological Survey as a Level‐1 data product, which is already georeferenced to full precision. Given that our study area is entirely within a single image, and detailed atmospheric condition data for the acquisition date are not available, we used the raw pixel values without attempting atmospheric correction.

    Landsat TM has seven bands, and we used four of them as predictor variables in SDM. We used band 3 (red) from the visible spectrum of light and bands 4, 5, and 7 from the near and middle infrared spectrum. Bands 1 and 2 (blue and green, respectively) were excluded because they had extensive striping errors, and band 6 (thermal infrared) because its spatial resolution is only 120 m (vs. 30 m in the other bands). Even though methods exist to alleviate striping problems, we chose to include only the bands that were free of striping as predictors (bands 3, 4, 5, and 7). NDVI was also used as a predictor variable, derived from bands 3 and 4 as:

    Neighboring pixels in a Landsat image can have rather different pixel values due to, for example, gap dynamics. We tested if smoothing filters using 3 × 3 and 5 × 5 pixel windows help to produce better predictor variables for the models than do the original pixel values. As the fifth predictor variable in the models, we used elevation above sea level from ASTER GDEM (Aster Digital Global Elevation Model) S12W71 and S12W70. These data have the same 30‐m resolution as the Landsat image. Satellite data extraction and conversion was performed in QGIS 2.18. We will refer to the Landsat and ASTER data collectively as ‘environmental data’.

    Modelling process

    There are many alternative methods for performing SDM (Elith et al. 2006, Fitzpatrick et al. 2013, Cerdeira et al. 2014, Divino et al. 2015). We selected MaxEnt 3.4, which uses a maximum entropy algorithm (Phillips et al. 2017). It characterizes the background environment and thereby outperforms presence‐only models (Elith et al. 2006) and in general seems to perform equally well or better than other algorithms (Elith et al. 2006, Hernandez et al. 2006, Phillips et al. 2006, Wisz et al. 2008, Giovanelli et al. 2010, Merckx et al. 2011, Aguirre‐Gutiérrez et al. 2013, Merow & Silander 2014b, Villar Hernández & Pérez Elizalde 2015). Additionally, MaxEnt has been found to derive consistent predictions across different calibration areas (Giovanelli et al. 2010).

    Field data came from two rectangular census areas, which form part of Consolidado Otorongo concession management activities. We divided each of these areas longitudinally into two halves, such that one half of each census area could be used to build the SDM (training dataset) and the other to validate them (test dataset; Fig. 1) This division ensured that the training and test areas covered similar ranges of the environmental variables, but at the same time the points used for training vs. testing were spatially as separated as possible (given the available field data) to reduce the effect of spatial autocorrelation on the results.

    Before performing any analyses, we checked the range of pixel values in the training dataset for each of the five predictor variables separately. Any pixels falling outside of the training data range for any variable were masked out and excluded from all analyses. Thereby, the analyses were constrained to the environmental space covered by the training dataset and extrapolation of the predictions to undocumented environmental space was avoided. Model performance increases with the number of background points but performance barely increases between 10,000 and 80,000 points (Phillips & Dudík 2008). We used 50,000 background points, which were randomly selected from the unmasked part of the study area to extract background distribution of the predictor variables. Prior to modeling, we also combined multiple presence records of the same tree genus to retain just a single observation per Landsat pixel.

    Model performance was assessed through the area under the curve (AUC) of the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve. All models were built using the training dataset and validated using the test dataset as shown in Fig. 1. AUC values were calculated both for the training set (indicating how well the model fits the training data) and for the test data (indicating how well the model can be expected to make predictions using new data). We refer to these as training AUC and test AUC values, respectively. Relative contributions of predictor variables were estimated through jackknife test of variable importance. This resampling test allows to identify the individual effect of each predictor as well as the effect of omitting predictors from a full model that initially included all predictors.

    For model prediction, we used the default MaxEnt complementary log–log (cloglog) link function that in principle allows one to interpret the model output as a probability of presence (Phillips et al. 2017). However, as our data almost surely contained spatial autocorrelation, we do not apply this interpretation (Phillips et al. 2017) but instead use the model prediction as a relative indication of site suitability. All MaxEnt features (linear, quadratic, product, categorical, threshold, and hinge features) were used.

    Three models were produced for each tree genus using as predictors the Landsat TM data (bands 3, 4, 5, 7, and NDVI) from (1) non‐filtered images; (2) 3 × 3 filtered images; and (3) 5 × 5 filtered images. A second set of three models was produced with the same Landsat data but with elevation (DEM) as an additional predictor. Models with only Landsat TM data (whether filtered or not) are referred to as TM models, and models using Landsat TM data together with elevation data are referred to as TM + DEM models.

    Results

    Model performance

    Filtering generally improved model performance

    For the TM models, the inclusion of 3 × 3 filters increased the training AUC values by an average of 0.07 units and the test AUC values by 0.06 units, compared to models that used non‐filtered predictors. The average increase when using 5 × 5 filters was even larger: 0.10 units for the training AUC and 0.08 units for the test AUC. For the TM+DEM models, the inclusion of 3 × 3 filters increased the training AUC values by an average of 0.05 units and the test AUC values by 0.05 units, compared to models that used non‐filtered predictors. The average increase when using 5 × 5 filters was again larger: 0.07 units for the training AUC and 0.07 units for the test AUC.

    Discussion

    Model performance, predictors, and filters

    Previous studies have indicated that remote sensing variables can be used to complement bioclimatic and topographic variables and thereby improve the performance of SDM (Prates‐Clark et al. 2008, Figueiredo et al. 2015). A study by Figueiredo et al. (2015) found in southwestern Amazonian rain forests (Acre, Brazil) that for their target species, altitude, and NDVI were important predictor variables, whereas slope, aspect, and height above the nearest drainage barely contributed to model performance. Here we tested whether remote sensing variables on their own are adequate to provide useful models. Our results suggest that this is indeed the case: models based only on Landsat TM bands 3 (red), 4 (near infrared), 5 and 7 (both shortwave infrared) and NDVI (which is based on bands 3 and 4) yielded acceptable model performance for the distribution of tree genera in southern Peruvian Amazonia. We excluded the Landsat bands 1 (blue) and 2 (green) because they had severe striping problems. Their addition might improve the results further, but we chose to use only the error‐free bands here rather than try to remedy the striping problems (Sesnie et al. 2010).

    We also found that model performance was improved using the original TM bands in addition to NDVI, and by applying smoothing filters to the satellite imagery. In fact, both band 3 and band 4 were better predictors on their own than NDVI was. The importance of these particular bands is no doubt related to the fact that photosynthetizing vegetation absorbs a large proportion of the incident red radiation and reflects a high proportion of the near infrared radiation (Jones & Vaughan 2010). Because NDVI incorporates information from both of these bands, it is often used as a general index of vegetation greenness.

    Several authors have used different window sizes or filters in floristic studies. For example, Salovaara et al. (2005) used 5 × 5 pixel filters and 12 × 15 windows on Landsat images in vegetation classification and found that low‐pass filtering increased the accuracy of forest classification. Rajaniemi et al. (2005) also found that the use of moderate size pixel windows (7 × 7) instead of smaller windows or single pixels produced a better fit with plant species composition data. In agreement with these studies, we observed that a coarser averaging filter of 5 × 5 pixels performed better than a smaller 3 × 3 filter, and both filters were better than single pixel resolution.

    Figueiredo et al. (2015) found that the most important predictor variable for all SDM was elevation. In our study, elevation was also an important predictor, as it improved model performance for all tree genera. Elevation is an indirect environmental variable, that is, as such it does not affect the performance of biological organisms. However, often such environmental characteristics that do affect species occurrence are correlated with elevation, which can serve as an easily measurable proxy for them (Hof et al. 2012). The range of elevations was too small to be related to climate variation both in our study and in the one by Figueiredo et al. (2015), but elevation can also be related to geological substrate and thereby soil nutrient status in Amazonia (Vormisto et al. 2004, Costa et al. 2005, Higgins et al. 2011). Soils are a relevant factor for SDM (Figueiredo et al. 2018), but accurate enough soil data are not available for our study area. There are soil maps at a nominal 1‐km spatial resolution, but they have been found to contain major inaccuracies (Moulatlet et al. 2017). Topography is also related to hydrology and sedimentation (Wittmann et al. 2004), including the difference between inundated and terra firme forests. More subtle changes in drainage can also be related to local topography (Vormisto et al. 2004, Moulatlet et al. 2014), although slope is not necessarily a strong surrogate of soil moisture and drainage (Zuquim et al. 2009). Indeed, Figueiredo et al. (2015) found that height above the nearest drainage (HAND) and slope contributed little to the performance of SDMs. For these reasons, we only included elevation as a topographic predictor in our study.

    Our field data were highly concentrated to just one part of the study area. Random sampling across the entire study area would no doubt have given more robust results, but given poor accessibility in the area, it would have been impossible in practice. In this research, we took advantage of field data that were available from existing forest inventories. We avoided extrapolating predictions to undocumented environmental space, but suitability values close to the training dataset should nevertheless be considered more accurate than values further away.

    The reliability of data from forestry surveys in Amazonia has been challenged due to the ambiguity of vernacular names and complex tree taxonomy (Lacerda & Nimmo 2010, Guitet et al. 2014). For instance, Baraloto et al. (2007) found that only 50% of vernacular names corresponded to a single species of woody plants in Southern Amazonia. On the positive side, in most cases, the identifications have been found accurate at the genus level. Earlier analyses have also shown that there is considerable ecological signal in genus‐level and even family‐level data (Higgins & Ruokolainen 2004, Emilio et al. 2010), which justifies analyses at courser taxonomic resolution. In our study area, each genus is likely to contain only one or a few species. Nevertheless, misidentifications and species lumping can be present in our data. However, it is important to notice that such errors will only make it harder to identify any non‐random patterns. Accordingly, SDMs based on more accurate taxonomic data than those we used here might show better model performance.

    Implications for forest management

    Modeling the distribution of important tree species in Amazonia at landscape level using remote sensing imagery could contribute to mapping areas that are important for conservation, as well as areas that are suitable for forestry management purposes. Our study is among the first that have aimed at mapping tree genera using medium resolution satellite imagery. The results were promising, and the technical improvements over earlier modeling exercises (Figueiredo et al. 2015, 2016) open a possibility for making estimates of tree species occurrences in non‐inventoried forest areas.

    Estimating the distribution of tree species in Amazonia should be useful for conservation agencies, forestry companies, local and regional governments and possibly other entities interested in land use, conservation planning and forest resource management. Examples of potential applications include identifying areas suitable for species under conservation status and facilitating forest management planning by preliminary assessment of forest resources in areas where fieldwork is still missing.

    An interesting research question for the future is to assess whether bigger trees can be modeled better than smaller trees. It would be interesting to assess also if species abundances might be estimated using remotely sensed predictor variables. Such models could be used, for example, to assess likely timber volumes in forestry concessions, and to evaluate whether timber volumes claimed to originate from a given area are realistic.

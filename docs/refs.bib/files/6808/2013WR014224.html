    Abstract

    Flooding is the product of complex interactions among spatially and temporally varying rainfall, heterogeneous land surface properties, and drainage network structure. Conventional approaches to flood frequency analysis rely on assumptions regarding these interactions across a range of scales. The impacts of these assumptions on flood risk estimates are poorly understood. In this study, we present an alternative flood frequency analysis framework based on stochastic storm transposition (SST). We use SST to synthesize long records of rainfall over the Charlotte, North Carolina, USA metropolitan area by “reshuffling” radar rainfall fields, within a probabilistic framework, from a 10 year (2001–2010) high‐resolution (15 min, 1 km2) radar data set. We use these resampled fields to drive a physics‐based distributed hydrologic model for a heavily urbanized watershed in Charlotte. The approach makes it possible to estimate discharge return periods for all points along the drainage network without the assumptions regarding rainfall structure and its interactions with watershed features that are required using conventional methods. We develop discharge estimates for return periods from 10 to 1000 years for a range of watershed scales up to 110 km2. SST reveals that flood risk in the larger subwatersheds is dominated by tropical storms, while organized thunderstorm systems dominate flood risk in the smaller subwatersheds. We contrast these analyses with examples of potential problems that can arise from conventional frequency analysis approaches. SST provides an approach for examining the spatial extent of flooding and for incorporating nonstationarities in rainfall or land use into flood risk estimates.

    1 Introduction

    Flood response is the product of interactions of spatially and temporally varying rainfall [e.g., Arnoud et al., 2002; Ramos et al., 2005; Norbiato et al., 2007; Wright et al., 2013b], land surface properties [e.g., Mejia and Moglen, 2010; Wright et al., 2012], subsurface properties [e.g., Sivapalan et al., 1987; Smith et al., 2002; Borga et al., 2007], and drainage network structure [e.g., Smith et al., 2002, 2005a; Morin et al., 2006; Meierdiercks et al., 2010; Yang et al., 2013]. Flood frequency analysis for a particular location along a drainage network, therefore, should consider these interactions at the specific time and length scales of upstream rainfall, runoff production, and channel flow processes. These interactions can be particularly complex in urban settings where flow path lengths are short, runoff velocities are high, and the spatial distribution of land use and hydrologic infrastructure is highly heterogeneous [e.g., Schilling, 1991; Berne et al., 2004]. In practice, a range of approaches are used to assess flood risk depending on the location, data availability, watershed characteristics, and other factors. While these approaches can vary substantially, they all employ a range of assumptions regarding rainfall and its interactions with watershed features across a range of scales.

    In this study, we present an alternative multiscale flood frequency analysis framework that couples the concept of stochastic storm transposition (SST) with high‐resolution radar rainfall fields and a distributed physics‐based hydrologic model. SST is a technique for rainfall and flood frequency analysis that attempts to reconstruct the long‐term regional climatology of extreme rainfall and flooding by performing “space‐for‐time substitution” within a rigorous probabilistic framework. SST aims to effectively “lengthen” the rainfall record by resampling rainfall from a catalog of observed storms not only from the location of interest but also from the surrounding region. The inclusion of additional observations of nearby storms at least partially addresses the challenges of accurately estimating rainfall and flood hazards from short observation records. In addition, the SST‐based frequency analysis framework explicitly considers the interactions of rainfall with watershed features at the appropriate spatial and temporal scales to generate unique flood frequency estimates at every point of the drainage network.

    The general SST framework and associated challenges are described in Foufoula‐Georgiou [1989] and Fontaine and Potter [1989]. The method is employed for rainfall frequency analysis in Wilson and Foufoula‐Georgiou [1990] and Wright et al. [2013b], and extended to flood frequency analysis in Franchini et al. [1996].

    Wright et al. [2013b] presents an SST framework that has advantages over earlier studies because it uses high‐resolution radar rainfall fields, thus incorporating “intraevent” as well as “interevent” rainfall variability at the fine spatial and temporal resolutions necessary for urban flood risk assessment. Here we couple that framework with a physics‐based distributed hydrologic model for flood frequency analysis to examine the interactions of extreme rainfall with distributed land surface, subsurface, and drainage network features in a 110 km2 urban watershed. We compare the results against a simple “design storm” approach to highlight some of the advantages of the SST framework. We also use a simple storm classification system to illustrate the importance of time and length scales of rainfall and watershed hydrologic processes in flood response.

    Historically, modeling of the interactions among spatial and temporal rainfall variability with watershed features has been hindered by a lack of high‐resolution rainfall fields and of computationally efficient distributed hydrologic models. With the advent of the National Weather Service (NWS) Next‐Generation Radar network (NEXRAD, Heiss et al. [1990]), high‐resolution rainfall estimation has become routine across most of the United States. Bias‐corrected weather radar offers accurate high‐resolution estimates of extreme rainfall fields [e.g., Smith et al., 1996; Fulton et al., 1998; Baeck and Smith, 1998; Vieux and Bedient, 1998; Krajewski and Smith, 2002; Creutin and Borga, 2003; Einfalt et al., 2004; Villarini et al., 2010; Krajewski et al., 2010a; Smith et al., 2012; Wright et al., 2013c], and data records are reaching sufficient length (10+ years) to permit their use in rainfall and flood frequency analysis [see Overeem et al., 2009, 2010; Wright et al., 2013b]. Advances in physics‐based distributed hydrologic modeling, meanwhile, have provided computationally efficient platforms for examining the relationships between rainfall, runoff production, and channel processes at multiple scales in ways that cannot be accomplished with lumped models [see, e.g., Downer and Ogden, 2004]. As argued throughout this paper and by The National Research Council Committee on Levees and the National Flood Insurance Program Improving Policies and Practices [National Research Council, 2013], these modern data sets and modeling platforms are not only useful as research tools, but also provide opportunities for modernizing a wide range of flood risk management practices including infrastructure, physical planning, and flood insurance.

    2 Comparison of Flood Frequency Analysis Techniques

    Conventional flood frequency analyses make use of either rainfall‐runoff models or discharge‐based statistical models. Both approaches employ a range of simplifying assumptions regarding the interactions of flood‐producing processes and how these vary across the drainage network. The SST‐based framework, in contrast, explicitly considers these interactions across a range of scales.

    Frequency analyses based on rainfall‐runoff models use assumptions to link flood discharge estimates across all or part of the drainage network to the time and length scales of rainfall, runoff, and channel processes associated with a single user‐specified point (often the watershed outlet). These assumptions can include idealized area reduction factors (ARFs) [see Svensson and Jones, 2010] and spatially or temporally idealized design storms. A 1:1 return period equivalence between rainfall and modeled discharge is often assumed. The modeler must develop a T‐year storm event with the particular properties that are needed to accurately estimate the T year flood response at a specific point. The design storm approach, commonly used in small‐scale infrastructure design, attempts to resolve this challenge through intensity‐duration‐frequency (IDF) relationships, in which intensity and duration for given return period T are linked through some time‐based measure of flood response such as the time of concentration. Flood response at a particular location depends on a unique set of upstream watershed properties, and so the modeler should develop a unique storm design for every point along the drainage network. This is impractical, and so the modeler must instead select a portion of the drainage network over which a given storm adequately reflects the rainfall properties that produce T year flood response. This is nontrivial, and we are not aware of any guidance in the literature for making this selection.

    In this study, we use the design storm approach to illustrate that insufficient consideration of this challenge can lead to significant underestimation of flood risk. Moreover, we show that attempts to generate simple guidelines to help with this decision making would likely prove unsatisfactory. Practicing engineers are well aware of this challenge, and design storms are not often used outside of drainage infrastructure design. Rather, there has been a trend toward more realistic representations of extreme rainfall, in which the rainfall intensity from IDF relationships is combined with observed temporal or spatial rainfall distributions from nearby storms (M. Konieczki, personal communication, 2013). Additionally, several such storms with different spatial or temporal characteristics are often used. These efforts improve flood risk estimates, but do not resolve the fundamental question of how to properly link rainfall and discharge at all points along the drainage network. Continuous simulation approaches can address this question [see Bradley et al., 1996], but are limited to areas in which long and relatively complete records of extreme rainfall and other meteorologic data are available.

    Common statistical approaches for flood frequency analysis based on historical discharge records, such as U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) regional flood frequency equations or those outlined Bulletin 17B [Interagency Advisory Committee on Water Data (IACWD), 1982], require the assumption of temporal stationarity. They also do not explicitly consider the physical processes that produce floods [see Dawdy et al., 2012, and references therein]. Many 20th century stream gaging records in the United States exhibit nonstationarity due to river regulation and land use change [see Villarini et al., 2009a; Villarini and Smith, 2010; Villarini et al., 2011a]. Nonstationarity can be particularly pronounced in urbanizing areas [Villarini et al., 2009b, 2013; Wright et al., 2012]. Climate change impacts on rainfall and other hydrologic processes can further undermine the stationarity assumption. Statistical methods for frequency analysis under nonstationary conditions exist (see Khaliq et al. [2006] for a review) and can be useful for assessing past hydrologic changes. Because they are not physically based, however, it is unclear how these methods can be used to project future conditions.

    Estimating flood risk at ungaged locations using statistical methods is also a challenge. The analyst is forced to make assumptions regarding the statistical relationships between the ungaged location and one or more stream gaging sites, either in the same river system or from nearby basins. In the United States, for example, it is recommended to use the Log‐Pearson Type III distribution for flood frequency analysis, and Bulletin 17B provides “regional skew coefficients” for this purpose. Such methods are generally unsuitable for watersheds with substantial human impacts because the flood‐producing processes upstream of a particular point can deviate substantially from regional properties and suggested statistical distributions and parameters may be inadequate (M. Konieczki, personal communication, 2013).

    The results of conventional flood frequency analyses, therefore, are subject to joint errors arising from multiple assumptions affecting a range of interrelated physical processes. These assumptions are sometimes invoked without understanding the uncertainties that underlie them, and the impacts of these assumptions on subsequent risk estimates have not been quantified. For example, Wright et al. [2013a] demonstrate that commonly used ARFs are estimated using formulations that conflate storms with very different properties and fail to represent the significant variability in the relationship between observed point and area‐averaged rainfall extremes. A number of idealized numerical experiments point to the importance of rainfall variability and motion in flood formation [e.g., Ogden et al., 1995; Morin et al., 2006; Yakir and Morin, 2011; Seo et al., 2012; Seo and Schmidt, 2012], while the role of these processes is less clear when real storms and real watersheds are considered [e.g., Smith et al., 2002, 2005a; Tarolli et al., 2013].

    Physics‐based distributed rainfall‐runoff modeling approaches, such as that described in Cunha et al. [2011] and the SST‐based framework presented here, have advantages over statistical methods, particularly in nonstationary conditions and in heterogeneous settings such as urban watersheds. Namely, the modeler can incorporate current or projected land use or rainfall changes in order to simulate flooding along the drainage network.

    The SST‐based framework also has several advantages over other rainfall‐runoff modeling approaches. First, since it relies on storm rainfall structure and motion observed by radar, it frees the modeler from the aforementioned challenge of developing storms and deciding how storm properties should be linked to flood frequency at various points along the drainage network. It produces unique flood frequency estimates at every point of the drainage network that explicitly incorporate the interactions of rainfall with watershed features at the appropriate spatial and temporal scales.

    Second, since the SST framework uses observed rainfall to drive the risk estimation, it allows the examination of how realistic interevent and intraevent rainfall variability translates to variability in flood response. For example, a storm that produces a 100 year peak discharge at a particular point may only produce a 10 year peak discharge at another point up or downstream. Similarly, a different storm that also produces a 100 year peak discharge at that same point will produce a different pattern of discharge response elsewhere in the drainage network. This capability is particularly important for insurers, who need to predict the spatial extent of potential future losses from individual and multiple flood events.

    3 Study Region and Data

    The study region is centered on Little Sugar Creek in Charlotte, North Carolina, USA metropolitan area. Charlotte is well suited for flood hydrology research due to the quantity and quality of data resources and the variety of flood‐producing hydrometeorological processes [see, e.g., Smith et al., 2002; Turner‐Gillespie et al., 2003; Villarini et al., 2010; Wright et al., 2013c]. Like much of the east coast of the United States, flood risk in Charlotte is the product of both tropical cyclones and organized thunderstorm systems [see, e.g., Villarini and Smith, 2010; Smith et al., 2011; Wright et al., 2013a, 2013b]. Specifically, tropical cyclones produce long‐duration rainfall over large areas that controls the upper tail of flood risk in large watersheds [Villarini et al., 2011b] while spatially small short‐duration, high‐intensity, and rainfall from organized thunderstorm systems tends to produce extreme flooding in small urban watersheds [Smith et al., 2005b; Ntelekos et al., 2007].

    To compare the impacts of rainfall time and length scales on watersheds of different scales, we focus on four urbanized subwatersheds of Little Sugar Creek, ranging in size from 6.7 to 110 km2 and located within the Charlotte metropolitan area (Figure 1). We refer to these watersheds by their USGS stream gage site names. Characteristics of the four study watersheds can be found in Table 1. Little Sugar Creek at Archdale (110 km2) drains much of downtown Charlotte and encompasses Little Hope Creek at Seneca (6.7 km2), Little Sugar Creek at Medical Center (31 km2), and Briar Creek below Colony (49 km2). All four subwatersheds are extensively urbanized, but vary in the type and age of development and associated storm water management infrastructure. Rainfall and flooding in subbasins of Little Sugar Creek have been examined in Smith et al. [2002], Turner‐Gillespie et al. [2003], Villarini et al. [2009b], Villarini et al. [2010], and Wright et al. [2013c].

    This study uses 10 years (2001–2010) of high‐resolution (15 min, 1 km2) Hydro‐NEXRAD radar rainfall fields based on volume scan reflectivity observations from the NWS‐operated Weather Surveillance Radar 1988 Doppler (WSR‐88D) radar in Greer, South Carolina (radar code KGSP, see the top left map of Figure 1). The Hydro‐NEXRAD processing system was developed to generate radar rainfall estimates for hydrologic applications by converting three‐dimensional polar‐coordinate volume scan reflectivity fields from NWS WSR‐88D radars into two‐dimensional Cartesian surface rainfall fields [Krajewski et al., 2010b]. The standard convective rainfall‐reflectivity (Z‐R) relationship (R = aZb, where a = 0.017, b = 0.714; R is rain rate in mm h−1, and Z is radar reflectivity in mm6 m−3), a 53 dBZ hail threshold, and several standard quality control algorithms are used (see Seo et al. [2011] for more details). No range correction algorithms are used in this study. The data set has been extensively validated in Wright et al. [2013c] and used for rainfall frequency analysis in Wright et al. [2013b]. In this paper, mean field bias correction of the radar rainfall is done at the daily scale using 71 rain gages from the Charlotte Raingage Network (CRN) [see Wright et al., 2013c], operated by the USGS. In this study, we also use radar and rain gage data for the remnants of Hurricane Danny, which caused catastrophic flooding in much of the Charlotte area on 23–24 July 1997 [see Villarini et al., 2010].

    In this study, we generate two sets of SST‐based flood frequency results, one using all storm types (tropical and nontropical) and the other using only nontropical storms (mainly organized thunderstorm systems). As previously mentioned, the rainfall time and length scales vary between tropical and nontropical storms, and so this simple classification system serves as a useful way to examine the impacts of rainfall temporal and spatial scales on flooding [see Wright et al., 2013a, 2013b]. Rainfall associated with tropical cyclones is identified using the Hurricane Database (HURDAT) [see Jarvinen et al., 1984; Neumann et al., 1993] from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) National Hurricane Center. Any rainfall occurring 12 h before to 12 h after a HURDAT storm track passes within 500 km of Charlotte is classified as tropical in origin. Hart and Evans [2001], Kunkel et al. [2010], and Villarini and Smith [2010] use similar classification criteria for tropical cyclone rainfall and flooding.

    The USGS maintains a dense network of stream gages in the Charlotte metropolitan area. Streamflow data are available for the four study watersheds at 15 min resolution for the 2001–2010 study period as well as for the 23–24 July 1997 storm, and are used for validating the hydrologic model.

    4 GSSHA Hydrologic Model

    In this study, we use the Gridded Surface Subsurface Hydrologic Analysis (GSSHA) model to simulate multiscale flood response. GSSHA is a physics‐based distributed‐parameter hydrologic modeling system that uses explicit finite‐volume solutions of the diffusive wave formulation of the St. Venant equations of motion in two dimensions on a structured grid to simulate overland flow and in one dimension to simulate channel flow [Downer and Ogden, 2004, 2006]. Simulations of flood response across a range of scales due to the interactions of spatiotemporally distributed rainfall and heterogeneous basin properties can be adversely impacted by calibration, since calibrated parameters may only be suitable within a limited range of scales [see, e.g., Beven, 1995]. Rather than extensively calibrate the model, we use detailed geographic information system (GIS)‐based input data sets and parameter estimates from the literature. It should be noted that the SST‐based approach to flood frequency analysis can be implemented using other distributed hydrologic modeling platforms. The advantages and disadvantages of particular modeling platforms are beyond the scope of this paper.

    GSSHA offers three options for simulating infiltration: two Green‐Ampt schemes and one Richard's equation‐based scheme. Due to computational constraints and substantial layering of soils in Charlotte evident in the Soil Survey Geographic Database (SSURGO; developed and maintained by the National Resource Conservation Service of the U.S. Department of Agriculture), we have selected the three‐layer Green‐Ampt infiltration scheme. The spatial distribution of soil groups can be seen in the bottom right map of Figure 1 and the full set of infiltration parameters for all soil groups and layers is shown in Table 2. Model saturated hydraulic conductivity values for the soil groups are taken as one‐tenth of those given in the SSURGO database, and are roughly consistent with values used in Ogden et al. [2011], who also use GSSHA's three‐layer Green‐Ampt scheme for urban flood simulations. The remaining soil parameters are based on suggested estimates found on the GSSHA Wiki (http://www.gsshawiki.com, accessed 23 January 2013). We adjust the percentage of the grid cell that is able to infiltrate according to the percent impervious cover map from the 2006 National Land Cover Data set (NLCD) data set (Figure 1, top rightmap). We do not use the standard GSSHA options for retention or interception, which are unlikely to play significant roles in major urban floods.

    The role of soil moisture in urban flooding is unclear [see Pitt and Lantrip, 1999; Shi et al., 2007; Smith et al., 2013]. The spatial distribution of soil moisture is highly variable in space and time and tied not only to land cover, soil properties, and meteorological conditions but also to site‐specific construction (particularly soil compaction) and subsequent land use management practices [Gregory et al., 2006; Pouyat et al., 2010]. We are not aware of any urban area that has spatially extensive long‐term soil moisture observations. In light of this challenge, many frequency analyses assume “wet” initial soil moisture conditions for simplicity. We adopt this approach, using a initial soil moisture content equal to 90% of saturation for all validation and SST simulations.

    Storm event runoff production in small urban watersheds is predominantly Hortonian (infiltration excess) runoff due to the high degree of impervious cover and fast response times. For this reason, we neglect groundwater and subsurface runoff production, and any rainfall that infiltrates in our simulations will not be able to move to the channel network. It should be noted, however, that GSSHA does provide capabilities for simulating lateral groundwater flow, subsurface runoff, and base flow.

    We have modified the original GSSHA source code so that the Manning's roughness coefficient for 1‐D channel flow can vary across the width of a channel cross section. This enables the simulation of the effects of varying channel and floodplain roughness without resorting to the computationally expensive 2‐D overbank flow routine. We use cross‐sectional geometry and Manning's roughness coefficients for 328 surveyed cross sections available from FEMA [2009]. These roughness coefficients range from 0.040 to 0.050 for channels and 0.100 to 0.200 for floodplains. For lower‐order streams, where no surveyed cross‐sectional data are available, we assume a trapezoidal shape with cross‐sectional geometry estimated from relationships given in Doll et al. [2002], and we assume a Manning's roughness coefficient of 0.040. We also include 46 bridge crossings and 53 culverts derived from FEMA [2009] and 140 storm drains based on a stormwater inventory data set developed by Charlotte‐Mecklenburg County Stormwater Services (http://charmeck.org/stormwater/, accessed 20 May 2013). Bridge crossings, culverts, and storm drains are included as channel links with the appropriate cross‐sectional geometry. Concrete channels and culverts are assigned a roughness value of 0.024 (taken from Ogden et al. [2011]). Storm drains smaller than 0.91 m (3 ft) in diameter are not included.

    GSSHA has the functionality to simulate detention ponds and other water bodies. We do not include detention ponds or other water bodies in this study due to insufficient information regarding pond volumes, outlet structure configurations, and rating curves. The impact of detention ponds on flood response at the watershed scale is poorly understood [e.g., Emerson et al., 2005; Goff and Gentry, 2006; Fang et al., 2010; Meierdiercks et al., 2010; Wright et al., 2012; Smith et al., 2013] but likely decreases with increasing flood magnitude.

    5 SST Procedure

    A detailed description of the SST‐based rainfall frequency analysis procedure can be found in Wright et al. [2013b]. An abbreviated description, including extension of the method to flood frequency analysis, follows:

    1. Identify a spatial “domain” that contains the watershed of interest and over which the extreme rainfall climatology is homogeneous. In this study, we use a 3600 km2 domain centered on Little Sugar Creek at Archdale. See Wright et al. [2013b] for detailed examination and discussion of the homogeneity assumption.

    2. From the rainfall data set, identify the m largest t hour duration rainfall accumulations within the domain. This set of storms is henceforth referred to as a “storm catalog.” We refer to the m storms in the storm catalog as “parent storms.” Since the goal of the procedure is to estimate rainfall and flood exceedance probabilities of a specific watershed, the m storms are selected based on rainfall accumulations of the shape and orientation of that watershed. For example, the principal axis of Little Sugar Creek at Archdale is oriented roughly north‐south and has an area of 110 km2, so the m storms that constitute the t‐hour storm catalog are those associated with the m highest t‐hour rainfall accumulations over an area of 110 km2 with the same size, shape, and orientation as Little Sugar Creek. In the event that two or more distinct t‐hour periods of heavy rainfall occurred within 24 h, only the period with the higher rainfall accumulation is included in the catalog.

    The m parent storms are selected from an n year record, such that an average of λ = m/n storms per year are included in the storm catalog. We then assume that the number of annual storm occurrences follows a Poisson distribution with a rate parameter equal to λ storms per year. In this study, m = 50 storms over the 10 year radar data set, leading to λ = 5.0 storms per year. When tropical systems are excluded from the SST procedure, λ is decreased proportionally. The assumption of Poisson‐distributed storm occurrences is also used in Wilson and Foufoula‐Georgiou [1990] and Wright et al. [2013b].

    3. Randomly select a parent storm from the storm catalog. The homogeneity assumption allows us to assume that this storm could have occurred with equal likelihood anywhere within the domain. Accordingly, the radar rainfall fields are transposed an east‐west distance Δx and a north‐south distance Δy, where Δx and Δy are drawn from uniform distributions (see Wilson and Foufoula‐Georgiou [1990] for relaxation of this assumption, and Wright et al. [2013b] for further discussion). Since the radar data used in this study have a 15 min temporal resolution, a 1 h storm consists of four periods, for example, all transposed by the same Δx and Δy. The motion and structure of the parent storm, therefore, is unaltered during transposition and only the location is changed. This step is illustrated schematically at the 1 h scale for the 27 June 2006 storm in Figure 2 (reprinted from Wright et al. [2013b]). This procedure is repeated for a randomly selected subset of k storms where k is a Poisson‐distributed number of storm occurrences with rate parameter λ storms per year. This step can be understood as “reshuffling” observed storms in space and time within a probabilistic framework to synthesize a year of heavy rainfall over the domain.

    As noted in Wright et al. [2013b], the transposition step of SST is similar to that used in the estimation of the Probable Maximum Precipitation (PMP), except that transposition for PMP is performed deterministically to maximize rainfall over the watershed, rather than probabilistically, as in SST. The “peak‐to‐volume” method in Bradley et al. [1996] also makes use of deterministic storm transposition.

    4. For each of the k transposed storms, compute the t hour basin‐averaged rainfall accumulation in the watershed of interest. The largest of the k rainfall accumulations is retained. This step can be understood as the synthesis of one annual maximum t hour basin‐average rainfall accumulation (henceforth referred to as a “storm”) for the watershed.

    5. Repeat steps 3 and 4 to synthesize multiple years of t hour “annual” rainfall maxima for the watershed of interest. This procedure of repeatedly selecting subsets of data to make inferences is referred to as resampling. In this study, steps 3 and 4 are repeated 1000 times and the ordered series of “annual maxima” are then used to generate rainfall return period estimates of up to 1000 years. In this study, multiple realizations of 1000 year series are used in order to examine the variability in flood response of a given return period.

    6. For each synthetic annual rainfall maxima, a GSSHA simulation is performed and the resulting discharge time series is recorded at a large number of channel nodes. In this way, the peak discharges at each node for each simulation can be identified and ordered. In this study, flood response for return periods up to 1000 years are estimated from the ordered peaks.

    It should be noted that discharge return periods generated using this procedure need not match the return period of the rainfall used to generate them. The relationship between rainfall and discharge return period is examined in the following section. A variety of other analyses can be performed on the rainfall and discharge results generated using this procedure, some of which are examined in Wright et al. [2013b] and some of which are explored in section 7 of this paper.

    6 Model Validation

    We validate our GSSHA model of Little Sugar Creek using USGS streamflow observations at four gaging locations corresponding to the outlets of the four study subwatersheds for 10 events: the 23–24 July 1997 storm and nine storms that produced the nine largest flood peaks at Little Sugar Creek at Archdale over the 2001–2010 period.

    The central tendency of flood response is captured by the model (Figure 3). Some systematic bias in streamflow exists in modeled peak discharge, particularly for Little Hope Creek at Seneca. This bias could not be eliminated by varying model parameters and appears tied to small‐scale storm water drainage infrastructure that is not included in this study (results not shown). Several of the validation storms consist of two or more distinct pulses of extreme rainfall. Points that stray substantially from the 1:1 line in the right plot of Figure 3 are due to the model failing to correctly simulate which rainfall pulse produced the peak discharge event. Rainfall and discharge time series show that the model is capturing the main hydrograph features (Figure 4).

    Stream gage records for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale and Little Hope Creek at Seneca exhibit marked nonstationarity in flood response since the 1970s [see Villarini et al., 2009b]. This nonstationarity is not evident, however, if one considers only the 2001–2010 period (results not shown). Furthermore, the errors in Figure 4 do not appear to be correlated with calendar year of occurrence. We therefore conclude that the validation and frequency analysis results presented in this study are valid for the 2001–2010 period. Errors in hydrologic response may be attributable to errors in radar rainfall estimates or to features that were omitted or poorly represented in our model, such as detention ponds, the spatial distribution of layered soils, and, in particular, initial soil moisture. Further work is needed to better understand how to represent these features in GSSHA and other hydrologic models.

    7 Flood Frequency Analysis and Discussion

    We develop a storm catalog by identifying the storms from the 3600 km2 domain that produced the 50 largest 12 h rainfall accumulations of the size and shape of Little Sugar Creek at Archdale. Of these fifty storms, eight are classified as tropical. This storm catalog is used with SST to synthesize 50 realizations, each consisting of 1000 annual rainfall maxima. These rainfall realizations are then used to drive the hydrologic model.

    We estimate the quantiles of peak discharge from the 50 SST realizations at the four USGS stream gaging locations for return periods ranging from 10 to 1000 years (Figure 5). The variability in SST‐based peak discharge estimates in Figure 5 is relatively low except for short return periods estimated using only nontropical storms. This high variability relative to longer return periods is due to the tendency to underestimate short return‐period rainfall from spatially small storms using SST. Similar results can be seen in Foufoula‐Georgiou [1989], Wilson and Foufoula‐Georgiou [1990], Franchini et al. [1996], and Wright et al. [2013b]. Wright et al. [2013b] discuss this phenomenon and ways to mitigate it, while concluding that flood frequency analyses are typically focused on longer return periods, which are not subject to this “artificial” variability.

    In the largest three subwatersheds, peak discharge estimates differ significantly depending on whether SST is performed using all storm types or using only nontropical storms. These differences are most pronounced for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale, and diminish with decreasing basin area. The upper tail of flood discharge in Little Hope Creek, the smallest subwatershed, shows little difference regardless of whether or not tropical storms are included in the SST procedure. This points to the important role of rainfall time and space scales in flood response at a range of watershed scales. These results demonstrate that “large,” long‐duration tropical storms dominate the upper tail of flood risk for the larger three subwatersheds, because these storms lead to sustained heavy rainfall over large areas of the basin. These storms produce runoff that will tend to concentrate simultaneously in downstream channel reaches to produce large flood peaks. Spatially smaller and shorter‐duration thunderstorms do not produce high sustained rain rates over the entire basin, and so downstream channel response will not tend to be produced by simultaneous runoff contributions from all upstream reaches. On the other hand, flood response from thunderstorm systems can be greater than from tropical storms in smaller subwatersheds because these storms can produce more intense short‐duration rainfall rates over small areas. Flood risk in Little Hope Creek is due primarily to organized thunderstorms.

    SST‐based peak discharge estimates using all storm types are consistent with the nonstationary flood frequency analysis that are presented in Villarini et al. [2009b] for the 2001–2010 period. With the exception of Little Hope Creek, they are also consistent with estimates obtained using a conventional 12 h “alternating block” design storm [see, e.g., McCuen, 2005]. In Little Hope Creek, the estimates based on the 12 h design storm are lower than the SST‐based estimates (Figure 5). This underestimation stems from the discrepancy between the design storm duration (12 h) and the rainfall time and length scales that are responsible for producing flooding in the Little Hope Creek subwatershed (approximately 1–3 h) [see Wright et al., 2013c]. Reducing the design storm duration, and increasing its intensity accordingly, could improve estimates in Little Hope Creek, but would not be appropriate for estimating flood risk in the larger subwatersheds. This illustrates one of the inherent challenges of flood frequency analysis using conventional rainfall‐runoff modeling techniques: despite the fact that flood risk is scale dependent, the modeler must tailor a storm's spatial and temporal properties to a specific point on the drainage network (such as the basin outlet) and then assume that this storm also reflects appropriate flood‐producing rainfall properties for other points across some or all of the drainage network. While design storms are not state‐of‐the‐practice in flood frequency analysis, more recent approaches still face this fundamental challenge.

    A single storm can generate extreme rainfall across a range of spatial and temporal scales. As a consequence, SST‐based flood risk estimates across a range of watershed scales will not necessarily be highly sensitive to criteria used to create the storm catalog, such as rainfall duration or area. In our radar rainfall data set, for example, Hurricane Danny not only produced the largest 12 h rainfall accumulation of the size and shape of Little Sugar Creek at Archdale but also the largest 3 h accumulation of the size and shape of Little Hope Creek at Seneca. This implies that a storm catalog created from the fifty largest 12 h accumulations of the size and shape of Little Sugar Creek at Archdale should be useful not only for generating SST‐based flood risk estimates for that subwatershed but also for the much smaller Little Hope Creek at Seneca. We demonstrate this by repeating the SST procedure using two additional storm catalogs. The first consists of the 50 largest 3 h rainfall accumulations of the size and shape of Little Hope Creek at Seneca. For each event, we then find the time period of the maximum 12 h rainfall of the size and shape of Little Sugar Creek at Archdale that spatially and temporally “encompasses” the 3 h storm. In other words, the new storm catalog is composed of the fifty 12 h periods of rainfall that encompasses the largest fifty 3 h rainfall accumulations of the size and shape of Little Hope Creek. We create the second storm catalog by repeating this procedure to find the fifty 12 h periods that encompass the 50 largest 6 h storms of the size and shape of Little Sugar Creek at Medical Center. These two storm catalogs need not consist of the same storms as the catalog used to generate the results in Figure 5. There is, however, a high degree of overlap between these two storm catalogs and the one used to generate the results shown in Figure 5.

    Using the 3 and 6 h storm catalogs for Little Hope Creek and Little Sugar Creek at Medical Center, respectively, we estimate the median peak discharge at the four USGS stream gaging locations for return periods ranging from 10 to 1000 years from twenty 1000 year SST realizations (Figure 6). There is little difference between the results shown in Figures 5 and 6, showing that SST‐based discharge estimates (unlike design storm approaches) are not highly sensitive to the rainfall duration or size criteria that are used to generate the storm catalog. A single storm catalog should therefore be suitable for conducting SST in a large number of watersheds of various sizes within a given geographic domain. This points to the fact that the extreme tail of rainfall and flood peak estimates is driven by a relatively small number of the most extreme storms, which tend to produce intense rainfall across a range of temporal and spatial scales and which are likely to be included in any storm catalog regardless of the area or duration selection criteria. The remainder of the SST results presented in this study is based on the storm catalog used to produce Figure 5.

    We examine the temporal structure of modeled 100 year discharge for the four subwatersheds (Figure 7). The design storm hydrographs closely resemble the median SST‐based discharge using all storm types for the three larger subwatersheds but underestimate flood peaks in Little Hope Creek, consistent with the results shown in Figure 5. Tropical storms drive the 100 year flood risk for the largest three subwatersheds, whereas the 100 year flood hydrographs in Little Hope Creek are relatively unaffected by the exclusion of tropical storms. As noted above, the upper tail of flood risk, including 100 year flood peaks, are often driven by a relatively small number of events from the storm catalog. This is particularly true of larger watersheds in which tropical storms are the dominant driver of flooding, due to the small numbers of tropical storms that passed near Charlotte during the radar rainfall record period. For example, of the fifty realizations that produce 100 year peak discharge events at Little Sugar Creek at Archdale, forty‐seven are caused by different transpositions of rainfall from Hurricane Danny (23–24 July 1997) and the remaining three are caused by Tropical Storm Fay (27 August 2008). When only nontropical storms are considered, the number of parent storms that cause 100 year peak discharges at Archdale increases to six. Eight parent storms lead to 100 year peak discharge estimates in Little Hope Creek, though which parent storms contribute to these estimates varies slightly depending on whether or not tropical storms are included.

    Most rainfall‐runoff modeling approaches to flood frequency analysis are based on the assumption of a 1:1 return period equivalence between rainfall and discharge across at least some portion of the drainage network. For example, if a 12 h 100 year design storm is developed using an ARF corresponding to 110 km2 (the area of Little Sugar Creek at Archdale), the modeler will assume not only that the resulting modeled peak discharge at the Archdale station is a reasonable estimate of the 100 year peak discharge, but also that the resulting modeled peak discharges at other points in the drainage network are reasonable estimates of “true” 100 year peak discharge. Because the flood risk at any point along the drainage network is due to unique interactions of rainfall variability with upstream watershed properties, the modeler must decide over which locations this assumption is valid. SST gives us the opportunity to evaluate this assumption because SST‐based discharge return periods are uniquely derived for at all locations along the drainage network, independently of user‐selected rainfall return period.

    We examine the relationships between the return periods of SST‐based 12 h rainfall accumulations for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale and modeled peak discharge for the four subwatersheds using all storms (Figure 8) and using nontropical storms (Figure 9). For the three larger subwatersheds, the Spearman rank correlation coefficient (ρs) between rainfall and discharge return period is higher when SST is performed using all storm types than when tropical storms are excluded. For example, correlation between basin‐wide 12 h rainfall return period and peak discharge return period at Little Sugar Creek at Archdale is ρs = 0.91 when tropical storms are included, and drops to ρs = 0.36 when they are excluded. In Little Hope Creek, in contrast, correlation is higher (ρs = 0.53) when SST is performed using only nontropical storms than when tropical storms are included (ρs  = 0.14). When using all storm types, correlation between rainfall and peak discharge return periods increases with increasing basin area. When tropical storms are excluded, however, the role of basin area is less clear. Little Sugar Creek at Archdale (110 km2, ρs = 0.36) exhibits the lowest correlation, while Briar Creek above Colony has the highest (49 km2, ρs = 0.63). Correlations lie between these values for the two smallest subwatersheds, Little Sugar Creek at Medical Center (ρs = 0.49, 31 km2) and Little Hope Creek at Seneca (ρs = 0.53, 6.7 km2). These results suggest that flood response is not only a function of rainfall intensity and basin area but also the location and variability of rainfall relative to heterogeneous watershed properties such as drainage network structure, soils, and land use. The results also show that the assumption of a 1:1 return period equivalency between t hour rainfall and peak discharge does not hold under any condition. The greater discrepancy between the considered spatiotemporal t hour rainfall properties and the properties of rainfall that actually produce T year flood response at a particular point, the weaker the assumption becomes.

    To further demonstrate this point, we compute the Spearman rank correlation coefficient between rainfall return period and peak discharge return period for the four study subwatersheds (Table 4). Unlike the results shown in Figures 8 and 9, however, we consider rainfall durations of 3 and 6 h in addition to 12 h. Furthermore, the rainfall accumulations and corresponding return periods are calculated with respect to the specific subwatershed, rather than for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale. For SST using all storm types, correlation increases monotonically with basin area for 6 and 12 h rainfall accumulations. For 3 h accumulations, the effect of basin area is unclear. For example, the correlations are similar for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale (ρs = 0.57) and Little Hope Creek (ρs = 0.52), while the highest correlation is for Briar Creek (ρs  = 0.73). When SST is performed using only nontropical storms, correlation decreases with increasing rainfall duration for all subwatersheds, but the magnitude of this correlation does not vary systematically with basin area. For example, 3 h correlations using only nontropical storms are 0.60 for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale, 0.88 for Briar Creek, and 0.81 for Little Hope Creek. Likewise, 12 h correlations using only nontropical storms are 0.36 for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale, 0.63 for Briar Creek, and 0.51 for Little Hope Creek.

    The results shown in Figures 8 and 9 and in Table 4 paint a complex picture of the relationships between extreme rainfall and flood response, in which rainfall intensity is not always strongly coupled to flood response and the role of basin area in flood generation is not always clear. This finding conflicts with design storm principles, where the rainfall intensity and duration depend on basin area via the ARF and timing measures, which are functions of basin area. This finding also suggests that it would be difficult to generate straightforward guidelines for selecting the portion of the drainage network over which a modeler can confidently apply a single storm. Unlike IDF‐based approaches, SST does not require the modeler to select a rainfall intensity and duration or to assume that the selected rainfall properties adequately represent the complexity of flood‐producing rainfall across some or all of the drainage network. It should be noted as well that initial soil moisture variability, which was ignored in this study, further complicate the relationship between rainfall and discharge return period.

    There is increasing need, particularly on the part of insurers, not only for estimates of long‐term flood risk, but also to understand the potential spatial extent of flooding across the network in response to individual rainfall events. Most conventional techniques are limited in this regard, either because they do not consider spatial rainfall variability or if they do, because they are typically limited to one or several observed storms. SST also relies on a limited set of observed storms, but, as we demonstrate below, it can be used to examine how flood response across the drainage network within individual SST realizations.

    We examine four storms from the fifty SST realizations that produce the 100 year peak discharges at Little Sugar Creek at Archdale (Figure 10; see Figure 11 for associated 12 h rainfall depths). Discharge is normalized by the upstream drainage area. All four are produced by different random transpositions of the radar rainfall fields from 23–24 July 1997 parent storm. The four realizations exhibit very different patterns of peak discharge return period and normalized peak discharge across the drainage network, which are closely linked to the spatial rainfall patterns shown in Figure 11. In Realization 4, return period is relatively uniform across the drainage network. In Realization 5, return periods are higher in the upper reaches of both the Little Sugar Creek and Briar Creek branches, while in Realization 14, the return periods are higher near to the outlet. Finally, in Realization 17, return periods are high along Briar Creek branch and relatively low along the upper Little Sugar Creek branch. These relationships are evident in area‐normalized peak discharges, which are also generally higher for stream branches with smaller upstream areas.

    For the four realizations, we examine the modeled discharge hydrographs at five locations along the drainage network (Figure 12). The furthest downstream node is located at the Archdale stream gage. Two points are located along the main branch of Little Sugar Creek and two points are located along the Briar Creek branch. These four upstream points have been selected based on drainage area, so that discharge from similarly sized upstream areas in Little Sugar Creek and Briar Creek can be compared. In Realization 4, Briar Creek and Little Sugar Creek respond similarly at similar drainage areas. For the other three realizations, Briar Creek has slightly higher discharge values per unit area and earlier peaks which tend to broaden the downstream hydrograph at Archdale. Realization 14 has an unusual hydrograph at Archdale with three distinct peaks of similar magnitude. These results demonstrate that discharge hydrographs can vary significantly across a drainage network between storms, even if the downstream peak discharge return period is the same. These variations are at least partly due to differences in the spatiotemporal distribution of rainfall relative to watershed properties. Finally, these results show that very different hydrograph shapes can result at a particular point using SST even when the same parent storm is used. These results also point to the potential to use SST to assess other important measures of flood risk, including flow durations and volumes, that may vary significantly between different realizations even if modeled peak discharge is similar.

    We calculate the quantiles of return period and area‐normalized peak discharge along the drainage network from the 50 realizations that produced 100 year peak discharges at Little Sugar Creek at Archdale (Figure 13). The 0.50 and 0.95 quantiles of return period are generally higher along Briar Creek than along upper Little Sugar Creek. The 0.50 quantile return periods are comparable to the outlet (i.e., approximately 100 year) while the 0.95 quantiles have return periods of 200–1000 years. The 0.05 quantile return periods are generally consistent across both river reaches and are less than 100 years. Area‐normalized peak discharges again show a clear dependence on upstream area, and differences in discharge between points with similarly sized areas along Little Sugar Creek and Briar Creek are less pronounced than differences in return period. These results suggest that despite the fact that the upper reaches of Little Sugar and of Briar Creek produce similar peak discharges, 100 year peak discharges at Little Sugar Creek at Archdale are more closely associated with high return periods along the Briar Creek branch than along the upper Little Sugar Creek branch. This conclusion is consistent with empirical analyses based on observed radar rainfall and USGS discharge measurements presented in Wright et al. [2013a], suggesting this association is not an artifact of the hydrologic model. It is unclear from these results, however, which processes drive this flood peak coupling. Possible explanations include differences in land use and soil characteristics (see Figure 1), spatiotemporal rainfall properties (such as those demonstrated in the bottom right map of Figure 11), or flood wave attenuation.

    8 Summary and Concluding Remarks

    In this study, we present a flood frequency analysis framework that couples SST with high‐resolution radar rainfall fields and a distributed physics‐based hydrologic model. SST is a technique for rainfall and flood frequency analysis that attempts to reconstruct the long‐term regional climatology of extreme rainfall and flooding by performing “space‐for‐time substitution” within a rigorous probabilistic framework. The framework allows the incorporation of the interactions between spatially and temporally variable rainfall with watershed properties into robust flood risk estimates across the entire drainage network. The key findings are summarized and some concluding remarks are provided.

    We develop a model for a portion of Little Sugar Creek in Charlotte, North Carolina, USA using GSSHA, a physics‐based distributed hydrologic modeling system. We avoid extensive calibration because it can adversely affect modeling efforts that aim to represent the interactions of multiple hydrological processes across a range of scales. Instead, we take advantage of detailed GIS‐based input data sets and parameters including land cover, measurements of soil properties at multiple layers, storm water infrastructure inventories, and surveyed channel geometry. Parameter estimates are taken from the published literature. When forced with high‐resolution bias‐corrected radar rainfall fields, the model is able to reproduce flood event hydrologic response across a range of watershed scales.

    In the eastern United States, intense short‐duration rainfall over small areas is associated with organized thunderstorm systems, while intense long‐duration rainfall over large areas is primarily due to tropical storms. We perform SST with two storm catalogs to examine the effect of rainfall time and length scales on flood response. One storm catalog consists of both tropical and nontropical storms and the other consists of only nontropical storms. For all subwatersheds except Little Hope Creek, peak discharge estimates are substantially lower when SST is performed using only nontropical storms. For example, the 100 year peak discharge estimate for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale (110 km2) is approximately 425 m3 s−1 when all storm types are considered, but decreases to about 280 m3 s−1 when only nontropical storms are considered. In Little Hope Creek, on the other hand, 100 year peak discharge estimates are approximately 100 m3 s−1 regardless of whether tropical storms are included. This points to the importance of interactions between the spatial and temporal scales of rainfall and watershed properties such as area and flow path length in the formation of flood response. We also demonstrate that peak discharge results are not sensitive to the criteria (other than storm type) that are used to generate an SST storm catalog, suggesting that a single catalog is suitable for conducting SST for a large number of watersheds of varying sizes within a given region.

    Estimates of 10 to 1000 year peak discharge obtained using SST with all storm types are consistent with the estimates generated using a conventional 12 h design storm. The exception is for the small subwatershed of Little Hope Creek (6.7 km2), where design storm‐based discharge estimates are lower than the SST‐based estimates due to the large discrepancy between the time and space scales of the design storm and the flood‐producing runoff and channel processes of that subwatershed. This points to a fundamental challenge in rainfall‐runoff model‐based flood frequency analysis: because flood risk at any point along the drainage network is the product of unique interactions of rainfall variability with upstream watershed properties, the modeler must “match” a storm event of certain spatial and temporal characteristics to a portion of the drainage network over which that storm adequately represents rainfall that produces T year flood response. This is nontrivial and improper selection can result in significant underestimation of flood risk.

    We demonstrate that there can be considerable differences between rainfall and peak discharge return period for a particular event, contrary to the conventional assumption of a 1:1 equivalency. The relationship between rainfall and discharge return period is complex and depends on watershed features such as basin area relative to the size and location of the storm system. In Little Sugar Creek at Archdale, for example, discharge return period is highly correlated (ρs  = 0.91) with 12 h basin‐wide rainfall return period when SST is conducted with both tropical and nontropical storms. When only nontropical storms are used, however, correlation drops to 0.36 because flood response generated from spatially smaller and shorter duration storms will depend not only on how much rain falls but also where it falls within the basin. In Little Hope Creek, peak discharge is more highly correlated (ρs = 0.53) with rainfall return period when only nontropical storms are used than when all storm types are used (ρs  = 0.14). Results from Briar Creek and Little Sugar Creek at Medical Center point to further complexities, likely due to variations in land cover, soil properties, flow path length, and channel obstructions. This suggests that it would be difficult to generate straightforward guidelines for selecting the portion of the drainage network over which a modeler can confidently apply a single storm under a conventional rainfall‐runoff model‐based flood frequency analysis framework.

    Variability in initial soil moisture, which was ignored in this study, further complicate the relationship between rainfall and discharge return period. While the impacts of initial soil moisture on flood response are not addressed in this study, SST does provide a natural framework for its examination. Seasonally dependent distributions of initial soil moisture could be derived either from soil moisture observations or initial soil moisture states from continuous hydrologic model simulations. Then, in the resampling procedure described in section 5, one could also randomly sample from these distributions, depending on the season of occurrence of the transposed storm.

    Closer examination of four SST‐based storms that produce 100 year peak discharges at Little Sugar Creek at Archdale reveals that even between storm events that produce the same peak discharge return period at the basin outlet, the spatial organization of rainfall relative to the drainage network and subsequent upstream flood response can vary substantially. The 100 year peak discharges at Archdale are more closely coupled with high peak discharge values in the Briar Creek branch than with peak discharges in the Little Sugar Creek branch. This coupling is likely linked to spatiotemporal rainfall organization, land use and soil properties, or flood wave attenuation. This finding contrasts with the common modeling practice of spatially uniform rainfall and assuming that return period is constant throughout all or part of the drainage network. The ability to estimate the spatial extent of flooding across the drainage network using the SST framework is particularly useful for insurers who wish to understand the spatial correlation structure of losses during a flood.

    Unlike conventional approaches, the SST framework provides a direction for incorporating projected climate‐induced changes in different types of storm systems into flood risk estimates at multiple watershed scales. Flood risk in Little Sugar Creek at Archdale, for example, is driven by tropical storm rainfall intensity, which is predicted to increase in the future [see, e.g., Knutson et al., 2008, 2014]. To project changes in flood risk for Little Sugar Creek at Archdale, therefore, one needs to consider projections of future tropical cyclone rainfall. These projections could be easily incorporated into SST‐based flood risk estimates. In contrast, projecting changes in flood risk in Little Hope Creek would require projections of future thunderstorm frequency and intensity. Research and physical understanding of climate‐induced changes in rainfall from organized thunderstorm systems is limited [e.g., Changnon, 2001; Brooks, 2013].

    While not explored in this paper, SST also provides a natural framework for examining spatial scaling properties in rainfall and flood response. Ogden and Dawdy [2003] found that expected values of peak flows during individual flood events in a small watershed can be described by power law functions of catchment area. This scaling has been shown in other settings, including medium [e.g., Gupta et al., 2010] and large watersheds [e.g., Lima and Lall, 2010] and in a range of physiographic settings. Gupta et al. [2007] review existing research in the field of flood scaling. Such work shows promise because its predictive capability is rooted in the interactions of watershed features and hydrologic processes. This line of research, however, has focused mainly on scaling in discharge observations; SST provides the capability to extend this to scaling relationships between discharge and spatial and temporal rainfall properties.

    As previously mentioned, the extreme tail of SST‐estimated flood risk in this study is driven by a small number of “parent storms.” This shortcoming is not unique to SST; estimates of long return periods will be driven by the largest one or several events in the historical record regardless of the chosen analysis technique. At present, SST in a particular area will be sensitive to whether or not one or several extreme storms exist in the relatively short radar record (10–15 years in the United States). With time, this sensitivity will decrease as more extreme storms are observed. Meanwhile, the principal advantage of SST over conventional rainfall and flood frequency analysis methods is that it permits the inclusion of the spatially and temporally complex structure of observed rainfall into flood risk estimates via a distributed hydrologic model. It can also free the flood modeler from stationarity assumptions or from having to decide over which portion of a drainage network a storm of certain spatial and temporal properties can be applied. Instead, it provides a flexible and robust way of estimating flood hazards across a wide range of watershed scales and in changing environments.

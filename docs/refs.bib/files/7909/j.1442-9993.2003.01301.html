Abstract

Abstract  Ants are widely used as bioindicators in environmental assessment in Australia, partly because the responses of ant communities to disturbance are relatively well understood. In particular, the use of functional groups has provided a predictive framework for analysing ant community responses to disturbance in the absence of reliable information on the responses of individual species. Here we review 45 studies of the responses of Australian ant communities to disturbance, in order to: (i) identify individual species or species‐groups that respond consistently to disturbance; and (ii) examine the usefulness of the functional group scheme as a framework for predicting ant community responses under different disturbance regimes in different biogeographical regions. The most common forms of disturbance in our studies were fire (17 studies), mining (12; mostly studies of minesite restoration) and grazing (7), with other disturbances including clearing, logging, flooding, recreation, urbanization and farming. Responses of individual species were inevitably variable because of differences in vegetation type, severity of disturbance and time since disturbance. However, we identified a range of widespread species that showed predictable responses, including species of the metallica group of Rhytidoponera in temperate Australia (‘increasers’ in relation to disturbance), species of the terebrans and denticulatus groups of Camponotus (increasers), the aeneovirens group of Melophorus (increasers) from the arid zone, and Iridomyrmex pallidus (increaser) from the monsoonal region. The functional group scheme assessed here was not designed specifically in the context of disturbance, but nevertheless in some situations provides a useful framework for analysing ant community responses. Three distinct syndromes of functional group responses can be recognized. First, Dominant Dolichoderinae and Hot Climate Specialists are groups that prefer open environments, and tend to be favoured by low levels of disturbance in well‐forested habitats. Second, Opportunists and often also Generalized Myrmicinae are broadly adapted taxa with wide habitat tolerances, but are particularly sensitive to competitive interactions such that their responses oppose those of Dominant Dolichoderinae. Finally, Cryptic Species and Specialist Predators have highly specialized requirements that make them especially sensitive to disturbance. Functional groups are of most use in situations where disturbance causes substantial change in habitat structure, particularly in the ground‐layer. Functional groups are of least use in very open habitats, where disturbance merely increases what is already extensive bare ground, and has relatively little impact on microclimate. The ant functional group scheme can play an important role in assessing disturbance in mesic Australia, but may be of more limited use for this in the arid zone.

Introduction

Of all terrestrial invertebrates, ants are the most widely used bioindicators in Australia. This is partly because of the great abundance, diversity and functional importance of ants in Australian environments, combined with their relative ease of sampling (Majer 1983; Greenslade & Greenslade 1984; Andersen 1990). However, it is also because an understanding of a taxon's responses to environmental stress and disturbance is fundamental to its effective use as a bioindicator (Andersen 1999), and such an understanding is better developed for ants than for any other invertebrate group.

An understanding of ant community dynamics in Australia is supported by a functional group scheme that classifies taxa at genus and species‐group levels according to their continental‐scale responses to environmental stress and disturbance (Andersen 1995; Table 1). The scheme is designed for analyses of ant community dynamics at biogeographical scales (Andersen 1997a), but has also provided a useful framework for predicting the responses of ant communities to disturbance at particular sites (Vanderwoude et al. 1997; King et al. 1998; Bisevac & Majer 1999). For example, disturbance in forested habitats of eastern and southern Australia typically leads to an increased abundance of Opportunists, particularly species of Rhytidoponera (Andersen 1990). The predictive capacity provided by such functional groups can play an important role in the use of ants as bioindicators (Andersen 1997b), helping to distinguish meaningful ‘signals’ from background ‘noise’, given the limited replication characteristic of most land‐use studies (Andersen 1999).

There are now more than 50 studies documenting the responses of Australian ant communities to disturbance, and it is timely that these responses be reviewed. In particular, the utility of the functional group scheme as a framework for predicting responses to disturbance at particular sites requires critical examination. The functional group scheme has been successfully applied in a disturbance context in forested habitats of higher rainfall regions, but recent studies suggest that it might be less useful in the arid zone (Hoffmann et al. 2000; Read & Andersen 2000). In addition, there are now sufficient studies to examine the extent to which individual species and species‐groups show consistent responses to disturbance.

Here, we provide a comprehensive review of the responses of Australian ants to disturbance. It needs to be noted that in most cases ‘disturbance’ does not refer to the actual removal of ant biomass (sensuGrime 1979), but to disturbance of the habitat in which ants occur. Habitat disturbance often has little direct impact on ants, especially those nesting in soil (Hölldobler & Wilson 1990), but acts indirectly through effects on vegetation structure, food supplies and competitive interactions (Andersen 1995). We specifically address two questions. First, can individual species or species‐groups be identified that respond consistently to habitat disturbance? Second, how useful is the functional group scheme as a framework for predicting ant community responses under different disturbance regimes in different biogeographical regions?

Methods

The data

We analysed all the studies we were aware of where quantitative data are provided on the responses of Australian ant communities to disturbance (n = 45; Fig. 1, Table 2), or where the responses of key species were described qualitatively without providing data (e.g. Neumann 1991, 1992). We sought to be comprehensive rather than selective in our inclusion of studies. This necessarily meant that the data we were dealing with were of highly variable quality. However, data quality was an explicit factor in our assessments of responses, and our aim was to seek recurring patterns of responses over a range of studies, so that our conclusions could not be determined by isolated spurious results. Our inclusive approach maximized the number of studies available for analysis, and avoided arbitrary judgements on what constituted a well‐designed study.

The most common forms of disturbance in our 45 studies were fire (17 studies), mining (12; mostly studies of minesite restoration, including the borrow pit study of Fletcher (1990)) and grazing (7), with other disturbances including clearing, logging, flooding, recreation, urbanization, shrub invasion and farming. There is a concentration of studies (17) in Western Australia, which reflects the long history of work there by J. D. Majer and colleagues. The other studies were distributed as follows: Victoria (9), Queensland (7), Northern Territory (5), South Australia (4), New South Wales (2) and the Australian Capital Territory (1). No studies were from Tasmania.

Where the same data were presented in multiple papers, only the paper containing the most comprehensive or revised data was used. Where multiple sampling techniques were employed, only the data from pitfall traps were considered, in order to maximize comparability given that virtually all studies used pitfall traps. In some cases we pooled data from two or more similar sites to simplify the analyses into disturbed versus undisturbed. These were: (i) all sites in the two background (control) zones in Hoffmann et al. (2000); (ii) the many control and disturbed sites in Dunlop and Majer (1985), Fletcher (1990) and Lobry de Bruyn (1993); and (iii) the controls and 4‐ and 8‐year‐old rehabilitation sites in Andersen (1993).

Counts of ant species abundances are often transformed in order to reduce distortions caused by large numbers of ants falling into a few traps when traps are placed beside colony entrances or along foraging trails (Andersen 1990). A lack of transformation can seriously compromise the reliability of comparative studies. When raw data had not been scaled at the trap level, ant abundances were recalculated using ln(x + 1) transformations at the site level. Although scaling at the trap level is preferable, trap‐level data were generally unavailable.

The functional group scheme is a relatively new one, and has undergone substantial evolution over the past 10 years. For example, Greenslade's (1978) original group ‘Large Solitary Foragers’ was renamed ‘Specialist Predators’ by Andersen (1990), and expanded to include genera such as Cerapachys, Colobostruma and Epopostruma. Similarly, ‘Sub‐cryptic Species’ was proposed as a new group (Andersen & McKaige 1987) but then withdrawn (Andersen 1995). Some taxa have been reclassified as more information on them has become available; for example, species of Tapinoma, Doleromyrma, Ochetellus and the turneri group of Odontomachus are now regarded as Opportunists. Where possible, whenever a study used an older functional group classification, this was updated (see Andersen 1997c; Brown 2000) and data recalculated accordingly. Climate Specialists within Majer and Brown (1986), Keals and Majer (1991), Scougall et al. (1993) and Cryptic Species within Majer and Brown (1986) and Scougall et al. (1993) were excluded as they contained species from multiple groups and we were unable to separate them.

When assessing the responses of individual species, we used their absolute abundances. However, when assessing responses of functional groups we considered relative abundance (percentage of total ant abundance), because this is how functional groups are typically analysed. This means that a functional group response will not simply reflect the individual responses of component taxa, as it is also affected by the responses of other taxa. For studies that only report the absolute abundances of species, we recalculated the data as relative abundance for functional group analysis. The relative abundances of functional groups were also calculated from some studies that did not report them (e.g. Majer 1978; Fletcher 1990).

Assessing responses to disturbance

Most of the studies report responses in the absence of formal statistical tests, and indeed the unreplicated nature of many studies was such that formal significance testing was inappropriate. Our assessments of the responses of taxa in any particular study are therefore necessarily qualitative. For each study, we assessed the responses of species and functional groups to disturbance according to a three point scale: 1, weak apparent response that very possibly represents background noise; 2, apparently clear and strong response; and 3, very strong response. We considered sample size and replication when assessing responses. For example, in both Andersen and Yen (1985) and Majer et al. (1982), Hot Climate Specialists showed an approximate halving of relative abundance with disturbance, but was scored 2 where there were only two sites (Andersen & Yen 1985) and scored 3 where there were 16 sites (Majer et al. 1982). We accept that our scoring involved considerable subjectivity, which is not ideal in meta‐analysis (Gates 2002), but we believe such an approach is unavoidable if the full range of studies is to be included. We use the terms ‘increaser’ and ‘decreaser’ to describe taxa whose abundances increase and decrease, respectively, with increasing levels of disturbance. Most species within any study did not show an apparent response, largely because there were insufficient data on them.

Results

Species responses

Only a small number of species were recorded in enough studies to assess the generality of their responses to disturbance (Table 3). Brachyponera lutea is one of Australia's most widespread species (Andersen 2000a), and its response to disturbance was reported in seven studies, covering a range of disturbances. In five cases its abundance appeared to increase with disturbance, sometimes strongly so. Rhytidoponera inornata (restricted to southern western Australia) was also recorded in seven studies, all associated with mining. It likewise tended to be an increaser. The related R. tasmaniensis from south‐eastern Australia was recorded in three studies, and was an increaser (mostly strongly so) in each case. Rhytidoponera victoriae was an increaser in five out of seven studies. Rhytidoponera violacea (a member of the convexa group) from western Australia showed more of a mixed response, being an increaser in three, and decreaser in two, studies.

Aphaenogaster longiceps is widespread in relatively open habitats of the higher rainfall zone of south‐eastern Australia (Andersen 1991b), and was a strong decreaser in each of the three cases considered. Anonychomyrma biconvexa (= A. foetans) occurs in wetter habitats of the same region, and appeared to be a consistent increaser. Iridomyrmex pallidus (occurring throughout the tropical savanna zone) and I. greensladei (the dominant meat ant in south‐western Australia) both also appeared to be strong increasers.

Responses of species‐groups and genera

Rhytidoponera inornata, R. tasmaniensis and R. trachypyx are all members of the widespread metallica group, whose species are widely regarded as increasers (Andersen 1990). There were collectively 21 records of species from this group, with 14 of these increasing. Interestingly, all eight records from higher rainfall areas of south‐eastern Australia (the first three and fifth records of unspecified species from the group, plus the four records of R. tasmaniensis; Table 3) were increasing, with six of these strongly so. Species of other groups of Rhytidoponera showed mixed responses. There were nine records of species from the convexa group (including R. violacea and R. rufithorax), with six increasers and three decreasers. There were single records of individual species of the tropical tenuis, reticulata, turneri, aurata and confusa groups; the first three (all small‐ to medium‐sized species of open habitats; Andersen 2000a) were all strong decreasers, whereas the last two were strong increasers. Considering all the 42 records of Rhytidoponera species, there were 28 (67%) increasers (14 strongly so), and 14 (33%) decreasers (10 strongly so).

There were seven records for the bull‐ant genus Myrmecia. The first three involved mining and agriculture, and were all decreasers. In contrast, the next three involved fire, and were all increasers; however, this might be a sampling artefact, as species of Myrmecia commonly forage on vegetation, and become more trappable in pitfalls following fire (Andersen 1988). Five of the seven records of Cardiocondyla (all involving the nuda group) were increasers. Species of Monomorium showed mixed responses, with those of the rothsteini group tending to be increasers, those of the nigrius group tending to be decreasers, and those of the laeve group showing mixed responses. Species of Pheidole tended to be increasers (13 out of 19 cases).

Species of the dominant genus Iridomyrmex showed mixed responses, although those of the anceps group were consistently increasers. The extremely speciose genus Camponotus also showed mixed responses. However, the five records of the closely related denticulatus and terebrans group were all increasers (four strongly so), and likewise the three records of the consobrinus group (including C. dryandrae and C. obniger) were all strong increasers. There was a marked tendency (seven out of nine records, including all five of those that were not from the minutula group) for species of Paratrechina to be increasers, as there was for species of Prolasius (eight out of 10 cases). Species of Melophorus showed mixed responses, although those of the aeneovirens group tended to be increasers (strongly so in four out of five cases). Species of Tetramorium and Stigmacros showed mixed responses, with insufficient data for analyses at the species‐group level.

Functional group responses

Dominant Dolichoderinae

The Dominant Dolichoderinae group was an increaser in 16 out of 24 cases, including 13 very strong responses (Table 3). The eight cases of decreasers represented three distinct disturbance regimes. The first was immediately following fire in woodlands and open forests of south‐eastern Australia, where the abundance (and relative abundance) of Dominant Dolichoderinae decreased, but increased in the longer‐term (Andersen & Yen 1985; Andersen 1988; Neville et al. 1997). The second was when the disturbance (or rehabilitation following disturbance) involved the formation of dense shaded areas with a thick layer of litter, such as occurs under plantation forestry (Burbidge et al. 1992), invasion by weeds (Abensperg‐Traun et al. 1996) or colonization by early successional acacias (Andersen 1993). The third was within areas subject to agriculture, where Generalized Myrmicinae (particularly species of Pheidole) became numerically dominant (Majer 1978; Greenslade & Mott 1979; Lobry de Bruyn 1993).

Subordinate Camponotini

The Subordinate Camponotini group showed mixed responses, with slightly more (11 out of 19) cases showing a negative response. However, when only strong and very strong responses were considered, then there was a very marked tendency (seven out of eight cases) for a negative response (Table 3).

Climate Specialists

Hot Climate Specialists showed a relatively even mix of positive and negative responses, reflecting the response of the predominant genus Melophorus (Table 3). In contrast, Cold Climate Specialists showed a marked tendency for a negative response (seven out of nine cases, and mostly strongly or very strongly so).

Cryptic Species

The Cryptic Species group was a consistent decreaser, showing a negative response in 12 out of 14 cases, including 10 strong or very strong responses. This could be readily explained by the consistently deleterious effect of disturbance on the litter layer.

Opportunists

The Opportunists group were commonly strong increasers (Table 3), especially when species of the metallica group of Rhytidoponera were involved. However, Opportunists also often showed negative responses.

Generalized Myrmicinae

The Generalized Myrmicinae were decreasers in most (13 out of 21) cases. If only strong or very strong responses were considered, there were 10 negative responses compared with only four positive (Table 3). Interestingly, there was a strong negative relationship between the response of Generalized Myrmicinae on one hand, and that of Opportunists on the other (Fig. 2).

Specialist Predators

The Specialist Predators typically showed a weak response (Table 3), which was likely to be an artefact of low sampling efficiency because of the generally low abundance of its component species. The group tended to be a decreaser (nine out of 15 cases).

Discussion

Variable responses

Our analysis showed that ant species, species‐groups and functional groups all show a variable response to disturbance. Such variation is inevitable because of at least three confounding factors.

First, a species' response is likely to vary with habitat because of differential effects of disturbance on habitat suitability (Pickett & White 1985; Pimm 1991). In particular, disturbance is likely to have an adverse effect when a species occurs in its most preferred habitat, whereas disturbance might actually improve habitat suitability when a species occurs in a non‐preferred habitat. For example, species of Melophorus and other Hot Climate Specialists prefer open habitats, and in well‐forested (i.e. non‐preferred) habitats they are typically increasers in response to moderate disturbance such as fire (Majer 1978; Andersen 1991a; Vanderwoude et al. 1997). In contrast, they are decreasers in open habitats when disturbance results in an increase in plant cover through invasion by weeds or colonization by early successional species (Burbidge et al. 1992; Andersen 1993). The same disturbance can differentially affect environmental variables even within similar habitats. For example, grazing has different effects on vertical complexity, grass cover, litter cover and soil strength in different grasslands of western USA, and ant communities accordingly show different responses (Bestelmeyer & Wiens 2001).

Second, the responses of species are unlikely to be linear, as is the case of species' responses to environmental gradients in general (Austin 1987). For example, most increaser species are likely to show a bell‐shaped response, increasing under conditions of low to moderate disturbance, but decreasing as disturbance becomes more severe. The disturbances considered here varied enormously in their severity, and this clearly contributed to the variable responses that we have documented. In particular, disturbances such as mining and agriculture that have a direct impact on soil and therefore ant nests (i.e. strict disturbance from an ant's perspective, sensuGrime 1979), are qualitatively different from those, such as fire and grazing, that act primarily by removing plant biomass. It is therefore hardly surprising that mining caused a greater proportion of strong responses than did either fire or grazing (Table 4). The confounding influence of curvilinear responses is illustrated by patterns of ant colonization at minesites undergoing rehabilitation. Few species occurred at the very early stages of rehabilitation (i.e. most severe disturbance), such that virtually all species would be classified as decreasers on this basis. However, many species occurred at higher abundance at mid‐succession than they did at undisturbed sites (Majer et al. 1982; Jackson & Fox 1996; Majer & Nichols 1998; Bisevac & Majer 1999), and would therefore be classified as increasers on that basis.

The third confounding factor is time since disturbance, because an immediate response (incorporating direct mortality) may differ from one in the longer term (reflecting habitat suitability). Many studies, for example, have shown that the abundance of Dominant Dolichoderinae, such as species of Iridomyrmex and Anonychomyrma, increases in the longer term in response to fire (O'Dowd & Gill 1984; Andersen 1988, 1991a; Neumann 1991, 1992; Vanderwoude et al. 1997), because of increased insolation in the ground‐layer. However, fire resulted in an immediate reduction in the abundance of Iridomyrmex in one study (Andersen & Yen 1985), and the authors postulated that species of Iridomyrmex might have an unusually high proportion of workers foraging at any given time, and therefore be disproportionately affected by direct mortality during fire.

Species and species‐group responses

Arguably the most useful bioindicators for particular studies are widespread species that show well‐known and predictable response to disturbance. However, for the reasons outlined herein, such species will not always show a consistent response in the sort of analysis we have presented here; responses will always need to be interpreted in the context of habitat type, severity of disturbance and time. With this caveat, widespread species and species‐groups can be identified as target indicators in each of the major biogeographical zones in Australia. The most striking examples are species of the metallica group of Rhytidoponera in temperate southern Australia, which were consistently increasers. The most notable of these were R. tasmaniensis and R. metallica in the south‐east (Yeatman & Greenslade 1980; Andersen & McKaige 1987; Lobry de Bruyn 1993; Neville et al. 1997; York 2000), and R. inornata in the south‐west (Majer 1980; Majer & Brown 1986). Anonychomyrma biconvexa also appeared to be a consistent increaser in temperate south‐eastern Australia (O'Dowd & Gill 1984; Andersen 1988; Neumann 1991, 1992), whereas Aphaenogaster longiceps appeared to be a consistent decreaser (Andersen 1988; Lobry de Bruyn 1993; Jackson & Fox 1996; Vanderwoude et al. 1997).

Species of the terebrans group of Camponotus appeared to be consistent increasers throughout the southern semiarid zone. We are only aware of one published study that documents such a response (Bisevac & Majer 1999), but our unpublished observations indicate that such species commonly have inflated abundances in disturbed areas, such as around homesteads. Species of the closely related denticulatus group of Camponotus, which occur throughout the central and northern arid zones, also appear to be consistent increasers (Andersen & Hoffmann 1999; Hoffmann 2000; Hoffmann et al. 2000; Andersen et al. 2002). Camponotus denticulatus itself is the major representative of the group throughout the central arid zone, and is one of the most conspicuous ants in suburban Alice Springs.

Species of the aeneovirens group of Melophorus also appeared to be relatively consistent increasers in the northern arid zone (Andersen 1991a; Vanderwoude et al. 1997; Andersen & Hoffmann 1999; Hoffmann 2000). Iridomyrmex pallidus is one of the most common ants throughout monsoonal Australia, and appeared to be a consistent increaser (Andersen 1991a; Andersen & Hoffmann 1999; Hoffmann et al. 2000). However, in a very recent study not included in this analysis, this species apparently decreased in abundance in relation to grazing in North Queensland (Woinarski et al. 2002). Finally, Brachyponera lutea occurs throughout most of mainland Australia outside the central arid zone, and has been widely reported as an increaser (Majer 1984; Jackson & Fox 1996; Vanderwoude et al. 1997; Andersen & Hoffmann 1999; York 2000).

Functional group response

There are several reasons why the responses of functional groups to disturbance would be expected to be more variable than those of individual species. The first and most obvious is generic to any functional group scheme: a single species is a much ‘tighter’ biological entity than is a combination of species. Second, the functional groups used here are based on a range of ecological factors rather than specifically on disturbance (i.e. they are not disturbance‐response groups; Lavorel et al. 1997). Indeed, stress factors relating to temperature, habitat structure and competitive interactions play the dominant role in shaping the functional group scheme, with disturbance acting indirectly through its effects on these (Andersen 2000b). For example, Cryptic Species are classified because of a specialist lifestyle in soil and litter, which comes with a suite of morphological characters such as small body size, reduced eye‐size, and tubular body. They are often sensitive to disturbance because of the effects of disturbance on the litter‐layer, but this is not the basis of their classification. Similarly, Opportunists are typically ruderal species (sensuGrime 1979) that often proliferate at disturbed sites. However, this only occurs when disturbance puts them at a competitive advantage over other ants. When disturbance favours other taxa, such as the case of fire promoting Dominant Dolichoderinae in forests (Neville et al. 1997; Vanderwoude et al. 1997), then the abundance of Opportunists is actually reduced.

Third, the functional group scheme classifies ants at higher taxonomic levels (genera and species‐groups, rather than species) according to that taxon's broad behaviour at biogeographical scales. It does not aim to account for the behaviour of individual species at particular sites. As an analogy, ‘grass’ represents a monophyletic plant life‐form that plays a key role in analyses of vegetation dynamics. From a global perspective it is a dominant functional group under conditions of moderate to high levels of disturbance (Grime 1979), but this generalization hardly means that all grass species are always increasers.

Fourth, some taxa are not known well enough to be able to classify them with confidence, or simply do not fit neatly into any particular group. For example, Adlerzia has been placed by different authors into different groups, including Hot Climate Specialists (Andersen 1995; Vanderwoude et al. 1997; Bisevac & Majer 1999) and Cryptic species (Majer & Brown 1986; Andersen et al. 2003). It is considered here to belong to the Cryptic Species because of its small body size, reduced eyes, and membership within the predominantly cryptic tribe Pheidologetonini. However, unlike most other Cryptic Species, Adlerzia occurs primarily in open woodland rather than wetter forest (Shattuck 1999), is common in urban areas (Majer & Brown 1986) and is promoted by fire (Andersen & Yen 1985; Vanderwoude et al. 1997).

The responses of functional groups to disturbance are therefore necessarily context‐specific, and species within a functional group are likely to show a range of responses. Given these qualifications, the following can be identified as recurring patterns of functional group responses to disturbance in Australia:

  • 1

    In well‐forested areas, low to moderate disturbance such as fire and grazing favours Dominant Dolichoderinae and Hot Climate Specialists, because of habitat simplification and increased insolation of the ground‐layer (Andersen 1991a; Neville et al. 1997; Vanderwoude et al. 1997; King et al. 1998; Woinarski et al. 2002). This often leads to a reduction in Opportunists (Andersen 1993; Neville et al. 1997; Vanderwoude et al. 1997).

  • 2

    Conversely, the abundances of Dominant Dolichoderinae and Hot Climate Specialists are reduced when disturbance involves the formation of dense shaded areas with a thick layer of litter, and this leads to an increase in abundance of Opportunists (Burbidge et al. 1992; Andersen 1993; Abensberg‐Traun et al. 1996).

  • 3

    Throughout higher rainfall regions of eastern and southern Australia, severe disturbance (involving extensive vegetation clearing) typically leads to a proliferation of Opportunists, especially small species of Rhytidoponera (Majer et al. 1982; Yeatman & Greenslade 1980; Andersen & McKaige 1987; King et al. 1998). This also occurred under a frequent fire regime in blackbutt forests of New South Wales (York 2000), but not in spotted gum forests of south‐eastern Queensland (Vanderwoude et al. 1997).

  • 4

    Cryptic Species are consistently decreasers in relation to disturbance (Majer et al. 1982; Andersen & McKaige 1987; Andersen 1991a; King et al. 1998; York 2000), presumably because of negative impacts on the litter‐layer.

  • 5

    In any particular case, Generalized Myrmicinae and Opportunists tend to show opposite responses to disturbance. The reasons for this relationship are not well‐understood. Both groups consist of broadly adapted taxa, but are known to have different habitat preferences and competitive abilities (Andersen 1995, 2000b).

  • 6

    Specialist Predators are often decreasers, and component taxa are typically among the last ants to colonize minesites undergoing rehabilitation. However, they typically occur in relatively low abundances in undisturbed ant communities, so there are often insufficient numbers collected in routine surveys for statistical analysis. Moreover, component species can show contrasting responses, even within a single genus; for example, Leptogenys adlerzi was a strong decreaser in relation to grazing in north Queensland, whereas closely related L. cornigera was a strong increaser (Woinarski et al. 2002).

Conclusion

We have identified a number of widespread ant species and species‐groups that appear to show a predictable response to disturbance, and they cover a wide range of environments. However, there are relatively few of them, and collectively they will be absent from many local ant faunas. This makes the broadscale predictive capacity of functional groups in the absence of detailed species‐level information an attractive proposition. Functional group analysis also has the advantage of providing a ‘whole of community’ response, rather than relying on what might be an idiosyncratic response of a single species, and does so from an explicitly ecological perspective.

The functional group scheme was designed to enable ant community organization to be compared across biogeographical zones, and is most useful in a disturbance context when the effect of disturbance on habitat structure is analogous to biogeographical‐scale change in habitat structure. In such cases disturbance causes vegetation structure to change beyond the range to which local species are typically adapted (Bestelmeyer & Wiens 2001). The scheme can be particularly useful in a minesite rehabilitation context, which involves major successional changes in vegetation structure, and this is reflected in comparable changes in functional group composition (Andersen 1993; Bisevac & Majer 1999). It is also useful in well‐forested habitats, where even relatively low levels of disturbance such as fire can have a marked impact on habitat structure, particularly at ground level.

The functional group scheme appears to be least useful as a basis for analysing ant community dynamics within the arid zone. This is because most xeric vegetation types are already very open, so that low levels of disturbance have relatively little impact on gross habitat structure (e.g. increasing what is already extensive bare ground; Bestelmeyer & Wiens 2001), and therefore on functional group composition. This possibly accounts for the relatively few responses of functional groups to grazing (Table 3), as most such studies have been conducted in arid and semiarid rangelands. Indeed, ant communities in such rangeland habitats of North America are also known to be particularly resilient (Whitford et al. 1999; Bestelmeyer & Wiens 2001). But in a recent study in arid New South Wales rangelands, analyses of ant abundance data at the functional group level discriminated habitat type and land condition better than analysis at the species level (A. Andersen, unpubl. data).

In conclusion, the functional group scheme can provide a useful predictive framework for assessing ant community responses to disturbance when such disturbance causes marked change in vegetation structure. This means that it can play an important role in assessing ecological disturbance in mesic Australia, but may be of more limited use for this in the arid zone.

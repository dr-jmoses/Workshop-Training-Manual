    Abstract

    1. Ants are abundant in natural and managed tropical ecosystems and can have an impact on herbivorous arthropods, as well as plant pathogens. Although it has been shown for plants that the diversity of communities can result in improved ecosystem functioning, it remains uncertain how the species richness of ants affects multiple ecosystem services and disservices.
    2. In the present study, we used experimentally enhanced natural gradients in ant species richness on 100 cacao trees in a plantation aiming to analyze the effect of ant species identity and species richness on predation pressure and the incidence of cacao pod borer (CPB), as well as the spread of black pod disease (BPD).
    3. Ant species richness did not significantly improve predation of experimentally exposed insects, and was not associated with a reduction in the incidence of CPB. However, the incidence of BPD was higher in ant species rich trees, presumably because more ant species were pathogen vectors. The identity of the dominant ant species affected the incidence of CPB and BPD, as well as predation pressure.
    4. Although both ant species richness and identity affected ecosystem services and disservices delivered by the ant community, the results of the present study suggest that the identity of dominant ants is the main driver for ecosystem services in these systems.

    Introduction

    Both the identity and the diversity of species are important in affecting the function that is exerted by a species community. Most evidence originates from pioneering research on the primary productivity of plant communities (Hooper et al., 2005). Recent studies have attempted to elucidate how predator diversity affects top‐down control of lower trophic levels (Snyder et al., 2008). This research is important both for improving our understanding of food‐webs and for contributing towards an increased natural regulation of herbivory in managed systems. The results of a recent meta‐analysis suggest that predator diversity is generally associated with lower herbivore numbers (Letourneau et al., 2009). However, published studies involve the exclusion of whole taxonomical or functional groups of predators, rather than investigating species richness within these groups (Piñol et al., 2011). Because whole functional groups are rarely totally absent, even in strongly disturbed systems, this limits the practical relevance of these results. Furthermore, these types of data do not allow the separation of species identity and species richness effects and thus cannot be used improve our understanding of the drivers of diversity.

    Ants are important components of most terrestrial ecosystems in terms of numbers and biomass (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990). Although they span a gradient between cryptic herbivores and predators (Davidson et al., 2003), it is expected that higher ant species richness contributes to the increased stability and strength of trophic control of herbivores (Philpott & Armbrecht, 2006; Bisseleua et al., 2009). Much knowledge of ants and their interactions with other components of an ecosystem originates from agroforestry systems such as coffee or cacao, where ants are expected to act as predators of arthropod pests (Bos et al., 2007). Other important functions for which ant species richness may be important include hampering the spread of plant disease and providing biotic resistance to invasive ants, which are often ecologically and economically detrimental (Lach & Hooper‐Bui, 2010).

    There are several mechanisms by which ant diversity may be important. Ants differ substantially in resource use, with some species acquiring more energy from nectar or honeydew and other species from different types of arthropods (Philpott et al., 2004). Foraging strategies and recruitment behaviours of ant species also differ (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990). Interspecific differences in foraging or behaviour may augment overall responses to herbivore communities, as has been shown for spiders (Schmitz & Suttle, 2001). Similar patterns have been suggested for ants (Philpott & Armbrecht, 2006), although the evidence is still scarce (Gove, 2007). Biotic resistance to invasion is considered to act via a reduced niche opportunity in species rich communities (Shea & Chesson, 2002). Thus, the benefits of functional diversity may be a result of complementarity between species (Hooper et al., 2005).

    In the present study, we attempted to separate the effects of ant species identity and species richness in a cacao plantation on (i) predation pressure and the incidence of cacao pod borer Conopomorpha cramerella (Lepidoptera, Gracillariidae); (ii) spread of black pod disease Phytophthora palmivora (Peronosporales, Pythiaceae) in the cacao tree and total disease incidence; and (iii) the establishment success of a recently spreading, aggressive Dolichoderine ant species Philidris cf. cordata (Wielgoss et al., 2014). We selected trees differing in ant species richness and identity of ants, including dominant species. In some of the trees, we manipulated the ant communities by setting up barriers to limit access of ground‐nesting ants such as Anoplolepis gracilipes (Formicidae, Formicinae). The incidence of pests and diseases in the trees was monitored. We conducted a predation experiment using larvae of Corcyra cephalonica (Lepidoptera, Pyralidae) aiming to compare the effect of ant species richness and treatment with predation success. The spread of P. palmivora from a standardized source inoculum and the establishment success of P. cf. cordata nests were recorded.

    Materials and methods

    Study site

    Field experiments were conducted in a cacao plantation in Ranteleda village, Palolo valley, Central Sulawesi, Indonesia. In Palolo valley, cacao plantations were mostly cultivated by small farmers in less than 1 ha (mean area) and usually with a high intensity of insecticide application (Rizali et al., 2013). The selected cacao plantation with an area of 0.6 ha (approximately 960 cacao trees) was managed without pesticide application. It was embedded in rice fields and situated approximately 4 km from natural forest (the Lore Lindu National Park) with an altitude of 565 m a.s.l. Cacao trees were planted with a regular spacing of 2.5 × 2.5 m interspersed with shade trees Gliricidia sepium (Fabales, Fabaceae) (dominant) and Durio zibethinus (Malvales, Malvaceae) (rare). When the experiment was started, the age of cacao trees was 6 years. We selected a plot of 10 × 10 rows of cacao trees (n = 100) with a mean ± SD stem diameter of 8.8 ± 1.56 cm and the mean ± SD tree height was 383.5 ± 44.89 cm.

    Ant observations

    We primarily relied on the natural gradient of ant species richness instead of manipulating the ant species richness on each cacao tree. This is because establishing and managing the ant diversity in cacao trees is very difficult. Before setting up the treatments, we conducted ant surveys on 100 cacao trees to quantify tree‐level ant species richness (Fig. 1). The first observation round was conducted in October 2009 (before pruning), where we confirmed that there was a sufficiently large gradient in species richness among trees. In January 2010, we then pruned the trees to limit exchange of species between trees and conducted a second observation in February 2010 (Fig. 2). These surveys consisted of visually searching and hand collecting ants in different microhabitats, including the stem base, stem and branch intersections, and canopy (twigs and leaves), which is the best method of recording all ant species inhabiting an area (Bestelmeyer et al., 2000). On each cacao tree, ants were observed for 5–10 min or until no additional new species were found. Ants were identified to genus (Bolton, 1994) and further sorted to units in accordance with their external morphology (morphospecies) (Lattke, 2000). Some ants were identified to species level based on reliable digital resources (http://www.antweb.org). The dominant ant species on the trees were A. gracilipes, Crematogaster sp.1 and Monomorium floricola, with A. gracilipes being the only dominant ground‐nesting ant species. The species Philidris cf. cordata was absent before the experimental introductions were made, described below.

    Ant species richness gradient

    Based on the ant species richness data from the second round of observations, we selected trees to obtain a gradient of ant species richness (Fig. 1). The gradient was selected considering the trees that dominated by Crematogaster sp.1 and M. floricola, or both of these species independently, to allow the separation of effects as a result of identity of the dominant ant from the effects of species richness of the community (Fig. 3a and Table 1). In addition, we randomly selected 45 trees and applied a tanglefoot barrier (The Tanglefoot Company, Grand Rapids, Michigan) to prevent access by ground‐nesting ants, especially the very common and abundant A. gracilipes (Fig. 3b). The tanglefoot barrier was monitored every week and renewed every month, weeds that may connect the cacao trees to the ground were cut, and the cacao trees were pruned to disconnect the canopies. We also conducted the establishment of aggressive ant P. cf. cordata, both on cacao trees with and without tanglefoot aiming to investigate the effect of ant communities. The establishment method is described below with respect to the establishment success of P. cf. cordata.

    Ants were monitored intensively by hand collecting and visual observation in April 2010 (after tanglefoot barrier treatment), May 2010 (before establishment of P. cf. cordata) and August 2010 (after establishment of P. cf. cordata) (Fig. 1). The range of tree‐level species richness was one to eight species in all three surveys.

    Pest and disease incidence observations

    We observed pest and disease incidence on the 100 trees every 2 weeks, from April 2010 (after barrier treatment) until August 2010. We counted all fruits, distinguishing between healthy ones and those that suffered attack by C. cramerella (Lepidoptera, Gracillariidae), the cacao pod borer (CPB), or were infected by P. palmivora, the black pod disease (BPD). CPB and BPD attack both young and mature cocoa pods. CPB causes unevenness and premature ripening of pods, whereas BPD causes the rotting or necrosis of pods (Keane & Putter, 1992).

    Black pod disease experiment

    This experiment was conducted after establishment of P. cf. cordata. Before BPD treatment, we removed all cacao fruits that were obviously infected by BPD. Then, two cacao pods bearing symptoms of infection by BPD were put in the main intersection of the branches (approximately 150 cm high) with minimum distance of approximately 50 cm from the nearest healthy fruit. After 3 weeks, we recorded the incidence of BPD on the pods on each cacao tree (after infection, pods develop symptoms within 2–3 weeks).

    Establishment success of P. cf. cordata

    To investigate the effect of ant communities on the establishment of aggressive ant P. cf. cordata, we imported the nests (artificial nests) of P. cf. cordata from other cacao plantation outside Ranteleda village that dominated by P. cf. cordata. To construct the artificial nests, bamboo (length 30 cm, diameter 10 cm) were filled with coconut leaves and placed near the colony of P. cf. cordata. After 2 weeks, the nests could be used and transported to the experimental field for establishment of P. cf. cordata. For this treatment, we placed two nests per tree on 22 randomly selected cacao trees without tanglefoot barrier and 23 cacao trees with tanglefoot barrier. The nests were placed on the branch, together with cacao fruit infested by the preferred homopteran trophobionts (Delabie, 2001) of this ant species (Planococcoides sp.).

    Predation experiment

    We conducted a predation experiment to study the effect of ant species richness and dominant species on predation success in cacao plantations. This experiment was conducted approximately 3 weeks after establishment of P. cf. cordata. In this experiment, we used larvae of the rice moth, C. cephalonica (Lepidoptera: Pyralidae) from third or fourth instar as prey. On each cacao tree, two larvae were put on a cacao pod and on a branch. The predation success of each ant species and the time needed (during 1 h of observation) for discovering and attacking each larva were measured in this experiment. The experiment was conducted during day and replicated at night (in the same 24‐h period) to cover the effect of both diurnal and nocturnal ant activity.

    Statistical analysis

    The change of species richness between observations was tested using a Wilcoxon signed rank test. To analyze the effect of ant species identity and species richness on CPB and BPD incidence, we used generalized linear mixed models (GLMM, binomial distribution; no overdispersion detected). In addition to the fixed effects (see below), we included tree identity as a random grouping factor to associate the repeated measurements of ant diversity and CPB and BPD incidence with the corresponding tree and to avoid pseudoreplication. Data recorded after introduction of P. cf. cordata on trees were not used in these analyses to avoid the bias effect of this species.

    We fitted several models per response variable. The first model had only ant species richness as an explanatory variable. The second model had ant species richness and the presence/absence of the dominant ants, A. gracilipes, M. floricola and Crematogaster sp.1 as predictors. This second model was simplified by computing all possible subset models and selecting the model with the lowest Akaike information criteria (AIC).

    We used generalized linear models (GLM, binomial distribution) to analyze the effect of ant species identity and species richness on predation pressure. The response variable was the proportion of attacked larvae. As above, we fitted a first model containing only ant species richness as an explanatory variable, followed by a second model including ant species richness and the presence/absence of the dominant ants M. floricola, Crematogaster sp.1 and A. gracilipes.

    GLMM (with binomial distribution) was also used to analyze the effect of ant species identity and species richness on the spread of P. palmivora. In this case, we used the data that was recorded after establishment of P. cf. cordata. All analyses were conducted using r (R Core Team, 2015) and the package lme4 (Bates et al., 2015).

    Results

    Ant species richness from 100 cacao trees

    Across all five ant survey rounds for the 100 cacao trees, we recorded 27 ant species (Table 1). Anoplolepis gracilipes, M. floricola and Crematogaster sp.1 were the most dominant species in this cacao plantation (Table 1; see also Supporting information, Figs S1–S5). We found comparable ant species richness on the 100 cacao trees when comparing the first and second observations (Wilcoxon signed rank test: V = 983, P  = 0.655) and the third and fourth observations, which were conducted after the application of the tanglefoot barrier (Wilcoxon signed rank test: V = 591.5, P = 0.998). However, ant species richness differed in the time before and after the treatment of tanglefoot (Wilcoxon signed rank test: V = 2203, P < 0.001) and before and after establishment of P. cf. cordata (Wilcoxon signed rank test: V = 1924.5, P = 0.004).

    Pest and disease incidence

    Ant species richness had contrasting effects on the incidence of pest and disease in the cacao plantation (Fig. 4). Our analysis showed that ant species richness did not influence incidence of pests (CPB) based on both the model with species richness as the only explanatory variable (Z = 0.247, P = 0.805, AIC = 473.5) and the model including also ant identity (Z = −0.555, P = 0.579, AIC = 467.2). Nevertheless, we found that the presence of M. floricola significantly enhanced (Z = 2.820, P = 0.005) the incidence of CPB in the cacao plantation (Fig. 5a).

    By contrast, species richness significantly enhanced the incidence of disease (BPD) (species richness only: Z = 3.477, P < 0.001, AIC = 1266), even when species identity was also included in the model (Z = 2.582, P = 0.009, AIC = 1262). Based on the latter model, the presence of M. floricola (Z = 2.044, P = 0.041) increased the incidence of P. palmivora or BPD (Fig. 5b).

    Predation experiment

    Ant species richness had no effect on predation pressure (Z = 0.462, P = 0.644, AIC = 133.29; when species identity was included: Z = −1.256, P  = 0.209, AIC = 126.98). We found 16 ant species that attacked prey during the experiment with respect to both day and night observations (Table 2). The presence of A. gracilipes (Z = 3.378, P < 0.001) significantly increased the predation pressure (Fig. 5c). Other dominant ants such as Crematogaster sp.1 (Z = 0.323, P = 0.746) and M. floricola (Z = −0.197, P = 0.843) did not significantly enhance predation pressure.

    P. palmivora dissemination

    Our BPD experiment showed that ant species richness had no effect on spread of P. palmivora in the cacao plantation (Z = 0.483, P = 0.629, AIC = 326.4; when species identity was included: Z = 0.596, P = 0.551, AIC = 331.8). Ant species identity [i.e. A. gracilipes (Z = −0.502, P = 0.616), P. cf. cordata (Z = 1.136, P = 0.256), Crematogaster sp.1 (Z = 0.442, P = 0.659) and M. floricola (Z = 0.328, P = 0.743)] had no effect on BPD dissemination either.

    Establishment success of P. cf. cordata

    From 45 cacao trees, in which nests of P. cf. cordata were placed, only five cacao trees were not successfully colonized, making the analysis of contrasts between ant communities impossible in terms of resistance to invasion (Table 1).

    Discussion

    The results of the present study show that ant species identity and species richness play different roles in influencing the incidence of pests and diseases in cacao plantations. Ant species identity appears to be more important than ant species richness in determining pest incidence. According to both our observational data and the predation experiment, ant species identity was strongly related to predation pressure, as suggested by Straub and Snyder (2006). Although ant richness effects did not occur in our BPD inoculation experiment, possibly as a result of the short duration of the experiment (3 weeks), ant species richness was related to increases in the incidence of disease in non‐manipulated cacao trees. We expected that higher species richness may (i) result in spatial compartmentation of ants in the cacao trees and thereby lower the likelihood of disease spread and (ii) result in decreased activity of other potential vectors. This does not appear to be the case. The observed pattern may have been a result of the increased likelihood of having ant species that are efficient vectors of the spores of P. palmivora (Evans, 1973) in rich versus poor communities via a selection effect. The selection effect functioned through direct and indirect vectors. Tent‐building ants act as direct vectors or as the primary infection, whereas other ant species acts as a casual or indirect vector by involvement in the transport of inoculum of P. palmivora. The establishment of P. cf. cordata, an aggressive ant species and a potentially major vector of P. palmivora (Wielgoss et al., 2010, 2014), had no effect on spread of the diseases in this study area, possibly because of the short period subsequent to its establishment. However, based on the results of the study by Wielgoss et al. (2014), P. cf. cordata can spread the disease by using detritus material from infected rotten fruits to build nests and tent structures in healthy cacao fruits.

    The dominant ant species, P. cf. cordata, A. gracilipes, Crematogaster sp.1 and M. floricola, are all potential predators of cacao pests. We showed that M. floricola significantly enhanced the incidence of CPB, which is an unexpected result. This may be because M. floricola is able to keep away other dominant ant species that are effective predators of CPB (Way et al., 1989). In addition, A. gracilipes significantly increased predation pressure in the predation experiment. These patterns revealed that ant species identity can be more important than ant species richness (Straub & Snyder, 2006). Predation pressure appears to be more directly linked to ant activity (Gove, 2007); therefore, increased ant activity as a result of higher A. gracilipes numbers appeared to cause increased predation pressure in the cacao plantation (Way & Khoo, 1992). However, species richness of predators had no effect on predation pressure in the experiment. Theory predicts that the importance of a diverse predator community for predation pressure will increase in a diverse prey community (Wilby & Thomas, 2002; Ives et al., 2005), whereas we used just one prey species in our experiment. Furthermore, other studies reported that top‐down pressure generally increases with higher predator diversity and multiple prey species (Cardinale et al., 2003; Snyder et al., 2006).

    The effect of ant species richness and species identity on incidence of disease in the cacao plantation differed between the correlational and the experimental study. Our experiment did not identify an influence of ant species richness or species identity on the spread of P. palmivora. Ant species richness appeared to be associated with a decreasing role of species that are known to effectively reduce disease transmission. For example, the presence of A. gracilipes can exclude Technomyrmex albipes as an effective vector of BPD in cacao plantations (McGregor & Moxon, 1985). In addition, introduced P. cf. cordata, which is known to often act as an important vector of P. palmivora (Wielgoss et al., 2010, 2014), can compete with other dominant ants such as A. gracilipes, M. floricola and Crematogaster sp.1. Philidris cf. cordata, an ant species building tents on cacao fruits, contributes to the vertical spread of the pathogen by transporting contaminated soil within the cacao tree (Evans, 1973). Therefore, we expect that the occurrence of P. cf. cordata might promote the occurrence of BPD in cacao plantations after a longer period of establishment.

    The introduction of the aggressive ant species P. cf. cordata was successful, regardless of the number of ant species already present on the tree. This species can adapt well to new habitats, even if they are dominated by other invasive species such as A. gracilipes. Based on our observations, this species can easily occupy new cacao trees where nests were not experimentally placed. Philidris cf. cordata can quickly invade homogenized habitats such as cacao plantations and displace other ants (Wielgoss et al., 2010, 2014), including the invasive species A. gracilipes, and also takes over the trophobiont (Planococcoides sp.), which was always found to be abundant when A. gracilipes was present. Philidris cf. cordata appears to only require the presence of homopteran trophobionts for establishment (Wielgoss et al., 2014).

    In conclusion, the identity of the dominant ant species affected the incidence of pests and diseases, as well as predation pressure. The impact of ants on pests and diseases depended on the combination of the ant species and the pest or disease species considered. Tree‐level species richness was of secondary importance, and appeared to favour disease incidence. These results show that the use of ant species richness as an indicator for ecological functions and ecosystem services in agricultural systems can be misleading. It is also suggested that changes in identity in dominant ants (Wielgoss et al., 2014) can lead to important changes even if the ant communities remain diverse.

AbstractAbstract

Agriculture and the exploitation of natural resources have transformed tropical mountain ecosystems across the world, and the consequences of these transformations for biodiversity and ecosystem functioning are largely unknown1,2,3. Conclusions that are derived from studies in non-mountainous areas are not suitable for predicting the effects of land-use changes on tropical mountains because the climatic environment rapidly changes with elevation, which may mitigate or amplify the effects of land use4,5. It is of key importance to understand how the interplay of climate and land use constrains biodiversity and ecosystem functions to determine the consequences of global change for mountain ecosystems. Here we show that the interacting effects of climate and land use reshape elevational trends in biodiversity and ecosystem functions on Africa’s largest mountain, Mount Kilimanjaro (Tanzania). We find that increasing land-use intensity causes larger losses of plant and animal species richness in the arid lowlands than in humid submontane and montane zones. Increases in land-use intensity are associated with significant changes in the composition of plant, animal and microorganism communities; stronger modifications of plant and animal communities occur in arid and humid ecosystems, respectively. Temperature, precipitation and land use jointly modulate soil properties, nutrient turnover, greenhouse gas emissions, plant biomass and productivity, as well as animal interactions. Our data suggest that the response of ecosystem functions to land-use intensity depends strongly on climate; more-severe changes in ecosystem functioning occur in the arid lowlands and the cold montane zone. Interactions between climate and land use explained—on average—54% of the variation in species richness, species composition and ecosystem functions, whereas only 30% of variation was related to single drivers. Our study reveals that climate can modulate the effects of land use on biodiversity and ecosystem functioning, and points to a lowered resistance of ecosystems in climatically challenging environments to ongoing land-use changes in tropical mountainous regions.

MainMain

Tropical mountains are centres of biodiversity and major refugia for plants and animals in a world of accelerating climatic change6,7,8. They support more than 70% of global bird diversity and approximately half of the global biodiversity hotspots7,9,10, despite the fact that they cover less than 10% of the terrestrial land area. Moreover, tropical mountain ecosystems perform functions such as carbon storage, nutrient retention, the provision of water, pollination and pest control, which provide essential services to human societies from local to global scales2,4,11,12. Increasing human populations, the expansion and intensification of agriculture, and the exploitation of natural resources have led to marked changes in tropical mountain landscapes. Habitats that have been disturbed or transformed by human land use (hereafter, ‘anthropogenic’) have increasingly replaced natural vegetation along elevational gradients1. Despite the documented negative effects of these forms of land use on global biodiversity and ecosystem functioning13, their effect on tropical mountain ecosystems is poorly understood3. This is because, in mountainous regions, the effects of land use are superimposed on rapidly changing climatic conditions along the elevational gradient, which causes shifts in biodiversity and ecosystem functions that cannot be predicted from the results of studies that focus on single climatic zones5. Understanding the potential additive and interactive effects of climate and land use on biodiversity and ecosystem functions is key to attempts to predict and mitigate the consequences of global change on the world’s mountains, and along other climatic gradients14,15,16,17,18. Here we analyse the combined effects of climate and land use on biodiversity and ecosystem functioning of Mount Kilimanjaro.

Along an elevational gradient that rises from 700 to 5,895 m above sea level (a.s.l.), Mount Kilimanjaro covers several major natural ecosystems found in tropical Africa, from lowland savannah through montane forest belts to afro-alpine ecosystems19. Over the course of the past century the human population in the Kilimanjaro region has increased tenfold, which has led to the retraction of large areas of natural habitat in the lowland and montane zones of the mountain and to the expansion of anthropogenic ecosystems19; similar processes have affected nearly all tropical mountain regions of the world1. We simultaneously measured the biodiversity of vascular plants, animals (including 14 major taxa), soil microorganisms (mainly bacteria) and indicators of 30 ecosystem functions (Extended Data Table 1) at 60 study sites that covered all of the major natural ecosystems found on the southern slopes of Mount Kilimanjaro, as well as anthropogenic ecosystems (Extended Data Fig. 1).

We adopted a multimodel inference framework to formally test the importance of climate and land use for biodiversity and ecosystem functions. We considered mean annual temperature (MAT) and mean annual precipitation (MAP) as major climatic variables. On Mount Kilimanjaro, MAT is closely correlated with elevation (r = −0.98) whereas MAP exhibits a unimodal distribution (r = 0.33) with a peak at 2,200 m a.s.l. To assess the effects of land use on each study site, we determined four variables that characterize land-use intensity and that operate at local-to-landscape scales20 (percentage plant biomass removal, agricultural inputs, modification of vegetation structure and percentage agricultural area in the surrounding landscape) and calculated a quantitative, composite index of human land-use intensity (LUI; see Methods). We compared the statistical support among models that included separate effects of climate and LUI, as well as their additive or interactive effects, on biodiversity and ecosystem functions. In the case of an additive effect, the overall effect of both drivers represents the sum of their individual effects; in the case of an interaction effect between climate and LUI, the effect of LUI varies depending on climatic conditions.

Climate and land use influenced broad-scale trends of biodiversity along the elevational gradient. In natural habitats, the total community-level species richness of plants and animals declined quasi-linearly with elevation21 (Fig. 1a, b, generalized additive models: plants, explained deviance = 83%, n = 30, P < 0.001; animals, explained deviance = 92%, n = 30, P < 0.001). Human land use reshaped these broad-scale diversity gradients on Mount Kilimanjaro from a linear decline to a hump-shaped pattern (Fig. 1a, b, plants, explained deviance = 63%, n = 60, Pinteraction < 0.001; animals, explained deviance = 87%, n = 60, Pinteraction = 0.014). For both plants and animals, models that included an interaction term between climate and LUI were better supported by the data than models that included either LUI or climate, or models that assumed an additive effect (Fig. 1g, h, Extended Data Table 2). This means that the negative effect of land-use intensity on species richness varied conditionally on the effect of climate, and vice versa. Whereas species richness was little-affected by land-use intensity in the submontane and montane zones, an increase of land-use intensity in the arid lowlands led to a 50% and 30% loss of plant and animals species, respectively. In contrast to plants and animals, the richness of soil microorganisms only slightly declined with elevation and was not significantly influenced by land-use intensity (Fig. 1c).

Alongside changes in species richness, biotic communities may change in their species composition with increasing land-use intensity and with changing climate. This is fundamental to nature conservation, as anthropogenic ecosystems may retain a large species diversity despite a complete change in community composition22. Non-metric multidimensional scaling, based on the presence and absence data of 992 plant species, 2,164 animal species and abundance of 15,925 microorganism molecular operational taxonomic units, revealed a strong spatial turnover of communities along the natural elevation gradient of Mount Kilimanjaro (plants, mean turnover = 90%, range = 18–100%; animals, mean = 87%, range = 33–100%; microorganisms, mean = 71%, range = 23–95%; Fig. 1d–f). The patterns of turnover reflected the elevational zonation of the mountain, which was highly similar across the three taxa (permutational Procrustes analysis, r = 0.84–0.93, P < 0.01). Land use strongly modified the composition of species communities in all three taxa, and in nearly all elevational zones (Fig. 1d–f). The effects of land-use intensity were slightly stronger for plants in the warm, arid lowlands and stronger for animals in the humid, submontane zone of the mountain (Extended Data Fig. 2). Consequently, climate–LUI interaction models explained considerably more of the variation in species turnover across plant and animal taxa than did simple additive effect models, or models with single predictor variables (Fig. 1g–i).

The degree to which most ecosystem functions are controlled by the interplay of climate and land use remains poorly understood, as studies of ecosystem functions are largely concerned with effects of plant diversity, conducted in small-scale manipulative experiments or in study regions with narrow climatic gradients23,24,25. Climate was a strong predictor of ecosystem functions along the Kilimanjaro elevation gradient, and explained—on average—47% (± 26% (s.d.), range = 0–93%) of the variation in ecosystem functions across the elevational gradient of natural habitats (Extended Data Table 3). In natural ecosystems, most soil- and plant-mediated functions peaked at middle elevations with highest precipitation and net primary productivity (Fig. 2). Among animal-mediated functions, dung decomposition, bat and ant foraging, insect parasitism rates and animal biomass declined with elevation whereas insect herbivory peaked at lower middle elevations (Fig. 2). MAP was more important than MAT in predicting plant- and soil-mediated ecosystem functions, whereas MAT tended to have stronger effects than MAP on ecosystem functions mediated by the animal biota (Extended Data Fig. 3).

A majority of ecosystem functions (83%) was altered by land-use intensity and, in most cases, the effect of land-use intensity varied with climate: the climate–LUI interaction model was best supported by the data for 63% of the 30 ecosystem functions (Fig. 3a). The inclusion of LUI in models increased the explained variation of ecosystem functions from 40% (±21% (s.d.)) for models that only include climate variables to an average of 55% (±20% (s.d.)) for climate–LUI interaction models (Extended Data Table 2). The elevational zones in which the strongest effects of land-use intensity on ecosystem functions were observed varied for different ecosystem functions (Fig. 2, Extended Data Table 2). For instance, soil organic carbon content decreased with increasing land-use intensity, especially at high elevations that are characterized by high MAP, whereas greenhouse gas emissions increased or decreased in anthropogenic systems depending on elevation. The large degree of support for climate–LUI interaction models across ecosystem functions was robust to different weighting of LUI components, and to considering potentially confounding environmental variables that systematically change with elevation (such as geology, land area, atmospheric pressure and solar radiation) (Extended Data Fig. 4, Supplementary Note 1, Supplementary Tables 1, 2, Supplementary Figs. 1–3). Changing climatic conditions along the elevation gradient shape the natural vegetation zones on mountains, and likewise constrain the types of potential anthropogenic land use. As organisms and ecosystem functions may respond more strongly to certain aspects of land use, we also tested whether specific components of the LUI (for example, biomass removal) were better predictors of response variables than the full LUI index. However, the full LUI index outperformed single LUI components in a majority of cases. Climate–land-use interaction effects were equally supported in models using single LUI components as in models using the LUI (paired t-test on differences in the mean support for the interaction model, degrees of freedom = 28, P = 0.89, Supplementary Table 3).

To quantify the overall differences in ecosystem functioning, we computed two measures of ecosystem multifunctionality: a multivariate index of multifunctionality26 and a measure of the average change of ecosystem functions in anthropogenic, compared to natural, habitats. The multivariate index of multifunctionality was calculated across all 30 ecosystem functions and indicates the overall dissimilarity in functional characteristics among study sites (Extended Data Fig. 5). Patterns of dissimilarity in multivariate index of multifunctionality strongly reflected the dissimilarity structure of species communities (Fig. 1d–f), which points to a close relationship between species composition and ecosystem functioning in real-world ecosystems. The multivariate index of multifunctionality was best explained by a climate–LUI interaction model (Extended Data Table 4). In addition, we quantified the average change in ecosystem functions induced by land-use intensity as the average Euclidian difference in ecosystem functions (z-standardized) of study sites relative to the conditions predicted for natural habitats along the elevation gradient. Across the elevation zones of the mountain, changes in ecosystem multifunctionality increased with land-use intensity (Fig. 3b), and stronger changes were observed for plant-mediated ecosystem functions than for animal- or soil-mediated ecosystem functions (Extended Data Fig. 6). The effect of land-use intensity on ecosystem multifunctionality was higher in the arid lowlands and in the cold montane zone than in the climatically moderate submontane elevations (Fig. 3c).

Owing to the variety of the biodiversity and ecosystem functions variables that we analysed, the potential causes for the relevance of the climate–land-use interaction effect are manifold. First, the human footprint may decrease with elevation (as in other mountain systems1), which would lead to stronger anthropogenic changes in lowland ecosystems. At Mount Kilimanjaro, LUI declined with elevation (r = −0.70) and increased with MAT (r = 0.73), which could cause problems in disentangling effects of land-use intensity from those of MAT. However, analyses that were restricted to lower elevations at which MAT and LUI were less-strongly correlated (n = 30 study sites, r = 0.18), and analyses of less-correlated random subsets of data, produced findings similar to those found when using the full dataset (Supplementary Tables 4, 5). Second, the relevance of interaction effects between climate and land use could be based on the fact that the biotas of different climate zones vary in their response to habitat modification. Species elevational ranges and environmental tolerance levels were found to increase from low to high elevations27, which suggests that the lowland biota is more sensitive to environmental changes. Likewise, because many organisms in the lowlands and montane zones are living closer to their thermal limits28, simplifications of habitats and subsequent stronger exposures to extreme climatic conditions (for example, aridity, heat or frost) may have more-detrimental effects on biodiversity and ecosystem functions in the warm lowlands and cold montane elevation zones than in the submontane zone, which has a temperate climate. Third, ecological communities in lowland and montane zones could be characterized by a lower functional redundancy, as environmental filtering in these environments may have caused a homogenization of species traits29. Differences in the environmental tolerances of species and subsequent changes in the properties of ecological communities suggest that temperate, submontane climates may have a higher resistance to ecosystem disturbance, and support faster ecosystem recovery after such disturbance, compared to less-resilient ecosystems in tropical arid and montane climates. Research initiatives that link elevational patterns with experiments will be necessary to better understand the nature of climate–land-use interactions.

Most previous studies on effects of land use have been conducted within a single climatic environment, although results have often been generalized to regional or global scales. Our finding of climate–land-use interactions along the broad climatic gradient of Mount Kilimanjaro reveals that land-use effects on biodiversity and ecosystem functioning depend strongly on the climatic context. With a scarcity of high-quality time series, studies along broad-scale climatic gradients provide—if interpreted with care—an opportunity to project the consequences of climate change for biodiversity and ecosystem functions30 (‘space-for-time-approach’). In this respect, the interactive effects of climate and land use detected in our study underpin how interactions between drivers of global change can lead to a fundamental reorganization of ecological communities, biotic interactions and biogeochemical ecosystem processes16. An understanding of the interplay of multiple drivers will be necessary to mitigate the far-reaching consequences of global change for tropical mountains, and the human populations that rely on their ecosystem services.

MethodsMethods

No statistical methods were used to predetermine sample size. The experiments were not randomized and investigators were not blinded to allocation during experiments and outcome assessment.

Time and area of study

All data were collected from January 2011 to December 2016 on the southern and southeastern slopes of Mount Kilimanjaro (Tanzania, East Africa; 2° 45′–3° 25′ S, 37° 00′–37° 43′ E). Mount Kilimanjaro has a northwest–southeast diameter of about 90 km, and rises from the savannah plains at 700 m to a snow-clad summit at 5,895 m a.s.l. Precipitation is bimodal: the main rainy season occurs from March to May, and the more-variable short rains arrive around November31. The MAT decreases in a quasi-linear manner with elevation; it has an overall lapse rate of ~0.56 °C per 100 m, starting at 25 °C in the foothills and decreasing to −8 °C at the top of the mountain31. The vertical precipitation distribution shows a unimodal pattern, with ~500–1,000 mm year−1 at the base of the mountain, approximately 2,100–2,700 mm year−1 at 2,200 m a.s.l. and less than 1,000 mm year−1 in the afro-alpine zone31. Owing to a long history of human effects, natural habitats in the lowland and submontane zone of the mountain were largely cleared. Habitats above 1,800 m a.s.l. are protected as Mount Kilimanjaro National Park19.

Study design

We established sixty 50 × 50-m2 study sites that spanned an elevational gradient of 866 to 4,550 m a.s.l. Elevations of lower than 866 m are only rarely found in the East African highlands, and the lowest elevations incorporated in this study can be considered as a typical, natural base of the mountain (within a square of 2° × 2° ≈ 222 km × 222 km, with the Kibo peak of Mount Kilimanjaro in the centre, 100% of the area is >500 m a.s.l. and 83% > 800 m a.s.l.). The study sites were equally distributed over the six major natural habitat types and six major types of anthropogenic ecosystems found along the southeastern slope of Mount Kilimanjaro. Natural habitats include lowland savannah (~800–1,150 m a.s.l.), submontane forest (~1,150–2,100 m a.s.l.), montane Ocotea forest (~2,100–2,800 m a.s.l.) and Podocarpus forest (~2,800–3,200 m a.s.l.), subalpine Erica forest and bushland (~3,200–4,000 m a.s.l.) and alpine Helichrysum vegetation (~3,850–4,600 m a.s.l.). At lower elevations, major anthropogenic ecosystems were maize fields (previously savannah), agroforestry systems (chagga home gardens), coffee plantations and grasslands (previously submontane forest). At higher elevations, the major anthropogenic habitat types were Ocotea forest affected by selective logging (previously Ocotea forest) and Podocarpus forest affected by human-induced fires (previously Podocarpus forest). In all cases, minimum distances among study sites of the same habitat type were at least 300 m—they were typically much larger (Extended Data Fig. 1). Five study sites per habitat type were distributed to reflect a within-habitat-type elevation gradient to detect fine-scale changes in biodiversity with changing elevation. If possible, study sites were established in core zones of larger areas of the respective habitat to minimize the effects of transition zones.

LUI

The LUI was calculated from four components as follows. (a) The annual removal of plant biomass was calculated by averaging standardized estimates of plant biomass removal on study sites: this included estimates of biomass removal by mowing, cattle grazing, ploughing, fire events, logging and firewood collection. With the exception of ploughing (which was estimated on an ordinal scale (no ploughing, ploughing by hand and ploughing with a tractor)), all estimates were calculated as a percentage of the standing biomass removed per year. All estimates depended on repeat visits to each of the study sites (>20 times per site), and by cross-checking personal estimates with information on activities and land-use information provided by local land owners. Estimations were always conducted by the same person (A.H.), who is a plant ecologist who knows and has visited the study sites for more than 25 years11,19,32. (b) Agricultural inputs were calculated by averaging standardized estimates of irrigation, fertilization, insecticide, fungicide and herbicide treatments. Estimates were done on an ordinal scale (no or very low, medium or high input), depending on information given by local land owners and personal observations (A.H.) of the study sites. (c) Structural characteristics that describe the dissimilarity to natural habitats are often used as an indicator of land use. To describe the vegetation structure, we measured the canopy closure using a canopy densitometer, the maximum canopy height using a laser rangefinder and the vegetation heterogeneity. The latter was calculated as the Shannon–Wiener diversity of canopy cover values estimated by an observer in the field at heights of 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 and 64 m. All measurements of canopy closure, canopy height and vegetation heterogeneity were conducted at nine points that were equally dispersed over the 50 × 50-m2 study site, and averaged. As the natural vegetation strongly changes along elevational gradients, we could not use the raw data as a measure of land use. Raw canopy closure can be a good measure of land use—for example, in submontane sites at which the natural vegetation (forest) has a canopy closure of 100% and managed ecosystems values of <100%. However, raw canopy closure is not a suitable indicator of land-use intensity for ecosystems in the lowland savannah zone, in which even the natural vegetation has a canopy closure of ~10%. Therefore, we calculated the mean Euclidian dissimilarity of vegetation-structure measures of the respective study site to the average vegetation structure that was measured for study sites that held natural vegetation (for which the dissimilarity to natural vegetation was assumed, by definition, to be 0). (d) As it is well-established that land-use intensification at the landscape scale influences ecosystem properties at a local scale33, we included a variable that describes the landscape composition—that is, the proportion of areas with agriculturally managed habitats in the surrounding landscape (radius = 1,500 m)—as a fourth indicator of land use. The calculation of the relative proportion of natural versus managed habitats was based on a land-cover classification of the Kilimanjaro region that identified 27 different habitat types (18 natural and 9 managed habitat types) in the study area. This product is based on a maximum likelihood classification applied to four almost-cloud-free Terra-ASTER scenes from 11 February 2005, 2 November 2008, 28 February 2011 and 24 February 2013. All four components of the LUI were standardized, before averaging them to a final composite LUI. All standardizations were done by calculating the difference of individual measures from the mean of all measures, divided by the range of all measurements (yi − ȳ)/(maximum(y) − minimum(y)). This produced LUI values of between 0 (lowest land use) and 1 (highest land use). The full LUI index, which is based on all four components (biomass removal, chemical inputs, vegetation structure and landscape composition), was significantly correlated with a land-use index that is solely based on empirically assessed data (vegetation structure and landscape composition) (r = 0.95, P < 0.001). Land use is a complex variable that is often defined by different criteria in different studies, and therefore support for land-use effects may depend largely on the definition of the land-use index that is used. Using alternative measures of land use—either the single components of the LUI (Supplementary Table 3), a binary land-use variable (Supplementary Table 2) or a battery of land-use indices generated by randomly shuffling the relative weights of the four LUI components—in calculating the land-use index did not change the overall patterns (Extended Data Fig. 4). Furthermore, there was no single LUI component that outperformed the full LUI index across the range of biodiversity and ecosystem functions that we measured.

Species-richness data

Biodiversity was assessed using standardized approaches for all vascular plants, a total of 14 animal groups (terrestrial gastropods, millipedes, Orthoptera, ground-dwelling beetles, dung beetles, hoverflies, ants, bees, other aculeate wasps, parasitoid wasps, moths, insectivorous bats, birds and large mammals) and microorganisms. With the exception of those for large mammals and soil microorganisms, sampling methods have previously been described21.

Large mammals (performed by F. Gebert, I.S.-D. and M.K.P.)

Species richness and composition of large mammals were assessed using camera traps (Bushnell Trophy Cam) and analyses of dung remains. Five camera traps were installed at each study site for a total amount of 70 trap-days per site. The occurrence of dung was monitored along standardized transects per study site. Species richness per study site was calculated as the number of all non-domestic mammal species recorded on study sites. Large mammal biomass (here used as an indicator of ecosystem function) was estimated as the sum of the body mass of all mammals recorded with camera traps. For this measure, we also included domesticated mammals.

Soil microbial diversity (performed by M.K.P., A. Keller, R.B. and I.S.-D.)

Soil microorganisms were sampled at three positions per study site (about 20 m from each other). First, at each position four soil samples were collected by hammering a stainless steel tube (inner diameter 3 cm; depth, 10 cm) into the soil. The soil from these four samples was homogenized in a sterile plastic bag and a small quantity was placed in lysis tubes filled with ZR BashingBeads and 750 μl of Xpedition lysis–stabilization solution (Zymo Research). Second, at each position, 32 fallen dead leaves were collected and put in a 50-ml tube. The leaves were repeatedly stirred with a long cotton swap. The cotton end was then cut and placed into lysis tubes, as described above. Samples in lysis tubes were further processed by bead beating, with an Xpedition sample processor (XSP, Zymo Research) directly in the field. Samples were stored at room temperature until they were processed further in the laboratory. In the laboratory, stabilized DNA was isolated using the Xpedition Fungal/Bacterial DNA MiniPrep (Zymo Research), following the manufacturer’s instructions. PCR and library preparation were performed according to a previously published dual-indexing approach using primers for the V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene34 and a previously published protocol35, and sequenced on a Illumina MiSeq Platform (Illumina) using 2 × 250-bp v2 MiSeq chemistry. Bioinformatic processing followed a previous publication35. For the final MOTU dataset, we pooled the data from the six samples of each study site and removed all chloroplast and mitochondrial sequences. A few study sites had missing individual samples; however, excluding these sites did not change any of the detected patterns.

Climate data and indicators of ecosystem functions

MAT and MAP (performed by A.H., T.A., E.N. and T.N.)

All study sites were equipped with temperature sensors that were installed approximately 2 m above the ground, and continuously measured temperatures in 5-min intervals for a time period of approximately two years. We subsequently calculated the MAT as the average of all measurements per study site. As data from single sites were missing owing to data loggers being repeatedly stolen, we modelled MAT by elevation in a generalized additive model (R2 = 0.99), and estimated MAT for the study sites on the basis of this model. MAP was interpolated for every study site using a co-kriging approach on the basis of a 15-year-long dataset from a network of about 70 rain gauges on Mount Kilimanjaro. More details are available from previous publications21,31.

Soil pH, soil C/N ratio and soil N/P ratio (performed by J.N.B., H.P. and Y.K.)

Soil samples were taken from soil pits on horizon basis, and in 10-cm intervals, using a standard soil auger. The samples were sieved (2-mm mesh), and roots and plant materials were removed. Field samples were dried at 105 °C for 364 h, and ground for further analysis. For all study sites, results were standardized and averaged to 0–50 cm depth. Total C and N contents were determined using a dry combustion automated C:N analyser (Vario EL, Elementar) at 950 °C. The total content of P was measured using inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (Spectro Analytical Instruments) after preparative pressure digestion (HNO3 digestion). Soil pH was measured in a KCl solution (1 M, 1:2.5) using a pH probe. n = 60 study sites.

Soil organic carbon and δ15N signature of soil and litter (performed by F. Gerschlauer and R.K.)

Surface litter and soil (0–5 cm) were sampled at five locations per study site. Material of both layers was air-dried before being processed further. Soil was sieved to 2 mm before grinding with a mixer mill (MM200, Retsch). All samples were analysed with a dry combustion elemental analyser (Costech International) fitted with a zero-blank autosampler coupled to a ThermoFinnigan DeltaPlus-XL using continuous-flow isotope ratio mass spectrometry for determination of δ15N and elemental C (corresponding to soil organic C). All samples were separately analysed at the Centre for Stable Isotopes at the Institute of Meteorology and Climate Research (IMK-IFU/KIT). Individual values for soil and litter were averaged for each site. δ15N relative abundance (in ‰) is expressed as: δ‰ = (Rsample − Rstandard/Rstandard) × 1,000, in which Rsample refers to the 15N/14N molar ratio in the sample and Rstandard to the atmospheric N2 (N) standard. δ15N values can be used as an indicator of ecosystem N turnover (for example, mineralization, nitrification and denitrification) and associated N losses (gaseous and leaching). Higher N losses result in relatively higher δ15N in soil and litter caused by fractionation during N turnover processes and losses36,37; thus, enriched δ15N values reflect more open N cycles than the low δ15N values, which indicate tighter N cycling. n = 60 study sites.

Leaf decomposition (performed by J.R. and R.B.)

Maize straw was used as a standard litter to estimate the relative rates of decomposition on study sites. On each site, three litter bags (10 cm × 15 cm), with 4 × 4-mm2 mesh size, containing 5 ± 0.05 g maize husks were exposed for 69–86 days from March to June 2012. The litter originated from two fields managed by the same farmer, and was dried at 72 °C for 3 days to reduce microbial loads before it was used in the experiments. For logistic reasons, bags at lower elevations were partly exposed for longer than the bags at higher elevations. We therefore calculated decomposition rates per day to account for these differences. Upon collection, the bags were dried at 72 °C for at least 2 days or at 40 °C for at least 4 days to stop decomposition. To separate the remaining litter mass from mineral soil, the organic matter was burned at 550 °C for 30 min in a muffle oven (CEM SAM255). Additional litter bags that were not exposed in the field were used to estimate moisture content at the time of preparation (8.5 ± 3.0% (mean ± s.d.), w/w) and fractions of ash (2.5 ± 0.32%). These values were used to determine ash-free organic matter content in the litter samples exposed at the sites. Decomposition rates (k) were calculated as k = ln(weight after loss-on-ignition/original ash-free weight)/number of days exposed, and averaged per study site. n = 58 study sites.

Greenhouse gas emissions (performed by A. D., F. Gerschlauer and R.K.)

Soil core incubation measurements were used to estimate emissions of the three important greenhouse gases (CO2, N2O and CH4). At each study site, one undisturbed soil core (12.5-cm inner diameter, 25-cm length) was collected and shipped within a month to laboratory facilities at IMK-IFU/KIT. Before being measured for greenhouse gas emissions, the soil cores were pre-incubated (Lovibond incubator ET 651-8, Tintometer) for two weeks at the site-specific MAT, and watered equivalent to 20 mm of standard rain mixture38 with water contents kept stable at this level for a 5-day measurement period. Measurements of CO2, CH4 and N2O concentration changes were monitored with a dual quantum cascade laser (Aerodyne Research) at 1-Hz time intervals. CO2, CH4 and N2O fluxes were calculated from linear concentration changes in the headspace in R (version 3.0.0) with the HMR package39. For each soil core, a mean value of CO2, CH4 and N2O fluxes was calculated from five single flux values. Further details of measurement set-up and conditions have previously been published40. n = 60 study sites.

Available water capacity (performed by A. Kühnel, B.H. and C. Bogner)

Soil sampling rings (100 cm3) were taken from different horizons along the soil profile on 12 of the study sites and from the topsoil of 5 additional study sites. These samples were used to measure the water retention at different matric potential levels. Water retention curves were then estimated using the van-Genuchten equation41, and the available water capacity per horizon was calculated as water content at pF 4.2 − water content at pF 1.8. The total available water capacity in millimetres was determined by summing up the available water capacity of the individual horizons down to 100 cm, or until bedrock was reached. Bulk soil samples were taken either from individual horizons of the soil profiles or from soil auger samples at different depth intervals down to at least 50 cm (or until bedrock was reached) on all study sites. In these soil samples, soil organic C, cation exchange capacity and base saturation were measured. Together with the habitat type, this information were used for a random forest model (R2 = 0.88, root mean square error = 4.93) to predict the available water capacity for all other soil samples. Total available water capacity in millimetres was then calculated in the same way as for the measured samples. n = 60 study sites.

Net primary productivity (performed by T.A., F.D., I.O. and T.N.)

We used the normalized difference vegetation index as a proxy for net primary productivity. Normalized difference vegetation index estimations were based on MODIS Aqua product MYD13Q1 with a horizontal resolution of 250 m × 250 m, processed as previously described21 and subsequently averaged across multiple years (from 2003 to 2016). n = 60 study sites.

Vegetation biomass (performed by A. E., G.R., A.H. and M.F.)

We determined vegetation biomass per study site by summing up the biomass of trees, shrubs and herbs42. The biomass of trees and shrubs was estimated using pantropical allometric equations applied on measures of tree height, diameter at breast height and wood density (which were taken for each tree at each study site). Shrub biomass was estimated in subplots of 5 m × 20 m in the centre of each study site, and then extrapolated to the scale of study sites. Herbaceous biomass was determined by removing all plant biomass of the herbaceous layer in four representative subplots of 0.25 m × 0.25 m per study site during the time of year at which herbaceous biomass was maximal. Herbaceous samples were dried in a drying oven at 72 °C for 72 h and then weighed. n = 53 study sites.

Leaf area index (performed by A. E., M. R., G.R., A.H. and M.F.)

The leaf area index is a key variable for models of biosphere–atmosphere exchanges of energy, carbon dioxide and water vapour43. We measured the leaf area index at nine points per study site and calculated the mean from these measures44. The leaf area index was measured at ground level using a plant canopy analyser in combination with a remote ‘above-canopy’ sensor (LAI 2200, LI-COR Bioscience). The above-canopy reading was measured at ground level in an open area or forest gap as close to the site as possible, and leaf area index values were calculated using the program FV-2200 (LI-COR Bioscience). n = 60 study sites.

Fine root biomass (performed N.S.C. and D.H.)

At each plot, 10 to 15 soil cores (inner diameter 3.5 cm) were taken randomly down to a depth of 40 cm. Soil samples were kept in plastic bags and stored at 5 °C. In the laboratory, samples were soaked and washed through a sieve of 200-μm mesh size. All root fragments larger than 1 cm were extracted by hand with tweezers. Root fractions were dried at 70 °C for 48 h, weight and fine root biomass and necromass were expressed as Mg ha−1. Additionally, to obtain more-accurate data, we carried out a more-detailed analysis of the fraction with the remaining roots that were smaller than 1 cm. Following a previously introduced45 and modified46 method, in 6 of the 10–15 samples, after the extraction of the large rootlets fraction (> 1 cm) we spread the remaining material homogenously on a filter paper (730 cm2) subdivided into 36 squares. We randomly selected six of these squares and extracted the roots fragments under a stereomicroscope. The mass of each entire sample was extrapolated by using linear regressions that relate this small fraction to the larger fraction. When it was not possible to apply the regressions, a mean ratio of the small:large root fractions was used. n = 58 study sites.

Plant traits (performed by D.S.C. and M.K.)

We calculated community-weighted means of the C/N ratio, N/P ratio, wood density and specific leaf area of plants from the most-abundant species per study site. These species together contributed >80% of total plant biomass at a study site. Plant functional trait measurement followed the LEDA protocols (www.leda-traitbase.org)47. Laboratory analyses for leaf C, N and P contents were done at Oldenburg University. Further details have previously been published48. n = 60 study sites.

Flower density (performed by A.C., M.K.P. and I.S.-D.)

Flower densities were estimated on ten 4 × 5-m2 subplots established along two permanent transects49 of 50 m. In each subplot, we counted the number of flower units, including flowers of the lower canopy. We defined a flower unit as a patch of flowers that a small pollinator of about 1-cm length would exploit by walking instead of flying (for example, the composite inflorescence of Asteraceae plants was counted as one flower unit). At each study site, flower densities were estimated three times and flower density was calculated as the average of these estimates. n = 60 study sites.

Fruit density (performed by S.F., M.V., M.S. and K.B.-G.)

We determined fruit density by estimating the number of ripe fleshy fruits of all woody plants on each study site on a logarithmic scale. Fruits were counted once in the rainy season and once in the dry season within each of eight subplots (each about 1,300 m2 in size) per study site. Fruit density was calculated for each study site as the sum of these counts. Further details have previously been published50. n = 60 study sites.

Woody plant regrowth (performed by M.R., A.H. and M.F.)

Estimates of the density of woody plants regrowth were done in 20 × 5-m2 subplots in the centre of study sites. We counted all woody stems of shrub and tree species with a height between 25 and 130 cm, following previous studies on woody plant regeneration51,52. These small stems represent the vegetative and sexual regeneration of woody species—that is, saplings, root suckers and re-sprouting stems. n = 56 study sites.

Invertebrate biomass (performed by S.W.F., M.S. and K.B.-G.)

We estimated insect biomass in the understory using a transect-based sweep netting approach50. Once in the dry season and once in the rainy season, one hundred sweeps with a 30-cm-diameter sweep net were conducted along two parallel, permanently marked 50-m transects on each study site. All invertebrates (>1 mm) were lumped into one sample per study site and season, and weighed to obtain the fresh biomass. Invertebrate biomass was calculated as the mean of the two sampling rounds per study site. n = 60 study sites.

Frugivory (performed by M.V., M.S. and K.B.-G.)

We measured frugivory by quantifying the number of fruits removed by birds and mammals over a study period of 25 h in subplots of 30 m × 100 m. Study sites in high-elevation Erica forests and alpine Helichrysum vegetation were excluded in this analysis. To record frugivore interactions, we observed frugivores with binoculars by scanning the entire area of the subplot with equal attention. Observations were conducted within a period of four consecutive days: seven hours (hours one to five after sunrise, and the two hours before sunset) on the first 3 days and for four hours on the final day (hours one to four after sunrise). We counted the number of fruits each frugivore individual handled per visit. When there was more than one individual of a frugivorous species feeding simultaneously, we counted the number of fruit handled by one individual per visit and then multiplied by the number of individuals per species. Frugivory was calculated as the total number of fruits consumed by all individuals of birds and mammals per study site. n = 47 study sites.

Pollinator abundance (performed by A.C., M.K.P. and I.S.-D.)

The abundance of pollinators (hoverflies and bees) was quantified using pan traps49,53. A total of eight pan-trap clusters, each consisting of one UV–bright blue pan, one yellow pan and one white pan were installed along two 50-m transects on each plot with a minimal distance of 15 m between clusters. We sampled pollinators in different vegetation heights (35 cm (herbaceous layer) and ~120 cm (shrub layer) above the ground). At study sites in forests, we installed a subset of traps in the lower canopy (up to ~25 m). Pan traps were filled with water and a drop of liquid soap to break the surface tension of the water, and were recollected after 48 h. Three sampling rounds were conducted, summing up to a total of 24 pan trap clusters per plot, and we calculated pollinator abundance as the log-transformed sum of the number of hoverflies and bees collected per study site. n = 60 study sites.

Insect herbivory (performed by H.K.N., M.K.P. and I.S.-D.)

We estimated rates of standing insect herbivory in terms of the percentage of leaf area of understory trees or bushes that was damaged by herbivorous insects. At each study site, 15 trees were randomly selected and permanently marked. Twice in the dry season and twice in the rainy season, we randomly collected 30 leaves per tree and visually estimated the percentage damage caused by leaf miners, leaf chewers and gall-inducing insects. Leaf herbivory measured by this methodology was highly correlated with herbivory measures determined by a computer-aided quantification of leaf damage using images of leaves (P < 0.001). Leaf sizes of trees and bushes became very small at elevations >3,100 m, at which heights insects regularly eat whole leaves instead of parts of a leaf. Measures of leaf herbivory using the above-mentioned methods were therefore considered unreliable for these elevations, such that our analyses of herbivory cover an elevational gradient of only 866–3,100 m a.s.l. n = 49 study sites.

Bat foraging (performed by M.H.-B. and M.T.)

Foraging rates of aerial insectivorous bats were assessed by acoustic monitoring using a standardized point-stop method at the four corners of the study sites. Every corner was visited for five minutes and echolocation calls of all passing bats were recorded manually using a real time ultrasound recorder (Pettersson D1000x). All four corners of one site were visited four times between local sunset and 23:30, resulting in 80 minutes of recording per study site per night. Echolocation calls were classified following published methods54,55 and we calculated bat foraging rates as the mean number of final buzzes detected per sampling night. The final buzzes characterize the final phase of an attack on a flying insect and are therefore a good measure of bat foraging rates. n = 60 study sites.

Dung decomposition (performed by F. Gebert, M.K.P. and I.S.-D.)

We placed 750 g of fresh cow dung (about 120 g dry weight) at each study site in two different seasons. After 15 days, the remaining dung was collected, dried at 72 °C for 1 week and weighed. Dung decomposition was calculated for each study site as the average remaining dry mass of cow dung multiplied by −1. n = 58 study sites.

Ant foraging rate (performed by M.K.P.)

The foraging rates of ants were estimated with bait experiments. Thirty 50-ml Falcon tubes were filled with 10 ml of a nutrient solution (sugar, sugar–protein, protein, water, salt and oil; five replicates per nutrient) and placed along three 50-m transects at ground level. After two hours baits were recollected and the number of baits detected by ants was recorded as a measure of ant foraging. Details have previously been published56. n = 43 study sites.

Insect parasitism (performed by A.V.M., M.K.P. and I.S.-D.)

We quantified rates of parasitism of host insects (bees and wasps) by antagonist insects using trap-nests57. Initially we placed trap nests along the complete elevational gradient at Mount Kilimanjaro. However, as trap-nesting bees and wasps were not found from the forest belt upward, we had to restrict this analysis to a total of 24 study sites (in savannah, maize plantations, chagga home gardens, grasslands and coffee plantations) at which trap-nesting insects occurred. At each study site, eight trap-nests arranged in four paired trap-nests were placed above the ground (140 cm). At study sites with trees, such as savannah, chagga home gardens and coffee plantations, we installed two pairs of traps in the lower canopy (up to ~25 m height) and two pairs above the ground. Standardized trap nests were built with a mixture of ~120 internodes of common reed (Phragmites australis) and Japanese knotweed (Fallopia japonica) with diameters of 2–25 mm and lengths of 20 cm. Glue was used to prevent ants plundering the nests. Trap nests were controlled monthly over a period of 24 months and occupied nests were replaced by new internodes. Occupied internodes were closed with metal nets and were placed in a hatching box on the study site. Internodes were cut open and parasitism rates were recorded in the laboratory after hatching. Parasitism rate was calculated as the average quotient between the number of parasitized brood cells (in which the host was killed) and the total number of brood cells per study site. n = 21 study sites.

Statistical analyses

Generalized additive models were used to visualize the relationships between species richness or ecosystem function (response) and elevation (continuous predictor)58. In general additive models, the relationship between response variable and predictor variable is not specified by some explicit functional form, such that (for example) nonlinear relationships can be identified without prior (and often subjectively chosen) model specifications. Instead, nonparametric smoothers are used to describe the relationship58. All ecosystem function indicators were z-transformed before the analysis. For reasons of graphical clarity, land use was added as a dichotomous factorial variable (natural versus anthropogenic habitat) in models, even though we based our main statistical analyses (that is, the tests of five major climate and land-use models described below) on models in which land use was integrated as a continuous predictor variable (that is, the LUI). The data family was set to Poisson or Gaussian type in the case of species richness and ecosystem functions, respectively, and the basis dimension of the smoothing function was set to five (but adapted to smaller or sometimes larger values (up to six) in case of over- (or under-)parameterization). We first added land-use type as an interactive effect in generalized additive models, testing for different relationships of response variables and elevation between anthropogenic and natural habitats. If the interaction term was insignificant (P > 0.05), we deleted the interaction term in models but tested for additive effects of elevation and land-use type—that is, we tested for differences in the intercept between natural and anthropogenic habitats. If the effect of land-use type was not significant, we deleted land-use type from the set of predictor variables and modelled the response variables only by elevation—that is, assuming the same mean distributions of response variables in anthropogenic and natural habitats. If the smooth function of elevation was not significant, we assumed the null model to be best supported by the data and modelled the response variable simply by the intercept (that is, the mean across data values (predicting horizontal regression lines)).

To evaluate the importance of land-use and climate effects on biodiversity and ecosystem function, we evaluated the support for five different hypotheses with linear models within a multimodel inference approach based on information theory. Multimodel inference not only accounted for uncertainty in parameter estimates but also for uncertainty in model selection. Moreover, multimodel inference is a way to objectively deal with correlated predictor variables: strongly correlated predictor variables with the same explanatory power will get reduced support in multimodel inference (see supplementary figure 9 in a previous publication21). The five tested hypotheses or models were as follows. (i) The null model: we assumed that neither climate nor LUI is capable of explaining the variation in response variables, and simply modelled biodiversity and ecosystem function variables by the intercept. (ii) The climate (climate-only) model: we analysed whether climate alone is sufficient to explain the variation in biodiversity and ecosystem functions. MAT and MAP were included as predictor variables in the start model, and we selected the best model that included at least one of the two climate variables. (iii) The LUI (land-use-only model): we tested the hypothesis that biodiversity and ecosystem functions are best explained by the effect of the LUI alone. (iv) The climate + LUI (additive model): we assumed simple additive effects of climate and LUI variables. MAT, MAP and land use were added as additive predictor variables in start models, and we selected the best model (with the lowest value of Akaike information criterion with correction for small sample sizes (AICC)) that included LUI and at least one climate variable. (v) The climate × LUI (interaction model): in this model, we assumed interactive effects of climate and LUI (that is, we tested the hypothesis that climate modulates the effects of LUI). MAT × LUI and MAP × LUI were included as interaction terms in models. From the set of all possible combinations of these predictor variables (including simple additive effects of MAP or MAT), we selected the best model (with the lowest AICC value) that included at least one climate × LUI interaction term.

For each response variable (species richness of plants, animals, microorganisms and ecosystem functions), we calculated the support for each of the above mentioned models by calculating the AICC-based model weight (also known as ‘AIC weight’ or ‘model probability’). The model weight is directly calculated from AICC values of models, and is a measure of the statistical support that a model receives across the full model space. In addition to model weights, we also calculated the coefficient of determination (r2) for the best-supported models, and conditional significance values and variable importance values for each predictor variable, over the multimodel space composed of all most likely models (ΔAICC < 3). For multimodel averaging analyses, the R package MuMIn (http://CRAN.R-project.org/package=MuMIn, accessed 29 March 2017) was used.

To visualize and analyse the effect of climate and land-use intensity on species turnover (β-diversity), we calculated a dissimilarity matrix of each biodiversity dataset (plants, animals and microorganisms) using the Sørensen dissimilarity index based on presence and absence data in plants and animals, and the abundance-based Bray–Curtis dissimilarity index in microorganisms. Presence and absence data, rather than abundance data, were used because for plants the quantification of abundance is problematic and for animals the large dissimilarities in overall individual numbers among taxa (for example, bees versus large mammals) would lead to extremely biased patterns (biased towards the patterns of highly abundant taxa). In microorganisms, we used abundance data to reduce the effect of MOTUs that were found at very low abundances on study sites (possibly indicative of inactive MOTUs that were dispersed from other source habitats). Nevertheless, a dissimilarity matrix and corresponding non-metric multidimensional scaling ordination based on presence and absence data led to a highly similar results. We applied non-metric multidimensional scaling using the metaMDS function of the R-package vegan to ordinate dissimilarity matrices in a two-dimensional ordination space. For all three biodiversity datasets, stress levels were low, which justifies using this approach. Procrustes analysis as implemented in the vegan package was used to test for similarities in the configuration of ordinations calculated for the microorganism, plant and animal data. A permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) method (the function adonis in the vegan package) was used to test the effect of climate and land-use intensity on the dissimilarity in species communities. We again tested the support for the above-mentioned five hypotheses, here by using the explained variation as a measure of model support (as information-theoretic approaches and other comparable techniques have not—to the best of our knowledge—been developed). We started with the most-complex model (including both MAT and MAP and their interaction with LUI as predictor variables) and successively removed the least-significant predictor variables with P > 0.05 to obtain a final model for each of the five base hypotheses.

To derive the multivariate index of multifunctionality, we calculated a Euclidian dissimilarity matrix on the basis of the z-transformed 30 ecosystem functions by applying a pairwise deletion of missing observations. The latter was necessary because data were missing for some study sites for some ecosystem functions. Classical multidimensional scaling was used to visualize the dissimilarity matrix in ordination space. We analysed the effects of climate and land-use intensity on multidimensional scaling axis 1 and 2 scores using permutational MANOVA (using the function adonis of the R package vegan).

In addition, we calculated the dissimilarity in ecosystem functions of anthropogenic ecosystems to average conditions in natural ecosystems. The latter were calculated by modelling ecosystem functions in natural ecosystems in a generalized additive model using elevation as a predictor variable. We then modelled the average dissimilarity in ecosystem functions (the dissimilarity between observed values and those predicted by the data from natural habitats) as a function of land-use intensity (Fig. 3b), and compared differences in land-use intensity among elevational zones using simple ordinary linear models and AICC-based model inference. We excluded study sites above 3,060 m a.s.l. from these analyses (leaving n = 50 study sites), as anthropogenic habitats were restricted to lower elevations. This analysis was done for all 30 ecosystem functions and separately for soil-, plant- and animal-mediated ecosystem functions. All ecosystem function variables were z-transformed before analyses.

      1. Extended Data Fig. 1 Distribution and characteristics of study sites.

        a, b, Five replicate study sites were selected for each of the six major natural habitat types (circles) and the six major anthropogenic habitat types (square, diamonds and triangles) found on Mount Kilimanjaro. The five study sites of each habitat type were distributed in such a way as to achieve a fine-scale within-habitat elevational gradient. #Study sites, number of study sites per habitat type.

      2. Extended Data Fig. 2 Effects of land use on the composition of plant, animal and microorganism communities.

        a–c, The influence of land-use intensity on the overall change in species communities in anthropogenic ecosystems relative to predictions for species communities in natural ecosystems (linear model, for all taxa P < 0.01) is shown. n = 60 study sites for all analyses. d–f, In plants and animals, land-use intensity had stronger effects on the turnover rates in the arid lowlands (ANOVA on residuals of models shown in a–c, P = 0.052) and in higher elevations (ANOVA, P < 0.01), respectively. Box plots show the median (solid line), 25% and 75% quantiles (boxes); whiskers extend to the minimum and maximum within 1.5 times the interquartile range; more-extreme data values are drawn by individual circles. n = 60 study sites for all analyses. g, Calculation of response variables in a–f. Three animals communities that each consist of four species (shown in different colours), which partly overlap, are shown. An increase in MAP is associated with a 25% increase in the dissimilarity (d) of species communities i and ii in natural habitats (di–ii). Community iii is situated in anthropogenic habitats. As communities ii and iii live in the same climate zone, a model that is based only on climate variables would predict the same composition for each species community; however, community iii shows a dissimilarity of 0.5 to community ii (shown in red). In a–c, we analysed the degree to which LUI can explain the difference between these climate-based predictions and the observed composition of species communities (that is, dii–iii as realized in the non-metric multidimensional scaling ordination space).

      3. Extended Data Fig. 3 Effect of climate on ecosystem functions along the natural-habitat elevation gradient.

        For soil- and plant-mediated ecosystem functions, the absolute effect strength values are—on average—higher for MAP, whereas animal-mediated ecosystem functions are more strongly influenced by MAT (linear mixed effect model, interaction term (type of ecosystem function × type of climate variable), n = 30 study sites, P < 0.05). The height of the bar shows the mean. Error bars show the standard errors of absolute effect strength values for each type of ecosystem function and climate variable. The bar graphs have been calculated from the data shown in Extended Data Table 3. ESF, ecosystem function.

      4. Extended Data Fig. 4 Analyses of the support for climate and land-use models based on different land-use indices.

        For each response variable, 500 different land-use indices were calculated by the random weighting of the four components of the LUI (percentage biomass removal, agricultural inputs, modification of vegetation structure and percentage agriculture in the surrounding landscape) between 0 and 1. For each of the calculated land-use indices, we calculated the support (model weights) for the five major model types (null model, climate model, land-use model, additive climate + land-use model, interactive climate × land-use model), and determined the mean and 90% confidence intervals across the 500 runs. In the majority of runs with differently weighted land-use components, we found similarly high support for the five different model types (as with the original LUI). The climate × land-use interaction model was the single best-supported type of model across response variables and different land-use indicators.

      5. Extended Data Fig. 5 Effects of climate and land use on the multivariate index of multifunctionality.

        Dissimilarity in ecosystem multifunctionality across study sites (dots) in natural (red) and anthropogenic (orange) habitats. The position in ordination space illustrates the functional characteristics of sites in relationship to other sites; sites closer to one another have a more-similar ecosystem multifunctionality. Lines in the background show contour lines of elevation.

      6. Extended Data Fig. 6 Average change in ecosystem function with land-use intensity for soil-, plant- and animal-mediated ecosystem functions.

        a–c, Average change in ecosystem function (compared to predictions for natural habitats, log-transformed) increased linearly with land-use intensity (linear model, P < 0.01) for soil- (a), plant- (b) and animal-mediated (c) ecosystem functions. n = 50 study sites. d–f, The effect (strength) of land-use intensity on the mean change in ecosystem functioning (grey bars) was, on average, highest for plant-mediated ecosystem functions. The effects of land-use intensity significantly differed among elevation zones in plant- and animal-mediated ecosystem functions (linear model, Pinteraction < 0.05), but did not differ in soil-mediated ecosystem functions. n = 50 study sites.

    Abstract

    Aim

    Understanding the mechanisms controlling variation in species richness along environmental gradients is one of the most important objectives in ecology. Resource availability is often considered as the major driver of animal diversity. However, in ectotherms, temperature might play a predominant role as it modulates metabolic rates and the access of animals to resources. Here, we investigate the relative importance of resource availability and temperature in determining the diversity pattern of bees along a 3.6‐km elevational gradient.

    Location

    Mount Kilimanjaro, Tanzania.

    Methods

    We assessed bee species richness and abundance with pan traps and floral resources with transect records on 60 study sites which were equally distributed over six near‐natural and six disturbed habitat types along an elevational gradient from 870 to 4550 m a.s.l. We used path analysis to disentangle the effects of temperature, precipitation, floral resource abundance, bee abundance and land use on bee species richness. In addition, we monitored flower visitation rates during transect walks at different elevations to evaluate the temperature dependence of bee–flower interactions.

    Results

    Bee species richness continuously declined with elevation in natural and disturbed habitats. While the abundance of floral resources had a significant but only weak effect on species richness, the effect of temperature was strong. Temperature had a strong positive effect on species richness that was not mediated by bee abundance and an indirect effect via bee abundances. We observed higher levels of bee–flower interactions at higher temperatures, supporting the hypothesis that temperature limits diversity by constraining resource exploitation in ectotherms.

    Main conclusions

    Temperature and the availability of resources shape species richness patterns along environmental gradients. In ectothermic organisms like bees temperature seems to have the more important role, as it both limits the access to resources (abundance‐mediated effect) and accelerates other (abundance‐independent) ecological and evolutionary processes that drive the maintenance and origination of diversity.

    Introduction

    Elevational gradients in species richness belong to the best described patterns in ecology, but the causal mechanisms behind remain poorly understood (Rahbek, 1995; Lomolino et al., 2006; McCain & Grytnes, 2010). A variety of deterministic hypotheses have been put forward emphasizing the impact of history, climatic factors and energy availability on global and regional biodiversity patterns (Dobzhansky, 1950; Pianka, 1966; Srivastava & Lawton, 1998; McCain, 2007; Brown, 2014). However, the modes of operation of such environmental factors, influencing species richness patterns either directly or indirectly, are often unclear, hampering predictions about the impact of environmental changes on biodiversity.

    One of the most frequently discussed predictors for species richness is resource availability (Brown & Maurer, 1989; Gillman & Wright, 2006; Ferger et al., 2014; Hurlbert & Stegen, 2014). In highly productive ecosystems, resources are expected to be available in amounts that allow, on average, more and larger populations to persist. As the probability of demographic extinction of an individual population is a negative decelerating function of its abundance (Lande, 1993; Srivastava & Lawton, 1998), more and larger populations may persist under high than under low levels of resource availability. Primary productivity has often been used as a proxy for energy or resources when explaining latitudinal and elevational patterns of animal diversity (Hawkins et al., 2003). Rarely, species richness patterns have been directly related to the resources that are effectively used by the investigated organisms. Studies relating the species richness of endothermic birds to the availability of resources found strong positive relationships (Ferger et al., 2014; Price et al., 2014).

    However, in ectothermic organisms the amount of potentially available resources in the environment does not necessarily correspond to the amount of energy accessible to organisms. Instead, ambient temperature may determine how much of the principally available resources can effectively be used (Kaspari et al., 2000; Kaspari, 2005): energy consumption of foraging honeybees has been shown to decrease by more than 80% over a temperature gradient of 25 °C (Stabentheiner et al., 2003). Consequently, the net profit of foraging animals declines with decreasing temperatures, assuming the same resource density levels. Ectothermic organisms respond to this by restricting foraging to temperatures above a certain threshold (Willmer, 1983), so that cumulative foraging times and the net energetic intake may be higher in warm than in cold environments. Such temperature‐mediated limitations on resource exploitation should result in shrinking population densities and increasing probabilities of species extinction in cooler climates. Thus, both the resource availability and the resource exploitation hypotheses are based on the assumption that total abundances and species richness are positively linked (Evans et al., 2005) – a relation, that has rarely been tested in tropical insects (but see Beck et al., 2011).

    Temperature is also known to have effects on species richness, which are not mediated via an influence on the abundances of organisms (e.g. Brown, 2014). First, only a few species are expected to physiologically tolerate the harsh and cold climates of high‐elevation or high‐latitude habitats (Currie, 1991). Second, temperature may also act directly on speciation rates by, for example, curtailing generation times or increasing metabolic rates and related nucleotide substitutions (Laird et al., 1969; Gillooly et al., 2005; Gillman et al., 2012). Third, temperature might accelerate negative density‐dependent mortality (e.g. via predation or parasitism) thereby maintaining species diversity (Johnson et al., 2012; Bagchi et al., 2014).

    Wild bees (Hymenoptera: Apoidea, Apiformes) with more than 20 000 taxonomically described species (Michener, 2007) play a key role in natural and agricultural ecosystems as they maintain the reproduction, gene flow and diversity of wild plants, boost crop production and stabilize food security (Garibaldi et al., 2011; Albrecht et al., 2012; Brittain et al., 2013). Understanding the underlying mechanisms that shape bee richness is thus of high ecological and economic importance. Additionally, the discrete, limited and easily quantified nature of floral food resources makes bees a good system for testing resource and temperature hypotheses. We analysed species richness of wild bees and the potential drivers of species richness in natural and disturbed habitats along climatic gradients on Mount Kilimanjaro (Tanzania, East Africa). Using path analysis we separated the effects of temperature, floral resources and bee abundance on bee species richness. As precipitation, primary productivity and land use may alter the amount of floral resources, bee abundances and/or bee richness we included these variables in our path model and tested the following hypotheses (Fig. 1):

    1. The resource availability hypothesis.Resource availability directly limits bee species richness as larger populations with a lower extinction risk can be sustained in habitats with high amounts of resources. We therefore expect to find a positive effect of flower abundance on bee richness via a positive effect on bee abundance.
    2. The temperature‐mediated resource exploitation hypothesis.In ectotherms, temperature limits the amount of resources which can effectively be exploited. Under warm temperatures bee foraging activity and the net energy gain is higher than under cold temperatures, resulting in higher bee abundances. We therefore expect to find a positive effect of temperature on bee richness via a positive effect on bee abundance.
    3. The temperature–speciation hypothesis.Temperature limits the occurrence of species due to physiological constraints and/or by accelerating ecological and evolutionary rates. We therefore expect to find a ‘direct’ effect of temperature on bee richness, based on effects of temperature that are not mediated by abundances and resources.

    Methods

    Study region and study design

    The study was conducted at Mount Kilimanjaro (2°45′–3°25′ S, 37°0′–37°43′ E) (Tanzania), the highest free‐standing mountain in the world (700–5895 m a.s.l.). Temperature drops linearly at around 6.1 °C for every 1 km difference in elevation, while mean annual precipitation peaks in the forest belt at around 2200 m a.s.l. (Hemp, 2006a). We selected 60 study sites of 0.25 hectare in 12 major ecosystem types on the southern slopes of Mount Kilimanjaro, covering an elevational gradient from 870 up to 4550 m a.s.l. Investigated ecosystem types consisted of six natural habitats with no or only low human impact (savanna, lower montane forest, Ocotea (camphor) forest, Podocarpus forest, Erica forest, Helichrysum scrub vegetation) and six disturbed habitat types with strong to moderate human impact (maize fields, homegarden, commercial coffee plantations, grasslands, disturbed (by selective logging) Ocotea forests and disturbed (by fire) Podocarpus forests.

    Bee diversity assessment

    Species richness and the abundance of bees were assessed by installing eight pan trap clusters, each consisting of one UV‐bright blue, one yellow and one white pan (Campbell & Hanula, 2007; Westphal et al., 2008) (Fig. S1 in Appendix S1 in Supporting Information). In forest sites we installed three additional trap clusters in the canopy (up to a mid‐canopy layer of 25 m). However, for all sites we only used data from eight randomly chosen trap clusters per sampling round to guarantee equal sampling intensity on all study sites. Pan traps were filled with water and a drop of liquid soap to break the surface tension of the water, and were emptied after 48 h. We sampled bees on each study site a total of three times summing up to a total of 144 pan trap days per study site (three sampling rounds × eight clusters × three pan traps per cluster × two sampling days). We visited study sites outside the Kilimanjaro National Park (KNP) twice in 2011 and once in 2012, while study sites inside the KNP were visited three times in 2012. We carefully ensured that sampling rounds on each study site covered multiple seasons (i.e. warm dry season, cold dry season, small rainy season) so that the potential effect of seasonal changes in species communities on diversity estimates can be assumed to be similar. Traps that were destroyed (this occurred only outside the KNP) were replaced by pan trap samples from a fourth sampling round that was conducted in 2011. Species were sorted to morphospecies level and, wherever possible, identified to species level. We pooled species and abundance data from sampling rounds per study site for all analyses.

    Floral resources assessment

    During each sampling round floral resources were estimated on ten 4 m × 5 m subplots (Fig. S1 in Appendix S1). In each subplot we counted the number of flower units, including flowers of the lower canopy. We defined a flower unit as a patch of flowers that a small pollinator about 1 cm long would exploit by walking instead of flying (e.g. the inflorescences of Asteraceae were counted as distinct flower units) (Carvalheiro et al., 2008). Flower resources were then extrapolated for the study site and averaged for the different surveys per study site. In addition to flower numbers, the amount of nectar and/or pollen per flower unit may also vary with elevation (Stiles & Freeman, 1993; Scobell & Scott, 2002). However, we assume that differences between flower numbers were strong enough to outweigh possible variation in nectar and/or pollen production per flower unit along the gradient, which makes flower abundance a good proxy for resource abundance.

    Study site variables

    Temperature was measured on 53 study sites with temperature sensors that were installed approximately 2 m above the ground. For approximately 2 years temperature was recorded at 5‐min intervals. Mean annual temperature was calculated by averaging all individual measurements per study site. For the seven study sites without temperature sensors, temperature was modelled from the data. Precipitation data were interpolated for each study site, using about 70 rain gauges spread at different elevations on Mount Kilimanjaro (Hemp, 2006a,b). The normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI), with a horizontal resolution of 250 m × 250 m, was used as a proxy for primary productivity (Kerr & Ostrovsky, 2003). Land‐use intensity was described by an index, which considered the annual removal of plant biomass, chemical inputs to the ecosystem, vegetation structure and the proportion of managed habitats in the surrounding landscape.

    Methodological details about all single study site variables are given in Appendix S4.

    Bee–flower interactions

    Flower visitors were recorded during 58 variable transect walks that were conducted between 2011 and 2012 on a subset of 14 study sites from 984 to 3900 m a.s.l. To minimize trampling effects on the vegetation, study sites were extended from 0.25 to 1 ha. For 4 h we slowly moved along variable transect corridors and recorded each pollinator that touched the reproductive parts of a flower. Transect walks were conducted between 8 a.m. and 5 p.m. We did not conduct transect walks during rain or heavy wind. Transect walks were repeated up to seven times per study sites to account for seasonal differences in bee–flower interactions. We counted the number of flower visits conducted by bees within the 4 h and calculated the mean number of bee–flower interactions per hour. We additionally estimated the number of flowers on the study sites as described above (one estimation per transect walk) and calculated the mean temperature that was measured during the transect walk.

    Statistical analyses

    Elevational species richness and bee abundance patterns were analysed with generalized additive models (GAMs), in which the relationship between response and predictor is not specified by some explicit functional formula. Instead, nonparametric smoothers are used to identify and describe linear or nonlinear relationships (Crawley, 2007). We calculated GAMs using the ‘gam’ function in the R package mgcv, setting the data family to Gaussian type and the basis dimension of the smoothing function (k) to four to avoid over‐parameterization. We analysed the patterns for the complete data set and for natural and disturbed habitats separately. To test whether the patterns of disturbed and natural habitats deviate from each other, we included the interaction elevation × land use type in our models. We restricted the comparison to that part of the elevational gradient where both habitat types occur (860–3060 m a.s.l.). To simplify the interpretation of the interaction term we ln‐transformed elevation prior to analysis, which linearizes the relationship between species richness and elevation, and used linear models for the comparison (Appendix S2).

    To check whether our results were biased due to incomplete sampling in parts of the elevational gradient, we estimated the asymptotic species richness with the abundance‐based estimator Chao 1, using the ‘estimateR’ function in the R‐package vegan. The distribution of estimated bee species richness along elevation was analysed with a GAM (Gaussian family, k  = 4). We tested, whether the observed species richness pattern along the elevational gradient deviates from the estimated species richness pattern along elevation by correlating the predicted values of both models. For this correlation we eliminated study sites from the dataset where no bee was detected and, consequently, species richness could not be estimated (seven study sites) (Appendix S3).

    Path analysis was used to disentangle the direct and indirect effect of climate (temperature and precipitation), land use, primary productivity, flower abundance and bee abundances on observed and estimated bee species richness on Mount Kilimanjaro (Mitchell, 2001). Prior to analyses, we ln‐transformed bee species richness, bee abundance and flower abundance (+1) to improve the normality of residuals. All explaining variables were standardized by z‐transformation using the ‘scale’ function in R. Based on our ecological understanding of bees, we hypothesized that temperature, precipitation and land use predict species richness and abundance directly and indirectly via their combined effect on primary production and the amount of floral resources. We also assumed that primary productivity would be a predictor of flower abundance, and both would affect bee species richness and abundance. Therefore, we pre‐selected possible path combinations, by analysing the four response variables of our path model (bee species richness, bee abundance, flower abundance, primary productivity) with all explaining variables, i.e. temperature, precipitation, land use (plus flower abundance and primary productivity in the case of species richness and abundance; plus primary productivity in the case of flower abundance) with linear models. For each model we used the ‘dredge’ function of the R package ‘MuMIn’ to evaluate models ‘defined by all possible variable combinations and ranked them by their Akaike information criterion (AIC)‐based model weight. As our sample size was relatively low compared with the number of estimated parameters, we used the AIC with a second‐order bias correction (AICc) for inferring the support of individual models. For the best supported models (ΔAICc < 2) we calculated the relative variable importance (RVI), i.e. the sum of AICc weights across all models including the explanatory variable. The relative variable importance thus mirrors the statistical certainty with which a variable is included in competitive models (Conroy et al., 1995; Wintle et al., 2003). We then set up all remaining path model combinations and ranked them based on their AICc, using the R package ‘lavaan’. For all competitive path models (ΔAICc < 2) we calculated path coefficients, their statistical significance and multiple coefficients of determination (R2) of explanatory variables. The total effect of primary productivity on bee richness was calculated by adding the direct path coefficient to the product of the indirect path coefficients of that link (Mitchell, 2001). As our hypotheses were predominantly based on natural elevational patterns, we repeated path analyses for a data set that included natural habitats only (Appendix S5c).

    To analyse the effect of temperature on resource use by bees we related the mean number of (ln‐transformed) bee–flower interactions per hour to the mean temperatures measured on study sites during each 4‐h transect walk. We added flower and bee abundance as covariables in the model to control for an expected positive influence of both variables on the number of bee–flower interactions. Bee abundance data were derived from pan trap catches (total abundance across three sampling rounds). All explaining variables were z‐transformed for comparability. We fitted linear mixed effects models with temperature, (ln‐transformed) flower abundance, (ln‐transformed) bee abundance and the temperature × ln(flower abundance) interaction as fixed factors and included study site as a random factor to avoid pseudoreplication. Using the ‘dredge’ function we ranked all models by their AICc values and we present the summary output of all models with ΔAICc < 2 (see table S6a in Appendix S6). The model with the lowest AICc was analysed with a linear mixed effects model in which study site was included as a random term (Fig. 4 and table S6b in Appendix S6). The distribution of (ln‐transformed) flower abundances along the elevational gradient was analysed with a GAM (figure in Appendix S7), setting the data family to Gaussian type and k to five.

    Results

    In total we caught 4876 bees and sorted them into 127 morphospecies (32 genera). Species richness continuously declined with elevation [all habitats: explained deviance = 80.5%, estimated degrees of freedom (e.d.f.) = 2.73, F = 76.93, P < 0.001] (Fig. 2). The decline in species richness in natural habitats did not significantly differ from the decline in species richness in disturbed habitats (Fig. 2, table in Appendix S2). The predictions for observed and estimated species richness were strongly correlated (Pearson correlation coefficient r = 0.99, P < 0.001), suggesting that sampling intensity was sufficient to fit good models (figure in Appendix S3). Bee abundance showed a U‐shaped distribution along the elevation gradient, with maximal bee abundance in Erica forests and Helichrysum shrubs, (3500 and 4550 m a.s.l.) (Fig. 2) (all habitats: explained deviance = 45.6%, e.d.f. = 2.59, F = 16.49, P < 0.001). Interestingly, one morphospecies of the widespread genus Lasioglossum dominated the higher‐elevation habitats and accounted for 59% of all bee individuals sampled on Mount Kilimanjaro. Again, the abundance patterns of natural and disturbed habitats did not significantly deviate from each other up to the elevation where both habitat types occur (Fig. 2, table in Appendix S2).

    Path analyses indicated that all three of our hypothesized mechanisms (Fig. 1) simultaneously explain bee species richness on Mount Kilimanjaro, whereby the effect of temperature outweighed the effect of resources. As predicted by the resource availability hypothesis, the amount of floral resources had a weak but significant effect on bee abundance in all competitive models (standardized path coefficient = 0.23, P = 0.011), while bee abundance was a strong predictor of bee richness (standardized path coefficient = 0.54, P < 0.001) (Fig. 3 Fig. S5a in Appendix S5). In accordance with the temperature‐mediated resource exploitation hypothesis, temperature had a moderately strong direct effect on bee abundances (standardized path coefficient = 0.40, P < 0.001) (Fig. 3). Importantly, and in accordance with the temperature–speciation‐hypothesis, temperature also had a strong effect on species richness that was not mediated by bee abundance (standardized path coefficient = 0.68, P < 0.001) (Fig. 3). The best‐supported path model explained 88% of the total variance in species richness and 54% of the total variance in bee abundance (Fig. 3). RVI was maximal (i.e. 1) between all links in this model, except for one link (flower abundance → bee abundance; RVI 0.3).

    Precipitation and temperature had a strong positive effect on primary productivity. However, primary productivity was not positively related to flower abundance as expected. Instead, primary productivity had either no effect, or even a negative effect, on flower abundance (Figs 3 Fig. S5a in Appendix S5). The (ln‐transformed) mean flower abundance was lowest in the higher forest belt (2300–3200 m a.s.l.) (GAM: explained deviance = 29.6%, e.d.f. 4.7, F = 3.43, P = 0.007) (figure in Appendix S7). Also, primary productivity had a strong negative effect on bee abundance and thus a negative total effect on bee richness [i.e. a direct (positive) effect of primary productivity on bee richness (0.15) + an indirect (negative) effect, via abundance (−0.65 × 0.54) = −0.20]. Land use, in contrast, had a positive effect on flower abundance and did not negatively affect bee abundance or richness.

    A strong effect of temperature on bee richness and abundance and a weak effect of flower resources on bee abundance were also detected when analysing estimated species richness (Chao1) instead of observed species richness in path models (Fig. S5b in Appendix S5). When analysing exclusively the data for natural habitats, the link between floral resources and bee abundance, which was already weak in the path model including all data, was non‐significant and excluded from the best path model. Importantly, the temperature effects on bee richness and bee abundance remained highly significant (Fig. S5c in Appendix S5).

    In support of the temperature‐mediated resource exploitation hypothesis, the number of bee–flower interactions increased with increasing temperature during observations (Fig. 4). The slope of this relationship was dependent on flower abundances: the number of bee–flower interactions was generally lower at study sites with small numbers of flowers than at study sites with high amounts of floral resources. However, bee–flower interaction rates increased more strongly with temperature when flower abundances were low. The presented model had the highest model weight but was statistically indistinguishable from the model including bee abundance (ΔAICc < 2). However, the effect of bee abundance on the number of interactions was negative, i.e. the number of interactions did not increase with higher bee abundances as expected (Table S6a in Appendix S6).

    Discussion

    Predictors of bee species richness on Mount Kilimanjaro

    On Mount Kilimanjaro, bee species richness declined with elevation in both natural and disturbed habitats, a pattern which has already been detected along elevational gradients in bee communities of both temperate and tropical ecosystems (Arroyo et al., 1982; Hoiss et al., 2012). Our study suggests that temperature rather than resource abundance determined species richness, as temperature governs the access of pollinators to food resources and may accelerate ecological and evolutionary processes leading to the origination and maintenance of diversity.

    The idea that resources or ultimately the total productivity of ecosystems limits the number of species that coexist in ecosystems goes back to the roots of quantitative ecology. It is here further strengthened by empirical evidence from the field. In accordance with the resource availability hypothesis we found that the availability of floral resources had a positive effect on bee abundance, a result also found by Hoiss et al. (2012). However, the effect of flower availability on bee abundance was weak compared with the effects of temperature on bee abundance. Our data suggest that the temperature‐mediated resource exploitation hypothesis mainly explains the strong temperature effect on bee abundances: as energy consumption of active bees drastically declines with increasing temperature (Stabentheiner et al., 2003), bees might restrict foraging activity to temperatures where the energy gain through collected nectar or pollen exceeds the energy costs of the foraging flight. Accordingly, we found that the number of bee–flower interactions increased with increasing temperatures (also observed by Arroyo et al., 1985). Interestingly, the temperature dependence of bee–flower interaction rates was reduced when flower abundances were high, indicating that high resource availability can make foraging worthwhile despite low temperatures. The positive relation between temperature and bee–flower interactions, however, stresses that the maintenance of more and larger populations and higher species richness in ectotherms is especially facilitated in warmer habitats, independent of resource availability. That temperature indirectly affects species diversity by mediating access to resources was also suggested for ectothermic ants (Kaspari et al., 2000; Sanders et al., 2007). In contrast, resource exploitation of endotherms is expected to be much less affected by temperature. Indeed, it was found that the species richness of (endothermic) frugivore and insectivore birds on the same study sites on Mount Kilimanjaro was better explained by the availability of food resources (fruits and insects, respectively) and vegetation structure, than by temperature (Ferger et al., 2014). In endotherms, the availability and constant usability of food resources are likely to outweigh the physiological costs of keeping body temperature stable in cooler climates.

    In accordance with the temperature–speciation hypothesis we also detected an effect of temperature on species richness, which was not mediated by the temperature–bee abundance relationships. Because temperature controls the metabolic rate (Brown et al., 2004; but see also Hawkins et al., 2007, for weak independent empirical support), all kinds of biological processes that shape species richness, such as species interactions, mutations, adaptive evolution or speciation, depend on temperature (Brown et al., 2004). Nevertheless, we currently lack a detailed mechanistic understanding of how temperature affects species richness. We assume that evolutionary processes such as temperature‐mediated speciation rates (Allen et al., 2006) are complemented by ecological mechanisms, for example enhanced negative density‐dependent mortality rates at higher temperatures (de Sassi et al., 2012; Johnson et al., 2012; Bagchi et al., 2014; Brown, 2014). For instance, abundant bee and wasp species have been shown to be preferentially attacked by parasitoids and predators (Steffan‐Dewenter, 2003), allowing more species to coexist (Paine, 1974). It seems likely that due to increased species interaction rates these effects are more pronounced at warm temperatures.

    A potential caveat of our study is that the variables used in path models have a different measurement quality. While the error of measuring temperature and species richness can be assumed to be comparatively low, measurement of variables like floral resources and bee abundances are more error‐prone: estimates of flower abundances might, for example, not fully reflect the amount of available resources on a study site, as pollen and nectar production may fluctuate between communities, plant species and even within species along elevational gradients (Stiles & Freeman, 1993; Scobell & Scott, 2002; Feldman & McGill, 2014). Bee abundance data might scatter, because temperature differences during sampling could have affected bee abundances by restricting the flight activity of bees (Beck et al., 2011). This might have diminished the statistical power of the link between flower and bee abundances and should be considered when comparing the importance of the different pathways for explaining differences in bee species richness. Nevertheless, we expect measurement errors to be randomly distributed so that differences in data quality are more likely to influence the statistical significance rather than the effect strength of variables. Therefore, we are confident that the support for the resource availability hypothesis in relation to the temperature–speciation hypothesis and the temperature‐mediated resource exploitation‐hypothesis would remain low even if the precision of measurements increases. Furthermore, as in all elevational gradient studies, temperature is correlated with a broad variety of other biotic and abiotic factors changing with elevation (e.g. area, air pressure, radiation, competition). Although AICc values indicate that the presented hypotheses were more likely than other potential hypotheses to explain species richness (e.g. the species–area hypothesis or species–regional productivity relationships) (ΔAICc ≥ 8.0; data not presented), experiments are required that can more clearly differentiate the role of temperature and of variables correlated to temperature in determining species richness patterns.

    Primary productivity has often been used to explain biodiversity patterns of narrowly defined animal taxa. This assumes that the fraction of total primary production secured by these taxa varies little with changes in primary production along elevation or latitude. However, this assumption is unlikely to hold as narrowly defined taxa are often only capable of exploiting resources over a restricted part of a full environmental gradient and high‐level taxonomic turnover within functional guilds along environmental gradients is a well‐documented pattern (e.g. decomposers, termites–fungi; pollinators, bees–Diptera). In this respect, we showed that floral resources influenced bee richness, via abundance, more strongly than primary productivity. High levels of primary productivity had a negative impact on bee abundance, which overweighed the slight positive impact on bee richness. On Mount Kilimanjaro, primary productivity peaks in the lower forest belt around 2500 m a.s.l. Dense canopy covers restrict the amount of flowering plants in the herbal and shrub layer, up to the lower canopy cover. We are aware of the fact that high levels of primary productivity, which are linked to dense vegetation covers, could either reduce the effective survey area (Hurlbert, 2004), reduce the sample efficiency of pan traps, or shift pollinator activity to the highest canopy cover. However, as transect walks and canopy observations in the forest revealed similarly low bee species richness and abundance in forests (in the forests 96% of all bees observed during flower interactions belonged to the same species, Apis mellifera!) we assume that such sampling artefacts are rather negligible. Instead, we suggest that the clumped distribution of floral resources in tropical forests, with few mass‐flowering trees per km2 support few social and long‐distance foraging species, but to a much lesser degree small solitary bees, which often have foraging ranges of less than 600 m (Greenleaf et al., 2007) and make up the largest part of the species in bee communities on Mount Kilimanjaro. A similar negative correlation between primary productivity and species abundance and richness was found for birds within forests (but not grasslands) (Hurlbert, 2004) and for ants (Sanders et al., 2007). The authors suggested that forest communities respond differently to primary productivity than communities from more open habitats, or that factors other than productivity might limit species in habitats where primary productivity is very high, which is closely related to our suggestions. Further experiments that consider other parameters like vegetation structure and trophic interactions are required to fully understand these relationships.

    Land use and habitat disturbance did not have an impact on the bee species richness patterns on Mount Kilimanjaro nor on the basic mechanisms that explain these patterns. Increased flower abundance in more open (disturbed) habitats supported high bee diversity (Gikungu et al., 2011). However, land use and the human impact on ecosystems are currently only moderate on Mount Kilimanjaro. Subsistence farming is the dominant agricultural system which is of small scale (field sizes are mostly considerably less than 1 ha) and sustains rather high levels of semi‐natural habitats on or around crop fields. The small size of fields and the diversity of different cropping systems shape a mosaic‐like heterogeneous landscape. Agricultural lands are mainly weeded manually, which restricts the impact of soil compaction on soil‐nesting species. Stem‐nesting bees have been observed to profit from maize stems that partly remained on the fields a long time after the harvest. In general, small‐scale farming in its current form still supports a high diversity of bees on Mount Kilimanjaro, which is rewarded by stable pollination services across agricultural landscapes (Classen et al., 2014). Accelerating land‐use intensification and the increased use of pesticides and machines, however, might have a strong impact on pollinator diversity and pollination services, as reported from the Western world where declines in pollinator communities due to land‐use intensification are alarming (e.g. Biesmeijer, 2006; Potts et al., 2010). Furthermore, species richness patterns alone do not allow us to draw conclusions on possible impacts of land‐use change on species identity and community composition, which require further examination.

    Conclusions

    On Mount Kilimanjaro, bee species richness was explained by both temperature and food resources; temperature, controlling both the usability of resources and evolutionary and ecological rates, played a predominant role. The optimal foraging temperature range of ectotherms is narrower than their thermal range, which has often been ignored when disentangling the effects of resources and temperature on species richness. Although path analyses are correlation analyses, and additional experimental approaches are required to verify the role of temperature, with the data available so far we conclude that a consideration of temperature‐mediated resource use improves our understanding of how animals, and in particular ectotherms, respond to environmental changes on local and global scales.

    Summary

    1. Seed dispersal by frugivorous animals forms the basis for regeneration of numerous plant species. Habitat fragmentation has been found to be one major factor perturbing frugivore communities and dependent plant species. Yet, community‐wide consequences of fragmentation for both frugivore and plant communities are still hardly understood.
    2. Here, we studied the effects of habitat fragmentation on the seed removal by frugivorous birds and mammals from nine fleshy‐fruited plant species in Białowieża Forest (Eastern Poland). This last relict of old‐growth lowland forest in Europe poses an exceptional reference site for studying the impact of habitat fragmentation on seed dispersal processes in temperate forest ecosystems.
    3. In particular, (i) we tested for associations between forest fragmentation and response traits of frugivores, that is forest specialization and body mass; (ii) we studied the relationship between frugivore response and effect traits, that is centrality (number of consumed plant species) and interaction type (mutualistic vs. antagonistic); and (iii) we assessed the feedback of fragmentation‐induced changes on plant–frugivore interactions and seed removal rates.
    4. We found that fragmentation led to shifts in the frugivore community, associated with the response traits forest specialization and body mass, with fewer forest specialists and large‐bodied frugivores in fragmented than in continuous forests. However, forest generalists and small‐bodied frugivores were more central in the plant–frugivore associations than forest specialists and large‐bodied frugivores. Therefore, the loss of vulnerable species did not result in reduced seed removal rates in fragmented compared with continuous forest.
    5. Synthesis. These results indicate that seed removal may be relatively robust in spite of shifts in the frugivore community in forest fragments. The correlation between response and effect traits of frugivores highlights the importance of forest generalists and small‐bodied frugivores for maintaining dispersal processes in fragmented forests in temperate regions. Yet, future studies should aim at quantifying the consequences of seed disperser loss on other aspects of dispersal, such as long‐distance dispersal, spatial patterns of seed deposition, seed germination and plant regeneration.

    Introduction

    Seed dispersal by frugivorous animals forms the basis for regeneration of fleshy‐fruited plants (Herrera 2003) and is thus one of the key factors influencing the persistence of these plant communities (Janzen 1970; Howe & Smallwood 1982; Nason & Hamrick 1997). On a local scale, fleshy‐fruited plants depend on dispersal by frugivores to escape the high mortality near mother plants and to colonize habitats suitable for regeneration (Janzen 1970; Connell 1971; Howe & Smallwood 1982; Schupp & Jordano 2011). At larger scales, frugivores act as ‘mobile links’ between plant populations and even facilitate range shifts allowing plants to follow climatic changes (Nathan & Muller‐Landau 2000; Lundberg & Moberg 2003; Kremen et al. 2007; Schupp, Jordano & Gómez 2010).

    Habitat fragmentation has been found to be one major factor perturbing frugivore communities and dependent plant communities (McConkey et al. 2012; Newbold et al. 2013; Bello et al. 2015). The effect of fragmentation on species is not random, but mediated by ‘response traits’ that determine the susceptibility of the species towards alterations in, for example, habitat quality, habitat area or resource availability (e.g. Hooper et al. 2005; Larsen, Williams & Kremen 2005; Suding et al. 2008; Hagen et al. 2012; Luck et al. 2012; Díaz et al. 2013). Several studies could, for example, show that particularly habitat specialists, such as species specialized on large intact natural forests, are more susceptible to fragmentation than generalists (Kirika, Farwig & Böhning‐Gaese 2008; Melo et al. 2010; Albrecht, Neuschulz & Farwig 2012). Moreover, large‐bodied frugivore species disappear in fragmented landscapes around the world, as they require large tracts of forest and are often additionally threatened by hunting (e.g. Galetti et al. 2006; Wotton & Kelly 2011; Markl et al. 2012).

    The quantity and quality of seed dispersal services provided by different frugivore species directly reflect their ‘effect traits’ (Larsen, Williams & Kremen 2005; Suding et al. 2008; Schupp, Jordano & Gómez 2010; Díaz et al. 2013). The quantitative contribution of each frugivore to seed dispersal is mainly determined by the rate of fruit removal from plants (Vázquez, Morris & Jordano 2005). The qualitative contribution is, among others, determined by the fruit‐handling behaviour: frugivores that swallow entire fruits typically act as legitimate seed dispersers, whereas frugivores that peck on fruit pulp or crush seeds act as pulp and seed predators (Jordano 1992; Jordano & Schupp 2000; González‐Varo 2010).

    We can expect that the disruption of seed dispersal processes in fragmented landscapes is determined by the relationship between the vulnerability of frugivore species to environmental change and their functional roles in plant–frugivore associations, that is by the correlation between response and effect traits (Larsen, Williams & Kremen 2005; Díaz et al. 2013; Schleuning, Fründ & Garcia 2015). A strong positive correlation between response and effect traits, which is the case when vulnerable species are functionally important, should entail a severe disruption of seed dispersal in fragmented landscapes. On the contrary, a negative relationship would imply that the loss of vulnerable species has no effect on seed dispersal processes (Schleuning, Fründ & Garcia 2015) or, at least, on some of its components. As an example, fruit‐handling behaviour is strongly determined by the gape width of frugivores (Levey 1987), which is itself correlated with body size. Therefore, a loss of large‐bodied frugivores can result in a loss of seed dispersal services, because response and effect traits are correlated (Wotton & Kelly 2011; Galetti et al. 2013). In contrast, consequences of a loss of forest specialists for seed dispersal processes may be less obvious, as a rather weak correlation between response and effect traits is conceivable.

    Trait‐mediated cascading effects of fragmentation on frugivores and plant–frugivore interactions are still hardly understood, especially in temperate ecosystems. Here, we use an extensive data set of plant–frugivore interactions from the last lowland old‐growth forest in Europe, Białowieża Forest in Eastern Poland, to assess the consequences of a trait‐associated loss of frugivores for plant–frugivore interactions and seed removal rates. First, we determine whether fragmentation‐induced changes in the frugivore community are associated with the response traits of frugivores, that is forest specialization and body mass. Secondly, we study the relationship between frugivore response traits and effect traits that are associated with the roles of frugivores in plant–frugivore networks, that is centrality and interaction type (mutualistic vs. antagonistic). Thirdly, we assess the feedback of fragmentation‐induced changes on plant–frugivore interactions and seed removal rates.

    Materials and methods

    Study Area

    We conducted this study in the Białowieża Forest, the last old‐growth forest of the European lowland, extending across the border between Poland and Belarus (Fig. 1). On Polish territory, the forest covers an area of about 625 km². Within the Białowieża National Park (c. 100 km²), an area of about 60 km² is strictly protected and an area of about 45 km² has potentially never been commercially logged (Sokolowski 2004). Over 80% of the remaining forest has been shaped by commercial logging since the First World War (Bobiec et al. 2000; Bobiec 2002, 2012). Moreover, most riverine areas of the forest had been cleared during the 16th and 17th centuries for the purpose of hay production (Sokolowski 2004).

    Study Species and Design

    The majority of fleshy‐fruited plants in the study region are primarily associated with ash–alder floodplain forests (Fraxino‐Alnetum community; Matuszkiewicz 2001). Thus, we established our study sites within these floodplain forests. We used maps on the distribution of ash–alder forests for the selection of study sites (Falinski 1994) and verified the suitability in the field. In 2011, we established 10 study sites in the forest interior (n = 5) and at forest edges (n = 5). In 2012, we established additional study sites at forest edges (n = 3) and in forest fragments (n = 4; size c. 1 ha; mean distance to main forest: 0.66 ± 0.16 km, mean ± SD; range: 0.48–0.85 km) to extend the fragmentation gradient (Fig. 1; Table S1 in Supporting Information). Habitat fragmentation is usually defined as a landscape‐scale process involving both habitat loss and the breaking apart of habitat (Fahrig 2003). The most appropriate way to measure fragmentation is therefore a measure that adequately describes the above‐mentioned landscape‐scale processes. To do so, we selected a radius of 500 m around the study sites to quantify the landscape context in which the sites are situated, because the effects of habitat fragmentation on species richness of avian seed dispersers have been shown to be strongest at a scale of up to 500 m (see, e.g., Garcia & Chacoff 2007; Breitbach et al. 2010). Study sites in the forest interior were located at least 500 m from forest edges, and forest edge sites were located at transitional zones between closed forest and grassland or riverine meadows. Although the grasslands in the study area have been created through clearing of forest during the 16th and 17th centuries, today forest edges often form soft transitions between forest and grassland. Forest fragment sites were located in small ash–alder forest remnants entirely surrounded by meadows (for details on characteristics of the study sites, see Table S1; Albrecht et al. 2013, 2015). Thus, our study was conducted on a total of 17 sites scattered over c. 400 km2 covering two‐thirds of the Polish part of the Białowieża Forest. The pairwise distance between study sites ranged from 1.4 to 23 km (11 ± 5.8 km, mean ± SD).

    We searched for fleshy‐fruited plant species in a radius of 500 m around the centre of each study site. As the fragments were smaller than the 500 m radius, we searched the entire fragment area. We identified 15 woody, fleshy‐fruited plant species from 12 genera and five families (six tree and nine shrub species; Table S2). In ash–alder floodplain forests, these species occur in high densities and often form the dominant aspect of the mid‐ and understorey (Albrecht et al. 2013, 2014, 2015). Across study sites, the number of species varied between 7 and 14 (10 ± 1.7 species per site, mean ± SD). Plant densities varied between 10 and 103 individuals per 100 m2 across study sites (36 ± 27 individuals per 100 m2, mean ± SD). For the analyses, we selected the subset of plant species that occurred in both continuous (interior) and fragmented (edge and fragment) forest habitats. Thus, the data set included nine woody plant species that are common throughout Europe (Ellenberg 2011). The selected species included three tree and six shrub species. Among the tree species, Frangula alnus was the most common (5.0 ± 8.3 individuals per 100 m2, mean ± SD), followed by Prunus padus (4.2 ± 3.5) and Sorbus aucuparia (2.2 ± 3.4). Among the shrub species, Rubus idaeus was the most common (10 ± 11), followed by Ribes spicatum (5.9 ± 5.6), Ribes nigrum (4.3 ± 7.4), Viburnum opulus (1.6 ± 2.0), Euonymus europaeus (1.3 ± 0.97) and Lonicera xylosteum (0.011 ± 0.031). In the study region, the fruiting period of the focal plant species ranges from the end of June until mid‐October (Albrecht et al. 2015). During this period, the focal plant species represent critical resources for a diverse array of frugivorous birds and mammals (Table S3).

    Frugivore Observations

    From July to October in 2011 and 2012, we weekly monitored fruit ripening of the plant species on the study sites (for details on fruit monitoring, see Table S4; Albrecht et al. 2013, 2015). We observed each plant species on each study site and year three times for 6 h starting at sunrise (18 h per plant species per study site per year). Observation sessions were spread over the entire phenological periods of the plant species. We observed plant–frugivore interactions with binoculars from camouflaged tents simultaneously on different study sites (seven observers in 2011 and 14 observers in 2012). We recorded all frugivore species visiting the individual plants, as well as the number of frugivore individuals, duration of visits, their fruit‐handling behaviour and the number of fruits removed (for details, see Albrecht et al. 2013). In total, the data set for our analysis is based on 2268 h of seed disperser observations (630 h in 2011 and 1638 h in 2012), distributed over 158 days (67 days in 2011 and 91 days in 2012). After each sampling session, we counted the fruits of the observed plant individuals. For trees, we counted the fruits on representative parts of the tree crown and then extrapolated over the whole tree crown.

    Data Analysis

    Relationships between response traits of frugivores and fragmentation

    We selected forest specialization and body mass as response traits of frugivores that are potentially associated with the susceptibility of birds and mammals to forest fragmentation (Haskell, Ritchie & Olff 2002; Newbold et al. 2013). We classified frugivores as forest specialists and generalists (Jędrzejewska & Jędrzejewski 1998a,b; Svensson, Mullarney & Zetterström 2009; Albrecht et al. 2013). Forest specialists exclusively breed in forest habitats, while generalists also reproduce in non‐forest habitats (Albrecht et al. 2013). For avian frugivores, body mass was obtained from our own measurements. For mammalian frugivores, body mass was obtained from Wilman et al. (2014).

    We first tested for a relationship between habitat specialization (forest generalist and specialist, respectively) and body mass of frugivores and fragmentation using a quantitative fourth‐corner analysis (Dray & Legendre 2008). We refined comparisons of fragmentation to compare between continuous and fragmented forest (‘interior’ vs. ‘edge and fragment’). The fourth‐corner analysis requires (i) a site × species community matrix containing the presence or abundance of each frugivore species in each study site, (ii) a trait matrix containing the habitat specialization/body mass of each frugivore species and (iii) a habitat matrix containing information on the fragmentation of each study site (Dray & Legendre 2008). To construct the site × species matrix, we used information about the presence of each frugivore species in each study site across the two study years from the frugivore observations. For study sites that were only sampled in 1 year (n = 9), we used the data from the respective year (see Table S4). The significance of the relationships between fragmentation and habitat specialization as well as body mass was tested with χ2‐ and F‐statistics and a permutation test (9999 iterations), respectively. We chose permutation method 1, which permutes the abundances for each species independently and tests the null hypothesis that species are randomly distributed among the habitats (Legendre, Galzin & Harmelin‐Vivien 1997; Dray & Legendre 2008). Our conclusions remained unaffected when we used the more conservative combination of permutation methods 2 (entire rows of the site × species matrix are permuted) and 4 (entire columns of the site × species matrix are permuted; Dray & Legendre 2008).

    Relationships between the response traits of frugivores and their interaction impact and type

    We selected normalized species degree (i.e. the number of plant species that a frugivore species interacted with) and interaction type (i.e. seed dispersers vs. pulp and seed predators) as effect traits of frugivores, as they are traits determining the relative importance and qualitative effect of frugivores on the seed dispersal function, respectively. Here, we used normalized species degree rather than species strength as a measure of the importance of frugivores in the regional network. The reasoning behind this approach is that in spite of the strong positive correlation between species degree and strength (Bascompte, Jordano & Olesen 2006), estimates of species strength are subject to higher levels of uncertainty associated with proximate factors such as species abundance variability, changes associated with phenological sequences, and sampling errors that tend to decrease relationships with species traits (Rezende et al. 2007). We determined interaction type from our own observations of fruit‐handling behaviour of frugivores, whereby frugivores were categorized as legitimate ‘dispersers’ when they swallowed the fruits, and as pulp or seed ‘predators’ when they crushed fruits or pecked on the fleshy part only.

    We used generalized linear mixed‐effects models to analyse the relationships between the response and effect traits of frugivores. In the first model, we analysed the effects of forest specialization and body mass (log‐transformed) on the normalized degree of frugivores (as a binomial response). In the second model, we analysed the effects of forest specialization and body mass (log‐transformed) on the interaction type of frugivores (as a binomial response). Both models contained taxonomic class as random effect to account for differential life‐history traits of birds and mammals. In both models, we used a binomial error distribution with logit‐link function.

    Mean body mass, species richness and seed removal of frugivores on plants in response to fragmentation

    We used generalized linear mixed‐effects models to analyse the variation in the mean body mass, species richness and seed removal rates of seed dispersers and fruit predators on plants. To calculate seed removal rates, we first estimated the mean number of fruits that a frugivore species removed from a plant species per visit based on the frugivore observations. Then, we multiplied the number of visits of the frugivore species on the plant species by the mean number of fruits that the frugivore species removed from that plant species per visit. Finally, we multiplied the number of fruits removed from that plant species by the mean number of seeds per fruit (Table S2).

    In these analyses, we treated fragmentation and interaction type (seed disperser and fruit predator) and their interaction as fixed factors. Again, we refined comparisons of fragmentation to compare between continuous and fragmented forest (‘interior’ vs. ‘edge and fragment’). We fitted plant species, site and year as random grouping factors. We nested interaction type within plant species and plant species within study site, but treated year as a separate random grouping factor. Furthermore, we included the crop size of the observed plants as a continuous covariate in the models to account for variation in resource abundance across focal plants. For the response variables species richness and seed removal rate, we fitted models with Poisson error distribution and a square root link function (because both variables were not strongly skewed). Since the residuals of the model for seed removal rate were overdispersed (χ2 = 3577.5, ratio = 14.7, d.f. = 243, P < 0.001), we fitted an additional observation‐level random effect to account for this extra variation (Hinde 1982; Breslow 1984; Bates et al. 2015).

    All analyses were conducted in r version 3.2.3 (R Development Core Team 2015), using the packages bipartite (plotting of networks; Dormann et al. 2009), ade4 (fourth‐corner analysis; Dray & Dufour 2007) and lme4 (linear mixed‐effects models; Bates et al. 2015).

    Results

    Overall, we recorded a total of 4930 visits of 34 frugivore species (30 bird and four mammal species) on the nine plant species (Fig. 2; Tables S2 and S3). During these visits, the frugivores removed an estimated number of 14 247 fruits and 26 101 seeds, respectively. Four bird species were the most frequent visitors, that is Sylvia atricapilla (1589 visits), Turdus merula (1181), Erithacus rubecula (806) and Coccothraustes coccothraustes (320; in Fig. 2, these species are labelled A1, A2, A3 and A32, respectively). The first three species are legitimate dispersers and forest generalists, while the last species is a seed predator and forest specialist. Three understorey woody species received the most visits per 18 h, that is P. padus (126 ± 111 visits), E. europaeus (53 ± 46) and F. alnus (49 ± 44; in Fig. 2, these species are labelled P1, P2 and P4, respectively).

    Relationships Between Response Traits of Frugivores and Fragmentation

    Forest specialization and body mass were both associated with forest fragmentation (fourth‐corner analyses: forest specialization ~ fragmentation, χ2 = 3.524, P = 0.0045; body mass ~ fragmentation, F = 4.977, P = 0.012). We observed fewer forest specialist species in fragmented than in continuous forest (5.8 species in continuous forest vs. 3.5 species in fragmented forest; P = 0.0046), while species richness of forest generalists was unaffected by fragmentation (8.4 species in continuous forest vs. 8.9 species in fragmented forest, P = 0.60). Furthermore, the body mass of frugivores decreased with fragmentation (F = 3.96, P = 0.024).

    Relationships Between the Response Traits of Frugivores and Their Interaction Impact and Type

    Forest specialists interacted with a smaller proportion of plant species in the overall network than forest generalists (measured as normalized species degree), but the proportion of plant species used was unrelated to the body mass of frugivores (Fig. 3a,b; Table 1). Legitimate seed dispersers were larger than pulp or seed predators, but whether frugivores acted as seed dispersers or predators was not related to forest specialization (Fig. 3c,d; Table 1).

    The mean body mass of frugivores was lower on plants in fragmented than in continuous forest (Fig. 4a; Table 2).     Discussion

    We found that the effect of forest fragmentation on frugivore communities was not random, but rather associated with forest specialization and body mass of the frugivore species. Fewer forest specialists and large‐bodied frugivores were found in fragmented than in continuous forests. These response traits were correlated with effect traits of frugivores: we found a negative correlation between forest specialization and importance of dispersers in plant–frugivore networks; that is, forest specialists interacted with fewer plant species in the interaction networks than generalists. Moreover, larger frugivores acted predominantly as seed dispersers, while pulp and seed predators were on average smaller. Although the mean body mass of frugivore visitors was lower on plants in fragmented compared with continuous forest, we did not detect cascading consequences on species richness and rates of seed removal from plants in fragmented forests.

    Our results showed that forest specialization and body mass were associated with the response of frugivores to forest fragmentation, with specialists and large‐bodied frugivores being less abundant in fragments than in continuous forest. These findings conform to our expectations and are in line with numerous other studies documenting a loss of specialists and large‐bodied species from fragments (Swihart et al. 2003; Henle et al. 2004; Menke, Böhning‐Gaese & Schleuning 2012; Newbold et al. 2013). Habitat quality of forest fragments may be insufficient to support stable populations of species specialized on continuous old‐growth forest (Henle et al. 2004). As hunting probably only plays a minor role in our study system, we assume that the decrease in large‐bodied frugivores, such as woodpeckers, grouses or martens, is potentially caused by fragment sizes being too small for species with large home ranges (Carlson 2000; Kurki et al. 2000; Mergey, Helder & Roeder 2011). As mammalian forest‐dwelling species have been frequently documented in fragments (Pereboom et al. 2008; Mergey, Helder & Roeder 2011), this may particular apply to avian dispersers. Our findings are in contrast to the general assumption that fragmentation effects on the ecological structure of bird communities are generally less severe in temperate than in tropical ecosystems and are rather a product of random species loss than of guild‐specific extinction (Bregman, Sekercioglu & Tobias 2014). Here, we found a loss of frugivores from forest fragments that was clearly associated with the response traits forest specialization and body mass, leading to changes in the community composition.

    Forest specialist species, which were absent in fragments, only played a minor role in our networks, while fragmentation‐tolerant generalist species were most important for seed removal rates in our study system. Thus, species’ responses to fragmentation and their effect on seed removal were negatively correlated (sensu Schleuning, Fründ & Garcia 2015), in that the least susceptible species were functionally most important. In other words, this negative correlation suggests a low risk of losing seed dispersal services in fragments in our study area (Suding et al. 2008; Díaz et al. 2013). In fact, in both continuous and fragmented forest, our plant–frugivore networks were dominated by few habitat generalists (S. atricapilla, T. merula, E. rubecula), which together accounted for more than 72 per cent of all interactions (Albrecht et al. 2013). These species are potentially more efficient in tracking fruit resources, and are more reliant on fruits in their diet than the forest specialist species we recorded (e.g. Tetrastes bonasia, Dendrocopos leucotos, Dendrocopos major, Dendrocopos medius, Dendrocopos martius). Thus, habitat generalists may compensate for the functional loss of habitat specialists, which might allow for the maintenance of seed dispersal services even in forest fragments.

    Our results furthermore showed a correlation between the response trait body mass and the interaction type of frugivores in our community. Seed dispersers tended to be larger than pulp or seed predators. As a consequence, with fewer large‐bodied frugivores, we would have expected to find reduced rates of seed removal by legitimate seed dispersers in fragmented forest in comparison with continuous forest. Yet, we found no effect of fragmentation on seed removal rates by either seed dispersers or fruit predators. Thus, despite the direct link through plant–frugivore interactions, fragmentation effects on frugivores and plants were uncoupled in our study system. The reason for these uncoupled responses potentially lies in the loose dependencies between frugivores and plants owing to the relatively low degree of reciprocal specialization in plant–frugivore associations (e.g. Herrera & Jordano 1981; Howe & Smallwood 1992). Particularly, plants in temperate forest ecosystems that are mostly small‐seeded have a broader range of potential dispersers than large‐seeded predominantly tropical taxa (Herrera 1985). This explanation for uncoupled responses is in line with the size range of fruits in our study, which lies between 4 and 12 mm (Albrecht et al. 2015) and is thus seven times smaller than that in tropical plant communities (e.g. 2–84 mm; Kitamura et al. 2002). Thus, even small‐bodied frugivores are capable of swallowing the fruits of all plant species occurring in the community, and can compensate for the loss of large‐bodied frugivores in forest fragments. In fact, the three dominant frugivore species in our study area are at the lower range of body mass, and were frequently observed to swallow fruits of all plant species. Thus, in line with other studies, our results suggest that from a quantitative point of view, few small‐bodied generalist species can maintain in situ seed dispersal services for fleshy‐fruited plants likely with unaltered patterns of short‐distance dispersal events in forest fragments (Farwig, Böhning‐Gaese & Bleher 2006; Neuschulz, Botzat & Farwig 2011; Albrecht et al. 2013).

    Yet, losing interactions with forest specialist and large‐bodied frugivores in forest fragments may have an impact on the quality of seed dispersal of dependent plant species, for example in terms of gut treatment effects, microhabitats into which seeds are dispersed or the probability of long‐distance dispersal events (Wright et al. 2000; Markl et al. 2012; McConkey et al. 2012). It is well known that a set of multiple dispersal agents provides complementary services, in that, for example, some dispersers act on a local scale, while others maintain connectivity among populations (Chambers, Vander Wall & Schupp 1999; Spiegel & Nathan 2007). Thus, an impoverished set of frugivores may decrease chances for rare long‐distance dispersal, which is crucial for meta‐population dynamics, the persistence in a fragmented landscape and even for range shifts at large spatial scales (Trakhtenbrot et al. 2005; Montoya et al. 2008; González‐Varo 2010; Schupp, Jordano & Gómez 2010; Corlett & Westcott 2013). Consequently, further studies should link plant–frugivore interactions to qualitative aspects of seed dispersal, such as spatial patterns of seed deposition and regeneration, and ultimately to the recruitment of plant communities.

    Abstract

    Space use in mammals may be influenced not only by their primary foods, but also by localized sources of physiologically critical resources such as sodium‐rich plants. We examined how sodium acquisition influences habitat use in mountain gorillas (Gorilla beringei) in Rwanda which have increased the amount of time they forage on community land outside of Volcanoes National Park (VNP), where eucalyptus (Eucalyptus spp.) tree bark is their most frequently eaten food. We measured sodium content in samples from 34 main dietary items and quantified sodium intake by 22 gorillas in three social groups over one year. On a dry weight basis, eucalyptus bark contains 3100 mg Na/kg. In contrast, the four herbs most frequently exploited for food inside the park are relatively sodium‐poor (<70 mg/kg each). Further, sodium intake rates were highest when the gorillas were on community land. Of the two groups that fed outside of the park, one obtained 73% and the other one 45% of their sodium in that habitat despite only feeding for minimal amounts of time there. However, one group that did not feed on community land acquired 78% of its sodium in the subalpine and alpine zones through the consumption of pith of giant lobelias and groundsels. Obtaining sodium thus likely creates an incentive for the gorillas to leave the park and make forays into high‐altitude habitat. Both strategies are not without risks: exiting their natural habitat and feeding on crops may increase human‐wildlife conflict and visiting high‐altitude areas may increase the risk of hypothermia.

    The question of how spatiotemporal availability of resources influences ranging patterns and habitat use is one of the enduring topics in animal ecology, as it has relevance for population‐level demography (e.g., Gaillard et al. 2010). A combination of key/staple foods and rare/ephemeral foods are important in determining ranging behavior in mammals (Clutton‐Brock 1975a, Newton 1992, Nunes 1995, Tufto et al. 1996, Zinner et al. 2001, Pages et al. 2005, Campos et al. 2014, Zhang et al. 2014).

    Sodium is a micronutrient that is critically important for physiological processes such as muscle and nerve function and maintaining fluid levels in various parts of the body (Robbins 1993). Sodium deficiency can affect bone mineralization, growth, and reproduction. A sodium deficit can trigger a specific ‘hunger’ for it (Denton 1984). Tropical herbs are generally characterized by low levels of sodium (Weir 1972, McNaughton 1988, Rothman et al. 2006b), although there are some well‐documented counterexamples such as halophytes (Flowers & Colmer 2015) which are uncommon but have evolved independently in many lineages of flowering plants (Bromham 2015).

    Herbivorous mammals inhabiting forested habitats have ways of countering sodium deficiency. There are three principal ways in which sodium can be acquired: utilization of natural licks, consumption of sodium‐rich plants (both wild and domesticated), and geophagy. Orangutans (Pongo pygmaeus) utilize terrestrial natural licks to obtain sodium (Matsubayashi et al. 2011). Elephants and ungulates enter underground caves to exploit salty deposits (Denton 1984). Oates (1978) observed black‐and‐white colobus (Colobus guereza) in Kibale National Park, Uganda descending to the ground and wading through water to feed on swamp plants with high sodium concentrations. Similarly, western lowland gorillas (Gorilla gorilla gorilla) and other herbivores in central Africa are attracted to bais, that is, swampy forest clearings, where they consume sodium‐rich plants (Magliocca & Gautier‐Hion 2002). Wood from decaying trees and young branch piths constitute a key source of sodium for Geoffroyi's spider monkeys (Ateles geoffroyi) (Chaves et al. 2011). It has been suggested that ingestion of stems of Cecropia peltata serves to counter sodium deficits in black howler monkeys (Alouatta pigra) living in hurricane‐damaged forests (Behie & Pavelka 2012). Chimpanzees (Pan troglodytes) in Budongo, Uganda relied on pith of decaying Raphia farinifera palm trees as a source of sodium (Reynolds et al. 2009) until large‐scale anthropogenic harvest of this tree led to its decline, forcing the chimpanzees to switch to an alternative sodium source, the tree Cleistopholis patens (Reynolds et al. 2012). Koalas (Phascolarctos cinereus) living in subalpine regions have been observed eating eucalypt bark with high concentrations of sodium (Au et al. 2017). Consumption of crops such as bananas and maize by African elephants (Loxodonta africana) has been interpreted as a means of offsetting sodium deficiency in wild foods (Rode et al. 2006). Some mammals including primates engage in geophagy, targeting soil with high sodium content, for example, black‐and‐white snub‐nosed monkeys (Rhinopithecus bieti) (Li et al. 2014), African elephants (Holdø et al. 2002), axis deer (Axis axis) and Hanuman langurs (Semnopithecus spp.) (Moe 1993).

    Mountain gorillas (Gorilla beringei beringei) are largely herbivorous and forage mainly on stems and leaves of ground plants and vines (Fossey & Harcourt 1977, Watts 1984, McNeilage 2001, Grueter et al. 2013), many of which tend to contain high amounts of protein (Watts 1983, Rothman et al. 2006a), but are generally low in sodium (Cancelliere et al. 2014). Diet varies across the two remaining populations, with the one in Bwindi Impenetrable National Park, Uganda being more frugivorous than the one in the Virunga Volcanoes (Stanford & Nkurunungi 2003, Ganas et al. 2004, Rothman et al. 2007, Wright et al. 2015). The diet of the Virunga gorillas does not fluctuate much throughout the year with the exception of bamboo shoots which are consumed in large quantities when available during some months of the year (Vedder 1984, Watts 1984, Grueter et al. 2014). Little is known about how mountain gorillas meet their sodium requirements. Preliminary evidence shows that they forage for unusual resources to satisfy their sodium needs: in Bwindi they ingest decaying wood which is rich in sodium (Rothman et al. 2006b, see also Cancelliere et al. 2014) and can elicit aggressive competition (Wright & Robbins 2014). In the Virungas, they sometimes consume (hardened) sodium‐rich soils (Mahaney et al. 1990). Another possible source of sodium is eucalyptus, an exotic tree that has been planted extensively outside the natural habitat of mountain gorillas to provide fuelwood and timber for local residents (e.g., Ndayambaje & Mohren 2011).

    Crop feeding by mountain gorillas has become an issue of increasing attention by conservation practitioners and researchers alike, as it might be considered a safety hazard for both gorillas and people and a source of economic losses for the local communities living at the edge of the park. The risk of disease transmission is also a serious concern when gorillas get in close proximity to humans and their livestock (Goldsmith et al. 2006, Madden 2006, Rwego et al. 2008, Palacios et al. 2011). A detailed study of the effects of food availability inside and outside the park on the occurrence of gorillas ranging outside Bwindi Impenetrable National Park found that forays into agricultural land were primarily driven by the availability of herbaceous foods that were present in tea, pine plantations and on uncultivated land, as well as due to the availability of palatable crops such as bananas, eucalyptus, and sweet potatoes (Seiler & Robbins 2016).

    Mountain gorillas habituated for tourism and research in Volcanoes National Park (VNP) in the Virunga Massif in Rwanda have been known to leave the confines of the park on many occasions. Data for nine gorilla groups monitored from 2008 to 2011 show that combined they spent an average of 6 to 19 days a year outside the park (Musana 2012). In extreme cases they travel 1 km away from the park boundary (ibid.). One of the main proposed reasons for exploitation of community land by the gorillas in Rwanda is to acquire additional foods, in particular foods that do not grow inside the park. The bark (cambium) of eucalyptus (Eucalyptus spp.) is the main food resource the gorillas harvest when outside the park, but the overall contribution of this species to their diet is low (<1% based on time spent feeding, Karisoke Research Center, unpubl. data). Similarly, Rothman et al. (2006b) anecdotally noted for Bwindi gorillas that they range near park boundaries to indulge in eucalyptus bark, and Harris and Chapman (2007) suggested that habitat use by guereza was dictated by eucalyptus crops. We reason that eucalyptus must be nutritionally attractive in order to motivate the gorillas to trespass areas of agricultural use where risks including harassment by local people are involved.

    In this study, we aimed to understand how mountain gorillas in VNP meet their sodium needs by quantifying sodium intake across all vegetation zones used by the gorillas including community land. We conducted nutritional analyses on 34 plant food items eaten by the gorillas at various altitudes, including eucalyptus, to compare sodium content among them. Based on a previous study showing that eucalyptus contributes a large portion of total sodium intake for colobus monkeys in Uganda (Rode et al. 2003), we predicted that sodium intake rates would be highest when the gorillas forage outside the park where eucalyptus trees are located. We also predicted that the gorillas would be more likely to eat eucalyptus during the months that they frequent bamboo shoots because the bamboo zone borders the edge of the park. Our results show that eucalyptus has an exceptionally high sodium content and sodium intake rates are higher on community land than in any other vegetation zone inside the park. Moreover, our study demonstrates that one gorilla group ventured into high‐altitude habitat to feed on sodium‐rich giant lobelias and groundsels which can be seen as an alternative strategy of meeting their sodium mineral requirements.

    Methods

    Study site

    The study population of critically endangered mountain gorillas in Volcanoes National Park (formerly Parc National des Volcans) in Rwanda has been monitored by the Dian Fossey Gorilla Fund's Karisoke Research Center since 1967. The habitat of the study groups of gorillas stretches across a large altitudinal gradient and encompasses various plant communities of which the most important ones are (in order of ascending altitude) (McNeilage 2001): bamboo vegetation between 2500 and 2800 m, Hagenia‐Hypericum woodland interspersed with open herbaceous slopes between 2800 and 3300 m, and the subalpine and alpine zones with extensive thickets of blackberries as well as stands of giant lobelias (Lobelia stuhlmannii and L. wollastonii) and giant groundsels (Dendrosenecio erici‐rosenii) between 3300 and 3800 m (for more details on habitat composition, see McNeilage 2001, Plumptre 1991). The home ranges of almost all mountain gorilla groups monitored by the Karisoke Research Center include areas at the edge of the park (Caillaud et al. 2014) where they seasonally harvest bamboo shoots (Watts 1984, Grueter et al. 2014). Community land bordering the park are composed of Irish potato and pyrethrum fields, isolated stands of bamboo, some terrestrial herbaceous vegetation, eucalyptus trees, and grasslands. The local climate is characterized by two distinct rainy seasons, one during March–May and the other one during September–December, with dry seasons in between. Annual rainfall averaged 1950 mm in 2009/2010.

    Data collection

    From September 2009 to December 2010, to quantify sodium intake of the gorillas, we recorded food intake of 22 adult females (aged 8 or more) in three of the Karisoke study groups: Bwenge, Ntambara, and Pablo. The protocol consisted of 30‐minute focal observation sessions (Altmann 1974). Food intake was estimated by counting the number of food units consumed by a focal animal during a timed feeding bout, which was defined as the time when the individual commenced and terminated feeding (see, e.g., Rothman et al. 2007). A food unit refers to a clearly observable quantity of food such as a certain length of a herb stem (e.g., Peucedanum linderi), a handful of vines (e.g., Galium), a single berry (Rubus sp.), or a segment of the pith (e.g., Dendrosenecio erici‐rosenii). In the case of Eucalyptus bark, a food unit was defined as a single bite (done with the incisors) of the underside of the bark or the exposed trunk after external bark removal. Fifty samples of each food unit were collected, dried, and weighed, and based on these weights, an average dry weight for each food unit was calculated (see also Grueter et al. 2015).

    For sodium content analyses, ca. 400 g (wet mass) of plant parts (stems, bark, pith, roots, shoots, leaves, fruit) known to be consumed by gorillas were collected in the gorillas’ feeding area. Samples were weighed instantly after collection, dried overnight at 40–45 °C using a fruit dryer (A. & J. Stöckli AG, Switzerland, see also Grueter et al. 2015). Sodium content was determined at the Nutritional Lab of the Leibniz Institute for Zoo and Wildlife Research (Berlin, Germany). Prior to analysis, all samples were ground with an IKA A11 basic mill (IKA‐Werke GmbH & Co. KG, Staufen, Germany). Sodium was determined using an inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometer (model Optima 8000, PerkinElmer, Rodgau, Germany) after wet ashing with the microwave MLS “START 1500” (MLS GmbH, Leutkich, Germany).

    Data analysis

    Sodium intake rates were the product of the total dry matter ingested per hour of feeding (i.e., the number of food units consumed multiplied by the average unit weight per unit time) and the sodium content of each ingested food.

    To test the prediction that sodium intake rates were significantly higher when the gorillas fed in community land, we ran a linear mixed model (LMM) with Gaussian error structure (Baayen 2008). The analysis used a separate data point for each feeding bout. The response variable was sodium intake, which was log‐transformed. We chose to include duration as a control variable (also log‐transformed) as per Wright et al. (2015) and (Wright et al. 2014), instead of dividing sodium intake by duration of feeding bout (thereby obtaining a rate). The predictor of interest was habitat zone. We accounted for the potential influence of female reproductive state (cycling, pregnant, or lactating), dominance rank and group size, on sodium intake rates, which have been shown to influence feeding strategies in gorillas (Wright et al. 2014). Females were considered to be pregnant for 8.5 months backdated from the birth of an infant and lactating for three years following the birth of an infant (Watts 1991, Grueter et al. 2014). Dominance hierarchies were based on non‐aggressive approach‐retreat interactions and determined using a normalized David's score (see Wright et al. 2015 for further details). Group size was based on the average group size per study month. We further accounted for the potential influence of time of observation and time of observation squared (to account for a potential nonlinear relationship), temporal autocorrelation (data points spaced close in time being more similar to each other than data points further spaced in time) (see Wright et al. 2015 for details) and as mentioned above, feeding bout duration, on sodium intake rates. To avoid pseudoreplication, we included female ID, group ID, and date as random effects. We also included random slope terms to allow for habitat to vary in its relationship to group and focal, and for reproductive state to vary in its relationship to group (Schielzeth & Forstmeier 2008, Barr et al. 2013). All quantitative predictor variables were z‐transformed (to a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1).

    We checked for the assumptions of normally distributed and homogenous residuals by visual inspection of qq‐plots and the residuals plotted against the fitted values and found no violations. Variance inflation factors were derived from the model without random effects using the function “vif” from the “car” package (Fox & Weisberg 2011). No collinearity issues were found. We checked for model stability by excluding each level of the random effects and each data point one at a time from the dataset and comparing the estimates of each predictor to the estimates from the full model with the full dataset. No influential cases were found. The model was fitted in R (R Core Team 2014) using the “lme4” package (Bates et al. 2014) with the REML argument set to FALSE. P‐values were derived using likelihood ratio tests comparing a full model with a respective reduced model, without the variable of interest (excluded one at a time).

    Results

One group (Pablo) was not observed feeding on community land during our observation period, but it was seen foraging outside the park on a few occasions by field personnel (KRC records). Groups Ntambara and Bwenge never fed in the subalpine and alpine zones during the study period.

    All groups obtained sodium in all habitat zones in which they fed but sodium intake rates varied among vegetation types; for Bwenge and Ntambara, the sodium intake rate was highest on community land, and for Pablo it was highest in the alpine zone (Table 1). Of the two groups that foraged on community land, one (Bwenge) obtained 73% of its sodium there despite its feeding time outside the park accounting for only 3% of its total feeding time. The other group (Ntambara) spent 3% of its feeding time on community land and obtained 45% of its sodium there. The third group, which spent 47% of its time feeding in the subalpine and alpine zone, acquired 78% of its sodium in these two habitat zones (Table 1). Testing the effects of habitat type and covariates on sodium intake, the full LMM was significantly better at explaining the data than the null model (chi‐sq = 58.78; df = 7; P < 0.001). Habitat was the only non‐control explanatory variable that was significantly related to sodium intake (Tables 2 and 3). The overall significance of habitat appears to be largely driven by the very high sodium intake rates in the community land (25.3 mg/min) and in the alpine zone (9.5 mg/min). Sodium intake rates in other habitat types were much lower: subalpine: 1.3 mg/min; Hypericum: 0.4 mg/min; Hagenia: 0.3 mg/min; bamboo: 0.3 mg/min).

    Of all feeding bouts on community land, 72% involved feeding on eucalyptus bark (Fig. 1), and the remaining 28% feeding on Galium sp. vines, the leaves of Helichrysum cf. formossissimum, Rubus sp., Carduus nyassanus, and C. leptacanthus, the stems of Peucedanum linderi stems, the shoots of bamboo (Yushania alpina), and wild papayas. To determine if the gorillas were more likely to eat eucalyptus during the months that they were near the border when they were eating bamboo shoots, we tallied the number of eucalyptus feeding bouts for every month. The gorillas were observed to feed on eucalyptus only in March, April, October, and November. The distribution of eucalyptus feeding bouts was not uniformly distributed across the year (1 sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov, P < 0.0001, test statistic = 2.309). Twenty‐two of 36 eucalyptus feeding bouts were recorded in October and November. Moreover, on all 6 days on which the gorillas fed on eucalyptus they were also observed to consume bamboo (bamboo shoots on four days and bamboo leaves on two days).

    Eucalyptus is by far the most sodium‐rich plant (3104 mg Na/kg), followed by Lobelia wollastonii (2684 mg/kg) (Table 4). In contrast, the four key herbaceous food species, which cumulatively comprise >80% of the gorillas’ diet and are found primarily at mid‐elevation sites inside VNP, are relatively low in sodium (<70 mg/kg).

    Discussion

    Sodium is a micronutrient that is important for healthy body functioning, so wildlife such as mountain gorillas are expected to have developed a foraging strategy that yields adequate acquisition of this mineral. All studied groups of mountain gorillas in the Virunga Volcanoes obtained sodium in all habitat zones in which they fed. In most habitat zones (except community land and alpine) the amount of sodium intake was proportional to the total feeding duration each group spent in each habitat zone, that is, the longer time a group spent feeding in a habitat zone, the higher the total sodium intake. Considering the short periods of time the groups spent feeding on community land and in the alpine zone, sodium intake was extremely high in those areas. This is due to sodium intake rates being significantly higher in these zones compared the other zones.

    Nutritional attractiveness of cultivated plants has been shown to be a major impetus for crop‐foraging behavior in some species of primates (Forthman Quick & Demment 1988, Campbell‐Smith et al. 2011, Riley et al. 2013, McLennan & Ganzhorn 2016) and also non‐primates (e.g., elephants; Osborn 2004). In contrast, some primates show increased reliance on agricultural crops during times of forest fruit shortages (e.g., Naughton‐Treves et al. 1998, Chancellor et al. 2012, McLennan 2013).

    Our study shows that the commonly eaten food plants inside the park (with the exception of three plants growing at the upper altitudinal limit of their habitat) contain relatively low levels of sodium. The significant rate at which the gorillas can acquire sodium when feeding outside the park appears to underlie their occasional forays into cultivated areas. Eucalyptus trees—which grow only outside the park—are the gorillas’ prime target when they exit the park. Food choice in the Virunga gorillas is positively influenced by protein and digestibility (Watts 1983), so judging from the undesirable protein‐fiber ratio of eucalyptus (Grueter, unpubl. data), this plant is not expected to be a preferred dietary item. However, the extremely high concentration of sodium in eucalyptus bark must clearly outweigh these nutritional shortcomings. A similar link between eucalyptus feeding and sodium acquisition has been established for colobus monkeys at Kibale, Uganda whose available natural foods are low in sodium and complement their diet with sodium from eucalyptus (Rode et al. 2003). Other East African primates that have been observed feeding on eucalyptus are Ruwenzori black‐and‐white colobus (Colobus angolensis ruwenzorii) outside Nyungwe National Park, Rwanda (Grueter, pers. obs.) and chimpanzees in the corridor area between the Budongo and Bugoma Forest, Uganda (McCarthy et al. 2017). Mountain gorillas in Bwindi also targeted eucalyptus among a number of other crops when they foraged on community land (Seiler & Robbins 2016, see also Rothman et al. 2006b). The biological processes involved in the uptake and retention of sodium require additional research. While this study shows that large quantities of sodium are acquired during relatively brief periods of feeding outside the park, it is unclear if the gorillas are physiologically capable of uptaking and storing these amounts. In mammals in general, sodium is mostly in solution in the extra‐cellular fluid, and if sodium intake decreases, the kidney can decrease sodium excretion to a certain degree.

    Sodium acquisition may also explain why at least some mountain gorilla groups in Volcanoes National Park sometimes move up to very high altitudes where mean annual temperature is only 4–9 °C (Spinage 1972) and they may suffer thermal costs to feed on senecios and lobelias. The second highest sodium content (nearly as much as eucalyptus) was found in Lobelia wollastonii, a plant which grows exclusively in the alpine zone above 3500 m (Watts 1984, Grueter, unpubl. data). Dendrosenecio erici‐rosenii and Lobelia stuhlmannii are also quite high in sodium and are found only at high altitudes (alpine and subalpine zone). Feeding on eucalyptus appears to be a lower‐cost option for satisfying the gorillas’ sodium requirements than feeding on lobelias and senecios for two reasons. First, the sodium content of the former is higher than the latter, and mean sodium intake rate per hour of feeding is higher on community land than in the subalpine and alpine zones. Second, eucalyptus bark consists of 25% dry matter, whereas the dry matter content in the pith of the two lobelia species and Dendrosenecio erici‐rosenii is only 11 and 8%, respectively. Given the fact that the pith of these latter plants is largely water, the gorillas would have to consume more to get similar amounts of sodium out of these plants.

    Given the well‐documented attractiveness of sodium resources and the degree to which animals will alter their behavior to obtain them (e.g., Oates 1978, Denton 1984, Faber et al. 1993, Hellgren & Pitts 1997, Magliocca & Gautier‐Hion 2002, Laurian et al. 2008), sodium acquisition seems to be the primary driver of eucalyptus consumption in mountain gorillas. Nevertheless, it is possible that the gorillas also obtain other minerals that we did not investigate, such as iodine. Preliminary analyses show that no macronutrients (with the exception of saccharose) are particularly dense in eucalyptus compared to the herbaceous plants most frequently consumed by mountain gorillas (Grueter, unpubl. data). Eucalyptus bark is also used by local people in East Africa as a folk remedy for respiratory ailments (Tugume et al. 2016), which opens up the possibility that the gorillas derive medicinal benefits from ingesting this plant. Eucalyptus feeding may also be costly to the gorillas’ health because its bark contains phytoestrogens which can act as endocrine disruptors (Wasserman et al. 2012). Additional research into the health and reproductive implications of feeding on this resource is required.

    It appears that the gorillas make disproportionate use of eucalyptus when they range in the bamboo zone close to the park boundary. Eucalyptus bark is a year‐round available resource, but as the park boundary generally lies at some distance (>1 km in some areas) from the gorillas’ main habitat type (Hagenia‐Hypericum woodland), they may combine a foray into community land with a visit to the bamboo zone (which is extensively exploited during the bamboo growth season). Indeed, the majority of eucalyptus feeding bouts were recorded during the peak of the bamboo growth season in that year (Grueter et al. 2014). Moreover, on all six days when the gorillas fed on eucalyptus they were also observed to consume bamboo. Bamboo shoots are an extremely poor source of sodium (the main dietary item with the lowest sodium content), so the gorillas may need to counter the dearth of sodium in their diet after a prolonged stay in the bamboo zone with a sodium‐rich meal. Long periods of minimal sodium intake create a strong urge and specific appetite (Robbins 1993) which may explain physiologically why the gorillas ventured out of the park.

    The motives underlying mountain gorilla excursions outside Volcanoes National Park in Rwanda have thus far received limited academic scrutiny despite the importance of such an understanding for mitigating human‐wildlife conflict. Human‐wildlife conflict is often generated when wildlife exit their natural habitat, come in close contact with locally residing humans and raid their crops (Siex & Struhsaker 1999, Hill 2000, Osborn 2004, Hockings et al. 2009, Redpath et al. 2013, Seiler & Robbins 2016, Bryson‐Morrison et al. 2017, Spagnoletti et al. 2017). These encounters can foster negative attitudes by local communities toward wildlife, which can hamper wildlife conservation efforts (Dickman 2010).

    The results presented here may advance the discussion of how to adapt local human land use to effectively curb human–wildlife conflict in areas of mutual utilization. An ideal long‐term solution to the crop‐raiding problem is to have farmers living near the forest edge develop agricultural practices favoring plant species that are nutritionally unattractive to the gorillas; if sufficiently wide, such a buffer zone would discourage crossing into croplands (Naughton‐Treves et al. 1998, Webber et al. 2011). An alternative solution is to set up a buffer zone around the park bordering community farmland that contain some eucalyptus trees, and also secures the gorillas’ access to this tree and possibly limits contact with farmers. However, planting nutritionally attractive buffer crops may cause animals to frequent edge areas and increase the probability of crop raiding once the buffer crop has been depleted (e.g., Goldsmith et al. 2006). How the mountain gorillas in the Rwandan Virunga Volcanoes maintained sodium balance prior to the introduction of eucalyptus trees is unknown, but their occasional forays into the afro‐alpine zone (e.g., Watts 1998) coupled with extensive feeding on relatively sodium‐poor herbs found at mid‐altitude likely sufficed to prevent low sodium levels.

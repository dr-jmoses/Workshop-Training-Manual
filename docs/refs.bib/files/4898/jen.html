    Abstract

    The cocoa pod borer (CPB), Conopomorpha cramerella (Snellen), sex pheromone was previously identified as a blend of (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetates and corresponding alcohols. These pheromone components were synthesized by modification of an existing method and the relative attractiveness of synthetic blends that included different levels of non‐target pheromone components and chemical purities was tested in a cocoa field using Delta traps. Male captures were not significantly different among traps baited with pheromone blends containing 5% to 47% (based on four identified pheromone components) of other geometric acetates [(E,Z,E)‐, (Z,Z,Z)‐, (Z,E,Z)‐ and (Z,E,E)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetates], indicating that C. cramerella males did not discriminate among the pheromone components and other geometric isomers in the blends. Therefore, neither antagonistic nor synergistic effects from other pheromone geometric isomers were observed. The modified synthetic pathway offers the prospect of more economical production of CPB sex pheromone. During 17 weeks when C. cramerella monitoring coincided with the main cocoa pod harvest period in 2013–2014, CPB trap catch data from some blends showed a good correlation with the number of pods with C. cramerella infestation symptoms.

    Introduction

    The cocoa pod borer (CPB) Conopomorpha cramerella (Snellen) (Lepidoptera: Gracillariidae) is one of the most important biotic factors affecting cocoa (Theobroma cacao L.) production in South‐East Asia (Shapiro et al. 2008; Rosmana et al. 2010). The pest is held responsible for cocoa crop losses that range between 30% (Teh et al. 2006; Zhang et al. 2008) and 50% (Wielgoss et al. 2012). C. cramerella moths are about 7 mm long and have a wing span of about 12 mm (Ooi et al. 1987). Females C. cramerella lay between 60 and 200 eggs on the furrowed cocoa pod surface during their lifetime (Azhar and Long 1996; Santoso et al. 2004). Newly hatched larvae bore perpendicularly through the pod surface until reaching the sclerotic layer of the husk (Ooi et al. 1987). Larvae subsequently feed on the pulp and placenta until reaching maturity. When mature, larvae emerge from the pod shell and pupate (Rosmana et al. 2010). The pupal stage takes between 6 and 8 days to complete. Feeding by C. cramerella larvae results in premature ripening of pods, containing flat, small beans that are often stuck together in a mass of dried mucilage and make bean extraction for commercial purposes impossible (Beevor et al. 1993; Rosmana et al. 2010).

    Management of C. cramerella has heavily relied on pesticide applications (Wood et al. 1992; Beevor et al. 1993), which are neither environmentally sustainable nor economically effective. Much effort has been directed towards development of integrated pest management (IPM) strategies against C. cramerella in commercial cocoa cultivation, including the use of degradable plastic pod sleeves for exclusion, the nematode Steinernema carpocapsae (Rosmana et al. 2010), pheromone‐mediated mating disruption (Alias et al. 2004), pheromone‐based mass trapping (Beevor et al. 1993), Bacillus thuringiensis toxins (Santoso et al. 2004); and the fungus Beauveria bassiana. Some of the latter strategies (mating disruption, mass trapping) make use of behavioural manipulation of male insects through specific sex pheromone application.

    Being an environmentally benign approach, sex pheromone‐based pest control has received increasing attention in general and for C. cramerella specifically. The sex pheromone components of C. cramerella were identified in 1986 (Beevor et al. 1986a,b; Ho et al. 1987) and field‐tested in Sabah, Malaysia. They consist of 40 : 60 : 4 : 6 ratios of (E,Z,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetate [(E,Z,Z)‐16:OAc], (E,E,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetate [(E,E,Z)‐16:OAc], (E,Z,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl alcohol [(E,Z,Z)‐16:OH] and (E,E,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl alcohol [(E,E,Z)‐16:OH] (Beevor et al. 1986a). In a large‐scale (200‐ha plot) mass trapping pilot study using synthetic sex pheromones, pod damage due to C. cramerella infestation was significantly reduced compared with pods harvested from untreated plots (Beevor et al. 1993). In a mating disruption trial on a 22‐ha plot, using 1000 lures per ha (one per tree), the percentage of unextractable pods was reduced in comparison with that of the control plot (Tay and Sim 1989), although these results were inconclusive due to the absence of experimental replicates.

    After identification of CPB sex pheromone, traps baited with the same pheromone blend were reported to catch two Gracillarid species, litchi fruit borer (LFB) (Conopomorpha sinensis) and C. lichiella, which are pests of lychee (Litchi chinensis) and longan (Dimocarpus longan) fruit in Taiwan (Hwang and Hung 1996), although their species‐specific sex pheromones still need to be identified. Further study by Hung et al. (2006) indicated that a blend of (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetates (ratio = 92 : 8) with the corresponding (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl alcohols (ratio = 46 : 4) was the most attractive lure for male C. sinensis in the field test conducted in litchi orchards in Taiwan. In the same trial, a significant inhibitory effect was observed when the ratio of (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc to (E,Z,Z)‐16:OAc was increased to more than 50%. Surprisingly, traps baited with the CPB blend attracted more non‐C. cramerella specimens (probably C. sinensis or other closed Gracillarid species) than the LFB blend (proposed by Hung et al. 2006) in trials carried out in litchi orchards in India (K. R. M. Bhanu, personal communication). Therefore, exploring whether other geometric isomers of 4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetates and impurities generated during their chemical synthesis affect the attractancy of male C. cramerella could provide much‐needed insights into more effective CPB management.

    Research on and application of pheromones against C. cramerella was halted in the early 1990s for economic reasons and a lack of commercial quantities. The possibility of existence of more than one strain of C. cramerella in Asia that behaved differently to the pheromone blend (Beevor et al. 1993) would make it much harder to use species‐ or strain‐specific pheromones in IPM strategy. In addition, custom‐made sandwich trap recommended by Beevor et al. (1986a) is not commercially available. Since 2004, this research was revived with pheromone activity and trap design re‐evaluation experiments in Malaysia and Indonesia. Lures containing 100 μg of a synthetic sex pheromone blend were found to attract male C. cramerella moths in Sabah and peninsular Malaysia, and in Sumatra and Sulawesi (Indonesia), with no significant differences in catch numbers among traps baited with these lures and those with lures containing higher pheromone doses (300 μg; and 1000 μg per vial) (Zhang et al. 2008). It was also shown that the same pheromone strain prevailed in a wide stretch of the Indo‐Malayan archipelago (Zhang et al. 2008) and that among the three different trap types tested (Trecé™ Delta, Scale and Pherocon 1C), the delta trap was most effective.

    The economic viability of CPB control strategies using pheromone lures depends to a large extent on pheromone availability and cost; cost will increase if higher purity of the pheromone blend is required for optimal attractiveness. Modification of the existing synthetic method (Beevor et al. 1986a) for more economical production of these hexadecatrienyl acetates and alcohols may also raise the levels of other non‐target geometric isomers, resulting in possible antagonistic or synergistic effects on the behavioural response of male CPB, as has been shown for other moth pests (Witzgall et al. 1993; Durant et al. 1995). As a result, a trade‐off prevails between cost reduction of CPB pheromone production and pheromone effectiveness. In this study, we report the results of field experiments in which several synthetic CPB pheromone lures containing different geometric isomers and purity levels were compared for their effectiveness at capturing male CPB.

    Materials and Methods

    Chemical and formulation

    Five pheromone blends were evaluated in this study. Blend 1 was obtained from Dr. David Hall, the Natural Resources Institute (NRI), Greenwich, UK. (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc and corresponding alcohols were synthesized and formulated at the NRI (100 μg loading in polyethylene vials, 26 mm × 8 mm × 1.5 mm thick; Just Plastic Ltd., Norwich, UK) as described by Beevor et al. (1986a). Pheromone components in blends 2 and 3 were synthesized at Pest Control (India) Pvt. Ltd. (PCI), Bangalore, India, following the same procedure as above, but with a modification using stereochemical control during olefin synthesis. In the Wittig reaction, the ylide was prepared by n‐BuLi/ether instead of t‐BuOK/THF from triphenylphosphonium salt to react with aldehyde moiety at −40°C (Anderson and Henrick 1975), resulting in an enrichment of the 6E‐isomer to afford a mixture of (E,Z,Z)‐and (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc with ~40 : 55 ratio in one pot. The same procedures were repeated at the Invasive Insect Behavior and Biological Control Laboratory (IIBBL), Beltsville, MD, to generate pheromone components used in blends 4 and 5, but without column chromatography purification on silica gel impregnated with 20% silver nitrate used during pheromone syntheses in UK and India.

    Synthetic pheromone components, (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc and the corresponding alcohols, were blended in ratios close to 40 : 60 : 4 : 6 [(E,Z,Z)‐16:OAc : (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc : (E,Z,Z)‐16:OH : (E,E,Z)‐16:OH], checked by gas chromatography (GC) and stored as 100 μg/μl CH2Cl2 solution in a freezer at −20°C in Beltsville, MD (Zhang et al. 2008). The polyethylene vials (Just Plastic Ltd., same as NRI formulation) were impregnated with 1 μl 100 μg/μl solution of the different synthetic sex pheromone blends (blends 2–5) and 1 drop of 2,6‐di‐tert‐butyl‐4‐methylphenol (BHT) as antioxidant (10 mg/ml hexane). Blank control vials were treated with BHT only. After solvent evaporation in a fume hood for 5 min, polyethylene vial caps were closed and lures were packed separately using triple‐sealed plastic bags and shipped to Malaysia by express carrier on the same day. After arrival, all lures were stored in a freezer until deployed in the field.

    Instrumentation

    All chemical components were analysed on a Hewlett‐Packard (HP) 6890 GC coupled to a HP 5973 Mass (MS) Selective Detector in the electronic impact (EI) mode as previously described (Zhang et al. 2004). A DB‐WAXETR capillary column (J&W Scientific Inc., Folsom, CA, 60 m × 0.25‐mm ID, 0.25‐μm film thickness, oven temperature was set up at 150°C for 2 min, then programmed to 250°C at 15°C/min and held for 25 min) and a DB‐5MS capillary column (J&W Scientific Inc., 60 m × 0.25‐mm ID, 0.25‐μm film thickness, oven temperature was set up at 100°C for 2 min, then programmed to 280°C at 10°C/min and held for 20 min), with helium as carrier gas (2 ml/min) were used. A 70 eV electron beam was employed for sample ionization. GC quantitative analyses were conducted on an HP 6890 GC equipped with a flame ionization detector in the splitless mode using the same DB‐WAXETR capillary column and conditions as the GC‐MS analyses described above, except with hydrogen (1.4 ml/min) as the carrier gas. GC‐MS qualitative and GC quantitative analyses of synthetic pheromone components were conducted using blend 1 (NRI) as standard (a NRI lure extracted with 1 ml hexane). Chemical purities and ratios of pheromone components of each blend were calculated based on the size of individual GC peaks using ChemStation integrator (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA).

    Study site

    Field testing of the pheromone blends was performed in a 1.6‐ha subplot within a 146‐ha cocoa planting that was adjacent to a 1065‐ha rubber estate in Kuala Lipis, Pahang, Malaysia. The experimental subplot bordered other cocoa plots to the North and East, a 2‐year‐old rubber plot to the South and a 20‐year‐old rubber plot to the West. The cocoa subplot consisted of 30‐year‐old trees from seven clones that were planted so that trees from a single clone prevailed in four adjacent South‐West oriented rows per clone. The disease and insect management programme in the plantation consisted of spraying each tree with ~110 ml per tree of a solution containing 190 mg of Cu(OH)2 and 110 μl of cypermethrin at 6‐week intervals.

    Assessment of pod loss

    The presence or absence of C. cramerella infestation holes as well as black pod disease (Phytophthora palmivora) symptoms (necrotic blotches) on 5638 mature pods, harvested between 30 October 2013 and 4 March 2014 during 10 fortnightly harvesting rounds, from 480 trees on the experimental plot was recorded. After exterior evaluation, pods were opened and pulp was evaluated for defects due to C. cramerella larval feeding.

    CPB monitoring by pheromone traps

    Effectiveness of the pheromone blends was tested by monitoring CPB captures in traps during two sampling periods. The first period lasted 14 weeks (12 February–16 May 2013) and coincided with the end of the harvest season of September 2012–May 2013. Treatments were as follows: (i) standard CPB pheromone sample with 93% purity [with 3% (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc, 2% (Z,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, blend 1]; (ii) sample with 88% purity [with 11% (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc, 2% (Z,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, blend 2]; and (iii) unbaited control (table S1), each replicated six times and deployed in a vial in the centre of the sticky liner of a Delta trap (Koppert B.V., Berkel en Rodenrijs, The Netherlands). Lures of different treatments were deployed by different persons to avoid cross‐contamination of vials. Traps were mounted atop of 5‐m‐high wooden poles (attached with a rope to a nail at the top of the pole), so that they were raised at least 1 m above the cocoa canopy. The 18 poles with traps were randomly but evenly distributed in the experimental cocoa plot. Captures of C. cramerella moths were recorded weekly and moths (and bycatch) were removed by tweezers. After 4 and 8 weeks, respectively, liners started to lose stickiness and were replaced. Captures were transformed by log10(x + 0.5) following Yamamura et al. (2006), prior to statistical analysis using SPSS 22 (IBM Cooperation, New York, NY). Differences in captures between the two treatments (not considering the unbaited control) within single weeks were assessed by the nonparametric Mann–Whitney U test, whereas differences between treatments in average captures per trap for the whole monitoring period were evaluated by means of the nonparametric Kruskal–Wallis test. A t‐test was used to separately evaluate differences average captures per trap between the two treatments (not considering the unbaited control).

    The second sampling period was between 13 August 2013 and 4 March 2014 (30 weeks) (2.5 months before and during the harvest season of November 2013–March 2014). Treatments were as follows: (i) sample with 88% purity [with 11% (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc, 2% (Z,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, blend 2]; (ii) sample with 89% purity [with 5% (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc, 4% (Z,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, blend 3]; (iii) sample with 58% purity [with 19% (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc, 17% (Z,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, blend 4]; (iv) sample with 50% purity [with 22% (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc, 21% (Z,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, blend 5]; and (v) unbaited control (table S1), each replicated four times. Trap installation and monitoring followed the same procedures described above. Pheromone lures were replaced every 10 weeks (on 22 October and 31 December 2013) to allow evaluating duration of lure effectiveness. Captures in each trap were recorded weekly although data from 28 January 2014 were lost. Significance of differences in average weekly captures between treatments was assessed by analysis of variance (anova), using SPSS 22. Species identification was carried out by comparing the moths caught with illustrations in Wood (1975). The non‐target Gracillarid species captured in CPB pheromone traps have been reported only in Thailand, Vietnam, India and China. In addition, no longan or litchi gardens prevail anywhere near the experimental cocoa plot. Therefore, we assumed that all Conopomorpha‐like moths captured in the traps were C. cramerella.

    Results

    Chemicals

    The stereochemical controlled olefin synthesis allowed introduction of the conjugated double bond with the ideal relative geometry (Anderson and Henrick 1975). With modification of the initial synthetic method applied by Beevor et al. (1986a), aiming at enrichment of 6E‐isomer, a mixture of (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc in ~40 : 55 ratio was obtained via a one‐pot reaction at ~15% (blends 2 and 3) and ~30% (blends 4 and 5) overall yield. However, amounts of other non‐target geometric isomers of pheromone and impurities were also increased, whereby (E,Z,E)‐, (Z,Z,Z)‐ and (Z,E,Z)‐16:OAc were up to 45% (blend 5) based on four identified CPB sex pheromone components [(E,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc, (E,Z,Z)‐16:OH and (E,E,Z)‐16:OH]. Details regarding chemical purities, pheromone component isomeric ratios and percentages of four other geometric isomers are listed in table S1.

    The MS spectra of all hexadecatrienyl acetates produced during the chemicals synthesis were similar, showing five diagnostic ions. Four early‐eluting acetates, (Z,E,E)‐, (Z,E,Z)‐, (E,Z,E)‐ and (E,Z,Z)‐16:OAc, on both capillary columns showed m/z 79 as the base peak, while two later‐eluting acetates, (Z,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc, displayed m/z 107 as the base peak (table S2). As two remaining geometric isomers, (Z,Z,E)‐ and (E,E,E)‐16:OAc, did not elicit any electroantennographic (EAG) responses (Beevor et al. 1986a), they were omitted from the tables. The MS spectra of all corresponding alcohols showed m/z 79 as base peak, but with different retention times (Kovats index) on polar and non‐polar capillary columns. Details regarding Kovats index of each pheromone component and geometric isomer on DB‐Waxetr and DB‐5MS capillary columns, EI‐MS diagnostic ions (m/z) and relative abundances are listed in table S2.

    CPB monitoring by pheromone traps

    In the first sampling period, average CPB captures per trap per week in the control treatment were very low (mean: 0.05; SE: 0.02) and can consequently be considered coincidental. The capture data per trap from 14 sampling weeks showed that blend 1 (93% purity) yielded higher captures than blend 2 (88% purity) during the first 3 weeks (fig. 1). During the remaining 10 weeks, average catch data per trap of blend 2 (88% purity) were higher than those of blend 1 (93% purity). Differences between mean catch numbers of these two treatments within single weeks were significant only in weeks 2, 7 and 9 of the monitoring period. Distribution of average catch data per trap over the 14 weekly counts was not normal for control traps. The nonparametric Kruskal–Wallis test showed that distribution differed significantly (P < 0.05) across treatments. Assuming catch numbers in the control differed significantly from those in treatments, a t‐test was performed on distribution of both transformed and untransformed catch data per trap for the two treatments (i.e. excluding the control). Both transformed and untransformed catch data per trap per week did not significantly (P > 0.05) differ between treatments blend 1 (93% purity) (mean: 2.01 captures per trap per week; SE: 0.26) and blend 2 (88% purity) (mean: 3.61 captures per trap per week; SE: 0.77), although blend 2 caught numerically more.

    During the second sampling period, CPB captures before the first harvest (30/10) gradually declined for all treatments (fig. 2). In subsequent weeks, average captures per trap initially increased after each pheromone replacement, but decreased again after 6–8 weeks due to decreasing lure effectiveness. Average catch per trap per week was just 0.02 for the control traps and between 1.75 (blend 5, purity: 50%) and 2.62 (blend 2, purity: 88%) for treatments. Catch data per trap were transformed by log10(x + 0.5) to improve normality of data distribution. The Kolmogorov–Smirnov test of normality on transformed data revealed that all data distributions were normal for all treatments apart from control. Analysis of variance (anova) on the transformed data and subsequent post hoc multiple comparison tests of Tamahane T2 showed that average weekly captures (2.62 ± 0.38; 1.75 ± 0.28; 2.04 ± 0.44; and 1.77 ± 0.31 for blends 2, 3, 4 and 5, respectively) were significantly (P < 0.001) different from control (0.05 ± 0.02) for all treatments, but not significantly different among the treatments.

    Fortnightly recorded captures per treatment over 2 weeks before each harvest showed a good correlation with the number of harvested pods with C. cramerella infestation symptoms (R² = 0.60). Considering each treatment separately, best correlation is with aggregated catch numbers using blend 4) (table 1).

    Discussion

    Beevor et al. (1986a) identified CPB sex pheromone components in 1986 and synthesized (E,Z,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetate and the corresponding alcohol starting with 1,3‐propanediol in 15 steps at ~20% overall yield. However, (E,E,Z)‐ acetate and its corresponding alcohol were obtained by isomerization of the conjugated double bond of the (E,Z,Z)‐isomer at position 6 from Z to E configuration, which was cumbersome. Later, Yen et al. (1992) separately synthesized (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetates and their corresponding alcohols by two different routes. The (E,Z,Z)‐16:OAc and its corresponding alcohol were prepared by starting with 1‐heptyne and 4‐penten‐1‐ol in 12 steps at ~20% overall yield; (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc and its corresponding alcohol were prepared by starting with 4‐penten‐1‐ol and Z‐4‐decenal in eight steps at ~16% overall yield. This method not only involved two separate synthetic pathways, but also required column chromatographic purification on silica gel impregnated with 20% silver nitrate. Although Pereira and Cabezas (2005) reported a shorter synthetic pathway starting with propargyl bromide and 4‐pentyn‐1‐ol in five steps to produce one pheromone component, (E,Z,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl alcohol, at ~40% overall yield, their method did not provide a solution for preparing the major component (E,E,Z)‐16:OAc, which is needed to economically produce the CPB pheromone full blend for field application. Our modification of the existing synthetic method by Beevor et al. (1986a) allowed targeting hexadecatrienyl acetates with relatively high yields in ideal ratios at one‐pot reaction. However, it also raised the levels of other non‐target geometric isomers and impurities which might have affected lure effectiveness.

    In most cases, the sex pheromone system requires specific ratios of each component for each insect and the high chemical purity is required to elicit the whole range of biological activities as natural pheromones for the insect sex pheromone preparation. Some interspecific cross‐attraction (Gemeno et al. 2000), synergistic (Witzgall et al. 1996) and strong antagonistic (Zhang et al. 2005) effects from pheromone geometric isomers or analogs used by related species have been demonstrated in the pheromone‐mediated communication of insects. Beevor et al. (1986a) reported that besides the two pheromone components, (E,Z,Z)‐ and (E,E,Z)‐4,6,10‐hexadecatrienyl acetates, four other geometric isomers, (E,Z,E)‐, (Z,Z,Z)‐, (Z,E,Z)‐ and (Z,E,E)‐16:OAc, also elicited strong EAG responses. Furthermore, addition of (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc in increasing amounts to the pheromone blend progressively decreased C. cramerella male attraction (Beevor et al. 1986a). In our synthetic pheromone mixtures, amounts of (E,Z,E)‐ and (Z,Z,Z)‐16:OAc increased to 22% and 21%, respectively (based on four identified pheromone components, table S1). However, no significant difference was observed on C. cramerella male trap capture between blend 1 [93% purity, 3% (E,Z,E)‐ and 2% (Z,Z,Z)‐isomers] and blend 2 [88% purity, 11% (E,Z,E)‐ and 2% (Z,Z,Z)‐ isomers] in our first field test (fig. 1). The same result was obtained from our second field test. Male C. cramerella captures were not significantly different from traps baited with blends 2–5, in which chemical purities varied from 50% to 89%; (E,Z,E)‐isomer varied from 5% to 22% and (Z,Z,Z)‐isomers varied from 2% to 21% (fig. 2). Interestingly, blend 2 caught numerically more CPB than blend 1, indicating that (E,Z,E)‐16:OAc in the pheromone blend did not inhibit male attraction in the field.

    Our results also confirm previous findings of Zhang et al. (2008) from earlier experiments in Sabah, Malaysia. CPB pheromone lure longevity analysis using GC‐MS by the latter authors revealed that the decline of pheromone concentration over time was best expressed by Y = 101.49 e−0.4694t (t in months). This means that after 1 and 2 months, pheromone concentration is reduced to around 62% and 40%, respectively, of the initial level (in their case: 101.49 μg/lure). In our experiments, it was found that all pheromone lures tested (all with a concentration of 100 μg per vial at the beginning of the experiments) were effective for 6–8 weeks, after which effectiveness diminished rapidly. These findings indicate that pheromone concentrations of below 40 μg per vial will be ineffective in catching significant numbers of CPB. If CPB pheromone lures are used for pest monitoring or control purposes, they should be replaced with a frequency of at least 2 months. The decline of pheromone concentration and reduction of pheromone lure longevity over time may also contribute the isomerization, oxidation and decomposition of the active pheromone components under the conditions of high temperature, humidity and rainfall in the cocoa plantation in Pahang, Malaysia. Although the first sampling period coincided with the end of the harvest season of September 2012–May 2013, no data on pods with C. cramerella symptoms were collected during that period. During the second period, captures of male C. cramerella were positively correlated (R² was between 0.13 and 0.72) with numbers of C. cramerella‐affected cocoa pods (table 1). This shows that some of the pheromone lures used were well suited for monitoring cocoa pod damage from CPB. Zhang et al. (2008) showed no significant differences occur in mean trap catches between pheromone blends produced by NRI (UK) and Pest Control (India); the same pheromone blends were used in the present experiments. We were able, however, to determine the purities and other geometric isomers from different pheromone blends and conclude that occurrence of other geometric isomers, (E,Z,E)‐, (Z,Z,Z)‐, (Z,E,Z)‐, (Z,E,E)‐16:OAc, up to 47 per cent, do not significantly affect average C. cramerella catch data per trap per week. Neither synergistic nor antagonistic effect was observed. We therefore conclude that other geometric isomers in sex pheromone blends do not affect attractancy of male C. cramerella in cocoa fields. This is important because higher purity levels correspond with higher production costs of CPB pheromone lures. Mumford and Beevor (1987) claimed that a decrease of at least 5% of pod loss caused by to C. cramerella would make mass trapping by means of CPB pheromone lures cost effective. However, given these claims are more than 25 years old and given the constantly evolving costs of synthesis of organic molecules and cocoa production costs and prices, such financial analysis should be updated. Tay and Sim (1989) as well as Beevor et al. (1993) mentioned mating disruption (only using lures without traps) as a possibly cost‐effective C. cramerella control strategy by means of pheromones, but did not provide any financial analysis.

    Zhang et al. (2008) report between two and eight male captures per trap per week, depending on cocoa plot treatments (egg parasitoid & pod sleeving, pod sleeving, pesticides and agronomic practices). The significant numbers of male C. cramerella captured in traps baited with the pheromone lures used in our experiments might give ground to exploring more innovative C. cramerella control measures, such as an attract and kill strategy (Charmillot et al. 2000; Krupke et al. 2002; Mansour 2010; Witzgall et al. 2010). The latter involves attracting male C. cramerella moths by pheromone lures and subsequently killing them, by means of traps immersed in pesticides such as cypermethrin or deltamethrin. It was shown that an attract and kill strategy uses much less pheromone than mating disruption and mass trapping (Charmillot et al. 2000). In such alternative C. cramerella control strategies that use pheromones, a thorough financial analysis should be performed, including sensitivity analysis for the very volatile cocoa market prices and varying pheromone lure production costs. Lower costs for effective pheromone lures with acceptable purity levels, in combination with the lower cost of attract and kill technology in comparison with other pheromone‐based pest management methods, might prove to be an appropriate cost‐effective approach for exploration of a combination of agronomic, chemical and behaviourally based management option for C. cramerella in cocoa production in South–East Asia.

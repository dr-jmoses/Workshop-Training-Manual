    Abstract

    Many studies of postdispersal seed fate use seed removal as an index of seed predation. However, following primary seed dispersal, some seeds are transported intact by ants, dung beetles, scatter‐hoarding animals, or abiotic processes to new microsites (secondary dispersal) where germination is possible. Despite a growing realization that secondary seed dispersal can play an important role in plant recruitment, many researchers continue to use seed removal as a proxy for seed predation and are focused too intently on only the initial step of seed fate. We describe, using examples from the recent literature, how the results of some seed removal studies may have been misinterpreted, present plausible, alternative explanations for the fate of seeds in those studies, and discuss the importance of detailed studies of seed fates. Following the fates of seeds can be difficult, but such studies contribute much more to our understanding of seed dynamics and plant fitness.

    Introduction

    Secondary seed dispersal, any significant movement of viable seeds following initial (primary) dispersal, has emerged as an important process in the reproductive cycle of some plants (Chambers and MacMahon 1994, Vander Wall and Longland 2004). Ants, rodents, birds, dung beetles, and abiotic factors such as wind and water can move seeds after primary dispersal. Only since the 1980s have we appreciated the importance of secondary seed dispersal in the demography of plants. The view that has emerged is that seed dispersal is often a multistep process that often incorporates two or more different means of seed movement. Consequently, it is inappropriate to assume, as many studies of seed fate have done, that removed seeds have been consumed by seed predators (e.g., Heithaus 1981, Kollmann et al. 1998, Schreiner et al. 2000, Rey et al. 2002, Moles et al. 2003).

    A common methodology in the study of seed fates is to place a population of seeds on the ground, either singly or in clumps, and monitor their removal over time. Removal studies can provide some useful information on seed dynamics, but the results of these studies are often misinterpreted. In an incomplete survey of the recent literature, we identified 115 recent papers that assume, usually with little supporting evidence, that removed seed had been consumed and that use the rate of seed removal as a proxy for a measure of seed predation (K. M. Kuhn, unpublished data). In effect, seed fate is being defined rather than studied (Levey and Byrne 1993). To their credit, some seed removal studies do mention the possibility of secondary dispersal (e.g., Hulme and Borelli 1999, Moles et al. 2003), but even these suggest that secondary dispersal is relatively unlikely, and conclude that seed removal is essentially seed predation. However, as we learn more about the complexities of seed fates, we are beginning to realize that, for many plant species, survival following seed removal is a frequent occurrence. We argue that, given the growing appreciation of the importance of secondary seed dispersal, we need to be more careful before making conclusions concerning seed fates.

    Potential Fates of Removed Seeds

    Seed consumption is a common fate of dispersed seeds found by animals. Ants, beetles, rodents, birds, microbes, and a host of other organisms are proficient at finding and consuming seeds. However, a number of animal groups not only move seeds without killing them, but also transport them to microsites that favor seedling establishment. Secondary dispersal by ants is well known and many plants produce seeds with elaiosomes, food bodies attractive to ants, to facilitate this means of dispersal (e.g., Berg 1975). Ant dispersal of seeds is sometimes preceded by ballistic dispersal, which serves to scatter seeds and to reduce seed predation by other types of animals around the parent (e.g., Ohkawara and Higashi 1994). Elaiosome‐bearing seeds can also be dispersed through the feces of birds. The seed and elaiosome usually survive the short trip through the bird's gut, and then ants remove seeds from feces and carry them back to their nests. For example, ants (Aphaenogaster swammerdami) secondarily dispersed 45% of the seeds of a Malagasy tree (Commiphora guillaumini) initially dispersed in the feces of the Lesser Vasa Parrot (Coracopsis nigra) (Bohning‐Gaese et al. 1999). However, many species of ant‐dispersed seeds lack elaiosomes. Ants remove seeds from the feces or regurgitate of birds and feed on remnants of the fruit pulp (Pizo and Oliveira 1998, Passos and Oliveira 2002) or eat some of the seeds and store the rest. Levey and Byrne (1993) reported how leaf‐litter ants (Pheidole spp.) gathered 81% of small seeds from bird feces, cached ∼8–33% in their twig nests, and deposited an additional 6% in refuse piles. Many of these seeds germinated directly from cache sites or refuse piles. Levey and Byrne (1993) found 30 species of intact seeds in Pheidole nebulosa caches and 19 species in refuse piles. None of these seeds had any apparent adaptations for ant dispersal (e.g., they lacked elaiosomes).

    In recent years, a number of studies have focused on the importance of dung beetles (Scarabaeine) in secondary seed dispersal (Estrada and Coates‐Estrada 1991, Andresen 2002). The dung of herbivores and frugivores can contain many seeds. Dung beetles quickly locate fresh dung deposits, transport dung short distances, and bury it. Some of the dung is buried at depths that favor seed germination and seedling emergence. Estrada and Coates‐Estrada (1991) found that in protected plots, 44% of buried seeds germinated and established seedlings, whereas in unprotected plots (open to foraging rodents that acted as seed predators or secondary dispersers of seeds) 14% of seed established seedlings. Because dung beetles are widely distributed throughout temperate and tropical regions, it is likely that this mode of secondary dispersal will eventually prove to be very widespread and important.

    Secondary dispersal of seeds and nuts by food‐storing rodents and corvids is a well‐studied phenomenon. Many of the plants that are dispersed in this way drop seeds or nuts at maturity to facilitate seed harvest by ground‐foraging animals (Forget 1996, Peres and Baider 1997, Yasuda et al. 2000, Vander Wall 2001, Jansen and den Ouden 2004). Although many of these seeds and nuts are eaten, a large percentage is scatter‐hoarded. For example, rodents cached at least 56–77% of Jeffrey pine (Pinus jeffreyi) seeds that were initially arranged around three trees in patterns that simulated primary dispersal by wind (Vander Wall 2002). Seed‐caching rodents also remove seeds from the feces of large mammals and birds (Forget and Milleron 1991, Wenny 1999, Feer and Forget 2002). This plant–animal interaction is geographically widespread, but secondary dispersal of seeds by food‐caching animals is not well documented in Australia and Africa. However, several species of marsupial and placental mammals have been found to be avid scatter‐hoarders and effective dispersers of plant seeds in Australia (Thiemer 2001, Dennis 2003), and scatter‐hoarding of seeds may also prove to be an important means of seed dispersal in south Africa (e.g., Midgley et al. 2002). More research is likely to reveal that scatter‐hoarding is a widespread behavior and an important means of seed dispersal in many temperate and tropical habitats.

    Abiotic processes also redistribute seeds on the surface of the ground or transport seeds into the soil seed bank. Wind moves seeds across the ground or snow, and rain splash and sheet flow of water across the ground are known to move seeds (Matlack 1989, Johnson and Fryer 1992, Griffith and Forseth 2002).

    Interpretation of Seed Removal Studies

    In a recent study of seed removal in which seed predation was inferred, Moles et al. (2003) examined the rate of postdispersal seed predation of 81 seed species in three different ecosystems in New South Wales, Australia, by monitoring seeds placed along seed removal transects. We examined the list of species used in the Moles et al. (2003) study (Ecological Archives E084‐083‐A5–A7) and identified eight species of known myrmecochores, including Boronia ledifolia and Phebalium squamulosum (Rutaceae), Dodonea triquetra (Sapindaceae), and Pomaderris ferruginea (Rhamnaceae) at the Sturt National Park site (Berg 1975, Rice and Westoby 1981, Auld 2001); Dissocarpus biflorus, Sclerolaena convexula, and Sclerolaena diacantha (Chenopodiaceae) at Ku‐ring‐gai Chase National Park (Davidson and Morton 1981); and Monotoca elliptica (Epacridaceae) at Kosciuszko National Park (Rice and Westoby 1981). For these eight species (and perhaps others), ants may have removed seeds and taken them to their nests. This is a critically important distinction because for myrmecochores, seed removal by ants is an essential step toward successful reproduction. Moles et al. (2003) also compiled data from 46 studies of seed removal that included 280 plant species (Ecological Archives E084–083‐A9). Most of these studies equated seed removal to seed predation without supporting evidence of actual seed fate. Some of these studies (e.g., Ohkawara and Higashi 1994, Forget 1996, Peres and Baider 1997) were actually detailed investigations of secondary seed dispersal that were misinterpreted as seed predation by Moles et al. (2003). For example, Moles et al. (2003) listed Carapa procera seed removal as predation despite the fact that Forget (1996) found that 25–48% of seed removed by rodents were scatter‐hoarded and 9–43% were eaten.

    Blate et al. (1998) monitored the removal of 40 species of seeds in Kalimantan, Indonesia. Among these species was Lithocarpus sp. (Fagaceae), which produces large (8.1‐g) acorns. Lithocarpus, like most other members of this large family, is known to be dispersed by seed‐caching rodents and corvids (Vander Wall 2001). Mature acorns fall to the ground and removal is an essential step in the dispersal process; acorns that are not quickly removed die or are eaten by animals that offer no prospect of dispersal. In Kalimantan (island of Borneo), a number of food‐caching rodents, such as Leopoldamys, Maxomys, Niviventer, and Lariscus (Yasuda et al. 2000, Zhang et al. 2004), could have removed and buried the nuts. The other 39 plant species in the sample had fleshy fruits and seeds dispersed by frugivorous birds. Clear evidence of consumption was noted in only 26% of removal events (mean for all species). Seed mass ranged from 0.1 g to 11.6 g. The smaller seeds might be attractive to ants or moved by dung beetles. Scatter‐hoarding by rodents and corvids in this part of the world is not well studied, but we suspect that many of the medium‐to‐large seeds would be attractive to scatter‐hoarding animals that remove the seeds from feces (Vander Wall and Longland 2004). For example, Wenny (1999) found that scatter‐hoarding rodents secondarily dispersed Guarea glabra and Guarea kunthiana seeds in Costa Rica. The seeds are consumed by birds (primary dispersers) and regurgitated. Agoutis (Dasyprocta punctata) and other rodents gathered the seeds and cached 45.6% and 26.6%, respectively. Removal of Brazil nuts (Bertholletia excelsa, Lecythidaceae) is largely by scatter‐hoarding agoutis (Dasyprocta) (Peres and Baider 1997), and this species interaction is considered essential for the long‐term maintenance of these important trees in neotropical rain forests (Peres et al. 2003). These three plant species were included in the compilation of Moles et al. (2003) as examples of seed predation.

    Boman and Casper (1995) equated postdispersal seed removal to predation in their study of six plant species in hardwood forests of eastern Pennsylvania, USA. Among the species they studied were beech (Fagus grandifolia) and oaks (Quercus sp.), plants known to be dispersed by nut‐caching jays and rodents. Boman and Casper (1995) also included flowering dogwood (Cornus florida), a seed dispersed by frugivorous birds, in their sample. S. B. Vander Wall, K. M. Kuhn, and J. R. Gworek (unpublished data) found that rodents removed the seeds of Cornus stolonifera from bird feces and cached them. A similar process may be occurring in eastern hardwood forests.

    McCarthy (1994) found that 85–100% of mockernut hickory (Carya tomentosa, Juglandaceae) nuts (n = 300) cached under leaf litter were removed within 5 days, and he attributed this to gray squirrels (Sciurus carolinensis). He concluded that postdispersal seed predation was a major force limiting recruitment, although he did not report any evidence that the nuts had actually been eaten. However, squirrels and other rodents routinely recache items that they have pilfered, so it was more likely that nuts had simply been redistributed rather than consumed. Hau (1997) monitored the removal of Quercus, Lithocarpus, and Castanopsis (Fagaceae) nuts on degraded hillsides in Hong Kong, China, by rodents (very likely Niviventer and Rattus) and considered removal to be seed predation. However, Zhang et al. (2004) report that Niviventer and Rattus are important agents of nut dispersal at several sites in China.

    Several authors have studied the removal of Prunus stones (Rosaceae) (including P. annularis, P. virginiana, P. avium, P. spinosa, and P. tumeriana) by rodents (Webb and Willson 1985, Osunkoya 1994, Holl and Lulow 1997, Kollmann et al. 1998). Although some authors acknowledged that rodents might have cached removed stones, they assumed that most seeds had probably been eaten, despite the fact that only ∼5% of the seed stations had evidence of on‐site seed consumption. However, Zhang and Wang (2001) found that seed‐storing rodents dispersed the stones of Prunus armeniaca, and M. J. Beck and S. B. Vander Wall (unpublished data) found that rodents routinely scatter‐hoarded the stones of Prunus virginiana, P. andersonii, P. emarginata, and P. fasciculata, many of which successfully germinated and established.

    Ohkawara and Higashi (1994) described how two species of Viola are dispersed by a combination of ballistic dispersal followed by myrmecochory. Ants gather the elaiosome‐bearing seeds, carry them to their nests, consume the elaiosome, and dump the seeds in a refuse pile where many germinate and establish plants. Moles et al. (2003) attributed this ant removal to seed predation. Myster (1997) observed the removal of Miconia racemosa seeds by Pheidole ants at La Selva Biological Station in Costa Rica and attributed this removal to seed predators without confirming seed fates. However, Levey and Byrne (1993) found that removed Miconia nervosa and Miconia centrodesma seeds at La Selva were being dispersed by Pheidole ants.

    The potential for secondary seed dispersal by dung beetles is often underestimated in studies of seed removal. Those studying seed removal often gather seeds by hand, remove structures that would normally be lost during seed dispersal (e.g., fruit pulp), and set them out along transects. However, if seeds normally would be incorporated into bird feces or mammal dung during dispersal (e.g., Janzen 1982), the atypical treatment that they receive from experimenters would lower the seeds' probability of removal by dung beetles. For example, seeds in dung might be removed quickly by dispersers, whereas similar clean seeds would be ignored by dung beetles and exposed to increased risk from seed predators. An experiment with cleaned seeds might lead one to conclude, incorrectly, that potential secondary dispersers are ineffective at moving seeds while overestimating the rate of predation. Seed removal is context dependent, and to fairly evaluate hypotheses of postdispersal seed predation vs. secondary dispersal, one must present seeds in a manner that simulates natural processes.

    An Alternative Approach

    Postdispersal seed removal can be seed predation, but it can also represent the next step in a multistep seed dispersal process. The truth is that, for most species, we simply do not know what proportions of secondarily dispersed seeds are eaten, survive intact, or suffer other fates. Chambers and MacMahon (1994) reviewed the literature on seed fates and concluded that the latter phases of seed dispersal are likely to have at least as great an impact on the patterning of plants in communities as the first phase. Vander Wall and Longland (2004) examined the benefits of diplochory (two‐phase seed dispersal in which each phase is carried out by a different mechanism) in five dispersal syndromes and concluded that the primary benefits of phase one dispersal are usually predator escape and colonization of new patches, whereas the primary benefits of phase two dispersal are usually predator escape and directed dispersal. Through directed dispersal, plants attempt to deliver seeds to microsites where seedling establishment is likely to be successful. These reviews suggest that secondary dispersal provides important benefits to plants, and if we aim to understand the adaptive value of seed dispersal, the fate of seeds after initial dispersal cannot be ignored.

    We find the widespread assumption that removed seeds have been consumed by seed predators to be unwarranted for two reasons. First, such incorrect assumptions result in the accumulation of unreliable knowledge (Romesburg 1981). A number of studies (e.g., Whelan et al. 1991, Hulme 1997, Kollmann et al. 1998, Hulme and Hunt 1999, Rey et al. 2002, Moles et al. 2003) have justified their assumption that removal equals predation because others have done so in the past. Some studies (e.g., Schupp 1990, Holl and Lulow 1997, Pizo 1997, Alcantara et al. 2000, Schreiner et al. 2000, Obeso and Fernandez‐Calvo 2002) report that the levels of “predation” that they have observed are comparable to those reported by others and then cite studies that have made the same assumption that seed removal equals predation. Unfortunately, much of the research on postdispersal seed predation needs to be reassessed and, when found wanting, repeated, using methods that actually track the fate of seeds. Second, assuming that removed seeds are dead causes researchers to miss potentially important events in the seed dispersal process. Some authors seem to assume that even though not all removed seeds are consumed, seed removal is strongly correlated with seed predation and therefore is a useful estimate of seed predation. This may often be true, but the presumption of mortality obscures important events of seed escape and dispersal, whether common or rare. Even if death is the most common consequence of seed removal, the few seeds that survive are the ones that drive plant recruitment (Levey and Byrne 1993). If we hope to understand the details of plant recruitment, we need to determine how often seeds escape predation, how this occurs, and what are the fates of these seeds. Ignorance of the details of postdispersal seed fates prevents a clear understanding of the factors determining plant establishment and the nature of seed–disperser coevolution. We need to refocus our efforts from monitoring seed removal to following removed seeds to their ultimate demise or successful germination and establishment. The many alternative pathways that seeds follow between initial dispersal and their ultimate fate are much more difficult to study than simple seed removal, but such studies contribute much more to our understanding of seed dynamics and plant fitness. To understand how natural selection shapes seed and fruit traits such as size, morphology, chemical composition, and germination schedules, we need to understand better why some seeds fail while others succeed.

    Over the past 15 years, a number of techniques have been developed for tracking seeds reliably. Many of these have been described and evaluated by Forget and Wenny (2004). Large seeds and nuts can be followed using various line and tag methods (e.g., Forget and Milleron 1991), whereas small seeds can be tracked using isotopes and fluorescent dyes (e.g., Longland and Clements 1995, Longland et al. 2001). These methods can be used in conjunction with remote cameras to confirm the identity of animals removing seeds (Yasuda et al. 2000, Jansen and Den Ouden 2004). Following seeds is a challenging, but not an intractable, problem, but despite all of the difficulties, it is the only sure way to gain reliable knowledge of seed fates. Meanwhile, and in the absence of such detailed studies of seed fate, it would be appropriate to label postdispersal seed removal as just that, and to take greater care in describing the consequences of such removal.

    Interplay of succession and seasonality reflects resource utilization in an ephemeral habitat

    Author links open overlay panelFrantisek Xaver JiriSladecekaJanHrcekabPetrKlimesabMartinKonvickaab

    Abstract

    Ephemeral habitats such as dung pats provide an abundance of resource for their inhabitants. Despite the potentially severe competition, they host a very diverse array of species. The species coexistence is probably facilitated primarily by separation in time. Here we present a first complex study of such temporal segregation, along successional and seasonal gradients, focused on a complete community of coprophilous beetles from Central Europe. Specifically, both coprophagous and predatory species were studied. In addition to the successional age of dung pats we measured the decline of dung moisture. We captured 39, 627 beetles belonging to 85 species using artificially created dung pats in a sampling design robust against short-term variation in weather conditions. The optima of late successional species were shifted toward earlier days of succession and the whole course of succession took fewer days when compared to studies from Northern Europe (9 versus 14 days). This indicates constancy of the species successional sequence, but temporal plasticity when later successional species reach their optima. Our results suggest that the coprophagous species are separated successionally according to the development time their larvae spent in the original dung pat. The later in the succession, the more time the larvae live freely in the dung pat. The same phenomenon applies for the seasonal separation, where the latter are species or guild in succession the more effectively they are separated seasonally. The predatory species are separated both seasonally and successionally by their size with smaller predators in latter phases in both temporal aspects.

    Highlights

    ► Succession and seasonality of Central European coprophilous beetles were studied. ► The succession is more rapid than in other ephemeral habitats and cooler regions. ► The temporal segregation of coprophilous beetles reflects their resource utilization. ► The seasonality is more pronounced in late successional guilds and species. ► Species successional sequence is likely to be fixed in species coevolution.

    1. Introduction

    Ephemeral habitats such as dung pats, carrion, rotten fruit and the fruiting bodies of Macromycetes are characterized by high nutritional value, patchy distribution in space and time and often strong competition of inhabiting species (Finn, 2001). Despite the strong competition, they are colonized by very diverse arrays of species, e.g. up to 100 coprophilous beetle species inhabiting one pasture in Finland (Hanski and Koskela, 1977). The coexistence of such diverse communities is probably driven primarily by species separation in time, either by seasonality or the successional segregation of particular species.

    Among ephemeral habitats, the dung and carrion communities are the most studied, the latter mainly for the purpose of the forensic entomology (Lefebvre and Gaudry, 2009). In both those habitats, the Coleoptera and Diptera adults and larvae are considered to play a major role in their utilization. As the Diptera have been used as the focal taxon for determining the post-mortem index in carcasses (Matuszewski et al., 2010), only very little is known for the coprophilous fly species (Hammer, 1941). In contrast, coprophilous beetle communities have been extensively studied as a model community inhabiting ephemeral habitats (Finn and Gittings, 2003).

    In this study, we focus solely on the Coleoptera species utilizing dung as an example of an ephemeral habitat. The dung microhabitat itself contains large coarse cellulose and lignin particles utilized primarily by coleopteran larvae and also nitrogen rich particles like bacteria and dead epithelial cells, utilized primarily by specialized adult coleopteran dung feeders and dung fly larvae (Hata and Edmonds, 1983; Holter, 2004; Holter and Scholtz, 2007). The beetle communities colonizing this microhabitat are some of the most complex studied so far. The most extensive work was carried out in cooler regions such as Northern Europe (e.g. Desière, 1987; Gittings and Giller, 1998; Hanski and Koskela, 1977; Koskela and Hanski, 1977; Lee and Wall, 2006), montane areas of the European Mediterranean (Menendez and Gutierrez, 1999; Palestrini et al., 1995) and in Temperate North America (Kessler and Balsbaug, 1972; Mohr, 1943; Wingo et al., 1974). From these studies, the main conclusions regarding coprophagous species (i.e. species feeding on dung in at least one of its life stages) were: i) Beetles coprophagous in all life stages such as Aphodius spp. were well separated both seasonally and along the successional gradient due to differences in their oviposition behavior; ii) Hydrophilidae, whose adults are specialized dung feeders and whose larvae are predatory, showed no distinct seasonal segregation in most of the studies (but see Desière, 1983; De Graef and Desière, 1984) and most of them also formed an early-successional group with no distinct segregation among individual species. However, the patterns of predatory species in droppings had not yet been interpreted and data for dung communities from warmer southerly regions such as Central Europe, where the beetle communities are ecologically and taxonomically more diverse, are scarce. More comparable studies are needed to understand the mechanisms of species coexistence and resource utilization in dung microhabitats.

    In other ephemeral habitats, the pattern of beetle succession in carrion seems to be quite constant with necrophagous-predatory Silphidae, as early successional, and Dermestidae and Trogidae, both of which are specialized on dry carrion, as late successional (e.g. Grassberger and Frank, 2004; Horenstein and Linhares, 2011; Kocarek, 2003). In fruit falls, the first colonizers are usually the most specialized insects (e.g. Curculionidae beetles and Pyralidae moths) followed by more generalist Tenebrionidae and other insect groups (Lukasik and Johnson, 2007). In fungal bodies, rather conflicting results were found, showing a complete temporal differentiation between beetle species (Guevara et al., 2000) or no temporal effect on their whole insect communities at all (Yamashita and Hijii, 2007), perhaps due to better predictability of fungal fruiting bodies occurrence compared with other ephemeral habitats.

    Here we present the first complex quantitative study of both the successional and seasonal separation in a community of Central European coprophilous beetles that colonize cow-dung, covering all seasonal aspects of their population dynamics and targeting all beetle families colonizing the dung microhabitat. The coprophilous community in Central Europe is ecologically more diverse than in Northern Europe, with a higher proportion of dung relocating species. Also, the active season for beetles is longer in Central Europe. We asked three main questions: 1) How do the temporal, predominantly successional, dynamics of Central European communities differ from those of more northerly regions? 2) Is there any general ecological trait shaping the succession of coprophilous beetles? 3) How are the species and ecological guilds affected by both temporal aspects (i.e. successional versus seasonal trends in occurrence)?

    2. Material and methods

    2.1. Study site

    The study was carried out on a 23 ha pasture, situated 10 km west of Ceske Budejovice, Czech Republic (48°59′2.4″N, 14°24′34.957″E). This pasture hosts a permanent herd of 30 adult cows and has been continuously grazed for decades. It is situated at 380 a. s. l., in a region with a mean annual temperature of 8.1 °C, mean annual precipitation of 620 mm, and vegetation season spanning from March to October.

    2.2. Sampling design

    Five sampling cycles, covering the main active seasons for coprophilous beetles, particularly the Aphodius dung beetles (cf. Hanski, 1986; Holter, 1982), were carried out between April and October in 2009 (Sampling cycle one: 11 April – 29 April, two: 17 May – 4 June, three: 4 July – 22 July, four: 15 August – 2 September, five: 23 September – 11 October). The beetles were sampled from artificially created cow dung pats of 1.5 l volume (approximately 2 kg fresh weight). Fresh dung, no more than 3 h old, was collected from a barn with permanently stalled cows, assuring that it was not colonized yet. The dung was thoroughly mixed and homogenized before exposition.

    For each sampling cycle, the artificial dung pats were placed in five lines, 5 m apart from each other. Those lines were laid on five successive days to minimize short-time effects of weather variability on beetle activity. Each line contained nine pats, placed in 5 m intervals, each of them to be exposed for 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 9, 11 or 14 days. Dung pats exposition periods were randomized within the lines to minimize spatial variation. The sampling thus comprised 45 dung pats per seasonal sampling cycle and 225 dung pats in total.

    The beetles were extracted by floating the dung pat and a small portion of underlying soil in a bucket of water. The floated substrate was then hand sorted to assure that all beetles were sampled. The beetles were preserved in 96% ethanol and taken to the laboratory for identification using the following keys: Balthasar (1963), Hansen (1987), Freude et al. (1964, 1974), Mazur (1973) and Hurka (1996).

    The decline of dung moisture during succession was used as an additional environmental predictor. To measure the decline, we used specific mass of control dung pats of 1.5 l volume (the same size as the dung pats for beetle sampling). The line of control dung pats was set together with the first line of dung pats for the beetle sampling, as a parallel line 5 m apart from the sampling lines. At each successional point (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 9, 11, 14 days), a randomly chosen dung pat from the control line was weighed and the weight was divided by the initial volume of the dung pat (1.5 l).

    2.3. Beetle guilds

    We classified all beetle species to the following ecological guilds within the functional groups of coprophages and predators.

    I.

    The coprophages were classified into three guilds by larval and adult ecology: i) relocators, whose larva does not reach the original dung pat at any stage of its development, hence they “relocate” the larvae from the dung pats. Adults either build an underground nest provided by dung relocated from the original dung pat (Doube, 1991; Rojewski, 1983; Vitner, 1998) or oviposit in the soil away from the dung pat. Despite the larvae of these soil ovipositing species can probably occur in dung pats (Landin, 1961), the larvae of these species are considered generally saprophagous, while they can be reared without dung, being probably not exclusively restricted to the dung pats (Finn and Gittings, 2003; Gittings and Giller, 1997; Hanski and Cambefort, 1991). Adult relocators are all specialized dung feeders with modified mouthparts for microphagy (Holter, 2000; Holter and Scholtz, 2007; Holter et al., 2002); ii) dwellers, the larva lives in the dung pat (dung ovipositing) or reaches it at least at the last stage of its development (soil ovipositing) (Finn and Gittings, 2003; Gittings and Giller, 1997; Vitner, 1998). Adults are either specialized dung feeders with modified mouthparts (Holter, 2000) or more general saprophages utilizing other decaying matter (Eghtedar, 1970; Hammond, 1976); iii) hydrophilids, the larva is predatory (Gawaad et al., 1976; Sowig, 1997). Adults oviposit directly into the dung pat, those of genus Sphaeridium are known to possess modified mouthparts for microphagy (Holter, 2004), and the remaining genera are considered habitat generalists (Hanski and Koskela, 1978).

    II.

    Three predatory guilds were defined according to their body size: (i) large predators, with body length over 1 cm, bulky body and large head, (ii) small predators, with body size below 0.5 cm and (ii) medium predators (species in-between the former two guilds)

    2.4. Analyses

    We used Canonical correspondence analysis (CCA), computed in CANOCO for Windows 4.5. (Ter Braak and Smilauer, 2002) to analyze the successional pattern of the coprophilous beetles. CCA was chosen as an ordination technique suitable for our datasets with a unimodal distribution of species across the studied gradient (Leps and Smilauer, 2003). The analyses were computed separately for coprophages and predators, with Day (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 9, 11 and 14) and dung Moisture (weigh of dung pat at particular successional stage divided by its original volume) as environmental predictors. We used the log (x + 1) transformation of species counts, and the down-weighting of rare species option, and excluded species with <5 individuals before conducting the analyses. To test the statistical significance of environmental predictors, we used the Monte Carlo permutation test (999 permutations, reduced model) with the five lines of dung pats in each sampling cycle treated as whole plots and the individual lines as split plots (Leps and Smilauer, 2003). Under this design, the samples were permutated along the successional age within each line. A fictional species W with an abundance of 1 was added to one empty sample to balance the design when analyzing the successional pattern for predatory species.

    The seasonal segregation was investigated for the individual species using the generalized linear models in CanoDraw for Windows 4.0. A linear and quadratic model with Poisson's distribution of errors was fitted for each guild (or species). The final model, chosen from the significant models by the AIC statistics, enables us to asses: 1) whether a species responded significantly to season, 2) when the species had its peak abundance and 3) whether there was a seasonal displacement between species within guilds.

    The same analytical procedure for the seasonal segregation of individual species was applied to entire guilds both for successional and seasonal trends to facilitate comparison of these temporal aspects.

    3. Results

    3.1. Sampling results

    A total of 39,627 adult dung-inhabiting beetles, representing 85 species (Table S.1) were sampled. The most abundant taxonomic group were Hydrophilidae (21,350 individuals, 15 species), followed by Staphylinidae (10,035 indiv., 39 spp.), dung beetles (Scarabaeoidea) (8084 indiv., 22 spp.), Histeridae (114 indiv., 4 spp.) and Carabidae (44 indiv., 5 spp.).

    Exclusion of 16 species with <5 individuals reduced the dataset for analyses to 39,592 individuals (99.9%) and 68 species (80%). Anoplotrupes stercorosus (5 individuals), being a forest species, was further excluded because of its random appearance at the study site. As some individuals of genus Atheta (Staphylinidae: small predators guild, 45 indiv.) were not reliably identified to the species level, they were merged into a compound “species” Athetasp for analyses. After these corrections, the coprophages guilds contained the following numbers of individuals: 4116 relocators (9 species), 7537 dwellers (15 species), 21,349 hydrophilids (14 species), 376 large predators (7 species), 4456 medium predators (18 species) and 1758 (including the unidentified Atheta sp.) small predators (6 species).

    3.2. Trends in abundance and number of species

    The highest number of individuals and species was obtained from dung pats on day 1 (mean number of individuals per dung pat: 316.76 ± 1.82 SE; mean number of species per dung pat: 33.72 ± 0.25 SE) (Fig. 1). In general, these numbers initially decreased quickly until day 4, especially in number of recorded individuals (mean individuals/pat: 208.72 ± 1.65 SE; mean species/pat: 33.48 ± 0.28 SE), and then less significantly, until day 14 (mean individuals/pat: 54.4 ± 1.30 SE; mean species/pat: 18.12 ± 0.48 SE). However, exceptions to this pattern were observed from late autumn season (sampling cycle 5) for individuals (Fig. 1a), and early spring and late autumn (sampling cycles 1, 5) for species (Fig. 1b), where relatively higher numbers were obtained from intermediate days of the succession (days 3–7).

    3.3. Species successional segregation

    The composition of coprophagous beetle community was significantly affected by successional stage and moisture level (F = 31.773, P < 0.001, the first two canonical axes together explained 22.3% of variability in the species data) with two successional stages observed (days 1–4, days 5–14) (Fig. 2a). Members of the relocators guild (Onthophagus spp. Geotrupes spiniger, Aphodius erraticus) prevailed among the earliest species to emerge, with the exception of one hydrophilid species (Cercyon melanocephalus). These relocators were followed by hydrophilids and some dwellers (Aphodius luridus, Aphodius ater. A. rufus, Anotylus rugosus). The later phase of succession was dominated by dwellers co-occurring with some hydrophilids (Cercyon lateralis, Cryptopleurum spp. Megasternum concinuum). Predators were also significantly affected by succession time and moisture level, however, they were almost evenly distributed along the successional gradient, with a distinct pattern of small predators having their optima in the later phases of succession (F = 12.075, P < 0.001, the first two canonical axes together explain 9.9% of variability in species data) (Fig. 2b).

    Dung moisture content was generally positively correlated with beetles from the early successional stages (day 1–4) for both functional groups (Fig. 2). For instance, relocators Onthophagus spp., Aphodius prodromus preferred the high levels of dung moisture.

    3.4. Species seasonal separation

    Significant seasonal patterns reflected by the shapes of fitted general models were found for 57 out of 68 species analyzed (see Table S.1 for individual species significance).

    Four patterns of seasonal occurrence were noted at individual species level: I) early spring peak (e.g. A. ater) or overall decline across the season (e.g. Aphodius fossor, Cercyon haemorrhoidalis, Fig. 3a), II) distinct optimum during the season (e.g C. lateralis. Sphaeridium lunatum, Sphaeridium bipustulatum, Fig. 3b), III) autumn species or gradual increase in numbers over the season (e.g. Cercyon pygmaeus, Philonthus varians, Platytstethus arenarius,Fig. 3c). IV) a biphasic seasonal distribution represented by the U-shaped responses (e.g. A. rugosus, Aphodius fimetarius, Cercyon quisquilius, Fig. 3d).

    3.5. Successional and seasonal aspects at guild level

    The early successional guilds of relocators and hydrophilids displayed the same pattern of decline throughout the successional gradient (Fig. 4a). However, whereas the relocators displayed a corresponding decrease along the seasonal gradient, the hydrophilids had a distinct optimum in the later part of the season (Fig. 4b). The number of dweller individuals remained stable until the day 4 and then slowly declined. During the seasonal gradient, dwellers had an u-shaped distribution of overall abundance (Fig. 4). Among the predatory species, the small predators increased both throughout the succession and the seasons. The medium predators generally had their optimum later in the succession (i.e. around day 7) and showed no seasonal trends (Fig. 4). The large predators exhibited no temporal trend.

    4. Discussion

    4.1. General course of dung community succession and its comparison to other ephemeral habitats

    In contrast to most previous authors, who distinguished three phases of succession using either qualitative (Mohr, 1943; Lee and Wall, 2006) or quantitative (Koskela and Hanski, 1977) criteria, we observed only two mildly discrete successional phases in the coprophages community. However, similarly to Koskela and Hanski (1977), we also found an early phase of rapid changes (abundances, number of species), lasting until day 4, followed by a phase of slower changes until end of succession on days 7–9. The exceptions to this trend can be explained by the variation in species composition among seasons. The early spring community peaks in diversity on the day 4 because of the higher number of dweller species, particularly Aphodius spp. that occur in older dung and that were very abundant in early spring. The late autumn community differed in both abundance and diversity peaking around day 7 due to the proliferation of late successional Staphylinidae (dwellers, small predators).

    The overall duration of succession was shorter than in Northern Europe. It lasted until day 9 at our Central European site, with only two species having their optima on day 11; whereas in Northern Europe, the succession was prolonged up to day 14 (Lee and Wall, 2006). While the temporal optima of the early successional species appear to be constant in the two regions, late-successional species are shifted earlier in Central Europe. For example, the hydrophilid Cercyon lateralis peaked on the day 7 in Northern Europe (Hanski and Koskela, 1977) but on day 5 in our study from Central Europe. Based on these findings we suggest that whereas the species successional sequence is constant, the optima of late successional species depend on the environmental conditions (temperature, humidity). The reason why the entire succession is more rapid in Central Europe is probably due to the warmer climate that could affect the dung environment (drying of dung pats, etc.). A similar phenomenon was observed by Hanski (1980b) who compared two successional patterns from England and Finland, the latter being shorter, explained by the better weather conditions during the experiment (also probably due to higher temperature).

    Overall, the successional turnover of coprophilous beetles is more rapid than in other communities colonizing ephemeral habitats such as carrion (Kocarek, 2003; Matuszewski et al., 2008; Sharanowski et al., 2008; Tabor et al., 2004) or rotten fruit (Lukasik and Johnson, 2007). Studies of coprophilus beetles have shown that the number of individuals has usually peaked by the fourth day of succession (Koskela and Hanski, 1977; Lee and Wall, 2006), diversity peaks on the second day for coprophages and on the eighth day for predators (Koskela and Hanski, 1977). In our study both abundance and diversity generally decreased from the first day onwards. In contrast, species and individual richness is the highest in the later phases of decomposition in communities feeding on carrion or rotting fruit (Lukasik and Johnson, 2007). In a successional sequence on a dead rat, the diversity of necrophilous beetles reached its peak in the middle phase (days 5–11) (Kocarek, 2003). In studies of pig carcass succession the diversity of necrophilous beetles also reached optimum during the mid – or late succession from day 10 onwards (Matuszewski et al., 2008; Tabor et al., 2004). At least for the dung and carrion community the causal explanation probably lies in the size of resource patches, whereas very little is known regarding the temporal patterns in communities colonizing rotten fruits (Lukasik and Johnson, 2007). The dung pats are generally quite small and hence are prone to rapidly depletion, especially in warmer regions, while carrion can vary between small patches (i.e. rat carcasses), with dynamics similar to dung pats, to large (i.e. pig carcasses) with very slow dynamics of species. Another explanation could be related to decreasing moisture. There are necrophilous species specialized to utilize dry carrion as adults (Dermestidae, Trogidae) (Kocarek, 2003), but there are no such species in the coprophilous community (but it is important to note that we lack direct data on larvae). Therefore, the succession of necrophilous beetles could last longer in contrast to the succession of coprophilous beetles in which the species are generally disappearing throughout the succession.

    4.2. Successional patterns

    In the coprophage community, the relocators clearly preceded hydrophilids and dwellers. The explanation of this finding likely lays in differences of ecology between the guilds. The relocators utilize two different foraging strategies: they either relocate food for larvae into underground nests (Onthophagus spp. Geotrupes spp. A. erraticus) (Doube, 1991; Rojewski, 1983; Vitner, 1998) or their larva is saprophagous and lives outside the original dung pat (e.g. A. prodromus, A. sphacelatus) (Finn and Gittings, 2003; Gittings and Giller, 1997). Both strategies enable the larva to avoid potential competition for food in the dung pat and predation from coprophilous predators. Moreover, adult relocators are bound to the fresh dung because it either allows the relocation of dung for their larvae (cf. Halffter and Edmonds, 1982) or because they are microphagous and therefore dependent on the liquid dung (Holter and Scholtz, 2007); the latter applies to the Aphodius species with saprophagous larvae who consume the dung only in the adult stage (Finn and Gittings, 2003). The dwellers spanned most of the successional gradient with relatively few early species and a majority of late successional species. The very early dwellers species (e.g. A. ater) probably only occur in the early stages of succession as sexually immature adults (Hirschberger, 1998). The dwellers were segregated roughly according to their oviposition behavior with soil-ovipositing species preceding the dung-ovipositing species (Gittings and Giller, 1998). The Hydrophilids split into two species groups across the succession and this partitioning corresponds with an older ecological classification of hydrophilids on specialist dung feeders and more generalist species (Hanski and Koskela, 1978). This classification perfectly matches the species life histories: the coprophilous specialists as the early-successional species and the habitat generalists as the late-successional species (Hansen, 1987). Beside this, hydrophilid larvae are carnivorous, apparently preying on dung fly larvae, such as in the genus Sphaeridium (Gawaad et al., 1976; Sowig, 1997). The successional position of their adults should follow the fly larvae supply, as observed by Lee and Wall (2006). However, lacking data on fly larvae abundance in our study, we cannot further interpret the patterns found in Hydrophilidae adults.

    Based on our results, behavioral traits such as food relocation and oviposition behavior are the most likely to play the primary role in structuring the succession of coprophagous species, at least for those with coprophagous or saprophagous larvae. This phenomenon was also observed in studies of the successional pattern of tropical dung beetles communities where the dung relocating species always preceded the dwelling species (Horgan, 2002; Marchiori et al., 2003). Unfortunately it is not possible with the currently available data to decide if this dung relocating behavior is an adaptation to utilize wet dung, or is a consequence of competitive interactions between members of the dung community (cf. Halffter and Edmonds, 1982). However, similar behavioral successional segregation is also known for the carrion community where relocating Silphidae, namely the Nicrophorus spp., precede the “dwellers- like” Dermestidae and Trogidae (Grassberger and Frank, 2004; Horenstein and Linhares, 2011; Kocarek, 2003; Sharanowski et al., 2008; Tabor et al., 2004, 2005).

    The temporal pattern of predators probably reflects the trophic relationships in the community. Valiela (1974) observed that the early successional Staphylinidae prey not only upon dung fly larvae, but also on small adult hydrophilids (Cercyon spp.), which occur in the same successional stage. Intra-family predation within Staphylinidae (described by Valiela (1969): Philonthus cruentatus preying upon Anotylus tetracarinatus) may explain the late-successional optima of small predators (Atheta spp.). Alternatively, small predators may prey primarily on Colembolla species, abundant during the later phase of succession. However, trophic relations among predatory coprophilous insects remain largely unknown (Hanski and Koskela, 1979) and require further experimental study. The situation with carrion inhabiting predatory species is virtually the same. However, only turn over of abundances was reported for various taxonomical groups but with no causality (e.g. Matuszewski et al., 2008).

    In conclusion, the pattern of individual species colonization found in our study widely resemble the patterns observed in older studies from Northern Europe (Gittings and Giller, 1998; Hanski and Koskela, 1977; Lee and Wall, 2006), montane Mediterranean (Menendez and Gutierrez, 1999; Palestrini et al., 1995), and also from North America (Kessler and Balsbaug, 1972; Mohr, 1943; Wingo et al., 1974) where the majority of pastoral beetles are immigrants from Europe (Gordon, 1983). Our data for complex beetle communities, together with previous studies from other regions, supports the hypothesis that the species successional sequences, i.e. the particular species position in the successional line, are rather conservative with respect to species evolution rather than dependent on the ecological circumstances both in coprophagous and predatory species. Moreover, we suggest that similar mechanisms can also be partly attributed to succession in other ephemeral habitats, not just dung-beetle communities.

    4.3. Successional vs. seasonal trends

    In the community of coprophages, the two early successional guilds (relocators and hydrophilids) were obviously separated at the seasonal scale, possibly due to resource partitioning between the two groups. Dung relocating species can rapidly deplete the resource patch or accelerate the drying of dung pats (Hanski and Cambefort, 1991). Coprophagous adults of hydrophilids are therefore probably forced to aggregate in the alternative part of the niche space, in this case later in the season. Such separation has not been recorded for the dung community in majority of the older studies from Northern Europe, since they either did not observe seasonality of hydrophilid species, or observed just one early summer peak (Hanski, 1980a), perhaps due to relatively short vegetation season. Only the work of Desière (1983) and De Graef and Desière (1984) have shown the pattern of individual hydrophilid species' seasonality, intriguingly similar to our results. We have also shown that while the very early successional hydrophilid species (e.g. C. melanocephalus) display no obvious seasonality, the species following them in the succession (e.g. C. haemorrhoidalis, Cercyon quisqiulius) are all well separated seasonally. The late successional dwellers showed a U-shaped seasonal trend with the two main groups, Aphodius spp. and Staphylinidae:Oxytelinae, showing that they were seasonally separated, the former peaking in spring, the latter in autumn. Optima of both these groups were non-overlapping seasonally with the successionally preceding hydrophilids, possibly to escape potential predation of their eggs and small larvae by hydrophilid larvae.

    Among the predatory species, the small predators displayed the same late successional and seasonal trend like dwellers did, presumably due to a lower risk of predation while only a few other predatory species increased across the seasons. Species coexistence among medium predators is likely due to a more gradual successional species turnover, compared to the coprophages, and by less obvious seasonal optima of individual species. The large predator species also seem to be separated into two successional groups (in which at least a half the species displayed a seasonal trend). Their abundances, however, are very small in general, and apparently do not differ between those groups both at the successional and seasonal gradients.

    5. Conclusions

    1.

    The succession of coprophilous beetles is much more rapid than the succession of beetles colonizing other ephemeral habitats like carrion. The length of coprophilous beetle succession is shorter in southerly regions with late successional species shifting to earlier stages of the succession.

    2.

    Behavioral traits, such as food or larvae relocation, or oviposition behavior, are likely to play a major role in structuring the species composition along the successional gradient for both coprophilous and necrophilous communities.

    3.

    Small predatory species are significantly shifted later in the succession and season. Larger predatory species display less obvious successional and seasonal optima that probably facilitate their coexistence.

    4.

    The combined results of studies from European northern, southern temperate regions and the American temperate zone suggest that the individual species successional positions are constant in both coprophagous and predatory beetles, presumably reflecting species coevolution.

    5.

    The seasonal separation between and within guilds is more pronounced for late successional guilds of non-relocating species.

    Although the successional pattern of beetle species utilizing dung is well resolved, the causality in predatory species succession remains unknown. Also, until now, most studies only considered coprophilous beetles, leaving aside the residual dung fauna, especially dung flies. Our suggestions for further research are therefore: i) the detailed study of fly larvae dynamics throughout the succession and comparison with coprophagous beetle patterns, ii) the food web of dung community which could clarify the patterns of predatory species, iii) manipulative experiments to asses the impact of potentially competitively dominant relocating species on the rest of coprophilous community.

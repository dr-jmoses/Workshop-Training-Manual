    Abstract

    Most disturbed habitats in the tropics and subtropics harbor numerous species of invasive ants, and occasionally the same species has been introduced repeatedly from multiple geographical sources. We examined how experimental crossbreeding between sexuals from different populations affects the fitness of queens of the tramp ant Cardiocondyla itsukii, which is widely distributed in Asia and the Pacific Islands. Eggs laid by queens that mated with nestmate males had a higher hatching rate than eggs laid by queens mated to males from neighboring (Hawaii × Kauai) or distant introduced populations (Hawaii/Kauai × Okinawa). Furthermore, inbreeding queens had a longer lifespan and produced a less female‐biased offspring sex ratio than queens from allopatric mating. This suggests that the genetic divergence between different source populations may already be so large that in case of multiple invasions eventual crossbreeding might negatively affect the fitness of tramp ants.

    Introduction

    Human activities and globalization have led to the homogenization of the fauna and flora in disturbed habitats around the world. Many plantations, parks and beaches harbor a similar set of introduced species, and among these, nonnative ants often play a central role in the anthropogenic ecosystems. For example, Argentine ants, yellow crazy ants, or little fire ants are often extremely abundant in urban or agricultural areas and severely affect native plants and animals (e.g., Holway et al., 2002).

    Populations of invasive ant species often appear to result from a single introduction. For example, in Argentine ants, Linepithema humile, some populations have enormous “supercolonies” of connected, genetically similar nests among which ants can be exchanged without aggression (e.g., Giraud et al., 2001; Tsutsui et al., 2001). In contrast, in other introduced populations, the cooccurrence of several mutually hostile and genetically distinct “supercolonies” suggests multiple introductions (Giraud et al., 2001; Tsutsui et al., 2001; Thomas et al., 2006, 2010; Björkmann‐Chiswell et al., 2008; Sunamura et al., 2009; Mothapo & Wossler, 2011). In general, repeated colonization events may increase invasiveness by counteracting the reduction of genetic variability associated with founder effects (Sakai et al., 2001; Lavergne & Molofsky, 2007; Wilson et al., 2009). However, it might also result in outbreeding depression through disrupting coadapted gene complexes (Huff et al., 2011; Verhoeven et al., 2011). While multiple introductions obviously increase intraspecific competition among colonies of invasive ants and lead to the re‐establishment of territory borders, little is known about how they affect other fitness traits.

    While Argentine ants, fire ants, and crazy ants are quite conspicuous, most urban and agricultural ecosystems in the tropics and subtropics also harbor a set of much less conspicuous smaller ants, including Tetramorium bicarinatum, Monomorium floricola and several species of Cardiocondyla (e.g., McGlynn, 1999; Heinze et al., 2006; Wittman, 2014; Wetterer, 2017). Because of their small individual and colony size, Cardiocondyla are often overlooked, but more intensive studies typically reveal at least one species of this genus even in remote islands, which are free of native ants (e.g., Heinze, 2017). At present there are no data on the invasion history of Cardiocondyla tramp ants, but the occurrence of different strains of the endosymbiontic bacterium Wolbachia in C. obscurior populations in Brazil indicates repeated introduction events (A. Klein, pers. comm.).

    Most successful invasive ants have a life history characterized by multiple queens and mating in the nest (e.g., Passera, 1994; Yamauchi & Ogata, 1995; Aron, 2001; Tsutsui & Suarez, 2003; Heinze et al., 2006). In Cardiocondyla, wingless males mate with related, winged female sexuals. Inbreeding appears to be the rule and outbreeding is achieved by dispersing, winged males in some species and occasional adoption of alien female sexuals (Heinze, 2017). In introduced populations, adoption and outbreeding might involve individuals with different invasion history and geographical origin. In C. obscurior, mating between sexuals from different introduced populations negatively affected the longevity and the reproductive success of the queen, apparently because sperm and seminal fluid from alien males elicited an increased immune response in their mates (Schrempf et al., 2015).

    Multiple introductions may thus change population structure, colony growth, and reproductive success of invasive ants. To better understand these phenomena we collected colonies of the widely distributed ant Cardiocondyla itsukii (Seifert et al., 2017) and established new colonies with sexuals from the same nest (“inbreeding”) or from allopatric populations (“outbreeding”). In a previous paper we reported on the association of lifespan and reproductive success in colonies in which a queen had mated with a male from the same stock colony (referred to as C. cf. kagutsuchi; Heinze et al., 2013). Here, we compare the longevity and productivity of queens in a subset of these “inbred colonies” with those of queens that in a simultaneous experiment mated with an alien male.

    Materials and methods

    Cardiocondyla itsukii is a small (∼2 mm), dark brown ant belonging to the C. nuda complex (C. atalanta, C. mauritanica, C. kagutsuchi, and others; Seifert et al., 2017). This group of morphologically similar species is widely distributed throughout the tropics and subtropics (Seifert, 2003, 2008). C. itsukii is easily confused with other species of this genus and has previously been referred to as C. venustula (Nishida, 2002) and C. kagutsuchi (Yamauchi et al., 2005; Krushelnycky et al., 2005). It is probably native in mainland Asia and from there has been introduced to Hawaii, Kiribati, Réunion, and Okinawa (Seifert et al., 2017). While winged males cooccur with wingless males in several populations of C. kagutsuchi (Yamauchi et al., 2005; Okita et al., 2013, 2015; Okita & Tsuchida, 2016), winged males appear to be absent from C. itsukii and other species in this group (Seifert et al., 2017).

    Colonies for this study were collected in 2006 from their nests in the upper layer of soil in three different sites: two in the Hawaii islands (USA; seven colonies from Kauai, near Pu'u o Kila lookout and the trail heads of Awa'awapuhi and Nualolo trail, elevation approximately 1100 m; three colonies from Hawaii, Kilauea Iki, elevation 1180 m), and one in Okinawa (Japan; five colonies from Naha, elevation approximately 30 m). Despite the 7800 km distance between Hawaii and Okinawa, colonies from the three populations were identical in the sequence of a fragment of the mitochondrial CO I/CO II gene (AB723725, Okita et al., 2013).

    In the laboratory, colonies were housed in three chamber plaster nests at 12 h 15 °C/12 h 25 °C and day‐night cycles and fed with cockroaches and honey twice a week. Experiments started in January 2007 and lasted until December 2007. Sexuals came from seven different stock colonies from Kauai, three colonies from Hawaii, and five colonies from Okinawa. We established 30 colonies with pupae of male and female sexuals from the same nest (“inbred colonies”) and 52 colonies, in which sexual pupae originated from different populations (“outbred colonies”). Because of the availability of sexuals, we set‐up several replicates with the same combinations of source colonies.

    Each experimental colony was supplied with one pupa each of a male and a female sexual, 10 workers and a few brood items from the stock colony of the female sexual. After eclosion, sexuals mated inside the nest, the queen shed its wings and started to lay eggs. Dead workers were replaced by new workers from the stock colony until the experimental colony had started to produce its own workers. Colonies were monitored twice per week and the presence of the queen and the male, and the number of workers and brood items were noted. Colonies were allowed to grow without restriction, but pupae of sexuals were removed to avoid confusing sexual offspring with the founding pair.

    In several colonies, the queen died during the first days or weeks of the experiment, and/or failed to lay eggs. In 64 colonies at least, eggs were produced. In three colonies, these only developed into males and the queens were not inseminated (two inbred, one outbred colony). For the statistical analysis, only nests that produced at least one worker or queen pupae were taken into account.

    The following parameters were compared between inbred and outbred colonies: the lifespan of the queen, the age of the queen when shedding its wings and beginning to lay eggs, the average number of eggs per scan, the number of larvae, pupae, and workers produced (calculated from 20 d after appearance of first brood item until the death of the queen), the date of appearance of first worker, male, and queen pupae relative to queen eclosion, the sum of all queen and male pupae produced, and the numerical sex ratio (proportion of female sexuals in the sexual offspring) (Table 1). In several cases, queens disappeared from the nest. Though they presumably were had died and were dismembered by the workers we treated the data of their disappearance as incomplete, that is, censured.

    Normally distributed data were analyzed with t‐tests, and nonnormally distributed data and life spans were compared with Mann–Whitney U‐tests. Correlations among various traits were compared by Spearman's rank correlation tests, as it is less sensitive to outliers than other nonparametric correlations. Statistical analyses were performed using the software Past 3.07 (Hammer et al., 2001) and Statistica 6.0 (StatSoft, Tulsa, OK, USA).

    Due to the complexity of the dataset and the close correlations among various life history traits, we also used linear mixed models to analyze the life span of queens and sex ratio using the lmer function (lme4 package, Bates et al., 2015) and the statistical software R 3.3.2 (R Development Core Team, 2010). The models included breeding treatment (two levels: inbreeding/outbreeding) and populations as fixed effect factors. The “queen life span” values were square‐root transformed before the analysis to reach normality. The minimal model was obtained by a stepwise backward elimination procedure, that is, by successively removing all nonsignificant terms (P > 0.05) and by comparing the change in likelihood between the new and old model with a likelihood ratio test. Shapiro and Bartlett tests were used to check for normality and homoscedasticity of the model residuals. Several experimental colonies were set up from the same stock colonies. To account for the resulting pseudo‐replication, the factor colony was entered as a random effect factor in both models.

    The 30 inbred colonies in this study have previously been included together with 70 additional colonies in a larger study on the timing of reproduction in Cardiocondyla and the interrelation between lifespan and lifetime reproductive success (referred to as C. cf. kagutsuchi, Heinze et al., 2013). Queen lifespans and sex ratio in inbred colonies therefore widely match those reported in our earlier paper. Here we focus on the effect of outbreeding and of the much larger original data set include only those inbred colonies that were established at exactly the same time as the outbred colonies.

    Results

    We set up 30 colonies with sexuals from the same colony (“inbred”) and 52 colonies with sexuals coming from different populations (“outbred”). Eggs were laid in 28 inbred and 35 outbred colonies, that is, mating between nestmates was more successful than allopatric mating (Fisher's exact test, P = 0.007). Concerning outbred colonies, eggs were produced in 8 of 17 colonies with sexuals from Hawaii and Kauai, 10 of 11 colonies with sexuals from Hawaii and Okinawa, and 7 of 24 colonies with sexuals from Kauai and Okinawa (Fisher's exact tests, H × K vs. H × O, P = 0.04; H × K vs. K × O, P = 0.195, H × O vs. K × O, P = 0.388; not significant difference after Bonferroni correction for multiple tests). Because of the limited availability of sexuals and the early death of one of the two sexuals the crosses differed strongly in the number of successfully established replicates (inbred: H♀ × H♂ 9; K♀ × K♂ 9; O♀ × O♂ 10; outbred: H♀ × K♂ 4; K♀ × H♂ 4; H♀ × O♂ 9; O♀ × H♂ 1; K♀ × O♂ 12; O♀ × K♂ 5).

    In six inbred and 18 outbred colonies, eggs failed to hatch, and in two additional inbred and one outbred colonies, only male offspring was produced. Dissection of the latter three queens proved that the queen had not mated. Considering only colonies in which fertilized eggs were laid, success rate of inbred colonies was significantly higher than that of outbred colonies (20 of 28 vs. 16 of 35, Fisher's exact test P = 0.046). Success rate did not differ between crosses between sexuals from the neighboring islands Hawaii and Kauai (6 of 8) and crosses between sexuals of Hawaii/Kauai and Okinawa (13 of 27, Fisher's exact test P = 0.176). For 20 combinations of colonies, we set‐up two or more replicates. Interestingly, both replicates failed in only 4 of them (2 inbred, 2 outbred), while in 16 of these crosses one replicate queen laid viable eggs.

    We excluded all unsuccessful colonies from further analyses and in the following compare only those colonies that produced workers or female sexual offspring (20 inbred: K♀ × K♂ 6; H♀ × H♂ 7; O♀ × O♂ 7; 16 outbred: H♀ × K♂ 1; K♀ × H♂ 1; H♀ × O♂ 4; O♀ × H♂ 1; K♀ × O♂ 6; O♀ × K♂ 3).

    Successfully started inbred and outbred colonies did not differ significantly concerning the timing of wing loss of the queens and the appearance of their first eggs. Within the first 10 weeks of the experiment, two queens (one inbred H♀ × H♂, one outbred H♀ × O♂) died. In all other colonies, the average number of eggs increased constantly with time during the first 14 scans of the experiment (Spearman's rank correlation test, outbred: rs = 0.912, P < 0.001; inbred: rs = 0.919, P < 0.001) and outbred and inbred colonies contained similar number of eggs after 52 d (Table 1; see Heinze et al., 2013 for the temporal trajectories of egg laying rates in a larger sample of inbred colonies). Maximal egg numbers were observed after approximately 75% of the individual queen's lifetime (Table 1). Thereafter, egg numbers no longer increased but remained on more or less constant levels until approximately 1 week before the death of the queen. In both groups, egg numbers decreased significantly between 4 d before the queen's death and the day of the queen's death (median [quartiles], inbred, ni = 18 as eggs could not be counted in one colony; 23 [9, 40] vs. 10 [2, 25]; Wilcoxon matched pairs test, T = 14, P = 0.009; outbred, no = 15; 13 [6, 27] vs. 5 [0, 14]; T = 5, P = 0.003).

    Egg productivity varied greatly among colonies and the mean number of eggs counted per scan ranged from 3.5 to 47.8. Because of this large variation, inbred and outbred colonies did also not differ in average egg number, maximum egg number, and the maximum number of workers (Table 1). They also did not differ in the time of emergence of the first worker pupae and the first male pupae (Table 1).

    In contrast, inbred colonies produced female sexuals significantly later than outbred colonies and at slightly, but insignificantly higher worker numbers (Table 1). Inbred colonies showed a significantly less female‐biased sex ratio (female sexuals/all sexuals, only colonies, which reared more than five sexuals). Four inbred and two outbred colonies produced only male offspring (9–75 males) towards the end of the queen's life, suggesting sperm depletion. We therefore analyzed sex ratios separately for the time before the death of the queen and thereafter, and in both cases sex ratios were more female‐biased in outbred colonies. Total sex ratios were still different when we excluded the six sperm‐limited queens (Table 1). Interestingly, while these colonies produced similar numbers of female sexuals, they differed significantly in the number of produced male offspring (Table 1).

    The differences in the time of emergence of the first female sexual pupa, sex ratios, and the number of males produced by queens that were not sperm‐limited remain statistically significant at P < 0.05 after Benjamini–Hochberg correction for 17 U‐tests. Furthermore, using a few stock colonies for replicated crossings allowed us to directly compare sex ratios produced by sisters. This comparison supported the finding that sex ratios are less female‐biased in inbred queens (Wilcoxon matched pairs test, n = 6; W = 15, P = 0.0625).

    In a linear mixed model including breeding treatment and population, only breeding treatment remained as significant factor for sex ratio (F1,23.124 = 9.745, P = 0.0048). This matches the individual statistical tests above. Inbreeding queens had a significantly longer life span (median [quartiles], ni = 19; 147 [118, 172] d) than queens that mated with a male from a different population (no = 15; 123 [95, 150] d; Peto & Peto Wilcoxon test WW = 3.013, P = 0.0396; Fig. 1). Inbred queens lived longer than their outbred sisters (Wilcoxon matched pairs test, n = 6; W = 20, P = 0.0625). Despite of the difference in life span, reproductive success (total number of sexuals produced) did not differ between inbred and outbred colonies (median [quartiles], inbred: 40 [9, 61]; outbred: 23 [11, 65]; U = 129.5, P = 0.656). In a linear mixed model with breeding treatment and population as independent variables and “colony” as random factor, population had no effect on queen longevity and breeding treatment was significant (F1,32 = 4.263, P = 0.047).

    As expected, queens that did not mate or failed to produce offspring had a shorter lifespan than successful queens (median [quartiles], unsuccessful queens, n = 29; 77 [63, 98]; successful queens, inbred and outbred, n = 34; 131 [115, 160], Peto & Peto Wilcoxon test, WW = −8.116, P < 0.001). More than 80% of “successful” queens, but only 24% of “unsuccessful” queens survived the first 100 d of the experiment. Males (n = 47) survived for a median of 46 d [30, 76].

    Discussion

    Our experimental crosses between sexuals of the ant Cardiocondyla itsukii from Hawaii, Kauai, and Okinawa show that mating with a nestmate is more successful than mating with a partner from another population. Furthermore, mating with a nestmate leads to an increased queen lifespan and also changes the queens’ reproductive schedule: inbred colonies produced female sexuals later and reared more males, leading to a less female‐biased sex ratio.

    Almost 70% of all interpopulation crosses failed, regardless of whether sexuals came from neighboring (Hawaii × Kauai) or distant populations (Hawaii/Kauai × Okinawa). Most queens did not begin to lay eggs or their eggs did not hatch. This suggests that sexuals had not copulated or, in the case where queens rapidly shed their wings and started to lay eggs, that their offspring was less viable because of outbreeding depression and genetic incompatibility between sperm and egg. Similarly, forced outbreeding reduced egg hatching rates in an ambrosia beetle with regular sib‐mating (Peer & Taborsky, 2005). In most cases in which sexuals from the same combination of colonies were used for experimental crosses, at least one of the replicate queens succeeded in producing offspring. This indicates that these combinations of colonies are not absolutely incompatible, for example, due to harboring different strains of endosymbiotic bacteria (e.g., Zientz et al., 2005; Gotoh et al., 2007; Perlman et al., 2008; Brucker & Bordenstein, 2013). Furthermore, several crosses between nestmates also did not yield any offspring, showing that failure is not always due parental divergence.

    Schrempf et al. (2015) studied the consequence of interpopulation crosses in the tramp ant Cardiocondyla obscurior. While only few of the staged crosses were unsuccessful (A. Schrempf, pers. comm.), queens that had mated with an allopatric male had a shorter lifespan than queens that had mated with a male from their parental colony. Proximately, outbreeding appeared to be associated with an increased immune response, probably triggered by the seminal fluid of the alien male (Schrempf et al., 2015). Shorter lifespan was also observed in C. itsukii queens, indicating a similar cost of mating with an alien male for the queen. In contrast to an earlier study with only inbred C. itskuii (Heinze et al., 2013), we could not substantiate a difference in the lifespan of queens from Hawaii and Okinawa. This might be due to the lower sample size in the present study.

    Concerning offspring production, C. itsukii responds very differently to interpopulation crosses than C. obscurior. While allopatric mating in the latter resulted in decreased fecundity, both concerning egg laying rates and total reproductive success, male origin did not affect the reproductive output of C. itsukii queens. Both inbred and outbred queens had similar egg laying rates and similar numbers of female sexuals. However, outbred queens produced a significantly more female‐biased sex ratio than inbred queens, which stands in contrast to the result in C. obscurior, where sex ratios were less female‐biased in outbred than in inbred colonies (Schrempf et al., 2015). We previously compared offspring production among large numbers of sister queens from two C. itsukii colonies from Hawaii and Kauai and showed that sex ratio has a heritable basis: some colonies produce female larvae with a higher propensity to develop into female sexuals (“queen bias,” Frohschammer & Heinze, 2009). In this study, we did not set up replicates for each stock colony and therefore cannot substantiate a similar heritability. Furthermore, in this study, the origin of the mating partner affected sex ratio predominantly via the number of males produced the queen rather than queen bias. Inbred queens reared many males than outbred queens and this finding holds when the six colonies are compared in which sister queens were used in both the inbreeding and the outbreeding treatment.

    The proximate cause for this difference remains unclear. Sex ratio theory suggests that the mother's sex ratio optimum in haplodiploids becomes more female‐biased with inbreeding (Herre, 1985). However, inbreeding in social Hymenoptera may result in the production of diploid males from fertilized eggs due to homozygosity at the sex‐determining locus (van Wilgenburg et al., 2006). In C. obscurior and also other species of this genus, inbreeding is the rule and neither brother–sister nor mother–son mating leads to diploid males (Schrempf et al., 2006; Schmidt et al., 2014). Though we do not have exact data on the consequence of prolonged inbreeding in C. itskuii, the percentage of eggs developing into males in inbred colonies was much lower than the 50% expected from single‐locus complementary sex determination. Male production might also indicate sperm depletion, and indeed we found that the last eggs laid by a few queens developed exclusively into males. However, most of the inbred colonies with strong male production reared female sexuals and workers at the end of the queen's life.

    Because our colonies invariably contained only a single mother queen and Cardiocondyla sexuals mate in their natal nests (Heinze, 2017), local mate competition similarly selects for a minimal investment of queens in male offspring (Hamilton, 1967; Cremer & Heinze, 2002; Yamauchi et al., 2006). Again, our result does not match the expectation and further investigations on the causes of male‐bias in inbred colonies are needed.

    In conclusion, experimental outbreeding in a naturally sib‐mating tramp ant appears to strongly affect fundamental life history traits, in particular queen longevity and sex ratio. This opens a new avenue for empirical research into the consequences of multiple introductions of invasive ants. Previous studies have documented how the colonization of new areas by colonies originating from different source populations leads to the establishment of aggressively defended borders between different supercolonies (e.g., Thomas et al., 2006, 2010; Frouz et al., 2009; Mothapo & Wossler, 2011), but the frequency of crossbreeding between sexuals from different supercolonies and its consequences have rarely been investigated (but see Sunamura et al., 2011). Gene flow between colonies introduced from multiple geographical sources might help invasive species to overcome the genetic bottlenecks resulting from the accidental introduction of only few individuals into new areas (Suarez & Tsutsui, 2008). The low hatchability of eggs laid by C. itsukii queens that mated with a male from a different population indicates just the opposite: it appears that despite the similarity in CO I/CO II sequences the three studied populations have diverged so strongly that eventual admixture would have negative fitness effects (see also Dlugosch et al., 2015).

    Abstract

    Army ants are among the top arthropod predators and considered keystone species in tropical ecosystems. During daily mass raids with many thousand workers, army ants hunt live prey, likely exerting strong top‐down control on prey species. Many tropical sites exhibit a high army ant species diversity (>20 species), suggesting that sympatric species partition the available prey niches. However, whether and to what extent this is achieved has not been intensively studied yet. We therefore conducted a large‐scale diet survey of a community of surface‐raiding army ants at La Selva Biological Station in Costa Rica. We systematically collected 3,262 prey items from eleven army ant species (genera Eciton, Nomamyrmex and Neivamyrmex). Prey items were classified as ant prey or non‐ant prey. The prey nearly exclusively consisted of other ants (98%), and most booty was ant brood (87%). Using morphological characters and DNA barcoding, we identified a total of 1,103 ant prey specimens to the species level. One hundred twenty‐nine ant species were detected among the army ant prey, representing about 30% of the known local ant diversity. Using weighted bipartite network analyses, we show that prey specialization in army ants is unexpectedly high and prey niche overlap very small. Besides food niche differentiation, we uncovered a spatiotemporal niche differentiation in army ant raid activity. We discuss competition‐driven multidimensional niche differentiation and predator–prey arms races as possible mechanisms underlying prey specialization in army ants. By combining systematic prey sampling with species‐level prey identification and network analyses, our integrative approach can guide future research by portraying how predator–prey interactions in complex communities can be reliably studied, even in cases where morphological prey identification is infeasible.

    1 INTRODUCTION

    No spectacle of the tropical world is more exciting and mystifying than that of a colony of army ants on the march. Hölldobler and Wilson (1990)

    Consumer–resource interactions are central to understanding ecological interactions in nature (Elton, 1927; May, 1973; Mittelbach, 2012). Consumers may affect the structure and diversity of ecological communities by controlling the abundance as well as the spatial and temporal variance of resources (Berlow, 1999; Fairweather, 1988; Paine, 1966). For instance, predators can prevent single species from monopolizing important, limiting resources, thus increasing local species diversity (Paine, 1966, 1980). Ants are among the most important consumers in terrestrial habitats (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990; Lach, Parr, & Abbott, 2010). Within the arthropod community, they represent one of the largest groups of scavengers, omnivores, herbivores and predators, making them vital components of many ecosystems (Elmes, 1996; Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990; Jeanne, 1979). The most conspicuous predators among ants are the mass‐raiding army ants, which have sparked interest since pioneering naturalists such as Darwin and Bates wrote about their behaviour (Bates, 1863; Darwin, 1839).

    Army ants are distinct from other ants in that they have large nomadic colonies that perform mass raids and divide by fission (the “army ant adaptive syndrome”; Gotwald, 1995). Army ants are traditionally subdivided into either swarm raiders or column raiders (Gotwald, 1995; Schneirla, 1971). Swarm‐raiding species are characterized by the formation of a large carpet‐like area—densely covered with ants—which advances in the leaf litter to overwhelm a broad range of invertebrate prey (Gotwald, 1995; Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990; Kaspari, Powell, Lattke, & O'Donnell, 2011; Schneirla, 1971). In contrast, column raiders prey primarily on other social insects (e.g., Figure 1a,b) by forming single interconnected columns which usually consist of smaller groups of workers (Gotwald, 1995; Powell & Baker, 2008; Rettenmeyer, Chadab‐Crepet, Naumann, & Morales, 1983). In any tropical forest with 20 or more army ant species, only two or three of them may be swarm raiders, while the rest are column raiders (Kaspari et al., 2011). Noteworthy, the distinction between these raiding strategies is sometimes difficult as classical column raiders can perform small swarms at the raiding front and classical swarm raiders can perform column raids (e.g., canopy raids of E. burchellii).

    Irrespective of raiding strategy, the impact of army ant raids on tropical invertebrate communities is expected to be substantial (Kaspari & O'Donnell, 2003; Kaspari et al., 2011; Otis, Santana, Crawford, & Higgins, 1986; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983; Vieira & Höfer, 1994). For example, the prey biomass intake of the army ant Eciton hamatum was estimated to be 38 g arthropod prey per day and colony (Powell, 2011). In fact, army ants probably rank among the animals with the highest trophic position in certain tropical ecosystems (Gotwald, 1995; O'Donnell, Lattke, Powell, & Kaspari, 2009; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). Besides their pivotal role as predators, a diverse fauna of associated animals depends on the presence of army ant colonies, including ant birds and arthropod myrmecophiles (Gotwald, 1995; Kronauer & Pierce, 2011; Parker, 2016; Wrege, Wikelski, Mandel, Rassweiler, & Couzin, 2005). Army ants are thus considered keystone species in tropical ecosystems, and as such, they are indispensable for conservation efforts (Boswell, Britton, & Franks, 1998; Peters, Likare, & Kraemer, 2008).

    Despite being important for ecosystem functioning, the basic ecology of most army ant species remains poorly studied (Gotwald, 1995; Kaspari et al., 2011; Kronauer, 2009; Powell & Baker, 2008; Schöning, Njagi, & Kinuthia, 2008). For instance, little information exists about the diet of most army ant species (Gotwald, 1995; reviewed in Supporting information Appendix S1). A few conspicuous species have been studied in detail (Supporting information Appendix S1), and predator–prey energy flows have been estimated by quantitatively evaluating prey intake through biomass and abundance data in two of the most prominent Neotropical species, E. burchellii and E. hamatum (Franks, 1982; Powell, 2011). Community data about army ant diets, that is, evaluation of dietary preferences and possible dietary differentiation of multiple sympatric army ant species, are rare (e.g., Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; Powell & Franks, 2006; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983), and species‐level identification of prey has not been systematically carried out in most studies, except for a few notable exceptions (e.g., Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; Hirosawa, Higashi, & Mohamed, 2000; Powell, 2011; Powell & Clark, 2004; Rosciszewski, 1994; Kazan, 1972). This might be largely attributed to difficulties in identifying fractionalized prey and particularly in identifying immature stages, which make up a large proportion of booty in many species (Gotwald, 1995; Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; Hirosawa et al., 2000; Powell, 2011; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). The level of dietary specialization in army ant communities and the extent to which army ants overlap in food niches are thus not well known. Previous studies showed that most Neotropical army ants are specialized predators of ants with relatively high degrees of prey specialization (Gotwald, 1995; Powell & Baker, 2008; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). For instance, Powell and Franks (2006) found that E. burchellii preys upon ants of the genus Camponotus, while E. dulcium prefers ants of the genera Odontomachus and Pachycondyla. Similarly, sympatric species of African Dorylus army ants and sympatric species of South‐East Asian Aenictus army ants show different food preferences (Gotwald, 1995; Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; Hirosawa et al., 2000). Complementary diets like these support predictions made by general ecological theory; that is, competing, sympatric species can coexist if they use alternative food niches or foraging strategies, ultimately leading to competitive release and decreased interference (Mittelbach, 2012). However, this view has been recently challenged in various consumer–resource interactions by studies showing that higher diversity in several taxa, for example, along latitudinal gradients, is not associated with higher dietary specialization (Forister et al., 2015; Frank et al., 2018; Morris, Gripenberg, Lewis, & Roslin, 2014; Schleuning et al., 2012). Other factors such as top‐down control by keystone predators or disturbance as well as differentiation in other niche dimensions may thus also contribute to competition release and coexistence (Forister et al., 2015; Frank et al., 2018; Morris et al., 2014; Schleuning et al., 2012).

    The aim of the present study was to apply an ecological interaction network approach to systematically study prey specialization in a Neotropical community of surface‐raiding army ants. Prey specialization will therefore be defined here as a metric of incidence‐based interaction specificity (or exclusiveness of interactions) within the predator–prey network. By surveying army ant dietary preferences at La Selva Biological Station in Costa Rica, we asked (a) whether we can confirm that Neotropical army ants are specialized predators of ants, (b) whether and to what extent sympatric army ant species partition food niches and (c) whether the preferred raiding stratum and/or the army ants' phylogeny determine dietary preferences. To answer these questions, we surveyed representative prey samples of 11 surface‐raiding army ant species, which we identified using morphological and molecular characters. Interaction networks provide a framework to visualize and understand such complex, multispecies relationships (Ings et al., 2009; Paine, 1966, 1980), and we applied ecological network analyses to measure species‐level and network‐level interaction specificities and to quantify army ant diet niche overlap.

    2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

    2.1 Sample collection

    We collected army ants and their prey in the Costa Rican tropical wet forest at La Selva Biological Station (N10°25.847' W84°00.404′, 67 m asl). We searched for surface‐raiding army ants in an area of approximately 11 km2, including primary and secondary forest segments. Strictly subterranean army ants were not considered because prey collection was infeasible. Eciton burchellii was the only army ant species in this study performing large swarm raids, while other species primarily raided in columns. Samples were collected from February 2017 to April 2017 (9 weeks in total). We searched for army ant raids and army ant emigrations during daylight (7:00 hr–18:00 hr) and in the dark (19:00 hr–03:00 hr) by walking along the extensive trail system at La Selva. Detailed collection information is given in Table 1 and Supporting information Appendices S2 and S4.

    During mass raids, army ant workers capture and sometimes dismember prey, which is then transported back to the bivouac, the temporary nest of army ants. During colony emigrations, prey items are transported to the new bivouac site (Figure 1a). We collected the first prey items that we encountered in each raid (Ntotal = 2,551 prey items) and each emigration (Ntotal = 213 prey items). In addition, we opportunistically collected from prey caches (Ntotal = 499 prey items), sites where army ants temporarily store prey. We took about 10–20 prey items per collection event (mean ± SD: 14 ± 9 prey items; range = 1–64 prey items; N = 238 collection events). For each collection event, we also collected approximately 5–20 army ant workers for species identification. All samples were immediately stored in 2‐ml vials containing absolute ethanol.

    We aimed to minimize resampling of the same army ant colonies and thus omitted raids of the same species at the same day within a radius of ~50 m of the last sampling spot. We do expect that the prey collection should thus cover mostly different colonies of different army ant species, as sampling took place in a relatively large collection area with high army ant colony density (O'Donnell, Lattke, Powell, & Kaspari, 2007; see Supporting information Appendix S3). However, it is possible and not unlikely that we sometimes resampled the same colony due to the ants' nomadic habit and the long sampling period of over 9 weeks in a restricted area. However, given that the aim of the present study was to evaluate prey spectra of different army ant species, we do not expect occasional resampling of the same colony to have a strong effect on the results on diet preferences of a given species. In other words, a colony‐level prey specialization seems unlikely, although this possibility of course cannot be excluded.

    2.2 Broad classification of all prey items

    We first determined for each prey item whether it was an ant or another invertebrate. Only very few prey items were invertebrates other than ants. We did not systematically identify those prey items to the species level and designated them as “non‐ant prey.” For ants, we determined the developmental stage (i.e., egg, larva, pupa or adult) for each prey item and assigned them a unique identification code. We tested for army ant interspecific differences in raiding preferably immature vs. adult life stages of other ants using a Pearson's χ2 in r (R Core Team, 2018; version 3.5.1).

    2.3 Morphological species‐level identifications

    We used the species key of Longino (2010) to identify adult prey specimens. In total, we identified 228 adult prey ants morphologically. The ant species Aphaenogaster araneoides, Mayaponera constricta and Pachycondyla harpax have conspicuous larvae and pupae that we were able to distinguish morphologically from other ant species. Within samples of each collection event, we sorted all other brood morphologically into “brood morphospecies” and, if possible, identified those to genus or subfamily level (e.g., Figure 1b). We defined a “brood morphospecies” as eggs, larvae or pupae that looked anatomically similar within samples of one collection event. Within samples of one collection event means that these prey items were carried in short succession by army ant workers, making it likely that they stemmed from the same prey colony the ants were plundering. Voucher specimens of adult prey ants are deposited at the Museo Gustavo Orcés (EPN, Quito, Ecuador) and brood voucher specimens as well as army ant specimens are deposited at CvB's personal collection (see Supporting information Appendix S2). We took prey overview photographs for each collection event on a black or a white paper using a Nikon D5300 SLR equipped with a Sigma 50‐mm macro‐lens. Prey overview images are deposited at Dryad (https://doi.org/10.5061/dryad.s6743c5). In addition, 2D focus‐stacked images of 15 adult prey ants are deposited at BOLD (e.g., Figure 1c; Supporting information Appendix S2).

    2.4 Genetic species‐level identifications

    Army ant booty consisted primarily of ant brood, for which we mostly depended on DNA barcoding to identify species. DNA barcoding relies on the premise that intraspecific sequence differences in a standardized gene fragment are generally smaller than interspecific ones (Meier, Zhang, & Ali, 2008; Stoeckle & Thaler, 2014; Wiemers & Fiedler, 2007). This allows clustering of barcode data into distinct genetic groups, which in most cases reflect actual species boundaries (Stoeckle & Thaler, 2018).

    We chose representative samples of each “brood morphospecies” of a collection event (mean ± SD = 3 ± 2 prey items; range: 1–12 prey items) for DNA barcoding, totalling 774 prey items. Because only a few prey items per morphospecies per collection event were barcoded, we might have missed a few species records for network analyses in cases where prey samples from the same collection event contained morphologically very similar ant immature stages of distinct species that we inadvertently lumped into the same morphospecies. However, it should be considered here that we collected the first few prey items encountered in a collection event, which often seem to derive from the same prey nest. In the great majority of cases, DNA barcoding results indeed confirmed that a given morphospecies belonged to a single species. We are thus confident that the number of undetected prey species in our collection events is low, even though we genetically analysed only a subset of the morphologically nonidentifiable immature ant prey items (738 immature prey items analysed out of 2,588 immature prey items total).

    DNA was extracted in 96‐well plates using the Qiagen DNeasy 96 Blood & Tissue Kit (Hilden, Germany). The extraction followed the standard protocol except that specimens were not homogenized but kept intact to subsequently serve as morphological vouchers. However, some larvae and pupae suffered from the protein digestion, which destroyed the specimens' habitus. We did not barcode adult specimens of six prey species that we identified morphologically (Ectatomma ruidum, Ectatomma tuberculatum, Gnamptogenys haenschi, Gnamptogenys mecotyle, Odontomachus chelifer, Trachymyrmex opulentus). This is because reference barcodes for these species were already available in the Barcode of Life Data System (BOLD) allowing for an identification of immature prey items of these species.

    For molecular species identification, we amplified a fraction of the standard animal barcode, that is, the mitochondrial gene cytochrome oxidase I (COI), in standard polymerase chain reactions (PCRs). PCRs were set up as described previously (von Beeren, Maruyama, & Kronauer, 2016b). Initially, we had difficulties to consistently amplify COI in the genera Crematogaster, Camponotus and Acromyrmex. Consequently, we developed forward and reverse primers for Crematogaster and Camponotus that are located a few base pairs inward of the classical 658‐bp COI fragment. Alternative primers for Acromyrmex were kindly provided by Christian Rabeling. All primers and amplification strategies are listed in Supporting information Appendix S4—Tables S1 and S2. Purification and sequencing of amplicons were performed by Macrogen Inc. Europe (Amsterdam, the Netherlands). All amplicons were sequenced in forward and reverse direction using Sanger sequencing. Sanger sequencing of individual prey items allowed us to link each DNA barcode to a voucher specimen. In case of low‐quality reads, samples were resequenced with alternative primer combinations. Forward and reverse sequences were assembled and trimmed with the software geneious (Kearse et al., 2012; version R11.03).

    We performed several quality checks with the resulting consensus sequences. Due to stop codons in the COI alignment (possible pseudogenes), we omitted seven Acromyrmex sequences. More specific primers finally solved this issue (see above and Supporting information Appendix S4). Furthermore, we used prey images to detect apparent contaminations. As a result, we identified nine COI prey sequences as errors (1.2% of all DNA barcodes), likely deriving from pipetting errors. These sequences were not considered for statistical analyses. Apart from the errors, consensus sequences were uploaded to BOLD for further analysis and sequence deposition (for BOLD and GenBank accession numbers, see Supporting information Appendix S2).

    To identify distinct genetic units in prey barcodes, we used the sequence clustering algorithm RESL (Ratnasingham & Hebert, 2013). RESL applies single linkage clustering of uncorrected pairwise distances (p‐distances) by considering thresholds for operational intra‐molecular taxonomic units (intra‐MOTUs) and inter‐MOTUs designations (Ratnasingham & Hebert, 2013). Among others, it defines that the maximum intra‐BIN p‐distances need to be below 2.2% p‐distance—in other words, intra‐BIN sequences need to be above a threshold of 97.8% sequence similarity. BOLD then designates RESL‐based “Barcode Index Numbers” (abbreviated as BINs). BINs are unique alphanumeric codes used to define distinct genetic clusters for those MOTUs that are based on sequences that surpass certain data quality criteria (e.g., sequence length ≥500 bp; Ratnasingham & Hebert, 2013). These BINs, together with their DOI, are a persistent but dynamic registry for animal MOTUs in BOLD. We determined in which BINs our prey barcodes were clustered, and if any BOLD records within a given BIN registry were morphologically identified up to the species level, we assigned this name to all prey items in that BIN. If BINs contained no species name, we designated genus names according to closest similarity in phylogenetic tress (see below) and added the abbreviation “sp.” plus a number (e.g., Cyphomyrmex sp. 1–Cyphomyrmex sp. 3 in Supporting information Appendix S5; BINs of these record are found in Supporting information Appendix S2). We assigned the same species names to distinct BINs when BINs shared a species name in the database. This can happen if COI barcodes of a species includes more than one BIN cluster (e.g., Pheidole indagatrix in Supporting information Appendix S5).

    To assign species names to prey barcodes with <500 bp, we used two complementary approaches. We first ran a neighbour‐joining (NJ) tree, based on MUSCLE alignment (Edgar, 2004) and Kimura's 2‐parameter model (K2P) (Kimura, 1980) in BOLD. The NJ tree contained prey sequences and sequences derived from a reference data set of Neotropical ants, which included over 5,600 reference barcodes of several hundred ant species (BOLD project names: ASLAM, courtesy of J. Longino & MAS; BCIFO, courtesy of Y. Basset and the Smithsonian Barcoding Opportunity FY014; ARCAE, maintained by one of the authors, DD). A pairwise deletion approach was used for missing data, and sequences <400 bp were not considered. For prey barcodes <400 bp, we additionally generated a random accelerated maximum‐likelihood (RAxML; Stamatakis, 2014) tree based on GTR GAMMA as nucleotide model using the RAxML plug‐in for Geneious (range of sequence lengths: ≤156 bp–687 bp). In contrast to pairwise‐distance approaches, a maximum‐likelihood tree also allows to screen for sequence similarity within a data set containing shorter fragments.

    2.5 Prey specialization

    We used an ecological interaction network approach to assess network‐level and species‐level specificity of army ant–prey interactions. Analyses of prey specialization are based exclusively on prey items that were identified to the species level morphologically and/or via DNA barcodes (see Table 1 for sample sizes). We did not consider non‐ant prey for statistical network analyses, because non‐ant prey was not identified to the species level. Network analyses were based on quantitative interaction matrices (from hereon called “food networks”). An interaction was defined as a prey species detected in a raid/emigration/prey cache of an army ant species. Accordingly, one link between an army ant and its prey can have many interactions (see also Ivens, von Beeren, Blüthgen, & Kronauer, 2016 for definitions). The food network thus contained incidence data of ant prey; that is, we counted the number of interactions—in other words, the number of times ant prey species were present in different, spatiotemporally independent collection events (raids/emigrations/prey caches of army ants). Using spatiotemporally independent collection events is important to avoid an overestimation of specialization, because analyses of ecological interactions in social insects can be susceptible to pseudo‐replication (e.g., Blüthgen, Menzel, & Blüthgen, 2006). In the present study, such pseudo‐replication would have occurred if we had counted the repeated occurrence of the same prey species in one collection event—prey that most likely derived from a single raided nest. Note that this contrasts with a classical food web approach, which looks at specificity from another viewpoint by evaluating energy flows—often via biomass and abundance measurements (Dormann & Blüthgen, 2017). The relative importance of a particular prey species as energy source is therefore more precisely evaluated in a food web approach (e.g., Ings et al., 2009; Powell, 2011), while ecological interaction networks measure the importance/dependency of species interactions via the frequency of spatiotemporally independent interaction events. Hence, using incidence data yields a conservative estimate of dietary specialization, as prey items belonging to the same species collected from the same raid are only represented by a single interaction. For instance, we detected 50 prey items of the widespread ponerine ant Anochetus mayri in nine collection events: two raids of E. vagans, one raid of E. lucanoides and six raids of E. mexicanum. This results in values of 2, 1 and 6 interactions in the food network, respectively. To screen whether prey specificity in army ants is determined by relatedness of prey at higher taxonomic levels, we also created an army ant species–prey genus food network and an army ant species–prey subfamily food network.

    Based on these food networks, we analysed network‐level specialization and species‐level specialization by using the two‐dimensional Shannon entropy H2ˈ and the Kullback–Leibler distance dˈ, respectively. In contrast to unweighted/qualitative network metrics, which strongly depend on network size and sampling effort, these weighted metrics allow for an unbiased comparison of specificity between species within a network and across networks (Blüthgen, 2010; Blüthgen et al., 2006; Ivens et al., 2016). H2ˈ and dˈ values are normalized relative to minimum and maximum possible values and, as a result, range from 0 (lowest interaction specificity) to 1 (highest interaction specificity; Blüthgen et al., 2006). H2ˈ and dˈ values were tested against null models based on 10,000 randomized networks, which had the same total number of interactions and the same marginal totals as the original network (Patefield algorithm; see Blüthgen et al., 2006; Patefield, 1981; Wehner, Heethoff, & Brückner, 2018). We tested the robustness of H2ˈ calculations against our sampling intensity based on random subsets of the networks. For this, network subsetting was done by randomly deleting an interaction from each predator species in each step and by repeating this reduction until the network contained only two predator and two prey species. This analysis was based on the command “rrarefy” in the r package “vegan” (see also Blüthgen et al., 2006). This randomization of the original network matrix was repeated 50 times to produce Figure S1 in Supporting information Appendix S4.

    We also calculated the army ants' effective Shannon diversity (eH; Jost, 2006) and the rarefied Shannon diversity based on 100 permutations for 20 prey collection events (eHrare). The effective Shannon diversity summarizes the relative link strengths considering the diversity and evenness of interactions, thus serving as an intuitive measure of specialization. The rarefied Shannon diversity improves comparability and accounts for variation in the total number of collection events among army ant species.

    We additionally explored dietary niche partitioning between army ant species using two further approaches. First, we analysed network modularity. The quantitative modularity metric Q (QuanBiMo algorithm) describes the extent to which species interactions within a network are organized into distinct modules (Dormann & Strauss, 2014). These modules are characterized by a high density of within module links and few to no between‐module links, thus providing information about interaction overlap. Like H2ˈ and dˈ values, Q is normalized and can range from a value of 0 (randomly configured networks) to 1 (network composed of perfect modules). As described above for H2ˈ, we tested Q against randomized null models (Schleuning et al., 2014) and performed random subsetting of the network. Second, we tested the exclusiveness of prey links between army ant species pairs. For this, H2ˈ values of each army ant species pair were compared to 10,000 random networks (Blüthgen et al., 2006). Hence, pairwise H2ˈ comparisons tested whether a network, consisting of two army ant species and all their prey, differed from random networks allowing to test for prey differentiation between army ant species pairs (see also Wehner et al., 2018). All network analyses were analysed in r using the “bipartite” package (Dormann, Gruber, & Fründ, 2008; version 2.08).

    2.6 Spatiotemporal raiding preferences

    To determine spatial raiding specialization in army ants, we evaluated the characteristic nesting stratum (“ground nesters” vs. “arboreal nesters”) for each prey species based on Longino (2010), combined with own observations (Supporting information Appendix S2). “Ground nesters” included species nesting in leaf litter, dead wood and soil. “Arboreal nesters” included species nesting anywhere from lower arboreal zones to the canopy. Species for which nesting habit was unclear or species that were described to nest in both strata were excluded from statistical analyses. Statistical tests were based on a contingency matrix that summarized the number of times ground‐nesting vs. arboreal‐nesting prey was detected in raids of each army ant species. For instance, Acromyrmex volcanus was defined as arboreal nester and it was detected in five raids of Eciton hamatum resulting in a value of five for “arboreal nesters” and a value of zero for “ground nesters.” We tested whether spatial raiding preferences differ among army ant species by testing the H2ˈ value against null models as described above.

    Possible temporal raiding differences were assessed by comparing the frequencies of nocturnal and diurnal raiding activities across species. Raiding was defined to be “diurnal” if raids were discovered during daylight (07:00 hr–18:00 hr) and “nocturnal” if raids were discovered in the dark (19:00 hr–03:00 hr). Note that our search time was biased towards nocturnal collections (176 collection events) compared to diurnal collections (57 collection events; see Supporting information Appendix S2 and Supporting information Appendix S4—Figure S2). In addition, we did not search for army ant raids in the early morning (03:00 hr–07:00 hr). This means our data do not provide an unbiased overview of army ant activity throughout a 24‐hr day. Nonetheless, the collected data reveal general preferences for diurnal vs. nocturnal raiding activity in between species comparisons. We tested for army ant species differences regarding prey activity patterns by comparing the H2ˈ value against null models as described above. This analysis was based on a contingency table summarizing the number of times we detected nocturnal or diurnal raids (including prey cache collection events and excluding emigration collection events) for each army ant species.

    2.7 Possible predictors of prey niche

    We also evaluated whether army ant phylogeny or army ant abundance determines prey specificity. To test whether army ant phylogeny determines dietary preferences, we created two distance matrices. First, we extracted the pairwise species divergence times of army ants from a well‐resolved phylogeny of Eciton (Winston, Kronauer, & Moreau, 2016; see also Brückner, Hoenle, & von Beeren, 2018 for details of the analysis). Divergence data were not available for some of the studied Neivamyrmex and Nomamyrmex species, and we thus included only Ne. gibbatus and No. esenbeckii in the analyses. Second, we calculated a Bray–Curtis dissimilarity matrix of prey preferences (Bray & Curtis, 1957) based on the army ant species–prey species food network (Supporting information Appendix S2). We constructed a tanglegram of the two cluster dendrograms as implemented in the r package “dendextend” (Galili, 2015). Overlap of both dendrograms was maximized by rotating the trees' nodes without changing their topology. In addition to a topological comparison of the dendrograms, we performed Mantel tests, implemented in the r package “vegan” (Oksanen et al., 2007), to analyse whether divergence time and diet dissimilarity matrices were correlated with each other. As we only correlated small matrices with each other (eight army ant species), the statistical power for this analysis was comparatively low.

    Because rare species are often more specialized (e.g., Goulson & Darvill, 2004), we also tested whether the abundance of army ant species at the field site was correlated with their level of specialization. We used the number of times we encountered army ant raids and emigrations as an approximation of their abundance at the field site. A Spearman rank correlation as implemented in r was used to test whether army ant abundance was correlated with species‐level specialization (d'; see above). Note that the statistical power for this test was also low due to small sample size (11 army ant species).

    3 RESULTS

    3.1 Prey composition

    In total, we collected 3,262 army ant prey items (Supporting information Appendix S2). Booty consisted primarily of ants (N = 3,209 prey items), while other invertebrates were rare (N = 53). Non‐ant hymenopterans constituted 30% of the non‐ant prey items (N = 16). Ant pupae represented the most common prey (N = 1,692 prey items), followed by ant larvae (N = 1,128 prey items), adult ants (N = 360 prey items) and ant eggs (N = 29 prey items). The ratio of ant brood to adult ants in booty differed among army ant species (χ2 = 431, df = 10, p < 0.001; Supporting information Appendix S2). Most notably, Eciton dulcium captured a higher proportion of adult prey (58%) compared to other army ant species (≤14%; Supporting information Appendix S4—Figure S3).

    One noteworthy observation was that ant pupae retrieved from army ant raids were often nude pupae (i.e., not enclosed in a cocoon), including samples from species of the subfamilies Ponerinae, Ectatomminae and some Formicinae, which typically spin cocoons. This suggests that army ants often remove the captured pupae from their pupal cases on the spot.

    3.2 Prey identification

    In total, we identified 129 prey ant species by using a combination of morphological and molecular characters (see Supporting information Appendix S2 for details). Out of the 3,262 collected prey items from 238 collection events, we morphologically identified 427 and 676 ant prey items to the species level via morphological and DNA barcoding characters, respectively (mean ± SD = 4.86 ± 3.03 identified prey items per collection event; N = 1,103 identified prey items). We morphologically identified 26 ant species out of 232 adult prey items: Aphaenogaster araneoides, Camponotus atriceps, Camponotus JTL021, Ectatomma gibbum, Ectatomma ruidum, Ectatomma tuberculatum, Gnamptogenys alfaroi, Gnamptogenys banksi, Gnamptogenys haenschi, Gnamptogenys mecotyle, Gnamptogenys tornata, Mayaponera constricta, Neoponera apicalis, Neoponera bugabensis, Neoponera villosa, Odontomachus chelifer, Odontomachus erythrocephalus, Odontomachus hastatus, Odontomachus meinerti, Odontomachus opaciventris, Odontomachus panamensis, Odontomachus ruginodis, Pachycondyla harpax, Pheidole dossena, Pheidole nebulosa and Platythyrea prizo. In addition, we morphologically identified 185 brood items (107 pupae and 78 larvae) of Aphaenogaster araneoides, Mayaponera constricta and Pachycondyla harpax (see Supporting information Appendix S2 for details). Furthermore, 10 Trachymyrmex opulentus pupae collected from a single raid were identified using adult individuals collected directly from the raided nest. Of these morphological species identifications, all but seven species were additionally identified via DNA barcodes (i.e., Ectatomma ruidum, Ectatomma tuberculatum, Gnamptogenys alfaroi, Gnamptogenys haenschi, Gnamptogenys mecotyle, Odontomachus chelifer and Trachymyrmex opulentus; Supporting information Appendix S2).

    COI barcodes were successfully recovered from 681 prey items, while either PCR or sequencing failed repeatedly for 93 specimens. After sequence trimming, we obtained high‐quality reads of variable fragment size ranging from 156 bp to 687 bp (mean: 578 bp ± 98 bp; Supporting information Appendix S4—Figure S4). Of these barcodes, 27 came from adult ants, 649 from ant brood and 5 from non‐ant prey (Supporting information Appendix S2). No stop codons, unusual amino acid substitutions or internal sequence deletions were found, except for 12 sequences of Apterostigma ants, where we detected a 3‐bp deletion. This deletion did not alter the reading frame, nor did it result in an upstream stop codon. 3‐bp deletions in Apterostigma ants have been described previously, suggesting that the target region was successfully amplified (Hanisch et al., 2017; Sosa‐Calvo et al., 2017).

    BOLD's clustering algorithm RESL grouped 564 ant prey barcodes into 129 BINs (Supporting information Appendix S2), while no BIN was assigned to 112 ant prey barcodes (see below). In 11 cases, we assigned a single species name to distinct BINs, because two or three reference BINs contained records of the same species (species with two BINs: Anochetus mayri, Apterostigma dentigerum, Gnamptogenys interrupta, Odontomachus erythrocephalus, Pachycondyla harpax, Pheidole anastasii, Pheidole indagatrix, Pheidole sp. 2, Platythyrea punctata, Trachymyrmex cornetzi; species with three BINs: Nylanderia steinheili; for visualization, see Supporting information Appendix S5). Due to sequence lengths of <500 bp, no BINs were assigned to 112 prey barcodes, which we identified via sequence similarities in genetic trees instead (Supporting information Appendix S5). In one case, Gnamptogenys alfaroi, no DNA barcode reference was deposited in BOLD and we were not able to amplify COI in adults (e.g., Figure 1c). One of the unidentified Gnamptogenys barcodes might thus be G. alfaroi (unidentified species in this study: Gnamptogenys sp. 2, Gnamptogenys sp. 3, Gnamptogenys sp. 4). We also ended up with a few prey barcodes that did not match to any BOLD barcode reference, or reference data lacked species identifications (Supporting information Appendix S5). For a few of the latter cases, we were able to complement the COI reference database of Neotropical ants by providing species names obtained from morphological identifications (e.g., Odontomachus erythrocephalus and Gnamptogenys banksi). For identification of non‐ant prey, see Supporting information Appendix S4—Table S3.

    3.3 Prey specialization in army ants

    Based on the 1,103 identified prey items, we analysed the network‐level and species‐level interaction specificity of the army ant–prey network. The network (prey at the species level) showed a high level of complementary specialization (H2ˈ = 0.66; Figure 2) and differed significantly from random network models (p < 0.001). Calculations of H2ˈ were very robust to down‐sampling. Even with half of the army ant–prey interactions randomly deleted, H2ˈ values ranged between 0.62 and 0.70 (Supporting information Appendix S4—Figure S1). Network‐level specialization decreased with higher‐ranking classification (prey genus: H2ˈ = 0.55; prey subfamily: H2ˈ = 0.39; Figure 3a,b), yet both networks also differed from random network models (p < 0.001).

    Prey specialization in the network varied among army ant species, with Ne. pilosus showing the highest degree of specificity (d' = 0.97), followed by No. hartigii (d' = 0.80), E. hamatum (d' = 0.78) and E. burchellii (d' = 0.76). The most generalist predators were E. vagans and Ne. asper (d' = 0.58 and d' = 0.57, respectively; Table 2). Rarefied prey Shannon diversity was highest in E. lucanoides (eHrare: mean ± SD: 13.57 ± 1.49), followed by Ne. gibbatus (eHrare: mean ± SD: 12.30 ± 1.53) and E. vagans (eHrare: mean ± SD: 11.99 ± 1.65). The lowest prey diversity was found in E. dulcium (eHrare: mean ± SD: 3.53 ± 0.49) and in Ne. pilosus (eHrare: mean ± SD: 6.11 ± 0.99; Table 2).

    3.4 Spatiotemporal raiding preferences

    Army ants showed different preferences for hunting grounds, which was indirectly assessed through the nesting location of their ant prey. The army ant–prey strata network differed significantly from random networks (p < 0.001; Figure 4a). Preferences for ground‐nesting ant prey were detected in E. dulcium, E. mexicanum and Ne. gibbatus, while E. hamatum, E. burchellii and Ne. pilosus preferred to raid arboreal‐nesting ant prey (Figure 4a). The army ant raiding activity network also differed significantly from random network models (p < 0.001; Figure 4b), showing that army ants had distinct temporal raiding activities. Eciton hamatum and E. burchellii showed preferences for diurnal raiding activity, while most other species showed a preference for nocturnal raiding activity (Figure 4b).

    3.5 Possible predictors of prey niche

    We tested whether army ant phylogeny correlated with prey niche similarity. The topologies of the two dendrograms, that is, species divergence times and prey dissimilarities, were incongruent (Figure 5) and not correlated (Mantel test on Spearman rank correlation: ρs = 0.163, p = 0.152), indicating that army ant phylogeny did not determine prey preferences (see also Figure 3a,b). Furthermore, army ant abundance did not correlate with the degree of species‐level specialization (dˈ) (Spearman rank test ρs = −0.082, p = 0.811), indicating that common and rare army ants do not systematically differ in prey specificity.

    4 DISCUSSION

    Here, we present a species‐level predator–prey interaction network of a community of surface‐raiding New World army ants, including qualitative and quantitative data on army ant diets. Sympatric army ants showed a high degree of diet specialization with little niche overlap. In total, we detected 129 ant species in army ant booty, which represents about 30% of the known ant fauna at the La Selva Biological Station (Longino, Coddington, & Colwell, 2002). Considering that we did not collect prey of purely subterranean army ant species, our results support the hypothesis of Rettenmeyer et al. that for almost every species of ant in a tropical rainforest, there exists an army ant species to raid it (Rettenmeyer et al., 1983).

    Army ants are often described as generalist predators (Carroll & Janzen, 1973), roaming the forest floor in great numbers to overwhelm all creatures getting in their way. Our results, however, support the idea that this view is a misconception as most army ant species are specialized predators of ant brood (Gotwald, 1995; Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; Hirosawa et al., 2000; Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). Henry Walter Bates already recognized in 1863 that booty of Neotropical army ants often consists of “chiefly larvae and pupae of other ants” (Bates, 1863), a fact that was later confirmed by numerous studies (Gotwald, 1995; Powell, 2011; Powell & Baker, 2008; Powell & Franks, 2006; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). Ant brood represented 98% of booty in this study and 99% of booty in South‐East Asian Aenictus army ants (Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; Hirosawa et al., 2000), demonstrating that ant brood is indeed the dominant food source of many army ant species (see also Supporting information Appendix S1).

    Species‐level identification of army ant prey is rare. This might be largely attributed to the fact that ant brood is morphologically difficult to identify to the species level (e.g., Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; this study). Most studies providing data about Neotropical army ant diets had goals other than evaluating interaction specificity in an army ant–prey network, so that only subsets of prey items were identified to the species level, while other prey was identified to higher taxonomic levels, such as genus or subfamily (e.g., Gotwald, 1995; Powell, 2011; Powell & Baker, 2008; Powell & Franks, 2006; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). To provide a species‐level army ant–prey network, we used DNA barcoding—a method that distinguishes species boundaries remarkably well in many animal groups (Goldstein & DeSalle, 2011; Hajibabaei, Singer, Hebert, & Hickey, 2007; Hebert, Ratnasingham, & de Waard, 2003; Jinbo, Kato, & Ito, 2011; Waugh, 2007). Reliability in species identification via DNA barcodes greatly depends on the quality of the reference library where barcodes are ideally linked to vouchered specimens that have been identified by qualified experts (Miller, 2007; Taylor & Harris, 2012). We compared prey barcodes to a well‐maintained barcode library of Neotropical ants, and we are thus confident that DNA barcoding‐based identifications were mostly accurate. At the same time, we are aware that species identifications via DNA barcoding are sometimes problematic (Dupuis, Roe, & Sperling, 2012; Krishnamurthy & Francis, 2012; Meier et al., 2008; Rubinoff, 2006; Valentini, Pompanon, & Taberlet, 2009; Wiemers & Fiedler, 2007). For example, mitochondrial haplotypes can be shared among species (Ballard & Whitlock, 2004), or haplotypes within the same species can be deeply divergent, erroneously suggesting the existence of distinct species (von Beeren, Stoeckle, Xia, Burke, & Kronauer, 2015). The latter case was also present in our data set (e.g., Anochetus mayri in Supporting information Appendix S5). Future taxonomic studies might verify these identifications—or alternatively show that divergent intraspecific COI clusters actually represent distinct, cryptic species (e.g., Hebert, Penton, Burns, Janzen, & Hallwachs, 2004; Janzen et al., 2017; von Beeren et al., 2016b; von Beeren, Maruyama, & Kronauer, 2016a).

    Given that ants are usually classified as omnivorous (Lach et al., 2010; Rosumek, 2017), army ants clearly represent an exception and should be considered specialist rather than generalist predators. Army ants utilized the same type of resources (i.e., ant brood) but differed in the spectrum of species taken as prey. Our predator–prey network indicated that prey overlap between army ant species was small, but it should be noted here that we certainly did not uncover the entire diet niche breadth of any of the studied army ant species. Interaction networks are always incomplete, and species links are missing in any given example (Dormann & Blüthgen, 2017; Sørensen et al. 2011). The actual dietary niche overlap between certain army ant species might thus be larger or smaller than detected in the present study. The current study only includes few prey collections for several army ant species (e.g., Neivamyrmex asper and Nomamyrmex hartigii), and doubtlessly many prey links are missing for those species. As an example, we did not detect Atta prey in Nomamyrmex esenbeckii booty, a prey link that has been previously described in Central and South America (Powell & Clark, 2004; Sánchez‐Peña & Mueller, 2002; Souza & Moura, 2008; Swartz, 1998) and observed at La Selva (A. I. Bruce, personal communication, October 13, 2017; Longino, 2010; see Supporting information Appendix S1). Prey specialization and niche exclusiveness of some army ant species thus remain to be studied in greater detail in the future. Nonetheless, our study provides solid evidence that prey specialization of the army ant community is high, and niche overlap is small. This is because the applied network approach accounts for such missing data and variation in number of observations per species by using appropriate null models (e.g., Dormann & Blüthgen, 2017). Furthermore, a simulation of lower sampling intensity demonstrated that network specificity calculations (H2') and network modularity calculations (Q) were robust to down‐sampling and already well described by half of the sampling effort undertaken in the present study. This indicates that the sampling design was appropriate to unveil interaction specificity and exclusiveness of interactions. This is particularly noteworthy given that we only collected and identified few prey species per collection event, while army ants can retrieve several thousands of prey items per colony per day (e.g., Powell, 2011).

    Our results largely confirm previous records of prey ants at the genus level, providing further evidence of the relative robustness of the present study design. We provide a detailed comparison of our results to previous studies in Supporting information Appendix S1. As an example, prey preferences of the present study match remarkably well to reported prey preferences of four Eciton species in Panama (Powell & Franks, 2006; for details, see Supporting information Appendix S1). For instance, Acromyrmex ants constituted 23% of prey items of E. hamatum in Panama (see also Powell, 2011) and 24% of collection events contained Acromyrmex prey in the present study. Furthermore, both studies demonstrated that Camponotus ants are the most common ant prey of E. burchellii and the two genera Pachycondyla and Odontomachus are the most common prey of E. dulcium. For several other army ants, we have presented prey records for the first time (Supporting information Appendix S1).

    Interestingly, we detected variation in prey specificity between army ant species, although the level of prey specialization was generally high for all species. Even the swarm‐raiding species E. burchellii, often considered as a trophic generalist (Gotwald, 1995), showed a remarkable level of dietary specialization. While E. burchellii is known to be a generalist predator of various invertebrates (Gotwald, 1995; Kaspari et al., 2011; Powell & Baker, 2008; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983; Sazima, 2017; Vieira & Höfer, 1994), our systematic prey survey revealed that non‐ant prey made up only 7% of all collected prey items. This surprisingly low frequency of non‐ant prey might help explain why a study by Kaspari et al. (2011) detected only marginal negative effects of E. burchellii swarm raids on non‐ant invertebrate leaf‐litter communities compared to Labidus swarm raids . Our results rather suggest that E. burchellii raids impose strong negative impacts on arboreal Camponotus ant communities, which made up 72% of E. burchellii prey items in the present study (see also Gotwald, 1995; Powell & Baker, 2008; Powell & Franks, 2006; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983; Figure 3a). With most prey items being ants, our study supports the idea that army ants are among the top ant predators in tropical rainforests, probably exerting top‐down effects on the populations of other dominant ant species. However, the present study was not designed to quantify the ecological impact of army ant raids on other invertebrates, including ants.

    We analysed prey specialization in army ants by applying network analyses. Predator–prey interactions are typically imbedded into classical food webs, which incorporate all links and different trophic levels in a defined community (Ings et al., 2009). Both methods, food webs and bipartite interaction networks, have advantages and caveats (see Dormann & Blüthgen, 2017). For instance, classical food webs measuring abundance or biomass might better reflect the energetic dependency of a predator on particular prey species than incidence‐derived link strengths within a bipartite network. On the other hand, quantitative bipartite networks robustly describe the specificity of interactions (or exclusiveness of links) between two defined parties (functional groups) within a network, for example, pollinators and plants. Our study system consisted of two well‐defined parties, that is, army ants and their prey (intraguild predation is uncommon among army ants; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983; but see Leroux, 1979; Schöning, Kinuthia, & Boomsma, 2006; Supporting information Appendix S4—Figure S5). Army ant–prey interactions were therefore ideally suited to be analysed via bipartite network analyses. Such networks can be grounded on a weighted or an unweighted interaction matrix (Blüthgen, 2010). Unweighted or binary networks only distinguish whether links are present or absent. Analyses based on binary data are problematic as they may over‐ or underestimate specialization (Blüthgen, 2010). For instance, when a predator mostly preys upon a single prey species, but occasionally takes another prey, the number of links alone would underestimate its true level of specialization. Binary network metrics may thus be inadequate to estimate the dependence of predators on particular prey (Blüthgen, 2010). In the present study, we considered the link strength of predator–prey interactions by counting the number of times a given ant species was found as booty in army ant raids/emigrations/prey caches, allowing us to more reliably interpret the dependency of army ants on prey species.

    Generalism appears to be the norm in typical food webs, while specialism tends to be more common in mutualistic interaction networks (Ings et al., 2009). For instance, food webs of invertebrate predators in temperate agricultural fields were dominated by generalists (H2ˈ: 0.22; Roubinet et al., 2018; see also van Veen, Müller, Pell, & Godfray, 2008). Compared to typical food webs, the army ant–prey network showed an exceptionally high degree of interaction specificity (H2ˈ = 0.66). Blüthgen, Menzel, Hovestadt, Fiala, and Blüthgen (2007) presented a comparison between various ant–plant and ant–animal associations providing insights into variability and degree of partner dependency in mutualistic associations. Compared to those mutualistic webs, the army ant–prey network was generally more specific than nonsymbiotic ant–plant interactions (H2ˈ: mean = 0.23, range: 0.13–0.33; N = 7 networks; Blüthgen et al., 2007) and mutualistic ant–aphid interactions (H2ˈ: mean = 0.41, range: 0.09–0.63; N = 8 networks; Ivens et al., 2016), while only highly specific ant–plant symbioses such as those between Crematogaster ants and Macaranga plants showed similar network‐level specificities (H2ˈ: mean = 0.70, range: 0.23–1; N = 14 networks; Blüthgen et al., 2007). This indicates that the presence of army ants in a rainforest depends on the presence of a set of prey species, comparable to symbiotic ants depending on the presence of their mutualist plant partner.

    While our study highlights strong dietary preferences in army ants, the underlying processes of prey specialization remain unknown. We studied two potential predictors, but neither army ant phylogeny nor their abundance correlated with diet specialization. In the following, we outline a few possible mechanisms and pinpoint avenues for future research. First of all, competition‐driven niche differentiation in sympatric army ant species can conceivably cause prey specialization (Mittelbach, 2012). Army ant raiding activities involved niche differentiation on several ecological axes, likely relaxing interspecific competition. Modularity of the army ant–prey network was high. Each army ant species clustered in a distinct module; that is, few links were shared between species, demonstrating species exclusiveness in dietary niches. Besides differentiation in diets, we discovered distinct temporal and spatial preferences in army ant raiding activity—additional niche differences helping to explain the coexistence of more than 20 army ant species at La Selva, and other tropical sites (Longino et al., 2002; O'Donnell et al., 2007; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). We found strict arboreal raiders (e.g., Ne. pilosus) and relatively strict ground raiders (e.g., E. dulcium), and some species that raided both strata fairly equally (e.g., E. lucanoides). Stratum specialization is niche partitioning in space, possibly relaxing interspecific competition. Interestingly, spatial niche differentiation was also described in Aenictus army ants (Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014). In addition, army ants differed in temporal raiding activity, agreeing mostly with previous studies (O'Donnell et al., 2007, 2009; Powell & Baker, 2008; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). While temporal differentiation might release army ants from interference competition (Anjos et al., 2017; Rosumek, 2017), it cannot explain differences in the prey spectra of army ants. Nesting sites of prey species are mostly stationary, and prey availability should thus be similar during day and night.

    In other predator–prey systems, interactions are largely driven by metabolic constraints and body size limitations; that is, predators are typically larger than their prey (de Ruiter, Neutel, & Moore, 1995; Emmerson & Raffaelli, 2004; Ings et al., 2009; Mulder, Cohen, Setälä, Bloem, & Breure, 2005; Woodward et al., 2005). The latter might also partly govern army ant–prey interactions (Hashimoto & Yamane, 2014; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). Small‐bodied Neivamyrmex species mostly plundered nests of smaller ants, while larger‐bodied Eciton species additionally preyed upon larger ants (Rettenmeyer et al., 1983, this study). Interestingly, one of the largest ants in the world, the Neotropical bullet ant Paraponera clavata, has not been observed to fall prey to army ants (Rettenmeyer et al., 1983), although it is abundant at La Selva (Dyer, 2002) and many other Neotropical sites. Predator–prey body size relationships might thus constrain the potential prey spectrum of army ants, but it is not sufficient to explain prey specialization as similar‐sized Eciton species still differed in the species taken as prey (see also Powell & Franks, 2006).

    Predators and prey are expected to reciprocally evolve counter‐adaptations over evolutionary time frames (arms races sensu Dawkins & Krebs, 1979). As adaptations to antagonistic partners are expected to be costly, arms races ultimately enhance interaction specificity (Ali & Agrawal, 2012; Brodie & Brodie, 1999; Dawkins & Krebs, 1979; McGlothlin et al., 2016; Thompson, 2005). A variety of specific adaptations against army ant predation have been described in Neotropical ants (Dejean, Corbara, Roux, & Orivel, 2013; Rettenmeyer, 1961; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983). Based on behavioural observations, some prey species seem to recognize approaching army ants via raid‐associated volatile organic compounds (e.g., alarm and recruitment pheromones; Dejean et al., 2013; Rettenmeyer et al., 1983) and via the cuticular hydrocarbon profile of individual workers (J.A. Manubay, personal communication, November 26, 2018) and, as a result, efficiently evacuate their nest (Dejean et al., 2013). Others disguise their nest entrance or block it when army ants are detected, and some species even successfully counter‐attack approaching army ants (reviewed in Dejean et al., 2013). On the other hand, army ants do not raid certain ant species, which often seems to be related to chemical‐mediated defences or the sheer workforce of large colonies (reviewed in Dejean et al., 2013). It is noteworthy that Solenopsis and Megalomyrmex ants, both abundant genera at La Selva (Longino et al., 2002), were only found once as prey in a raid of Ne. gibbatus and once in a raid of E. vagans, respectively. We assume this might be related to their potent venom alkaloids (Adams, Jones, Longino, Weatherford, & Mueller, 2015; Adams et al., 2013; Andersen, Blum, & Jones, 1991; Obin & Vander Meer, 1985), a chemical weaponry known to repel opponent ants and other organisms (Adams et al., 2015, 2013; Andersen et al., 1991; Obin & Vander Meer, 1985). Finally, it should be noted that none of the mentioned processes possibly underlying prey specificity in army ants are mutually exclusive.

    Our study underscores the high prey specificity of army ants in tropical ecosystems. Surface‐raiding Neotropical army ants are specialized predators of other ants, supporting the idea that army ants are among the top predators of ants in tropical rainforests. Finally, we have also pinpointed towards possible future research and we hope our integrative approach helps to inspire such work.

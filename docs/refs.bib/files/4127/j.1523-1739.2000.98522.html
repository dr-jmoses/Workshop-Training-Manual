    Abstract

    en

    Abstract: Two species of Neotropical army ant, Eciton burchelli and Labidus praedator ( Hymenoptera: Formicidae: Ecitoninae), are swarm‐raiding species that require large areas of habitat in which to hunt. Behavioral and distributional studies of these two species have been confined largely to humid lowland forest. We conducted intensive systematic area searches at elevations between 1200 and 1800 m in western Panama to assess the distribution of both species in intact premontane moist forest, shade coffee plantations, and sun coffee plantations. Both species were repeatedly observed in forest, shade coffee plantations close to forest, and shade coffee plantations distant from forest. Neither species was observed in sun coffee plantations. We believe that retention of certain forest‐like characteristics in the traditional shade coffee farm contributes to the persistence of these forest organisms in modified landscapes. Large canopy trees not only provide shade that buffers temperature extremes but also supply the ground layer with regular inputs of leaf litter and coarse woody debris from fallen trunks. Both E. burchelli and L. praedator hunt in leaf litter, and E. burchelli uses coarse woody debris as nesting sites ( bivouacs). There were significantly fewer potential bivouacs available in sun coffee plantations than in forest and shade coffee habitats. Also, litter depth was less in sun coffee than in forest and shade coffee. Our results provide the first evidence that shade coffee plantations can provide additional habitat for E. burchelli and L. praedator, top predators of the leaf litter arthropod community. E. burchelli and L. praedator act as critical links between swarm‐attendant bird species and leaf‐litter arthropods, providing an easily exploited food resource that would otherwise be unavailable for many birds. Continued conversion of shade coffee plantations to sun coffee plantations could have negative effects on army ants and associated biodiversity.

Desarrollamos búsquedas sistemáticas intensivas en elevaciones entre 1200 y 1800 msnm en Panama occidental para determinar la distribución de ambas especies en bosque húmedo premontano intacto, en plantaciones de café con y sin sombra. Las dos especies fueron observadas recurrentemente en bosque y en plantaciones de café de sombra cercanos y lejanos al bosque. Consideramos que la retención de ciertas características del bosque en las plantaciones de café de sombra contribuye a la persistencia de estos organismos de bosque en ambientes modificados. Los árboles no solo proporcionan sombra que amortigua la temperatura, sino que proporcionan hojarasca y restos leñosos de troncos caídos. Tanto E. burchelli como L. praedator cazan en la hojarasca, E. burchelli utiliza restos leñosos para anidar (vivaques). Encontramos significativamente menos vivaques en plantaciones de café sin sombra al compararlos con bosque y plantaciones de café con sombra. Asimismo, la profundidad de la capa de hojarasca fue menor en plantaciones de café sin sombra en comparación con bosque y plantaciones de café con sombra. Nuestros resultados proporcionan la primera evidencia de que las plantaciones con sombra proporcionan hábitat adicional para E. burchelli y L. praedator, depredadores de la comunidad de artrópodos en la hojarasca. E. burchelli y L. praedator actúan como eslabones críticos entre especies de aves que se alimentan de hormigas y los artrópodos de la hojarasca, proporcionando un recurso alimenticio fácilmente explotado que de otra manera no estaría disponible para muchas aves. La continua transformación de plantaciones de café con sombra a plantaciones sin sombra pudiera tener efectos negativos sobre las hormigas arrieras y la biodiversidad asociada.

    Introduction

    Over the next 30 years, it is estimated that up to one‐fifth or more of the species of all groups will likely go extinct ( Raven & Wilson 1992). This loss and the simultaneous homogenization of both floral and faunal groups can be attributed primarily to human‐induced changes to natural habitats. In Central America alone, deforestation is currently estimated at 376,000 ha per year; thus, the outlook for remaining forests, which constitute only 25–30% of the original forested area, is bleak ( Heckadon‐Moreno 1997  ). As forests in tropical regions are continually fragmented and land‐use patterns altered, many forest species, such as top predators, are particularly vulnerable to habitat destruction and depend on preservation of large tracts of pristine habitat ( Noss et al. 1996). Likewise, certain insect species are also area‐sensitive but have been given relatively little conservation attention.

    Two species of New World army ant, Eciton burchelli and Labidus praedator ( Hymenoptera: Formicidae: Ecitoninae), are vulnerable to forest perturbation and fragmentation in the lowland tropics ( Lovejoy et al. 1986; Harper 1989), although one recent study suggests that E. burchelli may use more disturbed sites on a temporary basis ( Stouffer 1998). L. praedator has been reported swarming outside forest in traditional cacao plantations in lowland Brazil and in Ghana ( Belshaw & Bolton 1993; Gotwald 1995). Such reports of the use of disturbed habitat by E. burchelli and L. praedator are restricted to only a few lowland sites. Little quantitative information exists from habitats above 1000 m.

    It has been predicted that <5% of the remaining pristine habitat will attain protected status in the near future ( Western 1989). What role might agroecosystems serve in providing additional habitat for the preservation of area‐sensitive species? In many parts of Latin America, especially at mid‐elevation habitats, traditionally grown shade coffee has been reported to maintain high levels of local diversity ( Nestel et al. 1993; Parrish & Petit 1995; Perfecto & Snelling 1995). Historically, coffee farmers cleared portions of the understory and canopy in continuous forests and planted coffee shrubs underneath. The retention of diverse canopy shade trees can continue to supply critical ecosystem functions such as local climate control and erosion control; inputs of leaf litter, root, and woody debris; and fruits and nesting sites for wildlife; it can also provide timber for extraction ( Bruenig 1996). Within the last 20 years, however, a new intensive technique of growing coffee in full sun has become popular with farmers and has been promoted ardently by local and international extension agencies throughout Latin America ( Perfecto & Snelling 1995; Perfecto et al. 1996).

    Our research addressed the question of whether or not top predators of the forest litter arthropod community, E. burchelli and L. praedator, are distributed equally in forest, shade coffee, and sun coffee plantations of premontane western Panama. Although conversion of traditional shade coffee plantations to more intensified sun coffee plantations has been shown to adversely affect the species diversity of ground‐foraging ant communities ( Perfecto & Vandermeer 1996), no research to date has addressed how these changes may affect a highly mobile army ant. We predicted that conversion from shade to sun coffee might negatively influence army ants by creating an unfavorable microclimate, decreasing leaf litter, and eliminating downed woody debris. There is evidence that E. burchelli and L. praedator may suffer high mortality, especially of the small workers, if they raid or nest in dry, hot, or sunny sites ( Schneirla 1949; Schneirla & Brown 1950; Levings 1983). Studies of seasonal and annual changes in litter arthropod abundance have shown that fewer species and fewer individuals are collected at drier, sunnier sites ( Levings & Windsor 1982; Levings 1984). There is also a reduction of species diversity among litter fauna with conversion of natural forest into monoculture plantations and of shade coffee to sun coffee ( Ibarra‐Nuñez 1990; Nestel et al. 1993; Perfecto & Snelling 1995; Ananthakrichnan 1996; Perfecto & Vandermeer 1996). Besides litter inputs, large trees are a source of woody debris of various sizes. The importance of coarse, downed woody debris to the maintenance of diversity and as habitat and or food sources for many invertebrates, vertebrates, and plants has been noted for temperate forest systems ( Maser et al. 1988; Harmon et al. 1996). We present data on the distribution and abundance of army ant swarms and specific habitat features across forest, shade coffee, and sun coffee habitats in western Panama.

    Study Site and Organisms

    The study was conducted in premontane moist forest and coffee plantations (elevations 1200–1800 m) adjacent to the Hartmann Biological Station located on the Pacific slope in the Chiriquí Province, 120 km northwest from David, Panama. The tropical forests below 1000 m in western Panama are largely gone, replaced by cattle pasture and croplands ( Ridgely & Gwynne 1989). The forests above 1800 m are protected within the boundaries of the Parque International de la Amistad, a 207,000‐ha biosphere reserve shared between Panama and Costa Rica ( Heckadon‐Moreno 1997  ). Between 1000 and 1600 m, the remaining fragments of forest are interspersed with traditional shade coffee and sun coffee plantations and cattle pasture.

    The intact forest and shade coffee plots share many native canopy tree families, such as Fagaceae, Guttiferae, Lauraceae, Moraceae, and Theaceae. The shrub layer of shade coffee plantations is dominated by coffee (Coffea arabica var. typica and var. caturra), interspersed among grasses and herbaceous plants. In contrast, the sun coffee plantations lack canopy trees, and the shrub layer consists wholly of closely spaced coffee plants. Non‐native pine trees are often planted on the periphery of sun plantations to prevent erosion along roads. Grasses and other herbaceous plants are usually absent in these intensively managed sun coffee plantations, so the ground is often bare except for leaf litter from coffee shrubs.

    Our forest study plots were sections of continuous forest. Shade coffee plots close to forest were whole plantations connected with continuous forest on at least two sides. Shade coffee plots distant to forest and sun coffee plots were whole plantations and sections of a larger plantation, respectively, with pasture bordering at least two sides.

    The climate in this region of Panama is highly seasonal, with an annual rainfall of 3074 mm ( Instituto de Recursos Hidráulicos y Electrificación, Estación de Santa Clara, Provincia de Chiriquí, Panama [lat 8°49′N, long 82°45′W], 1983–1997 ) concentrated during seven months, May through November. During a normal year, rainfall in the dry season, January through April, is localized and of short duration.

    E. burchelli and L. praedator are polyphagous predators ( generalists) and form large swarm raids ( Rettenmeyer et al. 1983). The swarm front of a raiding colony consists of a large mass of ants 2–25 m across that moves forward through the leaf litter but remains connected to a bivouac, or temporary nest, by a main artery of “flowing” ants. Based on the reproductive cycle of individual colonies, E. burchelli alternates between a stationary phase and a migratory phase ( Schneirla 1933). E. burchelli may form a column from 75 to 150 m in length from the bivouac during a daily raid, with longer raids characteristic of the migratory phase. Previous research has determined an average hunting area used by E. burchelli of between 500 m2/day and 1050–2100 m2/day ( Schneirla 1971; Franks 1982). Because L. praedator nests underground, its life history is less well known ( Gotwald 1995); but it has been reported that the aboveground raiding patterns of L. praedator are less predictable than those of E. burchelli ( Willis 1967 ). Knowledge of the life‐history traits of E. burchelli and L. praedator are based almost exclusively on studies conducted in the lowland tropics ( Schneirla 1933; Rettenmeyer 1963; Franks 1982; Coates‐Estrada & Estrada 1989).

    Methods

    Field Methods

    We located ant swarms by means of systematic area searches conducted from February through June in 1996 and 1997, at elevations between 1200 and 1800 m. We began surveying three undisturbed, mature forest plots and three traditional shade coffee plantations in February of 1996 and conducted 2 surveys per month over 5 months in these two habitat types for a total of 10 surveys. Beginning in March of 1996, 2 surveys per month were also conducted in three sun coffee plantations, for a total of 8 surveys; 2 additional surveys were conducted in 1997 for a total of 10.

    An attribute of the first year's study was that all shade coffee plots were located near or adjacent to forest, whereas sun coffee plots were distant from continuous forest. Because no ant swarms of either species were detected in the sun coffee habitat, we chose during the second field season to compare the abundance of ant swarms and habitat characteristics in forests with those in shade coffee plantations both adjacent to and distant from forest. Thus, in 1997 we used the same three forest plots and shade coffee close to forest plots and added three shade coffee plots at a range of distances (2–3 km) from forest. This area of Panama is characterized by rugged topography of volcanic origins. We established an area of approximately 25 ha for all study plots by walking the perimeter with a hip chain and flagging transects within the plots for reference, resulting in approximately 75 ha for each habitat type.

    Surveys were conducted between 0800 and 1800 hours. We detected ants either visually by observing a column or swarm front or aurally by listening for calls of two obligate ant‐following birds, the Ruddy Woodcreeper (Dendrocincla homochroa) and the Gray‐headed Tanager (Eucometis penicillata), or other species of facultative ant‐following birds. If a column of ants was detected, we followed the ants to the swarm front. For each swarm detected, a sample of ants was collected for later identification. Because of the aboveground nesting habits of E. burchelli, we were able to clearly differentiate between raids of different colonies swarming on the same plot during the same survey round. For L. praedator, we kept detailed records of location on the plots for each swarm observed during any particular survey period.

    We also measured two potentially important microhabitat factors contributing to the persistence of army ants in modified habitats: the availability of large downed trunks that serve as bivouac sites for E. burchelli and the availability of leaf litter in which both ant species search for prey. During the two field seasons, 33 E. burchelli stationary nests, or bivouacs, were monitored. Of these, 29 were in large downed trunks. The remaining four were inside trunks of living canopy trees where heart rot had occurred. To determine the availability of potential E. burchelli bivouac sites, we chose 10 randomly selected points in each of the nine plots. We conducted a survey within a 10‐m‐radius circle and recorded the number of downed wood logs at least 2 × 0.10 m. We measured leaf litter depth during both years because differences in annual climate can influence litter fall ( Martinez‐Yrizar & Sarukhan 1990). We measured litter depth once a month for 4 and 5 months in 1996 and 1997, respectively, in each of the nine plots. Using a standard metric ruler, we recorded 12 depth readings and calculated an average depth (cm) for each of these subplots.

    Statistical Analyses

    Because the same forest and coffee plots were surveyed over a 5‐month period, we used repeated‐measures analysis of variance (ANOVA; von Ende 1993 to test for differences between the number of army ant swarms detected in forest versus coffee habitats. The two counts per month per plot were combined into a monthly total for each species, and the mean for each of the five time periods was used for this analysis. Potential bivouac and leaf litter data were analyzed by nested one‐way ANOVA ( Dowdy & Wearden 1991). Each of the three plots for each habitat type was subsampled for potential bivouacs and litter depth, and these subplots were nested within the plots for the statistical analyses. Differences between habitats were assessed with Tukey's honestly significant difference procedure ( Dowdy & Wearden 1991). All data were analyzed with the Statistical Analysis System, version 6.12 (SAS Institute 1996 ).

    Results

    Effects of Habitat Type

    Both E. burchelli and L. praedator were found repeatedly on all forest and traditional shade coffee plots. Neither species was found on any of the sun coffee plots. Mean number of swarms of E. burchelli and L. praedator per survey round varied over space and time ( Fig. 1). There was no difference in the number of swarms detected between forest and shade coffee plots for either species of ant ( Table 1). Numbers of E. burchelli swarms in shade coffee plots equaled or exceeded those in forest for 9 of 10 surveys ( Fig. 1a). Numbers of L. praedator swarms were usually greater in forest during the dry season but were more abundant in shade coffee during two survey rounds of the wet season ( Fig. 1b). Time, referring in all cases to date of sample and not to time of day, was not a significant main effect on abundance of E. burchelli swarms ( Table 1). Time, however, significantly influenced the number of L. praedator swarms ( Table 1) because the number of swarms increased in both habitats with the onset of the rainy season (10 May) ( Fig. 1b).

    Effects of Plantation Isolation

    Both E. burchelli and L. praedator were found consistently on all forest and all traditional shade coffee plots both close to and distant from forest (  Fig. 2). For E. burchelli, there was no significant difference in the number of swarms detected among the different habitats or over time ( Table 2). For L. praedator, in contrast, both main effects of habitat and time significantly influenced the number of swarms detected ( Table 2). During the first five survey rounds, the number of L. praedator swarms was consistently higher in forest than in shade coffee plots. From late April to mid‐June, the number of swarms detected in shade coffee plots, both close to and distant from forest increased, but it exceeded only those found in forest plots during one round in June ( Fig. 2b). Again, the numbers of L. praedator swarms increased in all habitats with the onset of the rainy season. The number of swarms in shade coffee close to forest surpassed that in forest only in June (the rainy season).

    Microhabitat Characteristics

    The number of potential bivouacs available varied significantly across the four habitat types (F = 8.35, df = 3 and 8, p = 0.0076), although similar numbers of bivouac sites were available in forest and adjacent coffee habitats. Isolated shade coffee provided only half as many bivouac sites as shade coffee adjacent to forest, and less than one potential bivouac site existed on average in sun coffee plots ( Table 3). Litter depth in 1996 varied across forest, shade coffee, and sun coffee habitats (F = 5.95, df = 2 and 6, p = 0.0377; Table 3), with the greatest depth in forest and the least depth in sun coffee. In 1997, litter depth again was significantly influenced by habitat (F = 20.58, df = 2 and 6, p = 0.0021). Forest habitat had significantly higher litter depth than either adjacent or isolated shade coffee. Litter depth in the latter two habitat types were similar ( Table 3).

    Discussion

    Although the distribution and abundance of E. burchelli and L. praedator varied over time (survey rounds) and space ( plots), the persistent nature of the swarming activity of both army ant species strongly suggests that traditional shade coffee plantations provide habitat comparable to intact forest. In contrast, neither species of ant was detected in sun coffee plantations. The total lack of swarm activity in sun coffee is presumably attributable to the intensive growing techniques used in sun coffee production, which resulted in highly modified habitats devoid of bivouac sites, diverse leaf litter, and favorable microclimates.

    For L. praedator, lack of swarm activity may also be explained by the fact that this species is capable of foraging underground ( Schneirla 1971). The activity of L. praedator could have been underestimated if this species conducted night or subterranean raids. For example, Perfecto (1992) encountered the closely related species L. coecus in sun coffee plantation in Costa Rica.

    For the purpose of this study, we were primarily interested in the distribution of aboveground raids of army ant swarms because they provide foraging sites for attendant birds. E. burchelli and L. praedator are the only ant species in the Neotropics that regularly attract avian followers. If L. praedator is forced to raid underground or at night by a hotter, drier microclimate in sun coffee and unshaded areas of shade coffee plantations, then their raids are unavailable to attendant birds. Our study is the first to report the regular occurrence of E. burchelli and L. praedator and 126 species of ant‐following birds (two of which were obligate followers; Roberts 1998 at elevations between 1200 and 1800 m in premontane moist forest and shade coffee plantations, in contrast to early reports that E. burchelli is rare above 1000 m ( Willis 1967 ).

    Our results also contradict conclusions based on research of E. burchelli habitat requirements conducted in lowland Panamanian rain forests. Franks (1982) conducted intensive research on Barro Colorado Island in lowland Panama and reported that each colony of E. burchelli required about 30 ha of continuous forest. These results are based on important yet geographically limited data sets. Our research in premontane habitats in western Panama does not support these generalizations. Both army ant species can persist in modified habitats above 1000 m. On our study plots, both adjacent and distant shade coffee were all approximately 25 ha in total area. E. burchelli colonies, however, were observed migrating across the landscape and may use seemingly inhospitable habitat (i.e., pasture), at least during migration, to reach more suitable habitat for the stationary phase of their life cycle.

    How can a vulnerable top predator persist in a human‐modified landscape? One important characteristic of the close shade coffee is its proximity to forest. Especially for the smaller ant, L. praedator, the forest may serve as a refuge from high temperature and low humidity during the dry season. In general, however, shade coffee plantations in western Panama possess certain characteristics that appear favorable for army ants regardless of the proximity to forest. These include a high canopy (up to 45 m) of wide crowned trees. Canopy trees not only provide shade to a large percentage of the plantation but also provide an annual input of leaf litter onto the floor of the farm. Although there was significantly more litter in forest than in shade coffee close to forest, traditional shade coffee plantations can maintain high canopy tree diversity. This heterogeneity of litter often corresponds to a greater complexity in the litter arthropod system ( Ananthakrichnan 1996). In contrast, sun coffee plantations are a monoculture of coffee, and litter is thus homogeneous. Preliminary data from pitfall traps (unpublished) show a marked reduction in both the diversity and abundance of litter‐dwelling arthropods in sun coffee compared to forest and shade coffee. Removal of the canopy eliminates a primary source of organic matter into the system (i.e., leaf litter and woody debris). In addition, canopy trees provide nesting sites for social wasps ( Hymenoptera: Vespidae), the larvae of which form an important food source for E. burchelli, which regularly sends probing parties up tree trunks in both forest and shade coffee plots in search of wasp nests (D. L. R., personal observation). E. burchelli is thought to be a major predator on social wasp colonies in many Neotropical areas (  Jeanne 1975).

    Large trees are also a source of downed woody debris, so there was no difference in the availability of nest sites between the forest and adjacent shade coffee plots. Farther from forest, however, there were fewer downed trees available. This may be due to a combination of forest trees falling into adjacent plantations and increased human settlement near shade coffee plots that are farther from the forest, where felled trees are often milled on site. In sun coffee plots, there were close to zero potential bivouac sites available. Apparently, coarse woody debris is necessary to the continued presence of E. burchelli in these systems. Large woody debris (i.e., fallen or felled tree trunks) may take decades to decompose, and can provide habitat for army ants and other ground‐dwelling arthropods. Intensification of coffee growing methods reduces the amount of woody debris of all sizes, but especially large tree trunks.

    Removal of shade increases daily temperature fluctuations and decreases humidity. Increased temperatures and direct sun can severely alter the behaviors of both species of ant. On several occasions D. L. R. observed E. burchelli avoiding direct mid‐day sun in open, sunny areas of shade coffee plots. Upon exposure to sun, the raiding ants immediately retreated to the shade provided by a coffee plant. This appeared to greatly disrupt normal foraging behavior by decreasing the width of the swarm front. Any increase in temperature and subsequent decrease in moisture may force L. praedator to raid belowground ( I. Perfecto, personal communication). The long‐term maintenance of key habitat characteristics of mature forest is important to the continued persistence of these forest species in traditional coffee plantations.

    Our research supports a view of conservation advocated by Western (1989) and Vandermeer and Perfecto (1997 )  that encompasses a wider, less purist view of nature preservation. We cannot hope to conserve much of Earth's biological diversity in a system of parks or reserves that make up <5% of the total land area. Even if we can protect enough areas within reserves and parks, as the human population increases, activities outside the park boundaries will continue to negatively affect populations within ( Machlis & Tichnell 1985; Janzen 1986). Areas outside of the protected La Amistad Biosphere Reserve—traditional shade coffee plantations and small forest corridors—provide critical habitat for army ants and some species of ant‐following birds. Our study is part of a growing body of literature showing the conservation importance of agroecosystems, specifically traditional shade coffee plantations ( Ibarra‐Nuñez 1990; Nestel et al. 1993; Perfecto & Snelling 1995; Moguel & Toledo 1999).

    We also believe that top predator invertebrates should be elevated to a high conservation status. E. burchelli and L. praedator are relatively rare invertebrate species that require large areas in which to hunt, similar to the large vertebrate predators that attract so much conservation attention. The role of army ants in the litter community has been described by some as keystone ( Franks & Bossart 1983; Coates‐Estrada & Estrada 1989). These ant species maintain high diversity among leaf‐litter arthropod communities, acting as an intermediate disturbance factor ( Franks & Bossart 1983; Otis et al. 1986). Furthermore, E. burchelli and L. praedator act as critical links between bird species and leaf‐litter arthropods, providing an easily exploited food resource that would otherwise be unavailable for many birds and other vertebrates.

    Although the shade coffee plantations we surveyed do not represent the range of variation found among shade coffee plantations in other areas where coffee is cultivated (our study did not survey commercial polyculture or shaded monoculture systems), shade agroecosystems throughout Latin America are being rapidly converted to sun coffee plantations ( Perfecto et al. 1996; Moguel & Toledo 1999; D. L. R., personal observation). The current trend in tropical agriculture to mimic industrial agriculture in the United States and Europe will likely have dire consequences for native tropical biodiversity. For example, we predict that if the removal of remnant shade trees continues at its current rate with no replanting of canopy trees, the local populations of E. burchelli and L. praedator currently using shade coffee plantations in our study area will decline in the near future. If adequate habitat is provided for wide‐ranging army ants, other forms of biodiversity may benefit. In the case of traditionally grown shade coffee, this agroecosystem provides not only habitat for forest species but also income for local human communities.

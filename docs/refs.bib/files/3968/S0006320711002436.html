    Biological Conservation

    Volume 144, Issue 9, September 2011, Pages 2362-2368

    Spatial α-diversity patterns of diverse insect taxa in Northern China: Lessons for biodiversity conservation

    Author links open overlay panelJan C.AxmacheraYunhuiLiubChangliuWangbLiangtaoLibZhenrongYub
    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biocon.2011.06.016Get rights and content

    Abstract

    Current approaches in terrestrial biodiversity conservation focus predominantly on plants and vertebrates. While these groups account for less than 4% of the estimated global species richness, it is commonly argued that especially the species richness in higher plants is a suitable indicator of overall biodiversity. We tested this assumption, investigating species richness and equitability patterns in three highly species-rich insect families and their links with the vegetation and other environmental factors. Vegetation surveys were combined with pitfall and light trapping to establish the α-diversity of ground beetles, geometrid moths and arctiid moths on 48 plots at varying altitudes between Beijing and the Inner Mongolian Plateau. Soil pH and nutrient status were also recorded. The α-diversity patterns in the three insect families were non-congruent, and links with phytodiversity were weak. The spatial α-diversity patterns in each of the three insect families were significantly correlated with the species density of individual plant families. These links varied between the three insect taxa and were mostly negative in moths. Furthermore, geometrid moth diversity decreased with increasing elevation and decreasing soil pH. Strongly diverging α-diversity patterns across different insect taxa illustrate that it is impossible to find a simple surrogate representing cross-taxon diversity for these highly diverse groups. Furthermore, phytodiversity and vegetation composition appear to play a limited role in governing insect diversity patterns. These results underline the significant risk that current plant-focused approaches in terrestrial biodiversity conservation are inadequate in addressing the conservation needs of the vast majority of species on earth.

    Highlights

    ► α-Diversity in three diverse insect families and links with environment. ► α-Diversity patterns are highly incongruous. ► Links with vegetation diversity are very weak. ► High risk that many current biodiversity conservation approaches are inadequate.

    1. Introduction

    Knowledge of spatial diversity patterns is a key prerequisite for the development of effective strategies in biodiversity conservation (Cabeza et al., 2004, Gaston, 2000, Lamoreux et al., 2005). Such knowledge is already widely available for many vertebrate and plant taxa. Nonetheless, recent estimates (Millennium Ecosystem Assessment, 2005) suggest that these taxonomic groups contribute less than 4% of global species richness. On current knowledge, insects are by far the most species-rich group, with their estimated share in global macro-biodiversity exceeding 50%, but huge knowledge gaps currently prevail in relation to their diversity and distribution.

    Not least due to their enormous species richness, insects perform a multitude of fundamental roles in ecosystems. They act as herbivores, predators, pollinators and as a food resource for organisms at higher trophic levels. Insects also control many pest species and greatly aid in nutrient cycling (Summerville et al., 2004). It seems logical to conclude that the great knowledge gaps relating to insect diversity, their distribution patterns and the factors causing these patterns need to be addressed to enable the effective conservation of the global species pool and ecosystem functioning (Leather and Quicke, 2010).

    One way to address the severe data scarcity on mega-diverse insect groups is to develop biodiversity indicators and surrogate taxa reflecting the overall status of biodiversity. Indeed, a range of biodiversity indicators is already being used. These indicators commonly employ vertebrate and vascular plant taxa (Butchart et al., 2010), with vascular plant diversity in particular often being suggested as an indicator of overall biodiversity (Myers et al., 2000, Xu et al., 2008). Strong positive links between plant diversity and diversity of herbivores and partly also at higher trophic levels are furthermore suggested by experimental evidence (Scherber et al., 2010, Siemann et al., 1998), while studies questioning this proposed positive relationships between plant and invertebrate diversity (Axmacher et al., 2004b, Axmacher et al., 2009; Hambler and Speight, 1995, Hawkins and Porter, 2003, Wolters et al., 2006) have been widely ignored. As Lewinsohn and Roslin (2008) point out, studies directly investigating the proposed positive links between plant diversity and diversity at higher trophic levels in the natural environment are rare, and this is especially true in relation to diverse terrestrial invertebrate taxa. It is also widely unclear if spatial diversity patterns across species-rich terrestrial insect taxa are congruent. However, this congruency forms a logical prerequisite for biodiversity surrogates to work. Consequently, further comparisons of spatial α-diversity patterns across different insect taxa and the nature and strength of their links to the vegetation are urgently needed, and this study provides important insights into these topics.

    Despite our general knowledge deficits in relation to highly diverse insect taxa, more detailed data are available for several groups. One such group are ground beetles (Coleoptera:Carabidae), a species-rich family containing more than 40,000 species (Erwin, 1985) whose taxonomy, distribution and ecology are relatively well understood (Lövei and Sunderland, 1996, Niemela, 1996). Ground beetles react with high sensitivity to habitat changes, and their activity-densities can easily be recorded using pitfall traps (Bowie and Frampton, 2004, Liu et al., 2006, Liu et al., 2010, Poole et al., 2003, Thiele, 1977, Vanbergen et al., 2005). They also play important roles in biological pest control, as many ground beetles and their larvae are predatory (Kromp, 1999).

    Geometrid moths are another species-rich insect family whose diversity patterns have been extensively studied for example in Southeast Asia (Beck et al., 2002, Intachat et al., 1997, Intachat et al., 1999, Willott, 1999), South and Central America (Brehm et al., 2003, Brehm et al., 2007, Hilt et al., 2006), Australia (Kitching et al., 2000) and Africa (Axmacher et al., 2004a, Axmacher et al., 2004b, Axmacher et al., 2009). More than 21,000 geometrid species are currently known (Scoble, 1999), and their caterpillars use a wide selection of food-plants, with levels of food-plant specialization apparently highly variable across species (Robinson et al., 2010).

    Arctiidae are a moth family comprising about 11,000 known species (Watson and Goodger, 1986). Some studies suggest that arctiids profit from anthropogenic disturbances (Kitching et al., 2000, Nöske et al., 2008), possibly due to a high level of polyphagy in many arctiid caterpillars (Holloway et al., 2001). Arctiid moths, and especially members of the subfamily Arctiinae which dominate in our samples, tend to be morphologically more robust than geometrid moths.

    Moths have very strong links with the vegetation, as caterpillars and many adult moths depend on plants as a food and nectar source, while moths in return act as important pollinators (Bawa et al., 1985). They can therefore be expected to react particularly sensitive to changes in the vegetation. Nocturnal moths can be sampled effectively on artificial light sources (Muirhead-Thomson, 1991), with weak light sources allowing for standardized sampling of highly localized species assemblages (Beck and Linsenmair, 2006).

    The aim of this study is to investigate spatial patterns and correlations in the α-diversity of species-rich insect families and their links to environmental conditions. Connections between insect diversity and the vegetation form a particular focus of this study. This allows us to establish the existence and extend of the suggested positive links between plant and invertebrate α-diversity, with important implications for the validity of the widespread use of plant-based biodiversity indicators and surrogates. The soil nutrient status is well known to alter plant defence mechanisms against herbivore attack, which consequently alters the structure of the herbivore community (Campo and Dirzo, 2003, Coley et al., 1985, Siemann 1992). Soil nutrients were therefore included in our study to establish if they exert a significant influence also on the α-diversity of different insect families.

    2. Methods

    2.1. Study area and site selection

    The study area is located in the mountain ranges stretching from Beijing in the south to the Inner Mongolian Plateau in the north. Geographically, this region falls within the transition zone between the sub-humid monsoon climate of the North China Plain and the semi-arid steppe climate of the Mongolian Plateau. Maximum precipitation is encountered during the summer months, while winter conditions are extremely cold and dry. The entire region has experienced severe habitat degradation in the past due to overgrazing and transformation of steppe and forests into cropland and pastures (Chen and Zhu, 2001, Zhao et al., 2005). Recently, the resulting soil erosion triggered extensive habitat restoration and reforestation projects (Chen et al., 2007).

    Within this study area, four distinct study sites were selected. These sites were located in the vicinity of the four typical rural villages Dayushu, Gaojiaying, Baiqi and Shizigou at elevations of 500 m, 900 m, 1400 m and 1650 m, respectively (Fig. 1). Twelve study plots were established at every study site. Four of these plots represented the dominant agricultural cropping patterns and a further four the dominant natural habitat at the respective elevation. The remaining four plots represented areas experiencing low intensities of human interference, for example forest plantations, orchards and pasture ecosystems (Table 1). Individual plots measured 20 × 20 m2 and were located at a distance of >50 m from neighbouring plots. Surveys conducted on each plot included records of a set of basic soil parameters, the vegetation cover and insect assemblages.

    2.2. Analysis of soil parameters

    At each plot, a composite soil sample of about 100 g was taken. Individual samples consisted of five sub-samples extracted from the upper 20 cm of the mineral soil, taken from randomly selected locations at each plot. The samples were immediately stored in a cool box and subsequently taken to Beijing for chemical analysis. Here, the rapid dichromate oxidation method (Tiessen and Moir, 1993) was used to establish the soil organic matter content (SOM) and the Kjeldahl method (McGill and Figueiredo, 1993) to measure total nitrogen (Ntot). Soil pH was recorded after drying and sieving (pore width: 2 mm) the soil samples and adding 10 g of dry soil to 25 ml of a CaCl2 solution (0.01 mol L−1). The solution was shaken for about 2 min and left to rest for 30 min before measuring pH. These soil parameters were selected to assess the potential influence of the soil nutrient status and soil acidity on α-diversity levels in the three insect families.

    2.3. Recording of vegetation, moths and beetles

    The % cover of all vascular plant species was recorded in June and September, 2007. Trees and shrubs were recorded on the entire plots, while herbaceous species were recorded at five 2 × 2 m2 subplots located in the centre and at the four corners of each plot. June and September data for each plot were combined, allocating the maximum% cover recorded in the two datasets to each recorded plant species.

    Moths were sampled in automatic light traps. These traps consisted of a 12 V-battery operated UV light tube (Sylvania blacklight-blue, F 15W/BLB-TB; Osram, Munich) placed above a plastic funnel leading into a 10 l plastic bucket. The weak light source allows the sampling of moth assemblages occurring in the direct vicinity of the trap. Three transparent plastic panels of 20 cm × 60 cm were positioned at 120° angles facing away from the central light source to intersect the flight path of insects circling the light. Each month between May and early October in both 2006 and 2007, four light traps were operated simultaneously at each of the study sites during 6-day periods around the new moon (Yela and Holyoak, 1997). Moths on each plot were sampled twice per month, with trapping occurring continuously between 19.00 h and 23.00 h local time.

    Ground beetles were sampled each month between May and early October in both 2006 and 2007 over the same monthly 6-day period when light traps were operated. To collect the beetles, an array of eight pitfall traps were positioned at distances of four and seven metres from the plot centre along two N–S and E–W-facing diagonal lines intersecting in the plot centre. Plastic beakers with a diameter of 8 cm and a height of 11.5 cm were used as traps. They were partly filled with 75% alcohol to kill and preserve the specimens. Each trap was protected from rain by a small aluminium roof positioned approximately 5 cm above the soil surface. Samples from individual pitfall traps were later combined for further analysis.

    2.4. Data analysis

    In a first step, measures of α-diversity were calculated for both vegetation and arthropod taxa. In the case of plant species, three diversity measures were used. The total number of plant species per plot was counted as a direct measure of plant species density (sensu Whittaker et al., 2001). Additionally, the two α-diversity indices Shannon diversity (Shannon’s H) and Simpson’s diversity (Simpson’s D) were calculated using the program Species Diversity and Richness 3.02 (Pisces Conservation, 2002). As outlined by Jost (2006), Shannon’s entropy was converted into a diversity measure using an exponential conversion. Simpson’s diversity was furthermore calculated as the inverse of the Simpson’s static, again following the approach outlined in Jost (2006).

    It is impossible to obtain complete inventories for species-rich assemblages of mobile organisms, so that the number of species per sample provides only a poor representation of α-diversity (Gotelli and Colwell, 2001). In these cases, the observed number of species depends strongly on sample size and sampling effort. Consequently, different sets of α-diversity indices need to be used to assess plant and insect samples. In this study, Fisher’s α of the log series was chosen as measure of insect α-diversity. This diversity index has been successfully employed in a wide range of studies on ground beetles (Fournier and Loreau, 1999, Liu et al., in press) and moths (Axmacher et al., 2004a, Axmacher et al., 2004b, Axmacher et al., 2009, Brehm et al., 2003, Schulze and Fiedler, 2003). Other measures commonly applied to assess α-diversity in mobile organisms are estimators approximating the total number of species occurring at a site (Colwell and Coddington, 1994). Nonetheless, Fisher’s α is generally more robust in relation to differences in sample size and therefore more reliable than these species richness estimators (Axmacher and Kühne, 2008, Brehm et al., 2003). Furthermore, Fisher’s α values for each insect family were significantly correlated (P < 0.05) with a variety of widely used species richness estimators, including ACE, Chao 1 and Chao 2. This indicates that Fisher’s α values provide a reasonable approximation of species density, too. It must also be acknowledged that both pitfall and light traps record activity-density, rather than providing a “true” representation of the species present. Nonetheless, as sampling was standardized across all plots, any bias created can be expected to influence samples in a similar way.

    To avoid the problem of multicollinearity and to account for the large number of plant species and families encountered, principal components (PCs) were used to represent the vegetation and environmental data as predictor variables in regression models (Axmacher et al., 2009). A total of three different sets of PCs were created: A first principal components analysis (PCA1) was based on the % cover of all individual plant species per plot. This ordination provides a representation of the community composition within the plant communities. A second PCA (PCA2) was calculated for the species density of each plant family per plot. The third analysis (PCA3) finally was based on the three plant diversity indices in combination with the complete environmental data set. The resulting sets of principal components were used consecutively as predictors of α-diversity of the three insect families in a series of step-wise multiple linear regression (MLR) models.

    3. Results

    A total of 410 plant species belonging to 244 genera and 69 families were recorded in the study area. The pitfall traps contained 3663 ground beetles, which were subsequently divided into 59 species and 14 morpho-species. The light traps caught 14,692 geometrid moths, which were divided into 96 species and 14 morpho-species. Light trap samples also included 1543 arctiid moths representing 19 species and one morpho-species (see Table S1 in Supporting information).

    3.1. α-Diversity

    The plant species density ranged from 15 to 98 species. The diversity indices representing phytodiversity varied between 2.1 and 52.4 for Shannon’s diversity, which represents values between 0.5 and 3.8 for Shannon’s entropy H, and between 1.2 and 31.5 for Simpson’s D. Fisher’s α varied between 0.3 and 12.1 for individual ground beetle samples, between 3.6 and 32.4 for geometrid moth assemblages and between 0.5 and 14.1 in arctiid moth samples. A combination of all samples resulted in an overall Fisher’s α value of 12.9 for Carabidae, 16.1 for Geometridae and 3.2 for Arctiidae.

    A comparison of α-diversity patterns between the four taxonomic groups (Fig. 2) revealed that neither ground beetle diversity nor arctiid moth diversity were significantly correlated (P < 0.05) with the α-diversity of the other taxonomic groups, while values of Fisher’s α in geometrid moths showed a significant negative correlations with plant species number per plot (Pearson correlation coefficient R = −0.34, P = 0.018) as well as with Shannon diversity (Pearson correlation coefficient R = −0.45, P = 0.001).

    3.2. Principal components analysis of floristic and environmental parameters

    Based on the percentage cover of all individual plant species per plot, PCA1 resulted in a total of 44 PCs (see Table S2 in Supporting information). Nonetheless, many of these PCs only represented a very small proportion of the variance originally contained in the dataset. It was therefore decided to only include PCs representing more than 2% of the original variance in further analysis. Jointly, these 19 PCs accounted for >75% of the original variance.

    Based on the species density of each plant family per plot, PCA2 resulted in 18 PCs (see Table S3 in Supporting information), while PCA 3 based on all environmental parameters and plant diversity indices yielded 2 PCs. One of the two PCs chiefly represented the set of all environmental parameters, while the second represented the plant diversity indicators (see Table S4 in Supporting information).

    3.3. Links between insect α-diversity and the floristic and environmental parameters

    3.3.1. Plant species composition

    A first set of regression models was calculated based on the components of PCA 1, representing occurrence and abundance of individual plant species as predictors of insect diversity. These PCs proved to be poor predictors of moth diversity, but they had a statistically significant influence on ground beetle diversity. Accordingly, Fisher’s α in ground beetles (F1,45 = 7.6, Radj2 = 0.13, P = 0.008) was strongly linked to changes in PC2 (β = −0.38, P = 0.008), indicating a positive link between the α-diversity of ground beetles and the 17 plant species having a high negative loading on this axis (Adenophora divaricata, Allium neriniflorum, Caragana microphylla, Cleistogenes caespitosa, Dracocephalum rupestre, Leontopodium smithianum, Lespedeza davurica, Leymus dasystachys, Ostryopsis davidiana, Patrinia rupestris, Phlomis tuberosa, Rhamnus parvifolia, Rosa davurica, Salvia umbratica, Scorzonera albicaulis, Sedum aizoon and Toxicodendron delavayi).

    3.3.2. Species density in individual plant families

    PCs based on the species density in individual plant families were significantly linked to the α-diversity of all insect families, but with different principal components and therefore different plant families being included in the individual regression models. Fisher’s α of ground beetles (F7,39 = 4.3, Radj2 = 0.33, P = 0.001) was strongly associated with PC 4 (β = −0.45, P < 0.001), reflecting a positive link between ground beetle diversity and the species density of four plant families: Anacardiaceae, Rhamnaceae, Crassulaceae and Berberidaceae. PC18 also contributed significantly towards the variation in ground beetle diversity (β = −0.26, P = 0.03), but no single plant family loaded >|0.53| on this component. The model predicting Fisher’s α of geometrid moths (F8,38 = 3.8, Radj2 = 0.32, P = 0.002) included PC13 (β = −0.35, P = 0.007), PC10 (β = 0.35, P = 0.007) and PC1 (β = −0,28, P = 0.025). According to this model, geometrid α-diversity was linked positively to the occurrence of Simaroubaceae, which had a high negative loading on PC13. Furthermore, α-diversity of geometrids appeared to be negatively linked to a wide range of potential host plant families including, for example, Fabaceae, Asteraceae, Rosaceae, and Ranunculaceae, which all had a high positive loading on PC1, and with Brassicaceae and Malvaceae, which both had a high negative loading on PC 10. PC 13 was the only significant factor included in the model for Fisher’s α of Arctiidae (F3,43 = 3.5, Radj2 = 0.14, P = 0.02), but reflecting a negative rather than positive association between tiger moths and species density in Simaroubaceae.

    3.3.3. Plant diversity and environmental parameters

    The two PCs of PCA3 had no significant contribution towards explaining ground beetle or arctiid moth α-diversity. The regression model predicting Fisher’s α in geometrids (F2,45 = 6.56, Radj2 = 0.19, P = 0.003) indicated a significant negative link between geometrid α-diversity and PC1 (β = −0.40, P = 0.004), which represented a decreasing pH as well as an increase in elevation, conductivity and soil nitrate. Furthermore, increasing geometrid diversity was also linked to a decrease in phytodiversity, although this link was distinctly weaker (PC2; (β = −0.26, P = 0.056).

    Subsequent ANOVA results showed that the diversity of none of the invertebrate taxa was significantly different at agricultural areas in comparison to either woodland or open grassland and bushland habitats. In contrast, all indices of phytodiversity were significantly lower in agricultural fields than in both woodland (Scheffé-Test: P < 0.026 in all cases) and open grassland/bushland (Scheffé-Test: P < 0.0001 in all cases), and also significantly lower in woodland than in open grassland/bushland habitats (Scheffé-Test: P < 0.006 in all cases).

    With increasing elevation, geometrid moth diversity significantly decreased (R = −0.55, P < 0.001). Overall, elevation on its own was a better predictor for geometrid moth diversity than the regression model based on PCA3. Neither Fisher’s α of ground beetles nor Fisher’s α of arctiid moths was significantly different at any of the four different altitudinal levels representing the four study sites (ANOVA: F3,44 = 0.63, P = 0.60 and F3,44 = 0.67, P = 0.58, respectively).

    4. Discussion

    Our results provide a clear indication that spatial α-diversity patterns diverge strongly between different insect taxa, and that they also vary significantly in relation to their associations with the vegetation and other environmental parameters. These findings lend support to the increasing number of studies reporting missing or weak positive links in diversity patterns between invertebrate taxa and other taxonomic groups (Axmacher et al., 2004b, Axmacher et al., 2009, Basset et al., 2008, Beck et al., 2011, Gaston, 2000, Hawkins and Porter, 2003, Holloway et al., 1992, Juutinen and Monkkonen, 2004, Kotze and Samways, 1999, Lawler and White, 2008, Lawton et al., 1998, Negi and Gadgil, 2002, Nöske et al., 2008, Reid, 1998, Ricketts et al., 2002, Wolters et al., 2006). Our results are of particular importance as they comprise four highly diverse taxa which additionally represent different trophic levels. The non-congruent patterns between the α-diversity in these taxa suggest that it is practically impossible to find a simple surrogate representing cross-taxon diversity within some of the most species-rich groups on our planet (see also Prendergast et al., 1993). These outcomes furthermore reflect the pronounced risks that current approaches in biodiversity conservation which use plant- and vertebrate-based surrogates to assess the overall status and trends in biodiversity (Butchart et al., 2010) are potentially highly misrepresentative of patterns in the vast majority of species. In this context, the assumption that plant-based “Biodiversity hotspots” sensu Myers et al. (2000) and Myers (2003) represent hotspots for the majority of species also appears extremely dubious.

    The weakness in links between the α-diversity of the three insect taxa and the α-diversity of the vegetation is particularly noteworthy given the fine grain size employed in this study, which could be expected to particularly emphasise the role of biotic factors in explaining changes in α-diversity (see Hortal, 2008, Whittaker et al., 2001). The apparent strong influence of abiotic environmental parameters somewhat contradicts arguments that species richness-environment associations are far less pronounced at such finely grained scales (Belmarker and Jetz, 2011). Perhaps even more surprisingly, the strongest direct link between the α-diversity in insects and plants showed that Fisher’s α in herbivorous geometrid moth assemblages actually decreased with increasing plant diversity. A similar pattern has already been described for geometrid moths from Mt. Kilimanjaro (Axmacher et al., 2004b). In this earlier study, we suggested that extreme levels of humidity might limit the habitat quality for geometrid moth species. More diverse moth assemblages were therefore encountered in open, disturbed habitats characterized by dryer, warmer microclimatic conditions and lower plant species richness. Nonetheless, in this study, plant diversity actually peaked in open grassland and bushland habitats, while geometrid diversity was not significantly higher in these habitats. This indicates that different factors must be involved. These factors appear to be widely independent of the vegetation, as neither individual plant species nor specific potential food-plant families were positively related to geometrid moth diversity. Species in Simaroubaceae, the only plant family positively correlated with geometrid diversity, are to current knowledge (Robinson et al., 2010) only used by very few geometrids as a caterpillar food plant. While large knowledge gaps still persist in relation to food-plant requirements of moths, it is highly likely that abiotic environmental parameters play the key role in explaining geometrid diversity. This argument is in line with observations by Hawkins and Porter (2003) on butterflies, who state that environmental conditions, rather than plant species richness, are chiefly responsible for determining butterfly diversity.

    In previous studies of pronounced altitudinal gradients, elevation has commonly been identified as a key driver in explaining distinct patterns in α-diversity of invertebrate taxa (Brehm et al., 2007, Rahbek, 1995, Rahbek, 2005). The important role elevation plays in explaining α-diversity patterns is not surprising given the multitude of factors generally associated with increasing altitude including temperature, radiation, air pressure and precipitation. Acknowledging these factors and the effects they appear to have on geometrid communities especially at the lower parts of our altitudinal transect, it was somewhat unexpected that both the diversity in arctiid moths and ground beetles did not vary significantly between any of the four distinct altitudinal levels. The complexity in our approach might partly explain this lack of a distinct altitudinal trend. As Axmacher and Fiedler (2008) reported from Tanzania, differences in habitat type can already result in widely differing responses in invertebrate diversity patterns. Furthermore, many of the factors generally put forward to explain clear altitudinal trends show highly unusual patterns in our study area. A general decrease in diversity is often related to an interaction of factors including a reduction in the overall available area with increasing elevation on mountains, a reduction in resource availability linked to either a decreasing plant diversity or decreasing levels in primary productivity, water and energy availability, mid-domain effects and overlaps between lowland and mountain faunas (Beck and Chey, 2008, Lawton et al., 1987), although clear empirical evidence for the role of any of these factors is scarce (see e.g. Beck and Kitching, 2009).

    A decrease in total available area with increasing altitude observed at many mountains and mountain ranges does not apply in our case, as our study area connects two very large altitudinal plateaus – the North China Plain (NCP) close to sea level and the Inner Mongolian Plateau with elevations mostly above 1000 m. Area is therefore most limited at mid-elevations, but given the large extent of the mountain ranges surrounding Beijing and parts of the NCP, extensive areas are available even at intermediate altitudes. The second argument, a decrease in resource availability, must again be clearly negated for the study area at least in relation to phytodiversity, which shows a clear tendency to increase with increasing elevation. This relates to the fact that the lowest sections of the study area experience the highest degree of human pressure, as they are located in proximity to the capital Beijing and utilized to produce a wider variety of crops under intensive cultivation. Anthropogenic pressure decreases with increasing elevation, as cropping regimes becoming less intensive and the proportion of semi-natural and natural habitats increases. On the other hand, the supply of energy strongly decreases with increasing elevation, with extremely cold temperatures experienced in the increasingly long winters at areas close to Inner Mongolia. To further complicate these settings, it might be expected that geometric constrains related to Mid-Domain effects and species range overlaps will lead to two α-diversity peaks, one in areas slightly above the NCP, as well as a second slightly below the Inner Mongolian Plateau. Taken together, the complexity of interacting factors potentially explains the lack of a clear trend in altitudinal diversity patterns for both arctiid moths and ground beetles.

    5. Conclusion

    Overall, it appears increasingly clear that vegetation composition and phytodiversity play a subordinate role in explaining the diversity patterns of herbivorous and predatory insects. With many current approaches in terrestrial biodiversity conservation focused on plants and vertebrates, which themselves represent only a minute fraction of global species richness, we are therefore running a severe risk of completely ignoring the conservation needs of the vast majority of species. As diversity patterns in species-rich insect taxa have proven to be highly complex and widely independent, more research is urgently needed to generate in-depth insights into the biogeography of such mega-diverse taxa, allowing us to adequately address their conservation needs.

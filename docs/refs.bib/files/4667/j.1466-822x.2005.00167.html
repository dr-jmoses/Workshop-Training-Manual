ABSTRACT

Aim To determine the main factors that control the distribution of palm species richness across the Americas, to understand the relative importance of climatic and other environmental factors vs. spatial variables (as substitutes for non‐environmental factors such as history), and to evaluate how robust the patterns found are to changes in spatial scale.

Location Tropical and subtropical America (34°N−34°S; 33°W−120°W).

Methods We provide a GIS‐based analysis of spatial variation in species richness at 10 grain sizes (1°−10° square grid cells). We use multiple regressions and variation partitioning to test patterns of species richness in relation to five environmental descriptors [mean annual temperature (°C), mean annual precipitation (mm year−1), number of wet days year−1, topographical range (maximum–minimum elevation), number of vegetation types] and nine spatial variables (the terms of a cubic trend‐surface polynomial).

Results We observe peaks in palm species richness in southern Mesoamerica, the Chocó area, western and eastern Amazon basin, and east Andean slopes, and a strong latitudinal richness gradient even within the tropical zone. Pure environmental and pure spatial variation components are of equal magnitude through all spatial scales analysed. Water‐related variables such as annual rainfall and number of wet days are the main richness predictors. Spatial variables, notably latitude squared are also important, whereas temperature‐ and habitat‐related variables are relatively unimportant.

Main conclusions Our analysis suggests that in the Americas, palm species richness at spatial scales from 1° to 10° is most strongly controlled by water availability, although unknown broad‐scale factors, perhaps soil, historical processes or geometric constraints, are also important.

INTRODUCTION

How patterns of species richness are shaped by the interaction of the present day environmental and historical factors remains an intriguing yet controversial issue in biogeography (e.g. Brown & Lomolino, 1998). Historical explanations dominated until the 1950s, after which local‐scale community processes in conjunction with the physical environment, notably climate, have been emphasized as the main drivers of large‐scale richness patterns (reviewed in Ricklefs, 2004). The importance of the physical environment for species richness patterns was emphasized in two recent studies that covered a wide range of plant and animal groups (Francis & Currie, 2003; Hawkins et al., 2003). They showed that in 82 out of 85 investigated cases, water and energy variables explained on average over 60% of the variation in species richness, and that water was the dominant factor at tropical latitudes, whereas water–energy dynamics appeared to control species richness in temperate and arctic zones. Given local determinism, we expect that richness will not vary between comparable habitats in different regions (Ricklefs, 2004). Nevertheless, examples of conspicuous differences in species richness between apparently identical habitats located in different regions suggest that historical factors also influence geographical species richness patterns (Ricklefs & Latham, 1999; Qian & Ricklefs, 2000; Ricklefs, 2004; Turner, 2004). Historical factors sensuRicklefs (2004) include past large‐scale environmental perturbations, region area and other factors, which affect species richness through speciation, migration and extinction rates (e.g. Rosenzweig, 1995; Rosenzweig, 1997; Ricklefs & Latham, 1999; Turner, 2004). A recent proposal is that richness patterns may be driven by geometric constraints, i.e. geographical features such as continental margins that limit species’ ranges by acting as dispersal barriers (Colwell & Lees, 2000; Willig et al., 2003; Colwell et al., 2004), perhaps through their impact on immigration and extinction (Grytnes, 2003).

In congruence with local determinism, greater palm species richness is traditionally associated with warm and humid climates (Corner, 1966). The intolerance of palms to low temperatures and their latitudinal range limitation are directly evidenced in the horticultural literature (Henderson et al., 1995; Jones, 1995; Francko, 2003). However, naturalization of palm species beyond their native ranges (Henderson et al., 1995; Landry & Reese, 1996; Walther, 2000; Sunderland & Morakinyo, 2002; Svenning, 2002) and known regional extinctions in the past (Uhl & Dransfield, 1987; Rull, 1998) suggest that current diversity patterns may also reflect history. Furthermore, Bachman et al. (2004) recently concluded that palm species richness patterns on New Guinea are largely governed by island geometry.

Here, we use regression analyses to investigate the factors that control palm species richness across the Americas. Specifically, we asked the following questions. Which factors are the main richness determinants? What is the relative importance of climatic and other environmental vs. spatial variables (as substitutes for non‐environmental factors such as history)? Are the patterns found robust to changes in spatial scale (i.e. map resolutions from 1° to 10° grid squares)? Because the processes that govern species richness may be scale‐dependent, both in time and in space (Willis & Whittaker, 2002; Willig et al., 2003), their relative importance may depend on the grain sizes employed in the analyses (e.g. Rahbek & Graves, 2000, 2001).

METHODS

We scanned distribution maps for all 550 native American palm species from Henderson et al. (1995) and imported them into ArcGIS 8.2 (ESRI Inc., Redlands, California, USA), digitizing and geo‐referencing them at a 1° grid square resolution. These distribution maps represent the only data currently available for all of our study area (34° N−34° S; 33° W−120° W). We generated 14 explanatory variables for each grid cell of this area. The five environmental variables were: (1) mean annual temperature (°C); (2) annual precipitation (mm year−1); (3) number of wet days per year (variables 1–3 obtained from and following definitions of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, IPCC, Data Distribution Centre, http://www.ipcc.ch); (4) topographical range (maximum–minimum elevation, extracted from the Digital Elevation Model from United States Geologic Survey, USGS; http://edc.usgs.gov); and (5) number of vegetation types computed at each grain size from a vegetation map with a resolution of 1 : 20,000,000 (Bletter et al., 2004) using the majority type option in the Zonal Statistics function in Spatial Analyst (McCoy & Johnston, 2002).

To the extent that historical factors or geometry‐induced mid‐domain effects (Colwell & Lees, 2000) are not completely collinear with the present day environmental factors, they will leave their mark as broad‐scale spatial patterns, which cannot be explained by spatial variation in the environment (Ricklefs, 2004). Hence, in our analysis such non‐environmental factors were represented by nine spatial variables (6–14), namely latitude (Y), centred longitude (X) as well as X2, XY, Y2, X3, X2Y, XY2 and Y3 (i.e. the terms of cubic polynomial trend surface), as these allow representation of broad‐scale spatial patterns (Legendre, 1993; Legendre & Legendre, 1998). Latitude, which was already symmetric in extent around the equator, was not centred.

We used stepwise multiple regression with mixed selection (P–enter and P–leave = 0.05) to estimate the best, non‐redundant set of the explanatory variables and rank the individual explanatory variables according to their importance at each scale. We employed a heuristic variable selection method, since the large number of explanatory variables precluded variable selection by consideration of all possible variable subsets, an otherwise preferable procedure. We emphasized the likely most robust aspects of the stepwise regression results, namely the strongest and most consistently selected variables. We used partial regression analysis to separate the variation in species richness into its pure environmental (RPE), pure spatial (RPS), mixed spatial‐environmental (RMX) and unexplained fractions (RUN) for each of 10 spatial scales with grid sizes varying from 1° to 10° (Legendre & Legendre, 1998). Following Legendre and Legendre (1998), the partitioning was implemented using multiple regressions on all 14 explanatory variables, the five environmental variables (1–5) alone and the nine spatial variables (variables 6–14) alone, to compute the total explained variation (R2 = RT), environmentally explained variation (RE) and spatially explained variation (RS), respectively. We calculated the pure spatial, pure environmental, mixed environmental‐spatial and undetermined fractions as RPS = RT–RE; RPE = RT–RS; RMX = RE + RS–RT; and RUN = RT−1 (Legendre & Legendre, 1998; also cf. Borcard et al., 1992; Legendre, 1993; Lobo et al., 2001).

Following Rahbek and Graves (2000, 2001), we assessed the scale dependency of our results by repeating the analyses 10 times at map resolutions varying from 1° to 10° grid squares.

RESULTS

The greatest palm species richness in the Americas is found in the Chocó region where the most species‐rich grid cell includes 83 species. Similar species richness is found in the contiguous Mesoamerican Isthmus (Panama, Costa Rica) where there may be as many as 78 species per grid cell. The third most species‐rich area, with a maximum of 69 species per grid cell, is found in the western part of the Amazon basin in northern Peru, which is contiguous with a long band along the eastern slopes of tropical Andes having up to 57 species per grid cell. The same maximum number of species per grid cell is found in the area stretching from the eastern part of the Amazon basin from Manaos and Santarem north to French Guiana (Fig. 1a).

Generally, the most species‐rich grid cells all have warm and humid climates with mean annual precipitation  2400 mm,  160 wet days per year and mean annual temperature  21 °C. Palm species richness declines strongly with latitude, even within 10° N to 10° S latitude (Fig. 2).

The final stepwise regression models explained 71−82% of the variation in species richness (Table 1, Fig. 1b,c,d), with no correlation between R2 and grain size (Spearman rank correlation, rS = −0.24, P = 0.51). One or both of the water variables (annual precipitation, number of wet days) were the most important variable(s) in the final regression models at seven out of 10 grain sizes, and annual precipitation was the only environmental variable that was significant at all grain sizes (Table 1). For the three remaining grain sizes, at least one of the water variables was among the second and third most important variables. However, latitude squared was also significant at all 10 grain sizes and was always the dominant spatial variable (Table 1). It is noteworthy that a strong latitudinal relationship persisted despite the inclusion of environmental variables in the modelling. Longitude (X) and squared longitude (X2) shared the position as the second‐most important spatial variable (Table 1). Habitat related variables and temperature were of minor and negligible importance, respectively (Table 1).

Variation partitioning of palm species richness for all scales (1°−10° grid squares) showed that RMX was the greatest variation component at all grain sizes (Fig. 3), whereas RPE and RPS were of equal magnitude, albeit with RPE generally slightly greater.

DISCUSSION

Across the Americas, the palm family is most species‐rich in the Chocó and the adjacent Mesoamerica, along the eastern slopes of the Andes, in the western and the eastern parts of the Amazon basin and in general along an Equatorial belt (Fig. 1a). This picture coincides in many ways with the global distribution patterns of angiosperm species (Barthlott et al., 1996) and to a lesser degree with the map of global variation in the number of angiosperm families (Francis & Currie, 2003). The Chocó and the eastern slopes of the Andes, which are both very rich in palm species, correspond to the richest and second richest global diversity centre (Barthlott et al., 1996).

Our findings support the commonly held view that palms are most species rich in humid tropical areas (Henderson et al., 1995; Jones, 1995) (Fig. 1a,b). The environmental component of the predicted species richness per 1° grid cells predicted well the extraordinary high species richness of the wet western Amazon and the Chocó region (Fig. 1c). Notably, water‐related factors (annual precipitation and/or number of wet days) were the most important predictors of palm species richness at all grain sizes investigated. At smaller grain sizes, the number of wet days was most important, whereas the annual precipitation was the dominant factor at larger grain sizes (Table 1). This agrees with the generally strong positive correlation between plant and animal species richness and precipitation in the Neotropics (Gentry, 1988; Rahbek & Graves, 2001), and also with the generalization that in tropical to warm temperate zones, water‐related variables represent the strongest environmental predictors of broad‐scale geographical patterns of species richness (Hawkins et al., 2003). According to Francis and Currie (2003), this pattern arises due to a globally consistent relationship between richness and climate (in particular water and heat) that persists worldwide in spite of regional differences in evolutionary and geological histories. Other environmental variables such as temperature and habitat heterogeneity were clearly subordinate to humidity in explaining continental‐scale variation in palm species richness. In contrast, topographical and ecosystem diversity are strong determinants of bird species richness in South America (Rahbek & Graves, 2001; also cf. Turner, 2004). Several aspects of our results suggest that in addition to climate, other broad‐scale factors are important controls of the observed patterns of palm species richness.

At all grain sizes, the largest variation fraction was the mixed environmental‐spatial variation fraction (Fig. 3). This fraction could reflect either spatially structured environmental factors or non‐environmental factors varying in a collinear, but non‐causal manner with the present day environment due to the underlying space‐structuring processes (Borcard et al., 1992; Ricklefs, 2004). For example, areas that today hold the wettest tropical forests and the highest palm species richness, such as the Chocó area and the western equatorial Amazon basin, also coincide with areas that remained wet during Pleistocene climatic fluctuations (Hooghiemstra & van der Hammen, 1998). Hence, the high species richness in these areas could reflect long‐term climatic stability, i.e. history (cf. Ricklefs, 2004 and Turner, 2004 for evolutionary scenarios), rather than current climate per se.

At all grain sizes, the pure spatial variation fraction was significant and close to RPE in size (Fig. 3), and latitude squared and, to a lesser extent, longitude and longitude squared were consistently important predictors of palm species richness in the stepwise regressions. These spatial patterns could reflect insufficient modelling of diversity environmental relationships, including missing environmental factors, e.g. broad‐scale variation in soils is known to occur in the Neotropical region (e.g. Sioli, 1984). However, Clinebell et al. (1995) found that soil was only a weak determinant of broad‐scale variation in local diversity of trees and lianas in the Neotropics. Alternatively, the spatial patterns could also be caused by broad‐scale non‐environmental factors. In fact, a number of plausible non‐environmental drivers can be hypothesized to influence continental scale palm species richness patterns in the Americas.

    Geometric mid‐domain effects (Colwell & Lees, 2000; Grytnes, 2003; Zapata et al., 2003) would cause species richness to peak in the centre of the large Amazonian region (cf. Diniz‐Filho et al., 2002), perhaps biased somewhat to north‐west by the Centroamerican connection.

  • 2

    If geographical variation in metacommunity area controls palm species richness patterns in the Americas (Turner, 2004), we likewise expect richness to peak in central Amazonia. In fact, a recent study of palm species richness patterns on New Guinea concluded that the effect of area and the mid‐domain effect explain most of the richness variation (Bachman et al., 2004).

  • 3

    Biogeographical mixing between North and South America (Burnham & Graham, 1999; Pennington & Dick, 2004) would cause species richness to peak in the Costa Rica‐Panama‐Colombia region.

  • 4

    Increased speciation as a result of Andean orogeny and subsequent spill‐over effects (Young et al., 2002) would cause species richness to peak in the Andes and neighbouring lowland areas.

  • 5

    If the Andean orogeny effect on diversity interacts positively with a humid, wet climate (Rahbek & Graves, 2001) or contains a nested mid‐domain effect, species richness would peak in the middle part of the Andes and adjacent lowlands.

  • 6

    If western Amazonia constituted the main Ice Age rain forest refugia in the Americas (Hooghiemstra & Van der Hammen, 1998), a similar species richness pattern is predicted.

As is evident from Fig. 1(d), the predicted species richness produced by the spatial variables in the final stepwise model for palm species richness only conforms well to the pattern predicted by the last two historical hypotheses. Hereby, our findings echo those of Rahbek and Graves (2001) for South American birds.

CONCLUSIONS

The present study documents that in the Americas, palm species richness is highest in humid tropical areas and that a strong decline in palm species richness occurs with latitude even within 10° N to 10° S. The continental‐scale patterns in palm species richness seem mainly to be controlled by water availability (annual precipitation and number of wet days) and by unknown broad‐scale spatial factors. We interpret our results to suggest that although current climate is probably the dominant driver of geographical variation in palm species richness on a bicontinental scale in the Americas, other broad‐scale factors such as soils, historical processes or geometric constraints may also be important.

    BIOSKETCHES
    Stine Bjorholm studies American palm biogeography with the aid of GIS techniques and is particularly interested in past and present macroscale distribution patterns.
    Jens‐Christian Svenning is interested in biogeography, macroecology and global change biology, focusing mainly on temperate and tropical forest plants.
    Flemming Skov is carrying out spatial modelling of species distributions and biodiversity using GIS. His main interest is management of temperate and tropical forest biodiversity in relation to global climatic change and human land use.
    Henrik Balslev is interested in Neotropical botany with broad emphasis on palms, their taxonomy, economic botany, ethnobotany and community ecology and heads the palm research group at the University of Aarhus.

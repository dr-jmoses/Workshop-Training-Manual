Beta diversity: synthesis and a guide for the perplexed

Robert K. Colwell
First published: 04 March 2010
https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1600-0587.2009.06412.x
Citations: 23

This issue hosts back‐to‐back papers by Hanna Tuomisto on beta diversity (Tuomisto 2010a, b), a milestone in Ecography's commitment to the review and synthesis of the concepts that lie at the heart of the study of broad spatial and temporal patterns. That biotas are complexly heterogeneous in space and time is obvious, but how best to quantify and compare that heterogeneity is far from obvious. When Tuomisto accepted Ecography's invitation to prepare “a synthetic and critical review on the topic of beta diversity” three years ago, she surely had no idea what was in store for her, but her commitment and determination were unswerving. Citing more than 200 references, Tuomisto plots a course through the conceptual and mathematical thicket that beta diversity has become since Robert H. Whittaker planted the seeds half a century ago this year (Whittaker 1960).

Tuomisto strives to develop a common mathematical and notational framework for the many different constructs that have been called “beta diversity”, unearthing some surprising and novel links and synonymies between some variants, while digging out and simplifying the fundamental differences that distinguish others. Do not expect a mere catalogue of begats. Tuomisto champions measures of “true diversity”, which treat species richness and the numbers equivalents of Shannon's and Simpson's diversity indices as points along a single, continuous mathematical continuum, as first proposed by Hill (1973). Partitioning “true” gamma diversity multiplicatively (rather than additively) into alpha and beta components allows a unified treatment of not only alpha and gamma, but also beta diversity as a count of an effective number of types of entities (Routledge 1977, 1979, Jost 2006, 2007).

The first paper (Tuomisto 2010a) sets out the framework and notation, focusing on beta diversity as a straightforward component of gamma diversity. In the second paper, Tuomisto (2010b) brings this conceptual and mathematical structure to bear on the remarkable variety of ways that the idea of “beta diversity” has been applied to data. Each of about thirty different variants of beta is not only defined mathematically, but painstakingly translated into a verbal statement of what it measures. Species turnover, distance‐decay of similarity, species‐area curves, and the biases and constraints that limit the extrapolation of these measures in practice all receive careful attention. In the second paper, many of the measures that have been called “beta diversity” are graphically distinguished in a single, conceptual figure, and all are summarized in a tabular guide to both papers.

As ecologists, biogeographers, and macroecologists have increasingly applied and reinterpreted classic ecological concepts on spatial scales from landscapes to continents or ocean basins, beta diversity and related concepts have risen high in the list of our conceptual tools, but often in incommensurate and even contradictory formalizations. Tuomisto's two‐part review offers, at the very least, a rigorously defined framework for further development, and at best provides a clearer view of the way forward.

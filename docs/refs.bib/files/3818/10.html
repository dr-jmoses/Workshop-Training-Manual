Spatial distribution of insect guilds in a tropical montane rainforest: effects of canopy structure and numerically dominant ants

Abstract

Insect guild abundance and species richness responses to numerically dominant ant species, seasonality, and canopy structure were analysed in a successional montane tropical rainforest. Samples were taken in wet and dry seasons at three sites that had been subject to different past land use (low, intermediate, and high disturbance) and have been protected since 1967. We took two habitat scales (isolated tree crowns and canopy segments) and three categories for numerically dominant ants (presence of only one, or more than one species, or absence of dominant ant). Our results show that the larger the crown is, the bigger the chance to find herbivores is. Total insect species abundance, sap-sucking species richness, and prey abundance were higher in the low disturbance site, where the largest crowns were found, but simpler canopies showed the greatest frequency of dominant ants, and the lowest abundance of chewing insects. Sap-sucking species were more abundant in the low disturbance site, but mostly on crowns with dominant ant species. Dominant ant abundance was higher, and leaf-chewer species abundance was lower, in the high disturbance site. At the canopy scale, sap-sucking and prey species abundance and richness were higher in the low disturbance site, where canopy heterogeneity was the greatest, whereas leaf-chewers did not respond to disturbance at all. Sap-sucking species abundance was higher in areas with a simple ant species dominance. This is the first work to show how canopy insects are concomitantly affected by numerically dominant ants and canopy structure in this Atlantic rainforest ecosystem.

Introduction

Insect herbivores may respond to the canopy and tree crown structures (Casas and Djemai 2002; Neves et al. 2013), or to leaf ecophysiologic traits (Ribeiro and Basset 2007). Nevertheless, little is actually understood about the distribution of herbivore feeding guilds in the canopy, or how much distribution relates to overall vegetation or to combined factors (but see Ribeiro and Basset 2007; Neves et al. 2010, 2013). Relative abundance and distribution of insect herbivores in the canopy of seasonal tropical forests differ from those observed in evergreen tropical forests (Gonçalves et al. 2005; Ribeiro et al. 2008; Neves et al. 2013). This difference may be partly due to the influence of vegetation, but also to the negative response of insect populations to cold or dry seasons (Oliveira-Filho and Fontes 2000; Dorneles and Waechter 2004; Nunes et al. 2005).

However, at any forest at a local scale, the quality and availability of resources, species-specific interactions, and enemy-free spaces are important in determining the distribution of insects (Lowman 1985; Campos et al. 2006; Ribeiro and Basset 2007). Plant phenology, especially in seasonal forests, will clearly interact with these above-mentioned traits (see Vilela et al. 2014). In addition, emergent properties of the canopy, being a set of traits from different tree crowns and other aerial characteristics of the forest, can also strongly affect the distribution of some guilds (Lowman 1995; Ribeiro and Borges 2010; Neves et al. 2013).

An important biotic factor emerging from the canopy structure is the presence of massive predators such as ants (Dejean et al. 2007). Ants present territorial behaviour, which can generate a mosaic of large, exclusive or restrictive territories of competing species, and which can cross several tree crowns (Majer et al. 1994). In Neotropical forests, the formation of ant mosaics has been considered the major biotic organisation capable of affecting the composition and diversity of other components of the arthropod community (Room 1971; Dejean et al. 2007; Ribeiro et al. 2013). However, some species or insect herbivore guilds may be more or less prone to be attacked by ants (Heads and Lawton 1985; Freitas and Oliveira 1996; Mody and Linsenmair 2004). Honeydew producers, for instance, may benefit from ant presence, which provides protection against other predators and parasitoids, as a response to their sugar production (Blüthgen and Stork 2007; Styrsky and Eubanks 2007).

Few studies have linked the habitat structure in the canopy with the predators/natural enemies (namely, enemy-free spaces) and the herbivore population distribution (see Ribeiro and Basset 2007; Ribeiro and Borges 2010). There might, however, be aspects in canopy ecology that transcend the specificity of herbivore–plant evolutionary interactions and influence their distribution. Entangled interactions ought to transcend feeding choices, as noted by Thompson (2014). Indeed, nearly 50 years of insect–plant studies have shown that top-down pressures may shift herbivores’ choices, especially considering the large proportion of oligophagous or polyphagous species in a tropical rainforest (Novotny et al. 2002). For instance, discontinuous distribution and herbivore-poor sites have been reported in the semideciduous forest canopies of the Brazilian Atlantic Forest (Ribeiro et al. 2008; Neves et al. 2013), and these patterns may be influenced by a complex interaction system (as proposed by Bronstein et al. 2014), but surely driven by predators. However, the relationship between territorial dominant ants and the presence/absence of free-feeding herbivores has not been explored at the canopy scale in these studies.

Although human disturbance may directly affect canopy heterogeneity and continuity, its impacts on the canopy insect fauna are hardly understood, in contrast with well-studied human impacts on ant assemblages (Costa et al. 2010; Ribas et al. 2012). Besides a few studies on butterflies (e.g. DeVries 1987; Brown 1991; Uehara-Prado and Freitas 2009; Freitas 2010), even less is known about impacts on herbivores. It is expected that herbivores will reflect changes in the vegetation (Novotny et al. 2002; Basset et al. 2012). However, for how long the effect of a change caused in the vegetation will last, or how impact intensity affects insects many years after the disturbance has ceased, are unknown aspects of canopy succession. Another aspect that will need to be addressed in the future is how changes in rainfall regime due to global warming will affect the canopy fauna—a thorough reason for describing seasonal insect variation.

The present study aimed to investigate the spatial patterns of herbivore guild abundance and species richness and their response to canopy structure, seasonality, and the effect of territorial, numerically dominant ant species at two spatial scales: (1) individual tree crowns and (2) canopy segments. The following hypotheses were tested: (1) the distribution of herbivores is determined by habitat structure (crown architecture parameters) and canopy heterogeneity (variability in these crown parameters between nearby trees in a continuous canopy segment). We hypothesise that abundance and species richness are higher where habitat structure and canopy heterogeneity are higher, but that it is affected by intensity of disturbance in the past within the same forest, as the history of disturbance changes the present canopy structure. Further, we expect that such patterns are more evident in wet than dry seasons due to resource availability in the former. (2) Numerically dominant ant species in the canopy affect insect herbivore species abundance and richness. The hypothesis is that canopy ants positively affect sap-sucking insects and negatively affect other guilds.

Methods

Study area

The study was conducted in the Itacolomi State Park, Minas Gerais State (20°22′30″S, 43°32′30″W), Brazil. The park was created in 1967 with an area of 7,543 ha, and the vegetation is composed of seasonal forests, gallery forests, forests of the candeia tree [Eremanthus erythropappus (DC.) MacLeish (Asteraceae)], Araucaria angustifolia forest remnants, and ferruginous and quartzite grasslands. The climate is mild temperate with wet and warm summers; the average annual temperature ranges from 17.4 to 19.8 °C and the average annual precipitation ranges from 1,100 to 1,800 mm. The relief is mountainous, with altitudes ranging from 900–1,772 m a.s.l. (Itacolomi Peak summit).

We chose a forest area of 160 ha around a former farmhouse land where tea culture had been grown until the 1970s, and its land use is relatively well known, which thus constitutes a unique example of how vegetation was changed and later abandoned for the creation of the park (Fujaco et al. 2010). Within this forest we selected three sites, equidistant from each other (roughly forming a triangle with 1,000 m on each side), at a same altitude (1,300 m a.s.l.). Each site presents a particular disturbance history due to the intensity of logging and replacement of native vegetation with tea [Camellia sinensis (L.) Kuntze] until the creation of the park. Therefore, the three sites comprise a disturbance gradient.

The first site (hereafter “low disturbance”) is composed of deep valley forest located on a mountainside with steep slopes, with a well-developed canopy and emergent trees up to 25 m in height, but an average upper canopy varying from 10 to 15 m. In this site, forest gaps were formed by natural treefall and small-scale logging. The second site (“intermediate disturbance”) is composed of secondary forest. This site is peripheral to old tea plantation areas, and the canopy is composed of trees rarely exceeding 10 m in height, with a distinct and dense understory. The “high disturbance” site had its vegetation replaced by tea and is currently composed of secondary forest. This site is occupied by small, spaced out trees that rarely reach 5 m in height, distributed over rocky soil.

Sampling design

In each site, we demarcate three transects, 80 m long and 20 m apart, as parallel as possible, resulting in sampling points at least 20 m from each other. This design aimed to provide two sampling scales: the tree crown scale, within each tree crown sampling; the canopy segment scale, which is a set of trees with continuous crowns. We sampled taking into consideration the likely existence of large continuous territories of dominant ant species, as well as territory-free spaces (sensu Ribeiro et al. 2013). The ant species distribution in these sites were studied before (Espírito-Santo et al. 2012; Fagundes et al. 2012), and the presence of numerically dominant ant species is common in these forests. A more widely spaces sampling design would not properly deal with this expectation of ant species distribution.

In each transect, five points were marked and on each one the nearest tallest tree was taken as the central sampled crown, with 15 trees selected within each site in total. From these, eight canopy segments were randomly sorted and used in our samples. Each canopy segment was composed of this central tree and the surrounding smaller trees with touching crowns. This group of trees (hereafter canopy segment) ranged from four to seven, due to continuity and accessibility, and all were sampled. The variation of the number of trees per canopy segment reflected the tree crown sizes; therefore, canopy segments with more trees were composed of smaller crowns, which may offset sampling area effect (Ribeiro et al. 2005; Ribeiro and Borges 2010). In total, 46 trees were marked in the low disturbance site, 42 trees in the intermediate disturbance site, and 44 trees were marked in the high disturbance site, accumulating 132 trees sampled in each sampling period.

It is worth noting that this design was built in order to describe the distribution of insect fauna in a randomly sampled set of canopy segments. In other words, it is a habitat-based sample design, and not an insect-based one. This implies that we are assuming zeros and low densities as part of our expected results (see below). Further, insect survey is not part of our goal.

Canopy parameters

Habitat structure at the tree scale was analysed according to Neves et al. (2013) with modifications in which four architectural parameters were measured in each sampled tree: (1) total tree height; (2) trunk—height to the first ramification; (3) crown depth—total tree height minus trunk height; and (4) CBH—trunk circumference at breast height. To analyse habitat structure at the canopy scale, the ratio between the value of the main tree and the mean value for the neighbouring trees was calculated for each parameter, i.e.:
$$ (i) \text{CBH ratio} = \frac{{\text{Main tree CBH}}}{{\text{Mean CBH of neigbouring}}} $$
$$ (ii) \text{ Tree height ratio} = \frac{{\text{Main tree height}}}{{\text{Mean heights of neighbouring trees}}} $$
$$ (iii) \text{Crown depth ratio} = \frac{{\text{Main tree crown depth}}}{{\text{Mean crown depths of neighbouring trees}}} $$
As far as we are concerned, this approach to the canopy properties emerging from the set of crowns is new.

Insect sampling

Insects were sampled in four sampling periods—two in both the wet (December 2007 and April 2011) and dry (October 2011 and October 2012) seasons. The insects were sampled in the tree crowns by beating the foliage using a modified entomological umbrella (Ribeiro et al. 2005). We thus focused on vagile insects, which are the guilds that may have distinct, eventually variable, responses to numerically dominant ants as well as to vegetation traits. Scale insects, for instance, will respond neutrally or positively to ants, but may be too specialised to respond to vegetation traits (Delabie 2001). On the contrary, flying Hemipteran and other sap-sucking groups will differ in their responses to ants and thus are likely to produce a bold hypothesis for verifying the effects of ant dominance on insect fauna as a whole.

The canopy was reached using the single rope climbing technique (Ribeiro and Basset 2007), and for safety reasons and to assure homogeneity in the sample habitats, emergent trees were avoided. Beating was performed in three randomly selected branches per tree. We beat mostly the top branches exposed to the sun or, when safety climbing procedures prevented us from reaching them, branches immediately below the crown surface were then sampled. Hence, despite the differences in tree and canopy average heights between the sites, we assured that similar, upper-canopy types of habitats were compared. For doing so, the samples varied from approximately 2–10 m to the ground. Each sample was composed of ten beatings per branch (Ribeiro et al. 2005; Neves et al. 2010).

Insects in the orders Coleoptera and Hemiptera were identified to family or subfamily level using identification keys (Borror et al. 2002; Triplehorn and Johnson 2011; Rafael et al. 2012) and separated into morphospecies by comparison with specimens from the reference collection of the Laboratory of Evolutionary Ecology of Canopy Insects and Natural Succession—DEBIO/UFOP.

After identification, insects were separated into trophic guilds according to the literature (Marinoni et al. 2006; Rafael et al. 2012). For some analyses, detritivore, fungivore, xylophages, and predator specimens were grouped as “prey”, because these specimens are likely prey of predatory ants. Sampled immature insects were not considered in analyses of species richness but in abundance.

The ants sampled were identified to genus level using the identification keys (Bolton 1994; Fernandéz 2003), and whenever possible to species level after confirmation from experts. Voucher specimens were deposited in the collection of the Laboratory of Evolutionary Ecology of Canopy Insects and Natural Succession—DEBIO/UFOP.

After ant identification, categories of numerically dominant species were determined according to Ribeiro et al. (2013): category 1—simple species territory, i.e., presence of only one dominant ant species; category 2—permeable territories, i.e., presence of more than one dominant ant species; and category 3—no territory, i.e., absence of dominant species. Thus, each sampling point was assigned to one category for subsequent analysis. In order to consider an ant species as dominant, we used previous data on most abundant and frequent species found in the area, as well as those known as being part of trophobiosis (Espírito-Santo et al. 2012; Fagundes et al. 2012, 2013) (Table 1).

Data analysis at the “crown” scale and the occurrence of empty samples

The analyses were performed considering trees individually per sampling period. Simple linear regression models were used to test the hypothesis that herbivore distribution is influenced by habitat structure, in which a mean number of trees without any herbivores (empty trees) was the response variable and habitat structure parameters were the explanatory variables. Herbivore presence was estimated by the number of sampling periods in which the tree was empty divided by the total number of sampling periods. The same analysis was repeated for each habitat structure parameter.

This direct approach to the large number of empty trees is a novelty. In general, canopy insect studies are focused on the occurrence of insects, but here the focus is on the suitable versus unsuitable habitats. Previous work in semidecidual Atlantic forests has shown a large number of empty trees (Campos et al. 2006; Neves et al. 2010, 2013), but this is the first time that this lack of insects is directly studied and related to the presence of territorial, numerically abundant ant species.

Analyses of covariance (ANCOVAs) were produced by generalised linear models in order to test the hypothesis that the insect herbivores species abundance and richness vary over time and space (fixed factor) and are influenced by dominant ant abundance per tree (covariate). The abundance and species richness of insect herbivores (specifically that of leaf-chewers, sap-sucking, and prey) were used as response variables, and (1) the different sites and (2) dominant ant abundance were used as explanatory variables. The analyses of abundance and richness were performed separately per sampling period for each guild, using data from the second, third, and fourth sampling periods, which had simultaneous data on ants. The analyses were performed using R software (R Development Core Team 2012).

Data analysis at the “canopy segment” scale

The analyses were performed considering 24 canopy segments per sampling period. Simple linear regressions were used to test the hypothesis that the insect herbivore species abundance and richness are determined by canopy heterogeneity, in which the mean insect herbivore species abundance and richness per segment (namely that of leaf-chewers, sap-sucking, and prey) were used as response variables, and canopy heterogeneity was used as an explanatory variable. The analyses of abundance and richness were performed separately for each guild. We used the variance in crown depth between the trees of a segment as a surrogate of canopy heterogeneity. As crown depth may be the most realistic measure of the actual size of the canopy, we opted for this simplest analysis. We used mean herbivore abundance and species richness per tree in each segment to correct for the effect of the number of trees on insect abundance and species richness (Campos et al. 2006).

Two-way repeated measures analysis of covariance were used to test the hypothesis that the insect herbivore species abundance and richness vary across sites and seasons, in which (1) the different past disturbance intensities (low, intermediate, and high), (2) the different seasons of the year, (3) mean tree height, and (4) mean CBH were explanatory variables.

Numerically dominant ant species categories were also included as a response variable in similar models to test the hypothesis that dominant ants in the canopy influence the abundance and species richness of insect herbivores. However, only data from the second (wet season/2011) and fourth (dry season/2012) sampling periods were used in the analyses, including territory categories, as such categories were more clearly detectable on these dates. Due to the slight loss of homoscedasticity, the data were log-transformed for analysis.

Species composition was examined by using a discriminant analysis, in order to determine the differences between sites and sampling periods. For this analysis, the taxa represented by only one or two individuals in each trophic guild were grouped as “rare species”. Regression analyses and ANCOVAs were performed using MINITAB 16 software (Minitab Statistical Software 2013). The ANCOVAs were calculated using the generalised linear model package. The discriminant analysis was performed using SPSS software (SPSS Software 13.0).

Crown scale analysis and empty samples

The average number of trees without herbivores was negatively affected only by crown depth. Hence, the larger the crown was, the bigger the chance of finding herbivores was (F 1,130 = 6.16, P = 0.014). Other habitat structure parameters did not affect herbivore presence (total tree height F 1,130 = 0.32, P = 0.57; trunk F 1,130 = 2.08, P = 0.15; CBH F 1,130 = 0.38, P = 0.54).

Regardless of the importance of crown depth for herbivore occurrence, neither the species abundance or richness of chewing insects (F 1,129 = 0.21, P = 0.65; F 1,129 = 0.14, P = 0.71), of sucking insects (F 1,129 = 0.25, P = 0.62; F 1,129 = 0.31, P = 0.57), or of prey (F 1,129 = 0.04, P = 0.83; F 1,129 = 0.12, P = 0.73) were directly affected by crown depth.

Leaf-chewer species richness was not affected by dominant ant abundance and disturbance level in the three sampling periods (second sampling period/wet season: \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 4.83, P = 0.43; third sampling period/dry season: \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 8.75, P = 0.11; fourth sampling period/dry season: \( \chi_{ 1 1, 1 2 1}^{2} \) = 7.16, P = 0.78). In addition, leaf-chewer abundance was not significantly affected by “dominant ant abundance” and “site” in two sampling periods (\( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 3.47, P = 0.63; \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 12.78, P = 0.02; \( \chi_{ 1 1, 1 2 1}^{2} \) = 10.35, P = 0.50). However, in the third sampling period (dry season), the interaction between the two factors was significant (\( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 12.78, P = 0.02): leaf-chewer abundance per tree was lower in the high disturbance site than in the other two sites, whereas dominant ant abundance per tree was higher in this same high disturbance site (Fig. 1a–c).

Sap-sucking species abundance and richness were higher in the low disturbance site in two of the three sampling periods analysed (second sampling period/wet season: \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 57.59, P < 0.001; \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \)= 16.68, P = 0.005; third sampling period/dry season: \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 67.38, P < 0.001; \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 33.08, P < 0.001). However, in one of the dry seasons, there was a composed effect of past disturbance and the presence of dominant ants on sap-sucking abundance (\( \chi _{2,128}^{2} \) = −11.03, P = 0.05). In other words, sap-sucking species were more abundant in the low disturbance site but mostly on crowns with dominant ant species. On the other hand, sap-sucking species richness was only influenced by past site disturbance, in any season or date, being higher in the site with lowest past disturbance than in the other two sites (second sampling period/wet season:\( \chi_{1,130}^{2} \) = 16.69, P < 0.005; third sampling period/dry season: \( \chi _{2,129}^{2} \) = 26.79, P < 0.001).

However, in the fourth sampling period (dry season), sap-sucking abundance was positively affected by dominant ant abundance (\( \chi _{1,131}^{2} \) = 53.26; P < 0.001), even though sap-sucking species richness was not affected by any factor (\( \chi_{11,126}^{2} \) = 18.56, P = 0.07). This pattern was influenced by a possible trophobiotic relationship between Membracidae sap-sucking insects and the ant Myrmelachista catharinae. When this particular species was removed from the dataset, no significant effect of ant abundance on sap-sucking insects was observed in the analysis (\( \chi_{11,119}^{2} \) = 9.79, P = 0.95).

Prey abundance and species richness were not significantly affected by dominant ant abundance or disturbance level in two of the three sampling periods analysed (second sampling period/wet season: \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 9.985, P = 0.07; \( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 9.67, P = 0.08; fourth sampling period/dry season: \( \chi_{ 1 1, 1 2 1}^{2} \) = 10.87, P = 0.45; \( \chi_{ 1 1, 1 2 1}^{2} \) = 13.16, P = 0.28). However, in the third sampling period (dry season), the complete model was significant and could not be simplified, both for prey species richness (\( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \)= 16.18, P = 0.006; Fig. 2a) and abundance (\( \chi_{5,126}^{2} \) = 20.47, P = 0.001; Fig. 2b). In the two cases, prey abundance and species richness were both higher in the low disturbance site, and especially in trees where dominant ant abundance was lower.

Canopy segment scale

The mean number of trees without herbivores per canopy segment was not significantly affected by any canopy heterogeneity parameter (simple linear regression: CBH F 1,22 = 2.87, P = 0.10; height F 1,22 = 0.12, P = 0.73; canopy depth F 1,22 = 3.50, P = 0.07). In fact, empty trees were observed in all canopy segments.

Mean species abundance and richness of leaf-chewers (simple linear regression: F 1,22 = 0.04, P = 0.83; F 1,22 = 1.21, P = 0.28), sap-suckers (simple linear regression: F 1,22 = 0.85, P = 0.36; F 1,22 = 1.79, P = 0.19), and prey (simple linear regression: F 1,22 = 0.67, P = 0.42; F 1,22 = 0.55, P = 0.47) were not affected by crown depth variance, i.e., canopy heterogeneity.

Mean leaf-chewer species abundance and richness were not affected by past disturbance levels (richness F 2,95 = 0.90, P = 0.41; abundance F 2,95 = 0.55, P = 0.58). However, mean sap-sucker and prey abundance were higher in the low disturbance site than in the other two sites (sap-suckers F 2,95 = 10.47, P < 0.001; prey F 2,95 = 5.22, P = 0.007; Fig. 3a–c). Similarly, mean sap-sucker and prey species richness were both significantly higher in the low disturbance site than in the other two sites (sap-suckers F 2,95 = 8.81, P < 0.001; prey F 2,95 = 5.48, P = 0.006; Fig. 3b–d), thus predominantly on the most heterogeneous canopy. Conversely, there were no significant differences in mean species abundance (leaf-chewers F 1,95 = 1.50, P = 0.22; sap-suckers F 1,95 = 3.01, P = 0.08; prey F 1,95 = 0.71, P = 0.40) or richness (leaf-chewers F 1,95 = 1.57, P = 0.21; sap-sucking F 1,95 = 0.11, P = 0.74; prey F 1,95 = 1.39, P = 0.24) between seasons for any of the analysed guilds.
Leaf-chewer and prey abundance and species richness, and sap-sucking species richness were not affected by dominant ant territories (Table 3). However, mean sap-sucking abundance was positively affected by the presence of dominant ants (F 2,47 = 457.0, P = 0.002) regardless of season and sampling site, being higher in canopy segments with dominant ants (categories 1 and 2) and lower in segments without dominant ants (category 3; Fig. 4). The largest number of samples with ant territories was observed in the low disturbance site, which had the highest mean sap-sucking abundance and species richness. Moreover, only prey species richness differed between seasons (F 1,47 = 5.45, P = 0.025), being higher in the dry season. Finally, species composition differed significantly among the sites (Fig. 5). A total of 55.7 % of the variance in species composition between the sites was explained by discriminant Function 1, which separated the low disturbance site from the other two sites (\( \chi_{204}^{2} \) = 280.9, P = 0.001). Discussion

Our study confirmed that canopy herbivores respond to environmental traits differently depending on spatial scales, and distinctly for each herbivore guild. The pattern found showed distinct responses to canopy structure and to the distribution of dominant ant species. At the tree crown scale, we observed that the probability of sampling any insect herbivore in a given tree crown was positively affected by its crown depth. Herbivores found in trees with deeper crowns may benefit from lower costs of locomotion, resource availability, and lower risk of attack by natural enemies (Ribeiro et al. 2005; Stiling and Moon 2005; Ribeiro and Borges 2010; Neves et al. 2013). Furthermore, the largest crowns were observed in the low disturbance forest site, which also had the highest herbivore species abundance and richness.

The abundance of chewing insects was negatively affected by both disturbance level and ant abundance in the dry season. The activity of dominant ants seemed to be important for determining the distribution of chewing insect herbivores, which varied between crowns and seasons. Other studies also have demonstrated that the abundance of chewing insects is lower in trees with greater abundance of ants, especially in trees with honeydew-producing insects and extrafloral nectaries (EFNs) (Oliveira and Del-Claro 2005; Alves-Silva 2011; Queiroz et al. 2013). Davidson et al. (2003) showed that ants that consume sugary liquids often supplement their diet through predation of herbivorous insects found on plants. Thus, the constant patrolling by dominant ants associated with honeydew-producing insects represents a risk of predation for other insect herbivores and may provide indirect protection to plants (Oliveira and Del-Claro 2005; Oliveira et al. 2012).

The occurrence of trees with large crowns that are more easily detected seemed to have favoured the abundance of sucking insects. Neves et al. (2013) found for another seasonal forest at the Atlantic rainforest that sucking insects tended to respond more promptly to tree crown architecture than to the overall canopy traits.

In agreement with findings by Neves et al. (2013), the responses at the canopy scale were quite independent of those at the crown scale. At the canopy scale, the chewing insect abundance and richness did not vary among sites with distinct canopy heterogeneity and between seasons, while neither were affected by dominant ant abundance, which was opposed to the pattern at the crown scale. Distinctly, sap-sucking distribution was affected by dominant ant abundance at the two scales considered in this study. This result can be explained by the protection conferred by ants to predictable and renewable resources obtained from honeydew or EFNs, an event dependent on conditions strictly within-crowns (Blüthgen et al. 2000, 2004; Schoereder et al. 2010).

Among our dominant ant species, a few may actually nest in the ground. For instance, Solenopsis sp.1 may likely nest in the ground and climb up the canopy for foraging (Longino and Nadkarni 1990). Nevertheless, Lescano et al. (2014) have shown that ground-nesting ants that defend aphids on plants keep some workers in place all the time to assure the resource’s protection. Thus, nest positioning has little effect on the canopy interactions when ants commonly forage on trees, especially on low trees such as ours. Previous work about Itacolomi forests has shown a similar pattern for Camponotus rufipes (Espírito-Santo et al. 2012). In this case, more than keeping workers in the canopy, this species, which has large litter nests on the ground, also keep satellite nests on twigs up in the canopy.

At the tree crown level, some sort of plant protection caused by ants may explain the lower abundance and richness of sap-sucking insects in trees with greater numbers of ants, as observed in both seasons, and regardless of sites. Unless species are capable of enrolling some trophobiosis, ants may tend to force out or predate any insect from the leaves. Conversely, protection by a few dominant ant species that establish into trophobiotic relationships with sap-sucking insects may have a positive effect on the abundance of the latter (Oliveira and Del-Claro 2005; Fagundes et al. 2012), as observed in one of our samples at the crown scale (see below).

Ant territory mosaics are stronger in the upper canopy of mature forests, more precisely those under direct sun exposure on the forest surface (Ribeiro et al. 2013). Thus, dominant ants more actively patrol the canopy habitat where they are more likely to find resources (see Blüthgen et al. 2000; Blüthgen and Stork 2007). Under this perspective, eventually one may assume that herbivores shape the distribution of dominant ants and not the other way around. Still, with ants acting as predators, this must result in a two-way path for any guild. If this is the case, it is expected that other guilds studied also respond negatively to dominant ants at some scale.

The other invertebrates (grouped into the “prey” group) also showed distinct responses to canopy heterogeneity and canopy traits at the two spatial scales. At the crown scale, the species abundance and richness of prey were higher in the low disturbance site, the more heterogeneous site, but only in one dry season sample. However, and as expected, the distribution of prey was negatively affected in a consistent way by dominant ant abundance. One may interpret that the aggressive behaviour of dominant ants as predators would reduce invertebrate numbers, as previously proposed. Nevertheless, it is also likely that the pattern may be, at least partially, driven by potential prey being capable of actively searching for enemy-free spaces (Ribeiro et al. 2005; Ribeiro and Borges 2010). However, at the canopy scale, the mean prey species abundance and richness were higher in the low past disturbance site but not affected by ant territories. This contrasting result reinforced the hypothesis that invertebrates respond directly to the locations where ants are foraging or patrolling, and not to the canopy structure.

The variance in species composition between sites discriminated the low past disturbance site from the other two sites, and this was strongly influenced by the presence of several sap-sucking species in the former. Thus, the species richness of sap-sucking insects correlated negatively with the high heterogeneity of the past disturbance site. Likewise, the variance in species composition between seasons separated dry season and wet season samples, but all three sites were more similar to each other in the dry season than in the wet season. As noted by Vilela et al. (2014), seasonal effect must confound with tree phenology, which is more likely to influence ant–plant–herbivore interactions.

The present study highlights the occurrence of different pattern distribution of abundance, species richness, and composition for each canopy insect herbivore guild and general invertebrates in a montane forest canopy. Such patterns were influenced by past disturbances and consequent canopy structure, as well as the distribution of territories of dominant ant species between seasons. Moreover, the results show that spatial scale should be considered for detecting the distinct effects of tree crowns, emerging canopy structure or the direct presence of ants on insect diversity. Also, the distribution of empty canopy spaces for herbivores, which has been so far viewed by many as a mere sampling artefact, may indeed have an underestimated ecological meaning.

The vaguely studied possibility that canopy fauna may be highly susceptible to human disturbance (Ozanne et al. 2003) is corroborated by our work. In these particular types of oligotrophic soil-adapted forests, human impact on canopy structure may take much longer to disappear than impacts on tree species composition and abundances. Such structural long-term changes will clearly change the insect–plant community for decades after the impact.

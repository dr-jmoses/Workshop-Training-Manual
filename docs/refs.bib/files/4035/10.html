Ant-mediated ecosystem processes are driven by trophic community structure but mainly by the environment

Abstract

The diversity and functional identity of organisms are known to be relevant to the maintenance of ecosystem processes but can be variable in different environments. Particularly, it is uncertain whether ecosystem processes are driven by complementary effects or by dominant groups of species. We investigated how community structure (i.e., the diversity and relative abundance of biological entities) explains the community-level contribution of Neotropical ant communities to different ecosystem processes in different environments. Ants were attracted with food resources representing six ant-mediated ecosystem processes in four environments: ground and vegetation strata in cropland and forest habitats. The exploitation frequencies of the baits were used to calculate the taxonomic and trophic structures of ant communities and their contribution to ecosystem processes considered individually or in combination (i.e., multifunctionality). We then investigated whether community structure variables could predict ecosystem processes and whether such relationships were affected by the environment. We found that forests presented a greater biodiversity and trophic complementarity and lower dominance than croplands, but this did not affect ecosystem processes. In contrast, trophic complementarity was greater on the ground than on vegetation and was followed by greater resource exploitation levels. Although ant participation in ecosystem processes can be predicted by means of trophic-based indices, we found that variations in community structure and performance in ecosystem processes were best explained by environment. We conclude that determining the extent to which the dominance and complementarity of communities affect ecosystem processes in different environments requires a better understanding of resource availability to different species.

Introduction

The conversion of forests into agricultural ecosystems is one of the major causes of species loss and homogenization worldwide (Chapin et al. 2000; Lindemayer et al. 2012). Such changes have direct repercussions on the structure of ecological communities and the maintenance of ecosystem processes. Ecosystem processes are generally understood as fluxes of matter and energy between the compartments of an ecosystem (Hooper et al. 2005; Jax 2005). Community structure refers to the diversity and abundance of organisms in a community, two attributes essential to understanding community changes in environmental gradients and their effects on ecosystem processes (McGill et al. 2006). Biodiversity can refer to the number of species but also to the functional diversity, which provides a measure of the range of ecological strategies among the species present (Hooper et al. 2005; Cadotte et al. 2011). During the last decade, many studies have tried to assess the influence of the taxonomic and functional structure of ecological communities in maintaining ecosystem processes in different habitat conditions (Hooper et al. 2005; Díaz et al. 2006). The general expectation is that biodiversity has a positive effect on such ecosystem processes as decomposition, predation or plant productivity via the complementary effects of the species using the resources (Petchey and Gaston 2002; Balvanera et al. 2006; Cadotte et al. 2011). This expectation is directly drawn from the niche theory since ecological partitioning theoretically decreases competition, increasing overall community performance in using resources (Poisot et al. 2013; Turnbull et al. 2013). On the other hand, other studies suggest that a few species frequently dominate the largest fraction of available resources and are responsible for most of an ecosystem’s productivity (Aarssen 1997; Grime 1998; McKane et al. 2002). In this case, the mass ratio hypothesis predicts that the contribution of biodiversity to ecosystem processes is mostly determined by the trait values of dominant contributors, which are better accounted for by considering the average community traits (Grime 1998; Garnier et al. 2004).

Nevertheless, there is great uncertainty as to whether biodiversity-ecosystem functioning relationships are driven by the complementarity and abundance of species or whether species diversity or activity respond to environmental factors such as temperature, precipitation or chemical fertility (Gross and Cardinale 2007; Scherber et al. 2010). In particular, the survival and abundance of species associated with different ecosystem processes depend on the availability of the resources required to maintain their populations (Kaspari et al. 2012). Therefore, a major challenge for ecologists is to understand the diversity and dominance of the communities associated with different ecosystem processes across environments, and how this is related to their performance in generating such processes (Gamfeldt et al. 2008; Mouillot et al. 2011).

In this study, we investigated how changes in the structure of Neotropical ant communities are related to community-level performance in ecosystem processes in different environments. Neotropical forests harbor one of the richest faunas in the world, as well as one of the most endangered by the rapid conversion of forest into agricultural areas in recent decades (Morris 2010). The assessment of biodiversity-ecosystem functioning relationships is particularly challenging in the tropics due to the large diversity of taxa as well as the variable responses of different types of organisms to land-use changes (Flynn et al. 2009). Ants constitute ideal organisms to study changes in ecosystem properties thanks to their abundance and participation in several ecosystem processes and functions through their use of food resources (Folgarait 1998; Philpott et al. 2010; Brandão et al. 2012). For instance, some ant species play eminent roles as insect predators (Philpott and Armbrecht 2006), the mutualist protectors of plants or insects while foraging for sugars (Blüthgen and Fiedler 2004), or detritivores consuming carrion and excrements (Kaspari and Yanoviak 2001; Houadria et al. 2015). Quantifying the exploitation of different resources by co-occurring species should be helpful in understanding the environmental and biotic constraints driving biodiversity relationships with ecosystem functioning in response to land-use changes (McKane et al. 2002; Poisot et al. 2013). Using six food resources representing such ecosystem processes, we attracted ants foraging in two ecosystem strata (i.e., ground and vegetation) in two habitat types (i.e., forests and croplands). The combination of habitat and strata provided four contrasted environments where we expected differences in the availability of nutritional resources that would affect the nutritional ecology of the species associated within them (Kaspari and Yanoviak 2001; Peters et al. 2014). The structure of ant communities, represented by four biodiversity variables—namely: (1) the number of species, (2) the dominance of particular species, (3) the trophic complementarity of species and (4) the dominant trophic phenotype—was then compared between these four environments and used to explain community performance in exploiting resources.

Specifically, we first tested whether community structure and community-level resource use intensity changed between resource types and between environments. Then, we hypothesized that each of the biodiversity variables positively affected community-level performances in exploiting individual resources (i.e., processes) and their combination in a multifunctionality index. Finally, we tested whether relationships between community structure and ecosystem processes differed between environments. We had no clear a priori expectations of which of these features govern, although we expected ant community structure and environment to affect ant performance in mediating ecosystem processes.

Materials and methods

Study site and sampling protocol

The study took place in the La Montagne des Singes area, Kourou, French Guiana (5°04′27′′N, 52°42′03′′W). The climate is equatorial and affected by the Intertropical Convergence Zone, with mean precipitation about 3000 mm and a mean humidity of 80–90 %. Temperatures vary between 18 and 35 °C. Sampling was performed in five forest and four traditional cropland areas. These habitats were chosen as representative of the tropical rainforest covering 90 % of the surface of French Guiana and croplands cultivated using traditional farming techniques. Thus, we expected to identify the typical pressures that ant biodiversity (and biodiversity in general) is facing in the land-use transformation process (Lindemayer et al. 2012). All the plots were located within a 3-km radius, separated by at least 250 m from each other, and relatively interspersed. The forests plots belong to the same forest continuum dominated by the botanical families Fabaceae, Chrysobalanaceae and Lecythidaceae, as is frequent in the species-rich forests of the coastal part of French Guiana. These plots were characterized by a great diversity of tree morphologies and sizes, a thick litter layer, and large amounts of wood debris. The croplands were areas where the original forest was burnt and replaced by mixed crops (e.g., banana trees, cassava, pineapple, and sugar cane). The vegetation was generally young (i.e., less than 4-years old and less than 3-m tall), providing irregular shade and no litter. Sampling was performed between January and July 2013, between 08:00 and 11:00 a.m. (no later than 10:00 a.m. in the croplands due to high temperatures) and only in the absence of rain and in dry soil conditions to avoid any bias linked to field conditions or frequently observed temporal variations (Wittman et al. 2010; Houadria et al. 2015). Sampling consisted of baiting trials where food items were placed on the ground and on the vegetation during separate sampling sessions. Habitat and strata combinations were considered separate environmental classes (hereafter, ‘environments’) because sampling revealed major variations in taxonomic composition and differences in ecosystem processes as is to be expected due to the different resource limitations between habitats and ecosystem compartments (Kaspari and Yanoviak 2001; Peters et al. 2014). Moreover, because the number of exploited baits and ant activity and diversity greatly differed between these four environments, a stratified sampling strategy was adopted to obtain sufficient species-wise information. More specifically, we limited the number of cropland plots since the species composition was very similar in all of them and the number of species was lower than in the forest plots. Also, the number of sampling locations on trees was increased in both habitats because discovery rates were lower, and, in general, a single species was found foraging on each tree in contrast to sampling locations on the ground where several species foraged simultaneously. Consequently, 20 sampling locations were distributed at ground level on a grid system of 4 × 5 grid points with a 10-m interval between the points. In addition, 40–50 trees and understory plants of various sizes were chosen in each plot, for a total of 400 plants in all the forest plots combined and 165 in the croplands. Only plants on which at least one of the baits was exploited were taken into account. All the calculations used in this study are based on relative occurrences which control for any bias related to the number of samples.

Ants were attracted using six food types associated with ecosystem processes. Two sugary solutions represented the use of carbohydrates which mediate the ant protection of plants (e.g., the sucrose in nectar) and sap-sucking insects (e.g., the melezitose in honeydew). The liquid carbohydrate baits consisted of 3 ml of a 25 %-weight water solution of sucrose and melezitose, respectively (Sigma®, Sigma-Aldrich Co.). Dead mealworms (Tenebrio molitor) and chicken feces (3 g each) represented two different sources of detritus consumed by ants (i.e., through the processes of scavenging and coprophagy). These detritus differ, however, in their nutrient content; excrements contain lower amounts of proteins and other nutrients. Finally, we separated predatory function into small prey (at least 20 live termite individuals: Anoplotermes sp.), and large prey (two, live 1.5 and 3-cm long mealworms) since prey size may determine the ability of different species to handle them (see also Houadria et al. 2015).

At each sampling location, all the resources were presented once. On the ground, each resource type was presented in a Petri dish (5 cm in diameter) which was covered with Parafilm® at collection time to prevent the ants from escaping. In the vegetation, the food resources were displayed in 2 ml Eppendorf® tubes attached to branches at between 80 cm and 1.5 m in height, so that the mouth was in contact with the trunk or branch. The food baits were presented during two different sessions, with first all non-prey items and then prey items, which enabled us to survey prey capture behavior (as otherwise many of the mobile prey escaped). On the ground, the food baits were simultaneously presented in a circle surrounding the sampling location, each of the baits separated by at least 50 cm from the others. This distance was chosen after preliminary trials confirmed it prevented several baits from being monopolized by ants using a single recruitment trail. In the vegetation, the baits were randomly distributed to prevent monopolization by a single species. Ants attracted to the baits were collected after 1 h by closing the Petri dishes or the Eppendorf tubes.

Biodiversity variables related to ecosystem processes

To investigate the relationships between community structure and ant-mediated ecosystem processes, we calculated four biodiversity variables and community-level performance in exploiting resources for each of the 18 communities studied (see below). The variables chosen provide an image of the diversity and dominance of the communities studied on the basis of taxonomic and trophic descriptors. We used species occurrences at the different food types, organizing the data into a matrix with resource types (i.e., processes) in columns and species in rows (Appendix 1). The cells indicate the number of times a species was found at each resource type. Standardization by rows provided an estimate of the relative use of the different resource types by the species considered and can be understood as a proxy of the species’ role in the ecosystem studied. Standardization by columns enabled the assessment of the relative contribution of a species to particular processes. The few species that were present in more than one environment were considered separate species. Only species present on six or more occasions per environment were considered in the analysis because any lower number of occurrences could not be distinguished from the random use of food types according to the Chi squared distribution.
  1. 1.

    Effective taxonomic richness: for each of the resources tested separately and in combination (i.e., multifunctionality), Hill numbers with a diversity coefficient of one were calculated (Shannon’s entropy conversion described in Jost (2006)). Effective taxonomic richness indicates the real number of species consuming each resource type considering their relative abundance, thus limiting any bias related to differences in sample sizes between communities. The Hill numbers were obtained using the ‘vegan’ package in R Oksanen (2010).

     
  2. 2.

    Dominance by particular species: the effect of individual species on processes was calculated as the percentage of baits of a resource type exploited or multifunctionality by the most abundant species (i.e., dominance index, D). This variable represented the ability of a single species to potentially ensure a given process.

     
  3. 3.

    Trophic complementarity: to test whether resource exploitation was related to the degree of trophic complementarity, we calculated the “functional dispersion” of species according to their relative use of different food types using the R-package ‘FD’ (Laliberté et al. 2014). This index provides a measure of the overall trophic space occupied by a community and the relative abundance of species distributed within that space, which is in keeping with the idea of interspecific food resource partitioning (Poisot et al. 2013).

     
  4. 4.
    Community-weighted trophic mean: the tendency of each community to exploit the different resources was assessed using the community-weighted mean value (CWM) calculated for each plot and process (Garnier et al. 2004) as
    {\text{CWM}}\; = \;\mathop \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{S} p_{i} x_{i} ,
    (1)
    where p i is the relative occurrence of species i at each of the 20 sampling points on the ground or each tree for the vegetation, and x i is the relative use of the resource by species i (standardized by rows in Appendix 1). This measurement is driven by the relative abundance of species and is, therefore, associated with the dominant trophic phenotypes within communities (Grime 1998).
     

To measure ecosystem processes, we considered the proportion of exploited baits of a given food type during the duration of the experiment (1 h) compared to the total number of sampling locations in a given habitat. This provides an estimate of the probability of a given resource type being exploited by any ant species within the community under examination. This is related to the fluxes or removal of these resources resulting from ant activity, which is directly related to the definition of ecosystem process (Hooper et al. 2005; Jax 2005). Each of the food types was considered an independent ecosystem process, and then the average of the six individual processes was used to portray multifunctionality (Mouillot et al. 2011).

Effects of food type and environment on community structure and performance in ecosystem processes

All the statistical analyses were performed using R software (R Development Core Team 2015) based on 108 values for each of the biodiversity variables representing community structure and resource exploitation rates. The data were derived from the six food resources tested on each of the 18 ant communities studied (five replicates for forest communities on the ground and on the vegetation, and four replicates for cropland communities).

First, we examined variations in the four biodiversity variables selected between ecosystem processes and habitats. Each biodiversity variable was regressed against food (a factor with six levels) and environment (a factor with four levels), controlling for interactions between both using a 2-way type II analysis of variance recommended for unbalanced factorial designs. According to the nature and distribution of the data, a linear model was used to fit each of the four biodiversity variables, and a generalized linear model using a binomial distribution was used to examine the exploitation of resources where the number of exploited baits was compared to the total number of trials. Pairwise comparisons were carried out using Tukey’s test or the Multcomp package in R for linear and binomial assumptions, respectively.

Relationships between community structure and ant community performance in ecosystem processes and their covariation with environment

To disentangle which biodiversity variables explained individual processes, we developed generalized linear models. We used the exploitation frequencies for each resource type (i.e., the number of exploited baits compared to the number of trials) as a response variable regressed against each of the biodiversity variables using a binomial distribution family. The same test was then repeated regressing multifunctionality (the average of the six processes considered) against the effective taxonomic richness, dominance and trophic complementarity. In this case, an arcsine transformation was applied to the proportion of exploited baits and regressed against biodiversity variables using a linear model. Community-weighted means were not included in the multifunctionality model because, when applied to proportions (i.e., relative food use), this model provides an equal value for all the plots (i.e., 0.166). Moreover, there is no real reason to infer multiple functions from community-weighted values, as these are expected to correlate to specific functions.

Because we wanted to understand whether ecosystem processes could be explained solely by community structure, the models were at first developed using biodiversity variables alone to predict each of the ecosystem processes or multifunctionality. In a second step, environment was included as a covariate (with four levels corresponding to habitat and strata combinations) of biodiversity variables.

Results

A total of 137 species were captured in all of the four environments studied combined (Appendix 1). The average number of species captured per plot was greater in the forests, particularly on the ground (mean ± SD = 38.8 ± 4.0), whereas only about the half of that number of species was found foraging in the vegetation (mean ± SD = 19.8 ± 3.6). As expected, the average number of species captured in the cropland plots was lower in comparison to the forests, both on the ground (mean ± SD 15.5 ± 1.3) and in the vegetation (mean ± SD = 12.8 ± 5.3). The percentage of baits occupied by the most abundant species (i.e., overall dominance) was considerably higher in the croplands than in the forests. For instance, more than a third of the ground baits containing insects in any form in the croplands were occupied by Solenopsis saevissima. Moreover, three ant species associated with the croplands (i.e., S. saevissima, Pheidole fallax and Crematogaster sp. 5) represented more than 75 % of the occurrences at the ground baits. Likewise, S. saevissima and Camponotus novogranadensis were present in more than a third of the vegetation baits of any kind. On the contrary, the baits placed in the forests were frequently exploited by many ant species, and a different species was the most abundant at each resource type. Indeed, when considering all the baits in combination, the most abundant species on the ground (i.e., Ochetomyrmex neopolitus and Wasmannia auropunctata) represented less than 10 % of the occurrences. The same was true for the forest vegetation, where the two most frequent ant species at the baits (i.e., Cr. brasiliensis and Azteca sp. 2) represented less than 15 % of the occurrences.

Variations in community structure and community-level performance between processes and environments

The taxonomic and trophic structure of the ant communities examined greatly differed between processes (i.e., food types) and environments (Table 1; Figs. 2, 4). The effective taxonomic richness varied between processes (F 5,81 = 15.58, P < 0.001, Fig. 1) and environments (F 3,81 = 13.19, P < 0.001), but no interactions were found between them (F 15,81 = 1.34, P = 0.19). The greatest number of species was found on the ground in forest habitats (Tukey’s HSD differences >1.4, P = 0.026), and more species consumed dead insects and sugars in general, while large prey and excrements attracted fewer species (Table 1).

Dominance varied between the different ecosystem processes (F 5,84 = 14.15, P < 0.001, Fig. 2) and environments (F 3,84 = 9.52, P < 0.001). No interaction existed between environment and processes (F 1,84 = 1.03, P = 0.44). Dominance values were lower on the ground in forest habitats, but significant differences were only detected between this environment and ground- and vegetation-foraging ants in the croplands (Table 1). Higher dominance values were also found for large prey compared to the other processes (Tukey’s HSD all differences >0.11, P = 0.001).
Trophic complementarity only differed between ground- and vegetation-foraging communities (F 3,78 = 24.85, P < 0.001, Fig. 3) (Tukey’s HSD mean difference >0.32, P < 0.001; Table 1), and for the ground-foraging communities in the forest plots compared to those in the croplands (mean difference = 0.35, P = 0.003), but not between the arboreal communities (mean difference = 0.015, P = 0.99). Significant variations in trophic complementarity were not detected between processes (F 5,78 = 2.12, P = 0.071) and there was no interaction between environment and processes (F 15,78 = 1.47, P = 0.13).
The community-weighted mean values were significantly different between processes (F 5,84= 216.20, P < 0.001, Fig. 4), but not between environments. Dead insects were the most consumed resource in the four environments (Tukey’s HSD minimal mean difference; Dead insects—Sucrose = 0.035, P = 0.02, Table 1). An interaction was detected between environment and process (F 15,84 = 11.42, P < 0.001) and this is illustrated by a significant increase in the capture of large prey by ground-foraging communities in the croplands, or the consumption of excrements by arboreal-foraging communities in the forest plots compared to the other communities.
Finally, the exploitation rates differed between processes (ANOVA, \chi_{5,84}^{2} = 148.4, P < 0.001) and between environments (\chi_{3,84}^{2} = 41.07, P < 0.001), but the factors were interdependent (\chi_{3,84}^{2} = 55.38, P < 0.001). Dead insects and sucrose were consistently the most exploited resources. Arboreal-foraging communities exploited the excrements relatively more frequently (Fig. 5), while the capture of insects, especially large prey, was relatively more frequent on the ground than in the vegetation. Large prey baits were less frequently exploited than those representing other processes, with the exception of the excrements (Table 1). This tendency dramatically changed, however, on the ground in the croplands where the exploitation frequencies of large prey reached levels similar to the other food items.

The values for the effective taxonomic richness observed within environments (i.e., post hoc tests) followed the hierarchy of the exploitation rates relatively closely (Figs. 1, 5). On the contrary, the expected positive relationship between the effective taxonomic richness and community-level performance in the exploitation of food items was not found. Moreover, there was a negative correlation between the effective number of taxa and the frequency of the exploitation of large prey (Table 2). More specifically, it was greater on the ground in the croplands where the number of species associated with this process was the lowest (Z = −3.4).

Dominance values (D) were weakly related to community-level performance in generating processes within environments (Table 1; Figs. 2, 5) or specific processes between environments (Table 2). An exception was found for small prey, indicating the key role of particular species in exploiting this resource type in some environments.

Trophic complementarity values were mostly constant for the majority of the processes within environments (Table 1; Fig. 3) and were thus weakly related to exploitation rates (Fig. 5). Nevertheless, trophic complementarity was positively related to community-level performance in different ecosystem processes between environments (Table 1), and the only biodiversity variable presenting a positive correlation with the exploitation of melezitose (representing honeydew), dead insects and multifunctionality (Table 2). These relationships are mostly explained by the greater levels of foraging activity recorded on the ground related to the greater trophic complementarity of these communities (Figs. 3, 5).

Community-weighted trophic means (CWM) accurately tracked the hierarchy of the exploitation of resources within environments (Table 1; Figs. 4, 5). Between environments, the CWM better reflected the differences in prey capture between the croplands and the forests, and was the only variable significantly related to the community-level exploitation of sucrose (representing nectar).

Effects of environment on the relationships between ant community structure and performance in ecosystem processes

The inclusion of environment as a co-variate in the models highlighted the consistent effect of this factor on all the processes with the exception of the exploitation of dead insects (Table 2). Moreover, the inclusion of environment offsets the relationship between community structure and processes with only the CWM having significant relationships with the same food resources, and trophic complementarity with the exploitation of dead insects.

Discussion

We report changes in the diversity and dominance structure of Neotropical ant communities with respect to different food resources between four environments using a set of baits. Our results confirm that Atlantic Amazonian forests bear a high taxonomic and ecological diversity in comparison to other regions (see also Houadria et al. 2016). In conjunction with certain authors, here we consider that resource exploitation represents the ability of ant communities to participate in ecosystem processes (Fayle et al. 2010; Šipoš and Kindlmann 2013).

We first investigated whether community structure, represented by four biodiversity variables, and community-level resource use differed between environments and between food types. We found higher levels of dominance and lower species and trophic diversity in the croplands in comparison to the forests, which are a frequent consequence of land-use changes (Fargione et al. 2007; Peters et al. 2014). Between strata, the greatest changes in community structure resulted from variations in trophic complementarity, which was greater among ground-foraging ants compared to those foraging in the vegetation. The diversity of trophic phenotypes might be related to the greater number of species foraging on the ground, although we could only confirm that pattern in the forest and not in the croplands. The percentage of baits exploited was greater on the ground than in the vegetation. These differences in ant activity between the two strata might be related to a lower availability of nesting habitats in trunks, resulting in a lower discovery rate of resources in the course of the experiments. The percentage of prey captured was greater on the ground, particularly in the croplands, while the exploitation of excrements was considerably greater on forest trees. One possible explanation is that this difference may be due to the ability of many arboreal ants to recycle nitrogen from excrements (Cook and Davidson 2006). Moreover, we found no evidence of differences in the relative consumption of proteinaceous vs sugary resources between the baits placed on the ground and those in the vegetation in contrast to findings in previous studies (Kaspari and Yanoviak 2001; Hashimoto et al. 2010). Nor did we find such differences when comparing croplands and forests, as did other authors studying ant communities in the Neotropics and East Africa (Bihn et al. 2008; Peters et al. 2014). Indeed, dead insects, which are the typical bait type representing protein limitations as well as a scavenging mode of foraging, and baits containing sugars (here representing mutualistic protection) were well represented in the four environments studied. Overall, this might indicate that the effects of land-use change on the trophic structure of ant communities may differ across biogeographical regions or even within regions depending on the environmental conditions (Arnan et al. 2014).

Our second hypothesis was that community structure, via mechanisms of complementarity and dominance, affected the performance of ant communities in generating ecosystem processes. Contrarily to that which is frequently expected, the number of species was not related to the execution of the processes (Balvanera et al. 2006; Fayle et al. 2010; Maestre et al. 2012). Indeed, the exploitation of baits was slightly higher in the croplands, particularly on the ground. This is likely the result of the generalist foraging behavior of many species in simplified environments (Andersen 1992; Fargione et al. 2007; Peters et al. 2014) and is corroborated by the lower trophic complementarity in croplands than in forests. Despite this finding, a positive relationship between trophic complementarity and the exploitation of several resources was found, supporting the expected positive effects of complementarity on ecosystem processes (Hooper et al. 2005; Cardinale et al. 2006; Poisot et al. 2013). This seems best explained by differences between the ground and the vegetation. In addition, community-weighted trophic mean (CWM) values captured process differences between environments as well as the hierarchy in the exploitation of the different resources within communities. This can be interpreted as providing evidence that ecosystem processes are largely driven by the dominant traits of communities (Cardinale et al. 2006; Laughlin 2011; Bílá et al. 2014). Nevertheless, the contribution of single species was rarely a major driver of ecosystem processes, although the positive relationship between dominance and prey capture on the ground shows that a single species can maintain a high functional performance for specific processes (Cardinale et al. 2006; Houadria et al. 2016).

Finally, the inclusion of the environment as a covariate in the models revealed that this factor was highly significant in explaining ecosystem process variations and their relationship with community structure. This finding, in conjunction with the strong influence of environment on the taxonomic and trophic structures of the communities examined, suggests that relationships between community structure and performance in generating processes are, to a large extent, context specific (Needham et al. 2011; Frainer et al. 2014). For instance, a comparison of croplands and forests considering only vegetation communities indicated similar values for the four biodiversity variables examined. In contrast, when ground communities were compared, we found that a shared contribution of CWM and trophic complementarity determined the processes in the forests. Also, community structure and resource use patterns differed between the ground and the vegetation in both habitat types. These findings indicate that the effects of land-use changes on community structure and participation in ecosystem processes may be very different depending on the ecosystem compartment considered. Furthermore, for each of the environments, the CWMs and, to a lesser extent, the effective taxonomic richness greatly differed between food types and tended to display greater values for the most exploited resources like sugars and dead insects. In contrast, dominance and trophic complementarity showed lower variations and exhibited greater values for poorly exploited items like large prey and excrements. Overall, these findings support the limitation hypothesis which states that species forage more intensely for the most limited resources in their environment (Kaspari and Yanoviak 2001; Kaspari et al. 2012; Fowler et al. 2014; Peters et al. 2014). Conversely, when substitutable resources (i.e., partially overlapping in their nutrient contents) are considered, species tend to specialize in the exploitation of the most abundant resources (McKane et al. 2002; Fox and Vasseur 2008). Therefore, whether differences in community structure between environments are a consequence of resource limitations or suggest an ecological convergence towards abundant resources requires further investigation.

Conclusions

Despite the partial success of using trophic biodiversity variables in predicting processes, we cannot conclude unambiguously whether these are the drivers behind ecosystem processes because of the predominance of an environmental effect. Such an effect can be significant even between the compartments of a same habitat as illustrated by the major differences found between the ground and the vegetation. Nevertheless, it can still be argued that biodiversity variables are indicators of these processes. Future studies need to consider species abundance, their ecological characteristics and performance in exploiting resources together with resource availability in different environments. Such studies would contribute to our understanding of how resource abundance affects the functional structure of the biological communities controlling the biotic and environmental components of ecosystem processes. Overall, our results emphasize the necessity of including different habitat compartments in the assessment of land-use change effects on biodiversity-ecosystem functioning relationships.

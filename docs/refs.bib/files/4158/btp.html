    Armed conflicts are human tragedies. When occurring in biodiversity hot spots, as has been the case in 80 percent of the armed conflicts in the last 50 years (Hanson et al. 2009), conflicts can also precipitate environmental disasters. Associated disruptions to governance can lead to increases in poaching, logging, and even assassinations of conservation professionals and environmental activists (Dudley et al. 2002, Daskin & Pringle 2018). However, when they cause or maintain the depopulation of wilderness areas, armed conflicts have provided incidental and ephemeral preservation benefits to biodiversity. The demilitarized zone splitting Korea (Smith & Rosenbaum 2004) and large swaths of forests in Colombia (Sánchez‐Cuervo & Aide 2013) and Myanmar exist only because conflict has prevented the encroachment of human development. When conflicts cease in these contexts, local ecosystems, as well as rural displaced peoples, enter a time of transition. If peace and security are durable, then territory formerly under dispute can open for economic development.

    Typically, the first order of business is to exploit untapped natural resources to provide a rapid economic stimulus. That is, logging, mining, and land clearance for agriculture will quickly lay waste to lands left preserved by conflict. This occurred in both Liberia (Brottem & Unruh 2009) and Cambodia (Loucks et al. 2009) as conflict resolution was immediately followed by rapid increases in deforestation and defaunation. How can governments work to make conflict conservation effects persist through more peaceful times? And how can they align conservation goals with those of vulnerable post‐conflict human communities?

    Certainly, the establishment of formal publicly run protected areas should be a conservation gold standard, but nation‐states overcoming conflicts rarely possess the political and financial resources to designate new protected areas, let alone enforce conservation rules within those already in existence. So, in most post‐conflict cases, conservation in protected areas must be complemented by ‘new conservation’ approaches, that is, market‐based solutions. Payment for ecosystem services schemes, such as REDD+, may be useful in some contexts, but there is substantial debate as to their general effectiveness (Petkova et al. 2010). Specific criticisms target the lack of benefit they provide for rural peoples, who find themselves facing newly imposed constraints, and the difficulty in achieving the set standards (Schroeder & McDermott 2014).

    Among the economically viable post‐conflict paths that can offer both ecological and social sustainability, an increasingly attractive option is ecotourism (Balmford et al. 2009): responsible travel to natural areas that conserves the environment, sustains the well‐being of local people, and involves interpretation and education (The International Ecotourism Society 2018). Ecotourism is a particularly attractive strategy for biodiverse countries with a wealth of natural attractions and human populations that depend on natural resources. Ecotourists exert a minimal environmental impact and have a high willingness to pay, supporting high‐wage jobs for rural people who would otherwise be dependent upon high‐impact natural resource extraction livelihoods. An ecotourism approach to post‐conflict conservation and social development has seen success in Rwanda, which has enjoyed a lucrative ecotourism industry based around Mountain Gorillas both before and after a bloody genocide in the early 1990s (Maekawa et al. 2013). In Myanmar, after 40 years of conflict, tourism has flourished and doubled in numbers annually since the end of conflict in 2011 (World Travel and Tourism Council 2017). What follows is a demonstration of what resources should be developed to grow an ecotourism industry that can promote both socioeconomic development and biodiversity conservation in post‐conflict settings (Fig. 1).

    To illustrate our recommendation, we make frequent use of the example of Colombia, one of the most biodiverse countries in the World whose tourism potential has remained mostly untapped. A 50‐year conflict between the Colombian government and illegal armed groups displaced thousands of people, kept many rural places off‐limits, and generated a feeling of fear in tourists. However, in 2016, the government signed an armistice with the largest group of guerrilla fighters, the Fuerzas Armadas Revolucionarias de Colombia—Ejército del Pueblo (FARC) after they agreed to disarm themselves (Colombian National Government & FARC‐EP 2016). Because the forest areas controlled by guerillas were too dangerous for outsiders to enter, many of them remain full of rare species sensitive to anthropogenic disturbance. With a peace process underway, however, these forests will be vulnerable to the extractive industry rush. If allowed to persist, these areas could instead make Colombia into a new global giant in ecotourism—in large part because of the unparalleled opportunities for bird‐watching it provides (Ocampo‐Peñuela & Winton, 2017; https://doi.org/10.1177/1940082917733862).

    A few preconditions must be met in order for post‐conflict ecotourism to be successful (Fig. 1). The first one is obvious: the presence and perception of security. Tourists simply are not willing to visit a place where they feel they will not be safe. In recognition of this fact, the Colombian government began (long before the recent peace agreement) a marketing campaign to counter war‐torn perceptions propagated by international news media with the slogan: ‘the only risk is wanting to stay’. Since 2010, foreign tourism has increased by 95 percent, reaching 5 million visitors in 2016 (http://www.mincit.gov.co/), with an increase of 600,000 in 2017.

    A second prerequisite for post‐conflict ecotourism is the presence of unique and charismatic fauna and/or iconic landscapes to attract visitors. Although there is a strong push of ecotourists into the market, the options are vast, and so countries must identify a compelling feature to pull in clients. In the case of Rwanda, a chance to encounter Mountain Gorillas, one of our closest genetic relatives remaining in the wild, is a powerful draw. As Mountain Gorillas persist in just a couple small protected areas, Rwanda along with its neighbors, Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda, together hold a monopoly on the world's chance to see this iconic species. Can Colombia capitalize on its unique charismatic fauna and iconic landscapes in the post‐conflict era? The country is famous for having more bird species than any other nation, but perhaps more importantly, many of its birds are beautiful, easy to encounter, and found nowhere else in the world. To dedicated birding tourists, a beautiful endemic bird, such as the Multicolored Tanager (Chlorochrysa nitidissima), can be a powerful attractant. And Colombia has endemic birds by the dozens (79 based on Chaparro‐Herrera et al. 2013). In addition to birds, Colombia hosts a greater diversity of plants, butterflies, amphibians, and freshwater fish, reptiles, and mammal than all but one or few other countries in the world (Instituto Alexander von Humboldt 2018).

    With a sense of security established and natural attractions identified, one final prerequisite for post‐conflict ecotourism remains the immediate availability of resources to invest. Post‐conflict communities and governments must work quickly to facilitate the growth of an ecotourism industry before extraction degrades a potentially valuable shared resource. Investments in physical amenities for visitors are required, such as access roads, hotels, restaurants, trail systems, boardwalks, and observation towers, but less obvious and no less important is the development of human and political infrastructure. The training of guides, drivers, and cooks; establishment of sound and enforceable labor laws; and the implementation of revenue‐sharing schemes with local communities can all be critical to the social sustainability of a post‐conflict ecotourism industry.

    The Colombian government appears to be aware of both the urgency and its key area of competitiveness, as it has moved swiftly to support bird‐focused ecotourism. It has launched a national bird‐watching strategy, established networks of bird‐watching trails, and supported an annual Colombian Bird Fair. These efforts seem to be making a difference so far as tourism numbers and revenue are concerned, but there is little evidence that it has yet delivered much in the way of sustainable conservation benefits to post‐conflict regions where deforestation rates jumped by 44 percent in 2016 (Brodzinsky 2017). So even with Colombia appearing to meet all the requirements at the national level, conservation success is not guaranteed without effective and complementary efforts at regional scales (Negret et al. 2017). Rural Colombia faces the challenges of resettling displaced peoples and reintegrating combatants, which have caused defaunation problems in Cambodia (Loucks et al. 2009). The inevitable disputes over land tenure could threaten peace and stability, as it has in Liberia (Brottem & Unruh 2009). On top of that, the power vacuum left by the FARC in Colombia is troubling.

    The possibility of catastrophic failure only makes building ecotourism in Colombia more urgent, as a profitable peace‐dependent industry can provide a strong disincentive against violence. In Rwanda, revenues from Mountain Gorilla tourism were so significant and dispersed across so many different local stakeholders that re‐ignition of violent conflict became a kind of mutual assured financial destruction (Maekawa et al. 2013). Even as tensions between the governments of Rwanda and neighboring Democratic Republic of Congo ran high, local authorities from opposite sides of the border maintained a spirit of cooperation built atop a foundation of mutual interest in the preservation of gorillas and the flow of tourist revenue. The recent assassination of six rangers at Virunga National Park in Democratic Republic of Congo in April 2018 represents a setback. Despite this regrettable event, ecotourism continues to provide a strong incentive for otherwise antagonistic parties to cooperate to keep the peace and maintain the integrity of Mountain Gorilla habitats.

    The stakes are highest for the rural communities, which stand to gain the most from ecotourism, or conversely lose everything if violence resumes. In Rwanda, local communities benefited from Gorilla tourism both directly through revenue‐sharing and indirectly through tourist‐associated business opportunities and therefore had strong financial incentives to support conservation. Locals switching to work in ecotourism in South Africa saw their wages rise from USD 114 to USD 362 monthly (Biggs et al. 2011). In Colombia, rural people and recently demobilized guerrillas have immense knowledge of the ecosystems and species in their surroundings, but lack training in service professions tailored to ecotourists. By offering training and jobs for these people, it may be possible to reduce their involvement in illegal activities in the future, thus contributing to overall peace.

    To stem the tide of deforestation following peace, national, regional, and local governments would be wise to enact policies that incentivize and support investments in the ecotourism industry. Countries must also resist the potentially corrupting influence of extractive industries, such as logging and mining. Not only are they destructive to ecosystems and ecotourism opportunities, but they also often exacerbate rural wealth inequality (Gamu et al. 2015). Social sustainability standards have been designed for bird‐watching tourism in Colombia (Ministerio de Comercio et al. 2017), and other types of tourism will need similar guidelines to insure the inclusion of local communities. For moral and political reasons, it is also important to facilitate accessibility of domestic citizens to ensure that ecotourism opportunities are not provided exclusively for the enjoyment of the foreigners who serve as the financial engine (Pardo‐Ibarra 2017). Ultimately, the key is to find a way to get local communities, former combatants, and the public at large to buy into a socioeconomic strategy that does not lead to rapid environmental destruction. It takes a portfolio of solutions to meet conservation and social development goals during post‐conflict transitions, but in the appropriate context, ecotourism can have great potential (Krüger 2005).

    Peace is cause for cautious optimism. The opportunity to improve the livelihoods and economies of communities impacted by conflict is exciting, but represents a major challenge for governments. The task is even greater for biodiverse nations that face an additional pressure to conserve ecosystems. Here, we have argued that biodiversity conservation is potentially an asset rather than a liability, so long as it is possible to build a successful ecotourism industry. We acknowledge that ecotourism cannot be the only solution and that it will not work everywhere. Other approaches must complement an ecotourism strategy to make progress in the domains of conservation and sustainable livelihoods in post‐conflict scenarios. It is our hope that the suggestions we have outlined serve as a useful framework for nations transitioning toward peace and pursuing ecotourism strategies.

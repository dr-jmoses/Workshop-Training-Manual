Abstract

Insect–plant interactions occur in several ways and have considerable environmental and ecological importance. Many feeding strategies have evolved among herbivorous insects, with host–herbivore systems likely being influenced by trophobionts with ants. We investigated how these interactions vary across elevation gradients by evaluating the structure of the herbivorous insect community and ants associated with Baccharis dracunculifolia at three distinct elevations (800, 1100, and 1400 m a.s.l.) on a mountain in southeastern Brazil. Moreover, we evaluated the diversity and specialisation of interactions between herbivores and host plants along the elevational gradient. We sampled herbivores and ants on 60 plants at each elevation (totalling 180 plant individuals). Herbivore species composition differed among elevations, as did interaction diversity and specialisation. Richness and abundance of chewing insects increased with elevation, while β‐diversity among patches of the host plant was higher at the lowest elevation, probably due to the patchy occurrence of B. dracunculifolia. Richness and abundance of sap‐sucking insects were higher at the intermediate elevation, possibly due to local environmental conditions. We observed a positive relationship between ant and herbivore trophobiont richness on B. dracunculifolia. We found that interactions were more specialised and less diverse at higher elevations compared to the lowest elevation. Changes in vegetation and environmental variables shaped species distributions and their ecological interactions along the elevation gradient. Our study demonstrates that increased elevation changes the structure and patterns of interactions of the herbivore insect guilds associated with the host plant B. dracunculifolia. Ant effects depend on the context, the environment, and the species of ants involved, and are essential for the presence of insect trophobionts.

Introduction

Changes in abiotic conditions and vegetation with elevation play essential roles in affecting α and β‐diversity of insect communities (e.g. Lawton et al. 1987; Fernandes & Price 1988; Kumar et al. 2009; Kraft et al. 2011; Fernandes 2016; Peters et al. 2016; Nunes et al. 2016a,b; Longino & Branstetter 2018). Climatic variation at higher elevations (see Rasmann et al. 2014) can represent an active driver for such changes. In the tropics, wind speed and solar radiation are more impactful at higher elevations, while temperature and primary productivity are lower; factors that play essential roles in determining species diversity and community structure (e.g. Fernandes & Price 1988; Lara et al. 2002; Fernandes et al. 2016; Longino & Branstetter 2018). Indeed, studies have found greater insect diversity at lower elevations (e.g. Sax & Gaines 2013; Fernandes 2016; Longino & Branstetter 2018). Nevertheless, knowledge on how elevation influences the distribution of animal–plant interactions remains anecdotal (but see Bascompte 2009; Ramos‐Jilibert et al. 2010; Bascompte & Jordano 2013; Chamberlain et al. 2014), particularly considering a single host plant species at different elevations.

Insect–plant interactions occur in several ways and therefore are of high environmental and ecological importance (Price et al. 2011). Interactions between herbivorous insects and plants can be affected by host plant traits or by variation in environmental conditions (Novotny et al. 2003). In general, there is high turnover of herbivore insect species host plant (Kemp et al. 2017; Silva et al. 2017). This pattern is associated with the high diversity of plants and high host‐specificity among herbivorous insects in tropical environments (Silva et al. 2017). Herbivorous insect feeding guilds (sap‐sucking and chewing) may respond differently to the effects of host plant spatial distribution (Denno & Roderick 1991). Thus, feeding guilds can be expected to exhibit different responses along elevational gradients. The diversity of herbivorous insects associated with a species of host plant is known as γ‐diversity (total diversity in a given region) and can be partitioned into at least two components, α‐diversity (i.e. the number of species at the local scale) and β‐diversity (differences in species composition among sites; Whittaker 1972; Tuomisto 2010). Understanding α‐diversity and β‐diversity is crucial to discovering the mechanisms underpinning patterns of biodiversity on local and regional scales (Whittaker 1972; Jankowski et al. 2009; Antão et al. 2019).

Baccharis dracunculifolia DC (Asteraceae) are a widespread dioecious shrub at various elevations in southeastern Brazil (Fagundes & Fernandes 2011), where it typically occurs in dense and well‐defined patches that vary from 18 to 12 000 m2 (Collevatti & Sperber 1997). The species is a host plant for a diverse array of herbivores, ants and parasitoids, and has been used to test many ecological hypotheses for more than two decades (e.g. Fernandes & Price 1988; Ribeiro‐Mendes 2002; Fagundes et al. 2005; Julião et al. 2005; Barbosa et al. 2016). Some of these studies have recorded mutualistic interactions between ants and trophobiont insects associated with B. dracunculifolia (Fagundes et al. 2005; Fagundes & Fernandes 2011; Neves et al. 2011; Barbosa et al. 2016).

Ants represent an important group of insects that strongly influence interactions in host–herbivore–parasitoid systems (Delabie 2001). Many ant species exhibit aggressive behaviour towards herbivores, while defending trophobionts, which produce honeydew as a ‘currency of exchange’ (Delabie 2001; Rosumek et al. 2009). Fagundes et al. (2005) and Neves et al. (2011) argued that interactions among B. dracunculifolia‐associated ants and insect trophobionts determine changes in the community structure of associated herbivores. Thus, we expect that elevations with greater ant abundance will have greater richness and abundance of herbivorous insect trophobionts and lower richness and abundance of herbivorous non‐trophobiont insects. However, the effect of biotic and abiotic changes over different elevations on specific interactions between herbivorous insects and ants on B. dracunculifolia is not well understood. Since this host plant occurs throughout the entire elevation gradient of Serra do Cipó, Brazil, it presents a suitable opportunity for studying variation in community structure and interactions among its associated insects at different elevations (Fagundes & Fernandes 2011).

Although it represents one of the most common interactions in nature, little is known about the plant‐herbivore antagonistic interaction from the perspective of interaction networks (López‐Carretero et al. 2018), particularly in tropical mountains. Ecological networks allow us to understand better the complex interactions among organisms by evaluating the consequences of environmental variation at community and ecosystem levels (Dáttilo & Rico‐Gray 2018). Quantification of these changes aids in the identification of factors that are important in structuring ecological networks and provides information on how such interactions are affected by variation in elevation (Maunsell et al. 2015). Network analytical tools have allowed the operationalisation of ecological concepts such as specialisation (Blüthgen et al. 2006, 2007). It has been postulated that conditions at different elevations should produce interaction networks that vary in specialisation and diversity (Ramos‐Jiliberto et al. 2010; Maunsell et al. 2015). Sites with more specialised interaction networks should have less species overlap, be less robust, and exhibit random extinction of species, while networks with greater diversity of interactions should be more stable (Dáttilo 2012; Maunsell et al. 2015).

We evaluated the structure of the herbivorous insect community and ants associated with B. dracunculifolia at three distinct elevations, as well as the patterns of interaction between herbivores and plants using tools derived from network theory. We tested the hypothesis that elevation affects the diversity of herbivorous insect species associated with a single host plant, with the following predictions: (i) the composition of herbivorous insects on plants of B. dracunculifolia will vary with elevation; (ii) richness, abundance and β‐diversity of herbivorous insects among patches of host plants will be higher at lower elevations; and (iii) ants will have a positive influence on the richness and abundance of herbivorous insect trophobionts, and a negative influence the richness and abundance of non‐trophobiont herbivorous insects, independent of elevation. Additionally, using network descriptors we predicted that (iv) the herbivorous insect‐host plant interaction would be more diverse but less specialised at lower elevations.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study area

This study took place in campo rupestre (rupestrian grassland) vegetation of the southern portion of the Espinhaço Mountain Range in the Serra do Cipó, in the central region of Minas Gerais, Brazil. The campo rupestre ecosystem is characterised by high plant species richness with high levels of endemism and several endangered species (Fernandes 2016; Silveira et al. 2016; Fernandes et al., 2016; Mota et al. 2018). In more mesic areas at higher elevations, the vegetation of the landscape consists of Atlantic Forest fragments in a matrix of campo rupestre, while at lower elevations the predominant vegetation type is Cerrado (see Mota et al. 2018).

Sample design

Twenty randomly selected B. dracunculifolia plants were collected at three different patches (minimum distance among them = 2 km) at each elevation of 800, 1100 and 1400 m (N = 180). Plants of B. dracunculifolia were found at elevations up to 1400 m (a.s.l). Individual plants were separated by a minimum of five metres. Only individuals of the host plant B. dracunculifolia between 1.5 and 2.5 m in height were chosen to avoid influence of plant size on insect herbivore richness and abundance (Espírito‐Santo et al. 2012).

Insect sampling

Insects were collected in August 2012, November 2012 and February 2013, using the beating technique (30 beats per plant) with an entomological umbrella. Herbivorous insects were identified to the level of family, and morphospecies were used to analyse richness, abundance and composition of two herbivore guilds: chewing and sap‐sucking (Borror et al. 2002). Identification of the insects to lower taxonomical levels was virtually impossible given the limited knowledge of the invertebrate fauna of the region. The herbivore insect families were grouped as trophobiont sap‐sucking (Membracidae and Aphididae), non‐trophobiont sap‐sucking or chewing. Ants (Formicidae) were identified, their abundance recorded as the total number of individuals collected per patch, and their frequency of occurrence per individual host plant determined. Collected insects were fixed in 70% alcohol and stored in entomological boxes at the Insect Ecology Laboratory of the Universidade Federal de Minas Gerais (UFMG).

Data analysis

To test our first prediction we used Permutational Multivariate Analysis of Variance (permanova) with Bray–Curtis dissimilarity and 1000 permutations to test for effects of elevation on host plant‐associated insect species composition (Anderson 2001, 2006). We used cumulative insect herbivore species composition for each host plant patch (N = 3 per elevation) for the analyses. We performed a multivariate analysis of permutational distance (PERMDISP) to test the dispersion of the data. The matrix of Bray–Curtis dissimilarity values generated by permanova was used to test for homogeneity of the herbivorous insect community among elevations (Anderson 2001, 2006). We used non‐linear multidimensional scaling (NMDS; Clarke 1993) to produce a graphical representation of the species composition analysis. All analyses were performed using the ‘vegan’ package (Oksanen et al. 2016) in R (R Development Core Team 2018).

To test our second prediction, that abundance, richness and β‐diversity will be higher at lower elevations, we calculated β‐diversity using the formula: β = 1 − (ᾱ/γ) (see Kraft et al. 2011), where γ represents the total richness of herbivorous insects per elevation (chewing and sap‐sucking) and ᾱ represents the mean number of herbivores (chewing and sap‐sucking) per host patch. We then constructed generalised linear mixed models (GLMMs) using the function GLMER from package lme4 (Bates et al. 2015) with herbivorous insect (chewing and sap‐sucking) richness per patch (α), abundance per patch and β‐diversity among patches as response variables; elevation as a fixed categorical variable; and sampling month as the random variable. For ants, we used ant abundance as the response variable, elevation as the explanatory variable and month as a random variable. When significant differences were observed between elevations, we performed contrast analyses to determine how the response variables differed at each (Crawley 2013).

To test our third prediction, that ants influence the richness and abundance of herbivorous insects associated with B. dracunculifolia, we used GLMMs with richness and abundance of sap‐sucking insects (trophobionts and non‐trophobionts) and chewing insects as response variables; ant abundance and elevation as fixed variables, and sampling month as the random variable. We used sampling month as a random variable to remove the effect of temporal pseudoreplication. For all models, distributions were tested through residual analysis.

To test our fourth prediction, that herbivorous insect‐host plant interactions would be more diverse but less specialised at lower elevations, we used metrics derived from graph theory (see Blüthgen et al. 2006, 2007). We accomplished this by constructing an interaction matrix (B) for each of the three collection sites at each of the three elevations, where Bij = number of interactions between an individual plant i with herbivorous species j, with zero representing the absence of interactions. The index was used to calculate the degree of specialisation (Blüthgen et al. 2006, 2007). This specialisation index ranges from  = 0 (extremely general) to  = 1 (extremely specialised) and is more robust than most other specialisation indices by incorporating frequency data (for more details, see Blüthgen et al. 2006, 2007). We calculated the diversity of interactions for each area using the ID index, which is based on Shannon entropy and has been widely used in ecological network analyses (Bersier et al. 2002; Dáttilo & Dyer 2014; Falcão et al. 2015). Both network analyses were calculated using the bipartite package (Dormann et al. 2008) in R (R Development Core Team 2018). We then constructed GLMMs with specialisation metrics and diversity of interactions as response variables, elevation as the fixed variable and months of sampling as the random variable. All analyses were performed in R (R Development Core Team 2018).

RESULTS

We collected a total of 7402 free‐living insect herbivores from the 180 individual B. dracunculifolia studied, which represented 132 different morphospecies. We recorded four families of chewing insect herbivores (Acrididae, Chrysomelidae, Curculionidae and Elateridae), nine families of non‐trophobiont sap‐sucking insect herbivores (Acanaloniidae, Alydidae, Cicadellidae, Cixidae, Lygaelidae, Miridae, Psylidae, Pyrrohoridae and Tingidae) and two families of herbivorous insect trophobionts (Aphididae and Membracidae). We collected 233 ants of 27 species (Table 1). The genus Camponotus was the most abundant with 86 records and eight species, while Camponotus crassus and Camponotus westermanni were the most abundant species (Table 1).

There was a greater abundance of ants at 1100 m (Fig. 3a, Table 2). For the interaction between ants and herbivorous insects, we found that trophobiont sap‐sucking insects increased in richness (but not in abundance) with increasing ant abundance (Fig. 3b,c, Table 2) and that this ratio seemed to be stronger at 1100 m (Fig. 3c). Ant abundance did not affect the non‐trophobiont herbivores (Table 2).

The herbivorous insect network metrics varied with elevation. The diversity of plant‐herbivore interactions was lowest at the highest elevation (Fig. 2g, Table 2), indicating a less stable system with more asymmetry (dependence) in the interactions. More specialised interaction networks were found at 1100 and 1400 m, indicating less overlap of herbivorous species per plant (Fig. 2h, Table 2).

Discussion

The B. dracunculifolia herbivore community and the interactions among herbivores differed among the three elevations. Thus, elevation influenced herbivorous insect composition and guild structure. For both chewing and sap‐sucking guilds, the composition of insects differed among the three elevations analysed. However, the richness and abundance of sap‐sucking insects were higher at the intermediate elevation, whereas for chewing insects they were higher at the highest elevation. These results are in contrast to studies on other insect groups in the study area, such as dung beetles, termites and ants (Fernandes et al. 2016), and on a multi‐taxa community on Mt. Kilimanjaro (Peters et al. 2016). We observed a higher degree of specialisation of interactions (i.e. less stable and with more asymmetry) and lower diversity (i.e. less overlapping of herbivorous species per plant) at the highest elevation, which is in agreement with our prediction. Ant abundance positively affected only the richness of sap‐sucking trophobionts, partially supporting our prediction that ants will have a positive influence on the richness and abundance of herbivorous insect trophobionts, and a negative influence on the richness and abundance of non‐trophobiont herbivorous insects, independent of elevation.

Differences in vegetation structure and environmental conditions among elevations are likely responsible for altering the herbivorous insect species composition associated with B. dracunculifolia. The Cerrado vegetation being more common at lower elevations, and campo rupestre vegetation being more common at higher elevations (Mota et al. 2018) and a difference of ~4°C was observed between the lowest (800 m) and the highest (1400 m) areas of our study site (Fernandes et al. 2016). Changes in temperature and vegetation structure have often been linked to substantial effects on the development and survival of many insect groups (e.g. Speight et al. 1999; Régnière et al. 2012). Despite the presence of the host plant throughout the elevation gradient, climatic differences among elevations may have filtered the species of herbivorous insects able to survive and succeed at different elevations. Since higher elevations have greater climatic severity (Lawton et al., 1987), such as higher wind speeds and solar radiation (Hodinkson 2005) and lower temperatures (Fernandes et al., 2016), climatic conditions are likely responsible for altering the herbivorous insect species composition associated with B. dracunculifolia.

Richness and abundance of sap‐sucking insects on B. dracunculifolia were highest at the intermediate elevation (1100 m), while β‐diversity was lowest at 1100 m. At the intermediate elevation, there may be increased overlap of the distributions of other host species (Colwell & Lees 2000). This overlap, combined with higher primary productivity and environmental boundaries of the distributions of populations, may explain the peak in richness and abundance at the intermediate elevation (Janzen 1973; Terborgh 1977); however, additional experimental studies are needed to understand this pattern better. The high β‐diversity of sap‐sucking insects at all elevations is mainly due to the high frequency of rare species within each environment (Novotný & Basset 2000). Only the families Psyllidae and Aphididae were common to all plants, likely due to the high frequency of gall‐inducing species such as Baccharopelma dracunculifoliae (Psyllidae) and Uroleucon erigeronensis (Aphididae) that occur on B. dracunculifolia (Lara & Fernandes 1994; Fagundes et al. 2005; Neves et al. 2011).

Richness and abundance of chewing insects were higher at the highest elevation (1400 m); however, β‐diversity was lowest, showing homogeneity of the chewing insect community in the area. This result contrasts with the findings of other studies of herbivorous insect communities that showed decreased diversity with increasing elevation (Ribeiro et al. 1998; Araújo & Fernandes 2003; Kumar et al. 2009). Baccharis dracunculifolia occurs in patchy habitats (Collevatti & Sperber 1997), which at higher elevations are composed predominantly of grasses; thus, there is less availability of other shrub hosts compared to the Cerrado vegetation at lower elevations (see Mota et al. 2018). In Serra do Cipó, the frequency of phanerophytes (woody plants) is highest at 800 m (67.08%) and 900 m (60.66%), and lowest at 1300 m (8.71%) and 1400 m (3.73) (Mota et al. 2018). Because B. dracunculifolia is one of the few shrub species at the highest elevation, it represents a more available resource for herbivorous insects (Feeny 1976; Louthan et al., 2014), and insect herbivores increase polyphagy at higher elevations (Rasmann et al. 2014), using whatever host plant is available. At lower elevations, patches of B. dracunculifolia in Cerrado vegetation are occupied by several other shrubby species (see Mota et al. 2018), so generalist herbivores can use other host plants, thereby increasing β‐diversity among B. dracunculifolia patches.

We found increased ant abundance to be associated with the higher richness of trophobiont sap‐sucking herbivores and that it did not affect non‐trophobiont herbivores. However, this effect on trophobiont richness may be greater at 1100 m due to greater ant abundance. Some experimental studies have also demonstrated the importance of ants in reducing the abundance of herbivores in ant‐aphid‐plant systems (e.g. Del‐Claro & Oliveira 2000; Fagundes et al. 2005; Styrsky & Eubanks 2007; Rosumek et al. 2009). Furthermore, a previous study has shown experimentally that the presence of ants reduces the abundance of non‐trophobiont herbivores on B. dracunculifolia (Neves et al. 2011). Thus, the effects of ants can vary among study sites, probably relative to the behaviour of the species involved in the interaction (see Del‐Claro & Marquis 2015). The effects of ants are commonly context‐dependent and change as a function of biotic (Koch et al. 2016) and abiotic (Jones et al. 2017) conditions. The ant species found on a particular host plant may be more critical than its abundance. Three species found in this study, Cephalotes pussilus, Camponotus crassus and Camponotus rufipes, are considered core species in insect–plant interaction systems in Campos rupestres, not because of abundance, but because of high recruitment rates (Costa et al. 2016). As a consequence of the low abundance of these ants, especially at higher elevations, their low recruitment rate reduces the risk of insects being attacked by them (Ribeiro et al. 2005; Lourenço et al. 2015) and provides an opportunity for other insects to use resources, as well as increased occurrence of non‐dominant ant species, such as Camponotus westermanni, and non‐trophobiont insect herbivores.

For networks involving interactions between herbivorous insects and the host plant, interactions became less diverse and more specialised at the highest elevation. Climatic variation along the sampled elevation gradient (e.g. precipitation and temperature) (see Fernandes et al. 2016) may affect the availability and quality of host plants and promote changes in the networks of interactions (see Rasmann et al. 2014; Dáttilo & Rico‐Gray 2018) as found in our study. Our results are similar to those found in studies conducted in the Chilean Andes and mountains in eastern Australia, where the number of interactions (plant–pollinator system) decreased with increasing elevation (Ramos‐Jiliberto et al. 2010). Another study, conducted by Maunsell et al. (2015), showed that most species of mining insects exploited only one host at the highest elevations. Elevation gradients have abiotic conditions that can impose restrictions on foraging for food. For example, low temperatures at higher elevations may decrease insect activity (Virtanen & Neuvonen 1999; Sunday et al. 2014), reducing the overlap of herbivorous species per plant. The lower diversity of interactions indicates a system with lower stability at the highest elevation (Dáttilo 2012), since, according to the models created by Mougi and Kondoh (2012), increased complexity of interactions leads to greater stability. Ecological interactions thus regulate biological populations and communities and play a key role in structuring biodiversity (see Mougi & Kondoh 2012; Dáttilo & Rico‐Gray 2018). In this way, our study contributes to understanding variation in antagonistic interactions (plant‐herbivore) in tropical mountains.

We found that variation in environmental conditions and resources at different elevations leads to changes in the community of herbivorous insects associated with B. dracuncufolia. Trophic guilds responded differently to variation among elevations, with sap‐sucking herbivores having greater richness at the intermediate elevation, which is probably due to abiotic conditions. The highest elevation showed the greater richness of chewing herbivorous insects associated with B. dracunculifolia, probably due to the high probability and predictability of host plant encounter. Ant effects are context‐dependent and the high elevation environment also contained ecological interactions that are less diverse and more specialised, making this environment more sensitive to environmental change.

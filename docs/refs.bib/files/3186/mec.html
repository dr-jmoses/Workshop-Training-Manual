Abstract

Island biodiversity has long fascinated biologists as it typically presents tractable systems for unpicking the eco‐evolutionary processes driving community assembly. In general, two recurring themes are of central theoretical interest. First, immigration, diversification, and extinction typically depend on island geographical properties (e.g., area, isolation, and age). Second, predictable ecological and evolutionary trajectories readily occur after colonization, such as the evolution of adaptive trait syndromes, trends toward specialization, adaptive radiation, and eventual ecological decline. Hypotheses such as the taxon cycle draw on several of these themes to posit particular constraints on colonization and subsequent eco‐evolutionary dynamics. However, it has been challenging to examine these integrated dynamics with traditional methods. Here, we combine phylogenomics, population genomics and phenomics, to unravel community assembly dynamics among Pheidole (Hymenoptera, Formicidae) ants in the isolated Fijian archipelago. We uphold basic island biogeographic predictions that isolated islands accumulate diversity primarily through in situ evolution rather than dispersal, and population genomic support for taxon cycle predictions that endemic species have decreased dispersal ability and demography relative to regionally widespread taxa. However, rather than trending toward island syndromes, ecomorphological diversification in Fiji was intense, filling much of the genus‐level global morphospace. Furthermore, while most endemic species exhibit demographic decline and reduced dispersal, we show that the archipelago is not an evolutionary dead‐end. Rather, several endemic species show signatures of population and range expansion, including a successful colonization to the Cook islands. These results shed light on the processes shaping island biotas and refine our understanding of island biogeographic theory.

1 INTRODUCTION

Understanding the dynamics that underpin island community assembly has long been a major fascination of ecological and evolutionary science (Gillespie et al., 2012; Losos & Ricklefs, 2009; MacArthur & Wilson, 1967; Whittaker, 1998). The basic dynamics involved: the dispersal of species across biogeographic barriers, their evolution into and out of ecological niches, and the rise and fall of population abundance, have been much investigated despite challenges to their direct observation. A central question is whether there are constraints on such dynamics that lead to repeated outcomes that recur across lineages (e.g., Losos, 2017), or whether such dynamics may be essentially random and unpredictable. In the latter case, dispersal may be unrelated to ecological niche, the eco‐evolutionary trajectories of lineages after colonization may follow unpredictable paths, and adaptive radiation may be driven by historical contingencies and not follow regular pathways (e.g., Fukami, 2015). However, these are not binary alternatives, some mix of predictable and stochastic processes should be expected.

The modern synthesis on island biogeography incorporates evolutionary and ecological processes against a backdrop of geoenvironmental dynamics (Whittaker, Fernandez‐Palacios, Matthews, Borregaard, & Triantis, 2017). A general question for any island system relates to the degree to which the biota is assembled through dispersal of lineages and ecomorphological forms to an island, versus in situ diversification. This difference is expected to be modulated by dispersal ability, island isolation, and island area (Gillespie et al., 2012; Heaney, 2007; Triantis, Guilhaumon, & Whittaker, 2012). For remote archipelagoes of at least moderate size and for groups that have high dispersal limitation, community assembly is thought to be predominantly seeded by long‐distance dispersal and in situ radiation of endemic lineages.

Aside from relative roles of dispersal versus evolutionary assembly, another question pertains to what happens to lineages after colonization. Many authors have argued that lineages arriving on islands should evolve in a particular direction after colonization, acquiring suites of traits beneficial for island life according to the taxonomic group. These reflect particular arrays of selection pressures, or lack thereof, from the island ecosystems or by the peculiarities of isolation, that may not represent the full selective repertoire faced by related taxa in continental biomes. This is notably evident in the tendency towards flightlessness in island endemic Rallidae (McCall, Nee, & Harvey, 1998). Other examples include a tendency towards diminished flight musculature and hindlimb modification among other species of birds, woodiness among typically herbaceous plant lineages, body size changes and naivety towards predators (see references in Whittaker et al., 2017).

While trends towards such island syndromes is one possible outcome, another is adaptive radiation, the rapid divergence of lineages to acquire different ecomorphologies. This is expected particularly when vacant niches are available (De Meester, Vanoverbeke, Kilsdonk, & Urban, 2016), which may in turn depend on the isolation of the archipelago and general richness of the local biota across taxa (Gillespie et al., 2012). The ecomorphological diversity of adaptive radiations can often match and even far exceed the same group on the mainland.

Aside from changes in ecomorphology per se, authors have posited that post‐colonization evolution could lead to eventual declines in the abundance of a species. This idea was a key component of the taxon cycle hypothesis (Wilson, 1959, 1961), an early idea in island biogeography proposing that species go through a phase of range expansion and contraction subject to niche shifts into and out of marginal habitats. First, the taxon cycle proposes that dispersal and island colonization is habitat‐dependent, in that species adapted to certain habitats more readily disperse and expand their range. For example, it is expected that dispersal by nonvolant species should primarily occur via infrequent rafting events across oceanic expanses by species adapted to marginal, coastal habitats rather than by species adapted to high elevations (Gillespie et al., 2012). Second, after arriving on new territory, species are initially very successful and abundant (potentially due to ecological release from competitors, predators, parasites, disease or due to a preadapted ability in using some novel resource). Over time, these species should evolve toward interior habitats, decline in abundance, and potentially become more specialized while their ranges disintegrate into complexes of geographically localized species. Species may eventually decline to extinction, potentially via ecological competition or reductions in landmass due to tectonic activity or sea level change. However, Wilson envisioned endemic species occasionally becoming “reactivated” into a new phase of range expansion and beginning  a new cycle (Wilson, 1959, 1961).

The Melanesian ant fauna at least partially inspired several important developments in island biogeography, including the taxon cycle and the Equilibrium Theory of Island Biogeography (MacArthur & Wilson, 1967), and continues to be a subject of biogeographic study (Clouse et al., 2015; Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Economo, Sarnat, et al., 2015; Matos‐Maravi et al., 2018, 2018). Considering the entire Fijian ant fauna, Economo and Sarnat (2012) tested and supported the taxon cycle prediction that more endemic faunas are concentrated in the interior, upland, and more pristine habitats, compared to nonendemics and exotics. However, these ecological correlations alone are consistent with taxon cycle‐like dynamics but do not represent definitive evidence.

Other approaches at the phylogenetic or population genetic levels are needed to reconstruct the historical dynamics underlying contemporary pattern. Phylogenetic approaches have been applied to different ant genera in the region in the context of taxon cycle dynamics including Camponotus, Prenolepis, Odontomachus, and Pheidole (Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Economo, Sarnat, et al., 2015; Sarnat & Moreau, 2011). These studies generally supported the existence of habitat‐dependent dispersal on large scales, finding that large‐scale biogeographic expansions are associated with open habitats.

Pheidole is particularly interesting as a model for testing ideas about community assembly. In Fiji it is the second most species‐rich genus (19 extant species; Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Fischer, Sarnat, & Economo, 2016; Sarnat, 2008), with species representing different eco‐geographic syndromes. Specifically, there are regionally widespread disturbance‐adapted generalists (e.g., Pheidole oceanica, Pheidole umbonata), more generalized (i.e., with typical morphologies and ecologies) but forest‐adapted endemics (the knowlesi group plus Pheidole onifera), morphologically and ecologically specialized endemics (the roosevelti group, featuring much more specialized turreted nest construction, morphological spinescence, and low‐vegetation foraging strategies than are typical of Pheidole; Sarnat, 2008; Sarnat & Moreau, 2011; Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Sarnat & Economo, 2012), along with a few human mediated exotics (Pheidole megacephala, Pheidole fervens).

Phylogenetic studies (Economo et al., 2019; Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Economo, Sarnat, et al., 2015; Sarnat & Moreau, 2011) have indicated that the Pheidole fauna of Fiji was more assembled through in situ radiation than would be predicted from morphology alone (including, the in situ evolution of unusual spinescent forms convergent with separate lineages in Asia and New Guinea). These phylogenetic studies, based on a few nuclear and mitochondrial genes, disagreed on whether the roosevelti and knowlesi groups are reciprocally monophyletic in Fiji, or whether the roosevelti group is nested within the knowlesi group, (Economo et al., 2019; Economo, Klimov, et al., 2015; Economo, Sarnat, et al., 2015; Sarnat & Moreau, 2011). Additionally, specimens resembling a knowlesi group species, Pheidole ululevu, were recently discovered by N. Porch 2,300 km east of Fiji on Raratonga in the Cook Islands (Fischer et al., 2016). If a Fijian endemic species has indeed colonized the Cook Islands, an area previously suggested as being devoid of native ants (Wilson & Taylor, 1967), it could represent a new expanding phase arising from an endemic radiation.

Despite this progress, we are far from a thorough understanding of whether the eco‐evolutionary processes shaping the extant Fijian Pheidole correspond with island biogeographic predictions. Here, we integrate population genomics, phylogenomics, and 3D X‐ray microtomography to probe deeply into the post‐colonization community dynamics of Fijian Pheidole. First, we reconstruct a new phylogeny for the group with genome‐wide (RADseq) markers to definitively settle the phylogenetic structure of the community and the number of colonizations of Fiji, as well as evaluate the status of the newly discovered form in the Cook Islands. Second, we use phenomic methods to quantitatively analyze the morphological evolution occurring in Fiji and determine whether these reflect morphological trends toward an island syndrome or diversification indicative of adaptive radiation. Third, we evaluate population genomic evidence for declines in population abundance and dispersal abilities in the native fauna. Specifically, we evaluate if level of endemism (e.g., widespread or endemic) and level of ecomorphological specialization within the endemics (knowlesi group vs. roosevelti group) are related to population structure across the islands and responses to landscape connectivity under historic sea levels, which would be expected if there are trends toward loss of dispersal ability. We furthermore use the genomic data to evaluate demographic trends and whether there is any evidence that the endemic species are declining relative to widespread species, and whether there is any evidence of secondary expansion of species nested within the endemic radiation. We interpret these findings in the context of island biogeography theory and ideas such as the taxon cycle.

2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1 Study system

The current study is based upon material collected during an extensive field inventory of the Fijian ant fauna, conducted from 2004 to 2011 and summarized in Sarnat and Economo (2012). The Fijian ant fauna is notable for its high endemism, with approximately 70% of the species occurring nowhere else. The high rates of endemism are primarily attributed to Fiji's origin as a Darwinian island (Gillespie & Roderick, 2002), and subsequent isolation relative to large landmasses of the Sahul tectonic plate. Fiji is situated 1,800 km north of New Zealand and >2,600 km east of Australia and New Guinea. The largest Fijian island, Viti Levu, has been above sea level for the longest, ~25 Ma (Hall, 2002). A second large island, Vanua Levu, emerged within the last 5 Ma (Rodda, 1994) whilst most of the smaller outer islands have emerged in the last 5 Ma. Bathymetric data (Figure 1) indicate that the oldest and largest island, Viti Levu (encompassing the coastal islets of Macuata and Nanui‐I‐Ra), lies in shallower waters that connect to the second largest island, Vanua Levu, and additional field survey islands of Ovalau, Beqa and Taveuni. The remaining survey sites on Koro, Kadavu, Gau, Moala and Lakeba are therefore more disconnected with deeper straits of water separating them. Recent bathymetric change data shows that a recent maximal landmass was reached during the Pleistocene (Sarnat, 2009) when inter‐island connectivity was relatively high compared to other periods of bathymetric oscillation.

In total, 15 recognized Pheidole species have material available for this study from 12 Fijian islands including all the larger islands and several small islands. Each island was considered a putative single local population except for the two largest islands, Viti Levu and Vanua Levu, which we broke into four and two local populations, respectively (Table 1). Sequencing sample sizes for individual species range from three (Pheidole caldwelli and Pheidole furcata) to 174 (P. ululevu) and are largely representative of sampling encounter rates during fieldwork (Table 1; exceptions to this are P. ululevu and Pheidole wilsoni for which field sampling yielded much higher specimen numbers).

2.2 Historical sea level connectance estimation

To evaluate the expected degree of population connectivity over geological time we used bathymetric data downloaded from GEBCO (Figure 1) and local sea level change derived from (Haq, Hardenbol, & Vail, 1987; Miller et al., 2005). We divided the currently submerged Fijian shelf into 10 m depth intervals and calculated the relative exposure times of each interval during the last 2.6 Ma (an historic timescale encompassing high interisland connectivity during Pleistocene bathymetric oscillations and when all investigated extant species had diversified from ancestral stock) using custom python scripts. We then used these relative times of exposure (%) as conductance values on a 0–100 scale (with currently exposed shelf zones attributed a score of 100) to rescale geographic distances between pairwise sampling localities under a circuit theory approach (McRae & Beier, 2007; McRae, Dickson, Keitt, & Shah, 2008). Pairwise bathymetric resistances between sampling localities of each species were calculated in circuitscape 4.0.5 (McRae, Shah, & Mohapatra, 2013). The resulting rescaled bathymetric distance matrices were then used to assess for the effect of historic sea level (a.s.l.) change on population connectivity (by comparing with pairwise FST using Mantel analyses), while considering the spatial connectance of land corridors that have intermittently formed among islands over the last 2.6 Ma.

2.3 Restriction site‐associated DNA sequencing (RADseq)

We use RADseq (Baird et al., 2008) to generate a molecular data set for both population genomics (Davey & Blaxter, 2010) and phylogenomics (Eaton & Ree, 2013; Rubin, Ree, & Moreau, 2012). We extracted genomic DNA of each specimen using a nondestructive protocol (Tin, Economo, & Mikheyev, 2014) based on overnight soaking in a chaotropic buffer. RADseq libraries were prepared following Tin, Rheindt, Cros, and Mikheyev (2015) using a Biomek FXP Laboratory Automation Workstation (Beckman Coulter). The libraries were sequenced using an Illumina HiSeq 2500 at the Sequencing Center of the Okinawa Insititute of Science and Technology. DNA reads were demultiplexed, filtered by quality and trimmed to 41 bp using Trimmomatic (Bolger, Lohse, & Usadel, 2014).

2.4 Orthologue identification

For within‐species population genomic analyses we used the stacks version 1.47 pipeline to build alignments of orthologous loci (Baird et al., 2008; Catchen, Hohenlohe, Bassham, Amores, & Cresko, 2013). A de novo assembly was constructed using the denovo_map.pl perl wrapper. For our 41 bp reads we set the ustacks parameter for minimum stack size (coverage) to three reads (‐m) and a maximum mismatch between stacks within a locus (‐M) of two base pairs. The cstacks parameter for threshold mismatch allowed between catalogue loci (‐n) was set to one. All other parameters remained as defaults.

For phylogenetic reconstruction of all Fijian Pheidole species we identified orthologous loci using the ipyrad pipeline (Eaton, 2014). We used default settings apart from the maximum SNPs per locus parameter (parameter eight) which we set to eight SNPs per locus for within species analyses and to 20 SNPs per locus for genus wide alignments. For genus‐level phylogenetic reconstruction we selected loci with a minimum 50% coverage of taxa possessing a locus. For species‐level reconstruction we increased this threshold to 70%.

2.5 Phylogenomics

Phylogenetic analyses were performed: (a) on specimens representing one individual per island per species plus a number of regionally and globally distributed outgroup taxa to test the island biogeographic prediction that endemic species on pelagically isolated archipelagoes typically result from a single radiation; and (b) in separate analyses for each species featuring all specimens to evaluate potential for cryptic lineages within recognized species. Concatenated loci from ipyrad ortholog identification were analyzed using Bayesian inference (BI) in exabayes version 1.4.1 (Aberer, Kobert, & Stamatakis, 2014). We used the unpartitioned GTRGAMMA model following Abadi, Azouri, Pupko, and Mayrose (2019). Two independent runs of 10 million generations and four chains were run and with 25% of burnin. Convergence was assessed by monitoring standard deviation of split frequencies and employing the default 0.05 threshold (manual: https://cme.h-its.org/exelixis/web/software/exabayes/manual/manual.html; accessed June 2019). We used the post‐processing consense function to obtain a consensus tree and calculate posterior probability clade support. Maximum likelihood phylogenetic reconstruction was also performed in raxml version 8.2.4 (Stamatakis, 2014) using the GTRGAMMA evolutionary model with 100 bootstraps.

2.6 Testing taxon cycle dynamics

We conducted numerous population genomic analyses on individual Pheidole species. However, analyses were also examined as groupings of species categorized according to endemicity, morphological and ecological specialization, and phylogenetic relatedness. First, we considered groupings that Economo and Sarnat (2012) suggested were redolent of Wilson's taxon cycle “stages” (see Wilson, 1959, 1961). Nonendemic species were considered as potential “stage I” colonizer taxa, whilst the separate endemic subradiations were considered different stages further along the taxon cycle continuum: the more generalist “knowlesi” group treated as “stage II” endemics, whilst the morphologically and ecologically specialized “roosevelti” were treated as “stage III” endemics; P. onifera, as an endemic species from a lineage that has not undergone endemic radiation, was treated separately. Collectively, we refer to these groupings of species as a priori groupings.

2.7 Population structure

A number of population genomic analyses (PCA, AMOVA and ANCOVA) were performed to determine: (a) whether taxon cycle predictions that late colonization stage species should exhibit greater levels of population structure; and (b) PCA were used to evaluate potential for cryptic lineages within recognized species. For species level population genomic analyses we filtered stacks outputs to a minimum 70% coverage of taxa possessing a locus, and used stacks parameter settings to select one SNP per locus (to avoid pseudo‐replication), remove private alleles (i.e., loci featuring only a single copy of a SNP variant across all taxa in a locus) and remove all loci with heterozygosity >0.5 (removing loci not at Hardy–Weinburg equilibrium). Basic population genomic analyses were conducted using the following software and methods: PCA were conducted using the r‐package “pcaMethods” (Stacklies, Redestig, Scholz, Walther, & Selbig, 2007); the pairwise population of Weir and Cockerham (1984) FST was calculated using the vcftools version 0.1.12b program (Danecek et al., 2011); AMOVA was conducted in arlequin version 3.5 (Excoffier & Lischer, 2010) using standard default settings. Population structure analyses and assessment of most likely genetic deme number levels were conducted using a sparse non‐negative matrix factorization in sNMF (Frichot, Mathieu, Trouillon, Bouchard, & Francois, 2014). Structure plots were made using the distruct graphical display program (Rosenberg, 2004). Data were formatted from Stacks VCF files using either custom python scripts or with pgdspider (Lischer & Excoffier, 2012).

We used ANCOVA to explore population genomic and geographical relationships including the pairwise population of Weir and Cockerham (1984) FST versus historic sea level connectivity during the last 2.6 Ma. (see below). Historic sea level connectivity was investigated as it no doubt more accurately reflects the degree of physical population separation across time than simple geographic distance across an archipelago. These analyses were performed in r (R Core Team, 2017) using the minimum adequate model method of Crawley (2007). Individual species’ linear regression coefficients were also calculated in r.

To objectively evaluate whether species' population structures exhibit distinctive signatures according to their grouping categorisations we fitted linear models on FST versus population connectivity for 10,000,000 randomly selected models (i.e., random species groupings) evaluating for (a) different intercepts only, and (b), different intercepts and slopes. These analyses were conducted in python 3.6 using the statsmodel library (Seabold & Perktold, 2010) using the ordinary least squares (ols) and ANOVA (anova_lm) functions. Next, using custom python scripts, we calculated Akaike weights (Akaike, 1979) to evaluate the best fitting model from the 10,000,000 candidates. We then computed the confidence set of candidate models (analogous to a confidence interval for a mean estimate) by including models with Akaike weights that are within 10% of the highest (sensu Royall, 1997). Finally, we investigated which groupings of species were most commonly represented among the confidence set to assess if they differed from our a priori groupings.

2.8 Population expansion/contraction

Analyses of population expansion/contraction were performed to determine: (a) island biogeographic predictions that extinction process might be occurring on an archipelago with a reducing landmass; and (b) whether taxon cycle predictions that late colonization stage species should exhibit greater levels of demographic decline. In general, these analyses test for recent demographic signatures.

In order to quantify the demographic history of Fijian Pheidole, (i.e., test for population expansion/contraction), we used dadi (Diffusion Approximations for Demographic Inference) version 1.7.0 to analyze one dimensional site frequency spectra (1D‐SFS) (Gutenkunst, Hernandez, Williamson, Bustamante, 2009). Four following alternative 1D models were simulated and the best‐fit models were chosen by comparing maximum likelihoods: (a) Constant population size model; (b) Instantaneous size change; (c) Exponential size change, and (d) Instantaneous size decline followed by exponential growth. Parameters optimizations for each model were performed by using the optimization routine procedure in (Portik et al., 2017). For the best‐fit model, we estimated the standard errors (SE) for each parameter using Godambe Information Matrix on 200 bootstrap data sets that randomly sampled from the original frequency spectrum using the built‐in sampling method in dadi. We then calculated 95% confident intervals for each parameter estimate using the point estimate ± 1.96 SE.

To cross‐validate dadi results, we also used fastsimcoal2 (Excoffier, Dupanloup, Huerta‐Sanchez, Sousa, & Foll, 2013) to simulate a simple exponential population expansion/contraction model for each species. We conducted 1 million coalescent simulations of this population expansion/contraction model with the following options: −N 1,000,000 (number of coalescent simulations), −L 40 [number of expectation‐maximization (EM) cycles], −M 0.001 (minimum relative difference in parameter values for the stopping criterion) and −c 10 (threshold for observed SFS entry count, pooling all entries with <10 SNPs). We set the prior distribution for this model as such: current effective population size (NCUR) ~U (100, 100K), ancient effective population size (NANC) ~U (100, 100K), and the time (TG) it took to grow/contract from NANC to NCUR ~U (1 K, 1 million generation). For each species, we performed 100 independent fastsimcoal2 runs to determine the parameter estimates at maximum likelihood (Excoffier et al., 2013).

2.9 X‐Ray micro‐CT scanning

We used an existing data set of X‐ray micro‐CT scans from dry‐mounted specimens, which were performed for a taxonomic revision of the Pheidole knowlesi group (Fischer et al., 2016). Additional species were scanned with similar settings on the same ZEISS Xradia 510 Versa micro‐CT scanner and with zeiss Scout and Scan Control System software. Standard procedure for all scanned material in this study was using the 4× magnification objective, binning of two by two pixels, source filter “Air”, and field mode “normal”. The exposure times were set between 1.2 and 25 s, voltage between 30 and 60 kV, and power between 2 and 5 W. The different combinations of voltage, power and exposure time were chosen to yield intensity levels of around 15,000 across the whole specimen. Full 360 degree rotations of a dried specimen glued onto a paper triangle, which was clamped into the specimen holder, were done with 1,601 projections and the resulting scans typically have resolutions of 1,013 × 992 × 993 (H × W × D) pixels, with voxel sizes ranging between 1.86 and 5.74 μm.

3D reconstruction of the resulting scans was done with XMReconstructor and saved in DICOM file format (default settings; USHORT [16 bit] output data type) which were opened with amira software version 6 and 6.5, wherein we used the isosurface function to export surfaces in.ply format. These surfaces were then processed in meshlab (Cignoni et al., 2008) to clean the specimen from isolated vertices (Filters > Cleaning and Repairing > Remove isolated pieces [wrt diameter] [set max diameter: 1%]) and create a hollow surface model without the internal structure of the ant (1. Filters > Colour Creation and Processing > Ambient Occlusion Per Vertex; 2. Filters > Selection>Select Faces By Vertex Quality (min = 0, max = 0.001); 3. Remove Selected Faces). The hollow surface models were opened in checkpoint (Stratovan Corporation, 2018), where 3D landmarks were placed on head and mesosoma.

2.10 Geometric morphometrics

We used two approaches to quantify size and shape characteristics of Pheidole species: (a) 2D landmark measurements performed using standardized photos from many specimens collected from Fiji and across Pheidole's global distribution, and (b) 3D landmark measurements performed using micro‐CT scans of Fiji Pheidole species. In both cases, we placed landmarks on specimens using identifiable features that were likely to be homologous across the genus, and which covered structures of interest evenly (Friedman, Bennet, et al., 2019; Friedman, Remes, & Economo, 2019; Zelditch, Swiderski, & David Sheets, 2012). In summary, for specimen photographs we used TPSdig2 to place 11 landmarks on the head in “head” view and six landmarks on the mesosoma in “profile” view (Table S1; Rohlf, 2015); for each micro‐CT scan we used Stratovan Checkpoint to place 20 fixed landmarks and eight sliding semi‐landmarks on the head, and 27 landmarks on the mesosoma (Figure S1; Stratovan Corporation, 2018). We reflected and averaged landmarks on opposite sides of the head and mesosoma to force object symmetry. Standardized photographs of Fiji specimens are described in more detail in (Economo, Sarnat, et al., 2015), but in brief these included roughly 1,000 photos taken in the laboratory, as well as images from Antweb (antweb.org) and Wilson's monograph (Wilson, 2003).

Since the head can articulate relative to the mesosoma, it was necessary to analyze these traits separately throughout to avoid including variation due to each specimen's articulation among variation in shape. We performed a generalized Procrustes analysis to align specimens and remove variation due to rotation and size, as implemented in geomorph 3.0.7 (Adams, Collyer, & Kaliontzopoulou, 2018). We used the landmark‐based centroid size of the mesosoma as an estimate of overall body size (as in Economo, Klimov, et al., 2015). We then employed a principal components analysis to construct axes of shape variation among Pheidole specimens, and plotted species in this morphospace using the plotTangentSpace function in geomorph.

3 RESULTS

3.1 Species concepts

We evaluated species hypotheses based on morphological taxonomy using phylogenomic analyses. Existing hypotheses are largely supported with all described species and putative morphospecies monophyletic (Figure S2). However, intraspecific phylogenomic analyses (Figure S3) revealed apparent cryptic lineages within two of the accepted species. While some species showed geographically structured clades, and some had little geographic variation at all, these two species (Pheidole knowlesi and Pheidole vatu) had specimens from the same populations that sorted into multiple well‐supported, paraphyletic clades, implying that there are separate lineages. PCA confirmed the same patterns (Figure S4f,k). Post hoc morphological reanalysis did not reveal any obvious divergence between the forms associated with each genetic lineage. Resolution of the taxonomic implications of this finding and whether they should be correctly described as separate species is beyond the scope of this paper. For the purposes of this study we considered P. knowlesi and P. vatu to be three and two putative species, respectively.

3.2 Phylogenomic inference

Bayesian and maximum likelihood phylogenomic reconstruction of RADseq data indicates that sampled endemic Pheidole in Fiji (except for P. onifera) are derived from a single colonist that radiated into 16 species (Figure 2, Figure S5). Within this Fijian clade, the roosevelti and knowlesi groups are reciprocally monophyletic. The other five independent colonizations of Fiji constitute one endemic (P. onifera), one widespread species (P. umbonata) derived from the Australia‐New Guinea clade, and three widespread species (P. oceanica, P. fervens, P. megacephala) from the Africa‐Asia clade.

3.3 Ecomorphological evolution

Morphometric analyses indicate extreme morphological diversification among Fijian Pheidole, as this small radiation has filled up most of the global morphospace occupied by the genus (Figure 3). Among the endemic radiation, the roosevelti and knowlesi clades are disparate in body size, and in head and mesosoma shape. Moreover, there is an increase in relative major worker to minor worker size among roosevelti group species compared with knowlesi group (Figure 4).

3.4 RADseq population genomic analyses

Most endemic species show geographic population structure within Fiji based on SNP‐based PCA and evaluation of deme number (K) from sNMF analyses (Figures S6a–q). Within the Fijian clade, there is no evidence to suggest that roosevelti group species display more clearly demarcated geographically distinct populations compared to other species groupings (i.e., knowlesi group, nonendemics).

ANCOVA regression analyses between pairwise population FST (FST/1−FST) and historic sea level (a.s.l.) impedance (between populations) shows statistical differences for relationships between nonendemic species and both knowlesi and roosevelti groups (Table 2), while knowlesi and roosevelti relationships are statistically indistinguishable. Statistical differences are also evident for the nonendemic versus endemic groupings. Nonendemic species have the lowest pairwise FST scores, followed by knowlesi group and roosevelti group species respectively (Figure 5a).

3.5 Population demography

Population demography inferred with dadi found that recent arrivals have high levels of growth, while most endemic species are undergoing demographic decline (Figure 6). The t tests indicated nonsignificant differences between roosevelti and knowlesi group species (t = –1.82, df = 37.700, p = .077). However, nonendemic species display significantly higher rates of demographic expansion compared to roosevelti (t = –4.026, df = 27.256, p < .001) and knowlesi (t = –2.692, df = 32.181, p = .011) species. Two of the endemic knowlesi group species, P. ululevu and P. wilsoni, are notable for their signatures of high demographic growth, similar to those observed among nonendemic species. fastcoalsim2 analyses largely corroborate patterns from dadi, although signatures of population expansion in nonendemic species and P. ululevu and P. wilsoni are not as pronounced (Figure S7).

4 DISCUSSION

Islands present microcosm‐like natural systems that typically provide tractable settings for testing biogeographic theories. Their geographic isolation and historical geo‐environmental dynamism often allow us to understand the interplay between colonization and diversification in assembling communities (Whittaker et al., 2017). A major obstacle in testing island biogeographic theories has been access to appropriate data sets and analytical tools. However, recent advances in next‐generation sequencing technologies and computing capabilities have facilitated the revisiting of model island biogeographic systems, allowing a robust assessment of evolutionary signatures that are predicted to vary with species’ colonization histories. Here we use genomic and morphological data to re‐examine the well‐studied (Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Economo, Sarnat, et al., 2015; MacArthur & Wilson, 1967; Sarnat & Moreau, 2011; Wilson, 1959, 1961; Wilson & Taylor, 1967) Fijian Pheidole (Hymenoptera, Formicidae). We examine the relative roles of dispersal assembly versus in situ radiation, evidence for demographic decline and reduction in dispersal ability after colonization, and evidence for morphological diversification rather than trends toward an island syndrome. We also interpret our findings in the context of the taxon cycle hypothesis (Ricklefs & Bermingham, 2002; Wilson, 1961) which has been an important framework for thinking about biogeography in the region. Our new analyses further resolve whether deterministic eco‐evolutionary processes have played a significant role in shaping the extant Fijian Pheidole.

Our RADseq based phylogenomic analyses corroborates previous mitochondrial and nuclear gene phylogenies (Economo et al., 2019; Economo, Klimov, et al., 2015) showing that the knowlesi and roosevelti groups, and thus all but one endemic species, are derived from a single colonization of Fiji from Asia. Our results agree with Economo et al. (2019) that the knowlesi and roosevelti groups are reciprocally monophyletic, rather than the roosevelti group being nested within knowlesi (Sarnat & Moreau, 2011). Upholding the view that most endemic Fijian Pheidole originate from a single radiation is consistent with island biogeographic predictions for isolated islands (Whittaker et al., 2017). And particularly for taxa with limited dispersal capabilities, such as ants (relative to birds or some plant species), that would limit both the frequency of colonization events and levels of gene flow with source areas (Gillespie et al., 2012).

However, that sequential colonizations did not give rise to the subclades is not consistent with the taxon cycle. Although, that the endemic radiation contains generalized and specialized subclades derived from a single colonization is consistent with the taxon cycle in one sense: that evolution into interior habitats and diversification into different specialized ecological niches were filled through in situ radiation instead of direct colonization from source areas. However, this observation also concurs with general predictions about lineages diversifying into virgin territory and encountering vacant niche space (i.e., priority effects; De Meester et al., 2016). Phylogenomic inference also suggests that the widespread generalist species, P. ululevu, has colonized the Cook Islands, separated by over 2,000 km of ocean, and indicates that isolated islands do not always constitute evolutionary cul‐de‐sacs. This also upholds the taxon cycle prediction that endemic species may evolve or retain highly dispersive modes. Finally, the presence of the endemic monotypic lineage, P. onifera, remains enigmatic. Dating uncertainty and a lack of sampling means we are unsure whether this species colonized Fiji before or after the knowlesi‐roosevelti radiation (Economo et al., 2019), and explaining its implications therefore remains speculative. An accurate ordered assessment of endemic lineage colonization dates would be useful in order to better understand the impact of secondary colonization on the incumbent fauna during periods that preceded anthropogenic influence.

Morphometric analyses do not support the prediction that island species evolve island syndromes as the endemic radiation shows a high degree of morphological diversification, nearly filling, and not clustering within, the global morphospace of Pheidole (>1,100 species). However, these data on morphology of the worker caste do not rule out that other aspects of the phenotype trend toward an island syndrome. For example, ant colony social structure or nuptial flight dispersal capabilities may differ among island ants relative to continental species. However, this finding does support the niche‐shift component of the taxon cycle and corroborates previous qualitative assertions of remarkable morphological shifts on the archipelago (e.g., Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Fischer et al., 2016; Sarnat, 2008; Sarnat, Friedman, Fischer, Lecroq‐Bennet, & Economo, 2017; Sarnat & Moreau, 2011). Additionally, intense morphological evolution may have also resulted from abundant ecological opportunity, consistent with priority effect predictions for initial colonizers (De Meester et al., 2016).

Population genomic analyses are particularly promising for testing the taxon cycle which predicts that species should trend towards ecological specialization, population fragmentation, and demographic decline under pressure from subsequent colonists. Following speculation by Economo and Sarnat (2012) that the knowlesi‐roosevelti subradiations may reflect distinct taxon cycle phases, we would expect the most recently arrived Pheidole to have the highest population growth rates and the lowest geographic population structure, followed by more generalist (knowlesi group) and then most specialized endemics (roosevelti group). There was a strong difference in both population structure and demography between nonendemic and endemic species lending some support to the idea that the new colonizations are pushing the original radiation toward low abundance and extinction. This pattern includes both widespread species that are most probably native to Fiji (i.e., P. oceanica and P. umbonata) and those that are probably (P. fervens) and definitely (P. megacephala) brought by humans. Thus, human‐assisted transfer and habitat modification may be instigating a contemporary taxon cycle dynamic of secondary colonizations (Economo & Sarnat, 2012). However, the signatures of demographic contraction among endemics also corroborates the basic island biogeographic prediction that declining landmasses (here due to post‐Pleistocene sea level change) should feature an increasing role for extinction processes, which may also be working in tandem with confounding anthropogenic forces.

The situation among endemic species appears more complex. The majority are highly geographically structured and in demographic decline across their range (with some notable exceptions, discussed below). However, in accordance with the taxon cycle, the roosevelti group tend to be limited to fewer islands than the knowlesi group, but regressions with historical impedance, AMOVA and global FST did not show any systematic differences of the degree of population structure within their distributions. There is also a modest (nonsignificant) difference in population change between the endemic groupings, with roosevelti showing more signs of contraction (primarily skewed by elevated values among the knowlesi group species, P. ululevu and P. wilsoni). Thus, this only partially supports the interpretation of Economo and Sarnat (2012) that the more ecologically specialized roosevelti group represented lineages that had shifted “further along” towards a later phase than the knowlesi group.

While there was limited systematic variation in population structure and demography among endemics at clade level, there is substantial variation at species level within groups. Notably, two of the widespread knowlesi group species, P. ululevu and P. wilsoni, show signs of demographic expansion, are widespread among the Fijian islands, and show little population structure according to AMOVA and global FST. Additionally, we have shown that P. ululevu has apparently colonized the Cook Islands. As we only observe this expansive syndrome among the nonspecialized ecotypes it implies that later stage conditions may represent an evolutionary dead‐end as is often proposed for other avenues towards ecological specialization (e.g., Vamosi, Armbruster, & Renner, 2014). Although it is difficult to definitively rule out human‐mediated dispersal, P. ululevu occurs primarily in more pristine habitats in both Fiji and the Cook Islands, and is not known to be associated with, or transferred by, humans. Given that these two species are nonbasal within the knowlesi clade, these findings are consistent with the idea that they have re‐entered a markedly “expansive phase” of the taxon cycle with signatures showing similarities with the nonendemic Pheidole. If this interpretation is correct, it seems the first evidence supported by molecular data that a species has reached the Polynesian islands east of Samoa through natural dispersal, arguing against the conjecture of Wilson and Taylor (1967) that eastern Polynesia was never colonized by ants before humans. Conversely, Pheidole kava and P. knowlesi sp.C show signs of extreme population structure. Together, these four species suggest that while there may be some general trends after colonization, individual species can move on idiosyncratic trajectories. This is further emphasized population structure analysis that places species from different a priori groupings into the same AIC clusters.

In conclusion, we sought to understand whether island lineages follow a predictable evolutionary progression after colonization, as captured by ideas surrounding island biogeography and the taxon cycle hypothesis. We confirmed the expectation that in situ evolution from a single colonization was the predominant mechanism building endemic diversity on the island, although other colonizations have deposited single species (including one endemic) in the community. This in situ evolution included intense morphological diversification, with Fijian species filling much of the global morphospace, with no evidence of any trends toward particular morphological island syndromes. Repeated differences in population genomic signatures between more expansive (nonendemic) generalists to more declining locally adapted endemics (but not generalist knowlesi vs. specialist roosevelti) offer support for the idea that endemics lose dispersal ability and decline demographically. The fact that exotic species are among the most expanding taxa raises the question of their long‐term influence on the island, and whether the worldwide transport of ants has initiated new global taxon‐cycle (Economo & Sarnat, 2012; Ricklefs, 2005). However, despite signatures of in situ morphological niche shifts, population genomic findings reject a strict model of an irreversible trend ecological specialization and population fragmentation. Rather, we show that population structures appear idiosyncratic, potentially resulting from unidentified historical events or evolutionary changes that may be difficult to identify, and several species nested within the endemic radiations seem to be entering new phases of range expansion. The latter was envisioned by Wilson (1961) to occasionally occur in his original formulation of the taxon cycle, with a new expanding species initiating a new cycle.

Much work remains to fully understand the assembly of remote island faunas and test island biogeography for ants and other organisms. Given the level of anthropogenic impact of most island systems, an ongoing challenge will be to untangle the confounding influence of eco‐evolutionary (i.e., natural) and anthropogenic forces shaping the patterns we observe today. Further use of new genomic and phenomic tools, combined with increased field study of species’ ecologies and new quantitative methods to synthesize this information will no doubt help further unravel the processes of community assembly and dynamics.

    Abstract

    Several factors may restrict the acquisition of food to below the levels predicted by the optimization theory. However, how the design of structures that animals build for foraging restricts the entry of food is less known. Using scaling relationships, we determined whether the design of the entrances of leaf‐cutting ant nests restricts resource input into the colony. We measured nests and foraging parameters in 25 nests of Atta cephalotes in a tropical rain forest. Ant flux was reduced to up to 60% at nest entrances. The width of all entrances per nest increased at similar rates as nest size, but the width of nest entrances increased with the width of its associated trail at rates below those expected by isometry. The fact that entrance widths grow slower than trail widths suggests that the enlargement of entrance holes does not reach the dimensions needed to avoid delays when foraging rates are high and loads are big. The enlargement of nest entrances appears to be restricted by the digging effort required to enlarge nest tunnels and by increments in the risk of inundation, predator/parasitoid attacks and microclimate imbalances inside the nest. The design of the extended phenotypes can also restrict the ingress of food into the organisms, offering additional evidence to better understand eventual controversies between empirical data and the foraging theory.

    1 INTRODUCTION

    Almost all vital functions of individuals, as well as demographic parameters, depend on the amount and quality of ingested food. The need for maximizing the ingress of food per unit of time is therefore a fundamental principle of optimal foraging theory (Pyke et al. 1977). Nevertheless, several abiotic and biotic factors may restrict foraging, maintaining the ingress of food below that predicted by the theory (Olsson et al. 2008). Most studies have demonstrated how predation risk, competition, social interactions and climatic variables can restrict the rate of food delivery (Alma et al. 2017, Roces 1994, Rozen‐Rechels et al. 2015). However, studies regarding how the design of the structures that animals build for foraging restrict the ingress of food are less common. This information may help to better understand some dissimilarities between empirical data and theoretical predictions related to optimal forage input.

    Natural selection operates on the builders through the success of the structures that they build. However, the structures that animals build to capture, access and/or facilitate the ingress of food (commonly known as extended phenotypes, Turner 2009) are often restricted by ecological trade‐offs (Nakata 2012). For example, some spiders use orb webs to intercept and then retain insects long enough to be attacked. Nevertheless, it is difficult to improve both functions simultaneously. Increasing mesh width maximizes web interception, while reducing prey retention because less silk adheres to insects (Blackledge & Eliason 2008). Some neuropteran larvae are sit‐and‐wait predators that dig conical pit traps in loose soil to capture walking insects, mainly ants. A successful capture depends on both efficiency in trapping prey and on minimizing the probability that the prey escapes. However, the probability of prey encounter and retention cannot be maximized at the same time. The larvae cannot enlarge the trap diameter to increase prey encounter without reducing trap depth or slope, which enhances prey escape (Lomáscolo and Farji‐Brener 2001). In sum, ecological restrictions may be reflected in the design of the structures that animals build for foraging, which affect the final amount of ingested food.

    Leaf‐cutting ants are a good animal group to study the adaptive significance of animal constructions because they build long‐lasting, conspicuous structures. These ants cut huge amounts of plant biomass from the surrounding vegetation and carry it to the nest, where the plant fragments are used as a substrate for a symbiotic fungus that they cultivate inside underground chambers (Hölldobler & Wilson 2010). The access to resources and the ingress of plant tissue into the nest depend on an extensive system of cleared foraging trails that facilitate leaf transport (Farji‐Brener et al. 2010, Kost et al. 2005). Accordingly, foraging trails are considered extended phenotypes of leaf‐cutting ant colonies and analogous to the transport and supply networks of cities (Burd 2006, Isalgue et al. 2007, Samaniego & Moses 2008). Recent studies have demonstrated that the design of these trail systems is restricted by ecological trade‐offs. For example, the scaling relationship between the rate of resource acquisition and the size of the trail system suggests that trail length may allow access to distant resources, but also imposes a severe penalty on the foraging rate (Bruce & Burd 2012). The building of trail branches generates a conflict between shortening travel distances and reducing trail maintenance work (Farji‐Brener et al. 2015). However, there is little information on whether nest entrances are also subjected to ecological trade‐offs that may restrict the input of food into the colony.

    Entrances of ant nests are the final frontier for resource ingress from the environment to inside the colony. As in cities, two characteristics of entrances may restrict the ingress of food inside the nest: their size and quantity. First, if ants behave optimally, they should adjust the width of their trails proportionally to the peak traffic volume (Burd et al. 2002, Fourcassié et al. 2010). To avoid bottlenecks at the nest ingress, foraging entrances should be of similar size to trail width. Second, leaf‐cutting ant colonies can grow from thousands to millions of workers in a few years, increasing their needs of food ingress (Hölldobler & Wilson 2010). Therefore, either the number of foraging entries or entry dimensions should increase with the size of the colony in such a way that allows for the ingress of an adequate amount of resources that accompanies such growth. However, an increased number or over‐enlarged nest entrances could allow the ingress of rain (Kleineidam & Roces 2000, Sendoya et al. 2014), parasites (Bragança et al. 1998, Elizalde et al. 2012, Feener & Brown 1993), and army ants (Powell & Clark 2004). In sum, several ecological factors may restrict the number and size of nest entrances despite their importance as an ingress portal of resources into the colony. Here, we ask whether nest entrances impose constraints on resource flow in colonies of the leaf‐cutting ant Atta cephalotes. If this were true, we would expect that (a) loaded ants be delayed entering the nest; (b) nest entrances be narrower than the incoming trails, and (c) increments in the dimensions of nest entrances be lower than those expected by isometry regarding increments in nest size or trail width.

    2 METHODS

    2.1 Study area and leaf‐cutting ant species

    We conducted this study at La Selva Biological Station of the Organization for Tropical Studies (10°26′N, 83°59′W) in the Atlantic lowlands of Costa Rica, between January and February (dry season). The area is a tropical lowland wet forest that receives a mean annual rainfall of 4,000 mm (see McDade 1994, for a full description). We sampled a total of 25 nests of A. cephalotes located in primary and secondary forests. This species is the most common leaf‐cutting ant in the studied area and exhibits large and well‐established colonies and long‐lasting trails that end in holes in the soil that are the entrances to ingress the carried loads inside the nest (Farji‐Brener 2001, Silva et al. 2013). The foraging nest holes of this species are stable in size regarding climatic conditions and season of the year (AGFB, personal observation).

    2.2 Methodology

    We estimated the foraging rate (i.e., ingoing loaded ants/time), trail width, and number of foraging nest entrances, as well as nest entrance width, height, and area in each nest. Foraging rates were estimated at trails and at nest entrances simultaneously, as the number of all ingoing loaded ants passing through a fixed point of a trail section at a 5 cm distance from the entrance hole and at the ingress of the nest entrance during 2 min. Samples were performed in one randomly selected trail per nest in all nests (n = 25), at different moments of the day during the study period (one time per nest). Despite the fact that nests were randomly selected, sampling included a wide range of entrance width with regard to the trail, which allowed for a better study of the effect of different entrance widths on ant worker flow. Moreover, in order to understand the constraint imposed by smaller entrances in comparison with foraging trails, we included peak activity times as well as low activity times. We used the nest area as a proxy of colony size. Ant nest size is conventionally estimated as the ground surface area that includes all soil excavations carried out by the colony and the bare area of understory vegetation above nests, which typically fits an ellipsoidal form (Meyer et al. 2011, Wirth et al. 2003). Therefore, we calculated the area of each nest as π * r1* r2, being r1 and r2 the semi‐major and semi‐minor axis of the nest ground surface, as previously defined. We counted the number of foraging entrances per nest and measured each one's width and height with a caliper. Foraging nest entrances were easily identified because they were always associated with foraging trails. Other nest holes, such as ventilation turrets and holes for waste and soil removal, were not considered. Foraging entrance holes were ellipsoidal, and their area was estimated with the same formula used to estimate nest area. Since ants never walked into the nest through the roof of the ingress tunnel (see results), we later employed the width of the entrances as a proxy of the gateway size of ingress into the nest. We also measured the width of the associated trunk trail at a 5 cm distance from the nest entrance. Since the presence of foraging ants is not required in order to relate trail width with its respective entrance width, for this measurement, we included all of the trails of each nest (a total of 92). In sum, for each nest, we obtained (a) its area, (b) the number of foraging entrance holes, (c) the mean width and height of the entrance holes, (d) the total area of entrance holes (i.e., the sum of the areas of all the entrance holes per nest), (e) the total width of entrance holes (i.e., the sum of the width of all the entrance holes per nest), and (f) the width of the trunk trails near nest entrances.

    2.3 Statistical analyses

    Paired t tests were used to compare the foraging rates between the trail near the nest entrance and the nest entrance and to compare the width of nest entrances and the width of the associated foraging trails. A linear regression was used to analyze whether ant flow affected the ants speed when they entered through the nest entrance. Ant delay was estimated as ([# ingoing ants at 5 cm from the nest entrance ‐ # ingoing ants at nest entrance]/# ingoing ants at 5 cm from the nest entrance). The relationship between the number of nest entrances and nest area was tested with a Pearson correlation. Scaling relationships may reflect how biological form is shaped by ecological trade‐offs (Waters et al. 2010, West et al. 1997). If ant nest entrances represent restrictions to food ingress, we predict that the total area of nest entrances will increase at rates lower than the size of the nest (i.e., lower than colony growth) and/or the width of the trails (i.e., lower than foraging flux). To test whether the ingress zone into the nest increased proportionally to the increment in nest size, we determined scaling relationships in the usual way from a linear regression analysis of log‐transformed variables. We used ordinary least squares (OLS) instead of reduced major axis (RMA) regression because OLS regression is better suited to test for allometric relationships (Kilmer & Rodriguez 2017). We calculated the bivariate scaling relationships (log‐log) between (a) nest area (m2) with the total width of nest entrances (cm) (n = 25 nests) and (b) trail width (cm) with its associated nest entrance width (cm) (n = 92 trails and their nest entrances). Slope coefficients and their 95% CIs were determined and compared with the predicted values under the assumption of isometric scaling, that is, that the size of parts change at the same rate (ß = 0.50 for linear vs. area measures and ß = 1 for linear vs. linear measures, respectively).

    3 RESULTS

    3.1 Foraging rates

    Foraging rates ranged from 3 to 108 ingoing loaded ants/min, but were reduced at nest entrances. While ant flux (ingoing loaded ants/min) was 31 ± 6 near the entrances, ant flux at the nest entrances was 21 ± 3 (mean ± SE, paired t test: 2.6, p = 0.01). This delay increased as ant flux increased (F1, 23 = 28.7, R2 = 0.55, ß = 0.75, p < 0.001). With a flux of 20 ingoing ants, the delay was around 20%, but reached up to 60% when the ingoing flux consisted of 65 ants (Figure 1). This slowdown in the rate of ingoing ants was due to traffic jams at nest entrances and the physical characteristics of entrance holes that hindered the ingress of large loads (Supporting Information Figure S1a–c). Loaded ants never entered the nest by walking on the roof of the ingress tunnel.

    3.2 Characteristics of nest entrances and their associated trails

    Entrances were mainly oblong and, in most cases, were ellipsoid‐shaped. Horizontal entrance axis ranged from 5 to 25 cm with a mean (±SE) of 6 ± 0.4 cm, and vertical axis ranged from 1 to 14 cm with a mean (±SE) of 4 ± 0.3. Entrance area ranged from 2 to 275 cm2, with a mean (±SE) of 23 ± 4 cm2. Trail and nest entrance widths ranged from 1.5 to 16 cm. However, trails often ended in nest entrances that were narrower than the trail. While the mean width for trunk trails near entrances was 7.04 ± 0.26 cm, the mean width of nest entrances was 6.1 ± 0.4 (±SE, paired t test = 1.98, p = 0.05). Nest entrances were narrower than their respective trails in 65% of cases (n = 60), wider than their respective trail in 23% of cases (n = 21), and both were similar in width in 12% of cases (n = 11, Figure 2).

    3.3 Nest entrances and scale relationship with nest size and trail width

    Nest size ranged from 7.5 to 155 m2 (70 ± 8 m2, mean ± SE, n = 25 nests). The number of nest entrances per nest ranged from 1 to 8 (4.3 ± 0.4) and was positively correlated with nest area (r = 0.72, p < 0.05). Nest area was not correlated with the mean width or with the mean height of nest entrances (all p > 0.66, Supporting Information Figure S2). The total width of nest entrances (i.e., the sum of widths of all entrances per nest) increased with nest size as expected by isometry, since the confidence interval includes the isometric scaling exponent of 0.5 (ß = 0.62, [0.4–0.9], 95% confidence limits, F1, 23 = 19.4, R2 = 0.46, p < 0.001, Figure 3). Finally, the width of nest entrance increased as its associated trail width increased, but at rates below those expected by isometry (i.e., with a slope <1; ß = 0.32, [0.13–0.52], 95% confidence limits, F1, 91 = 10.7, R2 = 0.32, p < 0.001, Figure 4).

    4 DISCUSSION

    Transportation paths play a key role in determining productivity and growth of the societies they serve. However, transportation paths should end in appropriate entrances in order to ensure a smooth entry of the products being transported. In leaf‐cutting ants, food is transported through a complex trail system that ends in nest entrance holes, which can be considered the final frontier to the ingress of resources from the environment to inside the colony. Previous research has demonstrated that the input of food into the colony may be restricted by the design of the trail system (Bruce & Burd 2012, Farji‐Brener et al. 2011, 2015). Our results suggest that the entrance design of leaf‐cutting ant nests can also restrict the rate of food input.

    Foraging flow at nest entrances was lower than that found on the trail a few cm away. Loaded ants showed a 30% delay at nest entrances, reaching up to a 60% delay at higher traffic moments. This slow ingress of food into the nest was often due to two main reasons: Entrance holes were narrower than their associated trail, generating traffic jams, especially when ant flux was high, and the entrance design sometimes hindered the ingress of large loads (Appendix S1a–c). Although not measured in this work, delays at nest entrances appear to be the rule rather than the exception. First, nest entrances were narrower than their associated trails in the majority of the sampling cases (Figure 2). Second, colonies reach high rates of foraging very quickly, and this is maintained during most of the foraging period (Lewis et al. 1974). Therefore, high ant fluxes entering the nest through proportionally narrow portals are very frequent events.

    Despite the apparent need for wider entrance holes, both the mean width and the mean area of entrance holes did not increase with nest size. However, the total width of nest entrances per nest, a good proxy for the food ingress capacity into a colony, increases at the same rate as nest size but at rates slower than trail width (Figures 3 and 4). The fact that entrance width grows slower than trail width suggests that the enlargement of entrance holes as the colony grows does not reach the dimensions needed in order to avoid delays, when foraging rates are high and loads are big.

    The width of the entrances grew at rates lower than their associated trails, generating traffic jams at nest entrances. Despite the fact that crowding at the nest entrance may play a role in regulating the number of ants that go out to forage (Gordon 2002), bottlenecks at entrances suggest that other ecological forces could restrict the enlargement of entrances during nest growth. The inundation risk, predator and parasitoid attacks, the need to maintain an adequate microclimate inside the nest, and digging restrictions may limit the design of nest entrances. First, oversize nest entrances could allow the ingress of rain, which negatively affects colony survival (Kleineidam & Roces 2000). Supporting this idea, Atta colonies in tropical rain forests often accumulate at higher sites with lower probabilities of flooding (Sendoya et al. 2014). Second, predators such as army ants employ the entrance holes to enter the nest and bring out the larvae and pupae from the colony (Powell & Clark 2004, Swartz 1998). In fact, to avoid the entry of army ants into the nest, Atta workers use leaf fragments to plug entrance holes from within by pulling the leaf fragments over the entrances or from outside as a barricade, holding the leaves vertically, forming a ring‐shaped barrier wall of leaves around the nest entrances (Swartz 1998). Third, nest entrances are the attack point of many phorid parasitoids, which use a piercing ovipositor to insert an egg inside loaded ants when they are passing through the entrance holes (Elizalde et al. 2012, Feener & Brown 1993). Fourth, large nest entrances may unbalance the abiotic factors that are crucial for the proper growth of the fungus and the brood, such as temperature, relative humidity and carbon dioxide levels inside the nest (Bollazzi & Roces 2002, 2007, Powell & Stradling 1986). Finally, the enlargement of entrance holes may be restricted by the size of underground tunnels and internal nest structures. Wider entrance holes are useless in avoiding traffic jams if the associated tunnels are narrow, but building wider underground tunnels implies energy‐demanding activities such as digging and transporting soil particles outside the nest (Camargo et al. 2011, Pielström & Roces 2013). Internal architectural components such as narrow tunnels and chambers and structures related to internal flux organization and fungus leaf processing may also restrict high ant flow inside the nest (Burd & Howard 2005,. Burd et al. 2010), rendering wider nest entrances ineffective. If the entrances were wider and incoming traffic was not delayed, the lag would occur inside the nest as traffic encounters the tunnels and chambers, thus generating an excavation cost to accommodate it. By having narrow entrances, the colony acquires a “waiting room” just outside the nest without any excavation costs. However, waiting outside the nest may imply other costs such as increased predator and parasitoid attacks, and the loss of forage because of rain (Farji‐Brener et al. 2018). In sum, larger nest entrances could facilitate the ingress of food into the colony, but this enlargement could increase the risk of inundation, predator and parasitoid attacks, microclimate changes inside the chambers, and also increments in digging effort and delays inside the nest.

    This study has limitations, but it also has strengths. First, colony growth was estimated through the above ground nest size. Estimating the real colony size in leaf‐cutting ants is a titanic and destructive task and is prohibited in protected areas like the study site. However, the above‐ground nest area is considered a reliable estimator of colony size for ants in general (Gordon 1995, Rasse & Deneubourg 2004), as well as for leaf‐cutting ants in particular (Farji‐Brener 2001, Fowler et al. 1986). Hence, we believe that in this case, above‐ground nest size is a reliable estimate of colony size. Second, since each colony was only measured once during the period of activity, our data only reflect a snapshot of the foraging delays described. Such delays might not be significant when considering the whole period of activity. However, between 60% and 80% of foraging activity is performed at high ant flow (Herz et al. 2007, Lewis et al. 1974). Moreover, colonies reach their peak of foraging activity very quickly, increasing the foraging effort from 50 to 300 ants per min in an hour, a rate that is maintained during most of the foraging period (Herz et al. 2007, Lewis et al. 1974). Hence, the delays reported in this work are probably the norm rather than a rare event and consequently difficult to compensate by way of the whole period of activity. Finally, an important percentage of increase in nest entrance is not explained by trail width (Figure 4), suggesting that other ecological forces besides foraging are modeling entrance size. We note that the nest entrances wider than expected by isometry were mostly built in association with exposed roots (Supporting Information Figure S1b). Other works demonstrated that the inclusion of exposed roots as part of the trail system in leaf‐cutting ants affected the foraging network and food discovery rate (Farji‐Brener et al. 2007, Freeman & Chaves‐Campos 2016). Our results also suggest that the inclusion of exposed roots influences the design of other nest features, such as entrances. Despite these potential limitations, the patterns we found (including that the width of nest entrance increases as its associated trail width increases, but at rates below those expected by isometry) come from a large sampling effort, which covers enough variation in nest size, trails widths, and number of nest entrances in order to detect reliable scaled relationships. Finally, with the use of relatively easy measurements, we were able to detect temporal patterns which are difficult to perceive in short time periods, such as changes in nest entrances and trail design as colonies grow.

    Empirical data about food size carried by central‐place foragers do not often fit with the optimum predicted by classical foraging theory. Several abiotic and biotic factors, such as temperature, wind, predation risk, competition, and the need to increase the rate of information transfer and reduce handling time, have been proposed to explain why the ingress of food per unit of time cannot always be maximized (Alma et al. 2017, Bollazzi & Roces 2011, Burd & Howard 2005, Olsson et al. 2008). This work, together with recent studies (Bruce & Burd 2012, Burd & Howard 2005, Farji‐Brener et al. 2015), suggests that the physical design of the extended phenotypes can also restrict the ingress of food into the organisms. Thus, the study of the physical structures that animal build for foraging, which are often easier to examine and measure than the animal's behavior itself, may help to better understand why the incorporation of food sometimes does not adjust to the predictions of the optimal foraging theory.

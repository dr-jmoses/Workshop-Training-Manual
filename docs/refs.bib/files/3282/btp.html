Abstract

enThis link goes to a English sectionfrThis link goes to a French section

In tropical rain forests, the ant community can be divided into ground and arboreal faunas. Here, we report a thorough sampling of the arboreal ant fauna of La Selva Biological Station, a Neotropical rain forest site. Forty‐five canopy fogging samples were centered around large trees. Individual samples harbored an average of 35 ant species, with up to 55 species in a single sample. The fogging samples yielded 163 observed species total, out of a statistically estimated 199 species. We found no relationship between within‐sample ant richness and focal tree species, nor were the ant faunas of nearby trees more similar to each other than the faunas of widely spaced trees. Species density was high, and beta diversity was low: A single column of vegetation typically harbors at least a fifth of the entire arboreal ant fauna. Considering the entire fauna, based on 23,326 species occurrence records using a wide variety of collecting methods, 182 of 539 observed species (196 of 605, estimated statistically) were entirely arboreal. The arboreal ant fauna is thus about a third of the total La Selva ant fauna, a robust result because inventory completeness was similar for ground and arboreal ants. The taxonomic history of discovery of the species that make up the La Selva fauna reveals no disproportionately large pool of undiscovered ant species in the canopy. The "last biotic frontier" for tropical ants has been the rotten wood, leaf litter, and soil of the forest floor.

Aquí presentamos un muestreo exhaustivo de la fauna de hormigas arbóreas de la Estación Biológica La Selva, un sitio de selva tropical neotropical. Cuarenta y cinco muestras de nebulización del dosel se centraron alrededor de grandes árboles. Las muestras de nebulización arrojaron 163 especies observadas en total, de un estimado estadístico de 199 especies. No encontramos ninguna relación entre la riqueza de hormigas dentro de la muestra y las especies de árboles focales, ni las faunas de hormigas de árboles cercanos eran más similares entre sí que las faunas de árboles ampliamente espaciados. La densidad de especies era alta y la diversidad beta era baja: una columna de vegetación típicamente alberga al menos una quinta parte de toda la fauna de hormigas arbóreas. Considerando toda la fauna, con base en 23,326 registros de ocurrencia de especies usando una amplia variedad de métodos de recolección, 182 de 539 especies observadas (196 de 605, estimadas estadísticamente) eran completamente arbóreas. La fauna de hormigas arbóreas es, por lo tanto, aproximadamente un tercio de la fauna total de hormigas La Selva, un resultado robusto porque la integridad del inventario fue similar para las hormigas terrestres y arbóreas. La historia taxonómica del descubrimiento de las especies que componen la fauna de La Selva no revela un grupo desproporcionadamente grande de especies de hormigas no descubiertas en el dosel. La "última frontera biótica" para las hormigas tropicales ha sido madera podrida, hojarasca y suelo.

1 INTRODUCTION

Tropical rain forests are renowned for their complex structure and high diversity. Ants are a dominant biotic element in rain forests (Davidson, Cook, Snelling, & Chua, 2003; Fittkau & Klinge, 1973; Floren, Biun, & Linsenmair, 2002; Stork, 1988), and a growing body of research documents the abundance, diversity, behavior, and habitat specificity of rain forest ants. Ant faunas are vertically stratified with respect to their nesting and foraging preferences, and the ground and canopy communities overlap very little (Longino & Colwell, 1997; Ryder Wilkie, Mertl, & Traniello, 2010; Yanoviak & Kaspari, 2000). One set is entirely ground‐based, nesting in the soil, leaf litter, or rotten wood of the shaded forest floor and foraging there or within a few meters of the ground. Another set is arboreal, nesting and foraging mostly or entirely in the vegetation. Only a few species routinely occur in both habitats.

Arboreality is a derived trait in ants that has evolved repeatedly in many ant lineages (Dejean, Corbara, Orivel, & Leponce, 2007; Nelson, Ree, & Moreau, 2018). A few of the arboreal lineages have radiated spectacularly, forming the bulk of the abundance and diversity of arboreal ants. The development of arboreal ant communities is primarily a tropical phenomenon; the abundance of arboreal ants is orders of magnitude greater in the tropics than in the temperate zone (Floren, Wetzel, & Staab, 2014). Tropical biologists have focused their research on assessing (a) the total richness of arboreal ants (LePonce et al., 2019; Majer, 1990; Wilson, 1987; Yanoviak, Fisher, & Alonso, 2007); (b) how arboreal ant richness compares to richness of ground ants (Floren et al., 2014; Ryder Wilkie et al., 2010; Yanoviak & Kaspari, 2000); (c) whether arboreal ants form "mosaics" of dominant and subdominant species (Dejean et al., 2016, 2019, 2007, 2018, 2015; Floren & Linsenmair, 2000; Jackson, 1984; LePonce et al., 2019; Leston, 1978; Majer & Camer‐Pesci, 1991; Room, 1971); and (d) whether ant communities are affected by habitat, tree species, tree size, or connectivity via lianas (Adams, Schnitzer, & Yanoviak, 2017; Floren & Linsenmair, 1997, 2005; Klimes, Fibich, Idigel, & Rimandai, 2015; Klimes et al., 2012; Osorio‐Pérez, Barberena‐Arias, & Aide, 2007; Philip, Fayle, & Yusah, 2018; Ribas, Schoereder, Pic, & Soares, 2003; Schonberg, Longino, Nadkarni, Yanoviak, & Gering, 2004; Schulz & Wagner, 2002).

La Selva Biological Station in Costa Rica, one of the best‐studied tropical rain forests, has been particularly well sampled for ants (Longino & Colwell, 1997; Longino, Colwell, & Coddington, 2002). Ant sampling at La Selva was carried out over a period of 13 years, with a combination of canopy fogging, Malaise traps, light traps, Berlese samples, Winkler samples of sifted litter, baiting, and hand‐collecting. Some of the data have been previously published in the context of inventory efficiency (Longino & Colwell, 1997) and richness estimation of the entire ant fauna (Longino et al., 2002). The previous publications used only a portion of the fogging data. Here, we present an analysis of the entire fogging dataset, along with an assessment of the arboreal fauna based on all collecting methods. We use these results to estimate the total richness of arboreal ants and to compare the richness of arboreal ants and ground ants. We test for an effect of focal tree species on sample richness and species composition, and we test for an effect of spatial proximity on species composition. The taxonomy of the arboreal ant species is carefully considered, and species are linked to specimens with unique specimen identifiers in museum collections, insuring that the study can serve as a reference benchmark for future work.

2 METHODS

2.1 Study site and fogging methods

The study was carried out at La Selva Biological Station, Heredia Province, Costa Rica (McDade, Bawa, Hespenheide, & Hartshorn, 1993), from 1993 to 2000. Mean annual rainfall is approximately 4 m. The habitat is a mosaic of mature lowland rain forest, second growth forest of various ages, and abandoned pastures. The study area comprised approximately 1,500 ha, ranging from 50 m to 150 m in elevation.

Canopy fogging was carried out using the general procedures discussed by Erwin (1983), Adis, Lubin, and Montgomery (1984), and Stork (1988). A total of 45 fogging samples were acquired (Figure 1, Table 1). For 11 samples, the focal tree was Pentaclethra macroloba (Willd.) O. Ktze. (Fabaceae), the most common tree species at La Selva. For 12 samples, the focal tree was Virola koschnyi Warb. (Myristicaceae), a species of intermediate abundance. The remaining 22 samples targeted focal trees from a wide range of species and plant families. A stratified sampling design was used for the first 18 fogging samples, with trees fogged in six sets of three closely spaced individuals (Figure 1). In each group of three samples, one tree was Pentaclethra macroloba, one was Virola koschnyi, and one was a third species, different in each triplet.

We chose focal trees that had large crowns (extending beyond the funnels below), little overlap with adjacent crowns, and good access for climbing. On the day prior to fogging a tree, the tree was rigged with mountain‐climbing ropes and a pulley system, so that the following day an operator could climb to the lower branches of the crown and the fogging machine could be hoisted on pulleys. Ropes were strung from trunk to trunk between the focal tree and neighboring trees to form an irregular network, 2–3 m above ground level. Forty 1‐m2 funnels were suspended from these ropes, distributed as evenly as possible in the area beneath the crown of the focal tree, and within 10 m of the focal tree trunk (Figure 2). The funnels were constructed of ripstop nylon mounted on a circular metal hoop, with a threaded ring at the bottom for the attachment of a plastic sample‐collection bottle. Palm leaves and other understory vegetation immediately above each funnel were clipped or bent back out of the way, but otherwise the understory vegetation was left intact.

Before dawn on the day of fogging, the funnels were suspended and the bottles filled with 70% ethanol. An operator climbed to the first branches at the base of the crown, 15 m to 20 m above ground level, and commenced fogging at about 0600 hrs. We used a Golden Eagle DynaFogger®, on setting 6, to fog 3.8 L of Pyrethrins 123® insecticide (Summit Chemical Co.). This is a 3% solution of a natural pyrethrin insecticide with synergists, in a petroleum distillate carrier. The operator gradually fogged in a 360˚circle, attempting to cover the crown evenly. Following fogging, a two‐hour drop time was allowed, after which the sides of the funnels were washed down with ethanol into the collecting bottles, to capture any insects adhering to the funnels, and the bottles were capped and collected.

2.2 Sample processing

Different sample processing methods were used during successive phases of a larger arthropod inventory project (Project ALAS, the Arthropods of La Selva, Longino & Colwell, 1997), of which the fogging samples were a part. Three methods were used (Table 1). Method 1: For 18 samples, project staff exhaustively selected examples of each putative morphospecies from each funnel for dry‐mounting; Longino identified the dry‐mounted specimens, resulting in a species list for each processed funnel; a variable number of funnels per sample were processed, typically 10 of the 40 funnels; when fewer than 40 funnels were processed, total sample richness was estimated (see below). Method 2: For 18 samples, specimens were pooled among all 40 funnels; project staff selected putative morphospecies from the pooled sample and placed them in a single vial; Longino identified the selected specimens in ethanol, mounting problematic specimens, resulting in a species list for each sample. Method 3: For 9 samples, project staff separated all ants from other arthropod specimens in the catch from each funnel and placed them in a separate vial for each funnel; Longino identified the ants in each vial, resulting in a species list for each funnel.

For each species encountered in the study, at least one specimen was mounted, labeled, tagged with a unique specimen code, and databased. All occurrence records for the fogging study, and permanent, unique specimen codes for mounted specimens, are openly available in Dryad Digital Repository at https://doi.org/10.5061/dryad.ncjsxksr0. Data and current identifications for the voucher specimens are deposited in the curated online repository AntWeb (www.antweb.org). Future name and classification changes can thus be tracked with the specimen codes.

Among the species encountered in the fogging samples, 26 were excluded as "tourists" (Longino et al., 2002). Two of these species were laboratory pests assumed to be contaminants. The others were very rare in the fogging samples but known from other records and observations to be exclusively ground‐nesting and ground‐foraging; it is possible that they were clinging to the funnels when they were lifted from the ground.

2.3 Richness

When fewer than 40 funnels were processed from a fogging event, the expected sample richness for 40 funnels was estimated by extrapolating to 40 funnels following models developed by Colwell et al. (2012) and Chao et al. (2014) and implemented in the R package iNEXT (Hsieh, Ma, & Chao, 2016). When funnels were individually processed, yielding incidence frequencies within samples, the iChao2 lower‐bound asymptotic richness estimate (Chiu, Wang, Walther, & Chao, 2014) was calculated, as implemented in SpadeR (Chao, Ma, Hsieh, & Chiu, 2015).

2.4 Tree species effect

To test for an effect of focal tree species on sample richness (observed and estimated), samples were grouped into three categories: Virola koschnyi (n = 12), Pentaclethra macroloba (n = 11), and "other" (a diverse assemblage of species from multiple families, n = 22; see Table 1). One‐way ANOVA was used to examine differences in mean sample richness. Differences in community composition were tested with PERMANOVA using the R package Vegan 2.5–4 (Oksanen et al., 2019). The data matrix consisted of species presence/absence (incidence) values within samples and the Jaccard distance metric. We also analyzed a subset of the data using just the first 18 samples, which were distributed in closely spaced triplets and processed with Method 1. For samples with more than 10 processed funnels, we randomly selected 10. We thus generated a replicated incidence matrix (sample × species) and enumerated the incidence frequency—the number of funnels in which a species occurred, a value from 1 to 10 (Colwell et al., 2012). We again tested for a tree species effect, treating these frequencies as indicators of abundance, and also tested for a "triplet" effect, using PERMANOVA. For these analyses, with abundance data, we used the Bray–Curtis distance metric.

2.5 Proportion of total ant fauna

To place the fogging results in the context of the entire La Selva ant fauna, a comprehensive species list for the site was compiled from a database of species occurrence records. The database contained 23,326 individual species occurrence records in 3,968 collection events. Using accumulated natural history knowledge of the ant species, they were categorized as (a) entirely arboreal, nesting and foraging in the vegetation; (b) entirely ground, nesting in soil, the litter layer, or rotten wood, and rarely foraging more than a meter above ground level; (c) arboreal foragers, nesting at or below the ground but foraging arboreally; (d) generalists, nesting and foraging in both habitats; or (e) pest ants, found mostly in and around buildings. Most species were either entirely arboreal or entirely ground. Inventory completeness for these two categories was assessed by the proportion of uniques (the proportion of species known from a single sample) and by coverage (the proportion of the total number of individuals in an assemblage that belong to species represented in the sample; Chao & Jost, 2012).

To assess whether the canopy has been historically undersampled, relative to the ground, and whether it holds a disproportionate number of new species, the rates of discovery of ground and arboreal species were compared. The distribution of discovery year was plotted separately for ground and arboreal ants. Year of discovery was assumed to be year of publication for described species and was set to 2019 for currently undescribed morphospecies.

3 RESULTS

3.1 Richness

Overall, considering both observed values in 40 funnels and values extrapolated to 40 funnels, mean sample richness was 34.5 species (S40 in Table 1, 95% C.I. 31–38, n = 45). The mean iChao2 estimate of sample richness was 49.05 species (iChao2 in Table 1, 95% C.I. 44–54, n = 27). Processing method had a significant effect on sample richness (1‐way ANOVA, F1,42 = 7.524, p < .01), with Method 1 being highest and Method 2 lowest. Mean richness for Method 3, the most accurate method, was 35.4 (95% C.I. 29–42, n = 9), similar to the mean for all 45 samples (34.5). For fully processed samples, the range of observed richness was 13 to 55 species.

A total of 163 species were found in the 45 fogging samples. The randomized species accumulation (rarefaction) curve was strongly convex, showing that relatively few fogging samples capture a large fraction of the arboreal community (Figure 3). The lower‐bound asymptotic richness estimate (iChao2, Chiu et al., 2014), based on the fogging samples alone, was 199 (95% confidence interval 183–228) species. Thirty‐two species (20%) were uniques (each found in only one sample) in the fogging dataset, implying a still‐rising species accumulation curve. If tourists had not been excluded (see Methods), the proportion of uniques would have been even higher. However, the 32 uniques in the fogging samples can be evaluated, and in some cases, their rarity explained as "edge effects" (Longino et al., 2002). Atta cephalotes is the common leaf‐cutter ant, but it appears to be rare in fogging samples. Pheidole passivaeferox is a specialized plant ant. Two species of Brachymyrmex, a species of Pseudoponera, and Strumigenys biolleyi are primarily ground ants. Of the 32 uniques, 28 species are known from additional collections at La Selva (hand‐collecting, Malaise traps, etc.). Only four species are uniques for the entire La Selva faunal inventory.

3.2 Tree species effect

Focal tree species had no significant effect on sample richness (1‐way ANOVA, F2,42 = 0.503, p = .61) or the iChao2 estimate of richness (1‐way ANOVA, F2,22 = 0.389, p = .68). Standard deviations for sample richness of Pentaclethra macroloba, Virola koschnyi, and "Other" were 12, 13, and 12, respectively, indicating that richness in single species of trees was no less variable than richness across multiple species.

We found no evidence that Pentaclethra macroloba, Virola koschnyi, and the "Other" category (multiple species) have distinctive communities of arboreal ants (PERMANOVA, F2,42 = 1.320, p = .09). Similarly, for the reduced dataset of the first 18 triplet‐grouped samples (the grouping of samples into six spatial and temporal clusters of three trees each), there was no effect of tree species on arboreal ant fauna (PERMANOVA, F2,15 = 1.018, p = .43). There was also no effect of the spatio‐temporal specification among triplets (PERMANOVA, F5,12 = 0.917, p = .71). Thus, beta diversity was minimal in this study.

3.3 Proportion of total ant fauna

The results for the entire La Selva inventory, which included Malaise traps and abundant hand‐collecting in addition to fogging, appear in Table 2. The observed total ant fauna of La Selva is 539 species, 182 (34%) of which are entirely arboreal. Estimated richness is 605 species for all ants (iChao1, 95% CI 584–636, Table 2) and 196 species (33% of estimated total) for arboreal ants (95% CI 189–210).

Coverage is a measure of sample completeness and is defined as the proportion of the total number of individuals in an assemblage that belong to species represented in the sample (Chao & Jost, 2012). iChao1 is the iChao1 lower‐bound asymptotic richness estimator and 95% confidence interval of the estimate (Chiu et al., 2014).

The fogging samples captured 30% of the observed fauna, and the estimated richness from fogging samples was 33% of the estimated total fauna. However, the species captured by fogging were not entirely congruent with the "entirely arboreal" category in Table 2. Among the 163 species in the fogging dataset, 138 were entirely arboreal, 17 were entirely ground, 4 were generalist, and 4 were ground‐nesting species with arboreal foragers. The 17 entirely ground species captured by fogging were species known to be in a "gray zone" with respect to stratification, nesting at or near ground level, but often foraging several meters above ground in shaded understory. Even though these species were classified as "entirely ground" in Table 2, they were not excluded as tourists (see Methods) because they were moderately abundant in samples and considered a legitimate component of the aboveground fauna. Thus, the fogging samples appeared to slightly underestimate the number of entirely arboreal species. This underestimation may in part be due to targeted collecting of specialized arboreal ants in the full faunal dataset. Only three of nine known specialized plant ants were captured by fogging. None of the arboreal genus Cylindromyrmex were captured, ants that nest deep in dead wood and are mostly known from alates in Malaise traps. In some cases, morphologically similar arboreal species could be more readily identified with nest collections, with greater likelihood of misidentification of scattered workers in fogging samples. Regardless, the results suggest that, at most, a third of the La Selva ant fauna is entirely arboreal.

Eleven percent of arboreal species are uniques (known from only one collection), and 14% of ground species are uniques. Coverage, a measure of inventory completeness, was nearly identical for arboreal and ground species (Table 2). Thus, the observed proportions present a robust finding and not an artifact of unequal sampling.

Most of the ant species in the inventory already have published names, but 10% of arboreal species and 14% of ground species are undescribed morphospecies. Names of ground and arboreal species were published at similar rates until the early 1900s, after which ground species were discovered at a higher rate (Figure 4). There is no evidence for a large reservoir of undiscovered species in the canopy.

4 DISCUSSION

La Selva Biological Station, a 1,500 ha area of mature lowland rain forest in Costa Rica, has an estimated ant fauna of about 600 species. An extensive canopy fogging program, combined with other forms of collecting, showed that about a third of the fauna is arboreal—nesting and foraging in the vegetation. The arboreal species are locally very rich, averaging 35 species in samples encompassing columns of vegetation that are about 20 m in diameter. The highest observed richness in a single sample was 55 species—nearly a third of the entire arboreal fauna.

Methods for sampling arboreal ant diversity vary among research groups, influencing the observed richness in the sampling units of different studies. Canopy fogging often produces high richness per sample (e.g., Yusah, Fayle, Harris, & Foster, 2012). Fogging samples, though often centered around individual trees, do not differentiate between species nesting in the tree and foragers entering the vegetation column from nearby parts of the canopy. Often, large trees are selected for fogging, which are complex environments with epiphytes, hemiepiphytes, lianas, smaller understory trees beneath the crown, and overlapping crowns of nearby large trees. For example, Wilson's (1987) study of fogging samples from a lowland rain forest in Peru reported 43 ant species "in a single tree," and a total of 135 species for the study as a whole, revealing the tight spatial proximity of species. Other rain forest canopy fogging studies include Floren and Linsenmeier (1997), averaging 36 species per tree with a maximum of 56 (Malaysia); Schulz and Wagner (2002), averaging 18 species per tree with a maximum of 37 (Uganda); and Floren et al. (2014), averaging 24.4 species per tree with a maximum of 53 (Malaysia).

Other methods are more rigorously focused on individual trees and use baiting or manual collecting to target individual foragers and nests. Klimes et al. (2015) felled entire trees to sample the arboreal ant fauna of lowland rain forest in New Guinea, allowing the careful dissection of individual trees of all sizes. Adams et al. (2017) climbed into the canopy of individual large trees and used a mixture of baiting and hand sampling to characterize the fauna (Panama). Yanoviak et al. (2007) used similar methods to sample arboreal ants in Gabon. These studies generally yield fewer species per sample than fogging, but they allow a more nuanced treatment of fine‐scale species density.

Regardless of sampling method, surveys of the arboreal fauna of tropical rain forests throughout the world yield a relatively narrow range of values for total richness. The above‐referenced studies ranged from 128 to 218 species. The fogging studies in particular reveal that the vegetation in the vicinity of a single large rain forest tree can house nearly a third of the total arboreal fauna.

We found no evidence of a “tree species effect” on community composition in the La Selva fauna. Most other studies of canopy ants that address tree specificity have likewise shown little or no tree species effect (Schulz & Wagner, 2002; Stork, 1987a, 1987b; Wilson, 1987), although tree species may influence the ant mosaic in the forest canopy in Gabon (Dejean et al., 2015). In contrast, structural complexity has often been cited as a driver of ant diversity. Although our study did not measure structural complexity, other studies have shown that ant diversity is higher on trees with epiphytes (Yanoviak, Berghoff, Linsenmair, & Zotz, 2011), on trees with greater connectivity to adjacent trees (Klimes et al., 2015), and on larger trees (Adams et al., 2017).

The high canopy has been referred to as "the last biotic frontier" (Erwin, 1983) because of the high number of previously unknown beetles found in fogging samples from the tropics. Floren et al. (2014) make a similar claim for tropical ants, suggesting that difficulties of canopy access have slowed discovery of canopy ant species. Erwin's conclusions were tempered by Stork (1988) and Novotny et al. (2007), who lowered estimates of canopy richness by questioning assumptions of high host specificity in canopy insects. Similar tempering might be warranted for canopy ants. There is no large reservoir of undiscovered canopy ant species at La Selva, and the proportion of new ant species from the canopy is not proportionately larger than the new species found on the ground. If anything, the reverse is true, and the true trove of unrecognized diversity has been for cryptic species hidden in the rotten wood, leaf litter, and soil of the forest floor. Early taxonomists described arboreal species at a similar rate to ground species (Figure 4). Arboreal species were readily collected from felled trees or recent treefalls, and many arboreal ants were large and active. Starting in the 1900s, relatively more ground ant species than canopy species were described, as mass extraction techniques such as Berlese and Winkler sampling revealed a large cryptic litter fauna.

The advent of canopy fogging has certainly contributed to the discovery of new species, but not to the same extent as Berlese and Winkler sampling. At La Selva, where intensive litter sampling and canopy sampling have both been carried out, about three times more new species have been discovered in leaf litter and rotten wood than in the canopy, proportions similar to the overall faunal composition. Floren et al. (2014) reported that close to 50% of Borneo's ant species are arboreal, basing the number of ground species on a faunal list of Borneo ants (Pfeiffer, Mezger, Hosoishi, Bakhtiar, & Kohout, 2011). But this conclusion could very well be a result of undersampling the litter fauna. Given the gradual and more recent emergence of large, globally distributed arboreal lineages from ancestrally ground‐nesting ants (Nelsen et al., 2018), we might expect similar ratios of ground and canopy ant species throughout the tropics.

Mature forest ecosystems are crucial to the conservation of biodiversity (Watson et al., 2018), and La Selva Biological Station protects a high‐quality mature forest ecosystem with a high diversity of arboreal ants. Floren and Linsenmeier (2005), Klimes et al. (2015), and Philip et al. (2018) have demonstrated a decrease in arboreal ant diversity as one moves from mature rain forest to younger forests with increasing levels of anthropogenic disturbance. Global climate change is a form of anthropogenic disturbance that affects all forests, mature and second growth alike. Longino, Branstetter, and Ward (2019) hypothesized that arboreal ants were less sensitive than ground ants to warming and elevational range shifts associated with the current interglacial, and thus may be less sensitive to anthropogenic climate warming in the future. This study of the La Selva fauna can facilitate testing of the hypothesis, by creating a benchmark that will allow assessment of changes over time in ant community richness and composition, in parallel with changes in regional land use and local climate.

Environmental Modelling & Software

Volume 94, August 2017, Pages 166-171

The suite of lumped GR hydrological models in an R package

Author links open overlay panelL.Coron1G.ThirelO.DelaigueC.PerrinV.Andréassian
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.envsoft.2017.05.002Get rights and content

Abstract

Lumped hydrological models are catchment-scale representations of the transformation of precipitation into discharge. They are widely-used tools for real-time flow forecasting, flood design and climate change impact assessment, and they are often used for training and educational purposes. This article presents an R-package, airGR, to facilitate the implementation of the GR lumped hydrological models (including GR4J) and a snow-accumulation and melt model. The package allows users to calibrate and run hourly to annual models on catchment sets and to analyse their outputs. While the core of the models is implemented in Fortran, the user can manage the input/output data within R. A number of options and plotting functions are proposed to ease automate tests and analyses of the results. The codes are flexible enough to include external models, other calibration routines or efficiency criteria. To illustrate the features of airGR, we present one application example for a French mountainous catchment.

1. Introduction

Hydrological modelling is essential for water resources management (Bellin et al., 2016), impact assessment and risk evaluation. Several types of models exist, among which lumped models are the most widely used tools for operational applications because they propose simplified catchment-scale representations of the transformation of precipitation into river discharge. As a consequence, these models generally have limited data requirements (typically climate inputs and streamflow) and can easily be implemented (Lampert and Wu, 2015). For these reasons, these models are also often used for training and education on hydrological sciences.

Irstea, a national applied research institute in France, has developed a suite of lumped hydrological models, the Génie Rural (GR) hydrological models (Perrin et al., 2009). They are lumped models that can be applied at various time steps, ranging from hourly to annual. Their conceptual parameters must be calibrated against streamflow observations. As they are simple, the GR models can easily be coded by end-users in their modelling environment. However, providing the models within a default implementation environment can ease their use and secure their correct application. This is the objective of the airGR package presented in this article, which makes the GR models available in R (R Core Team, 2015) in an open-source way.

Other hydrological modelling tools have been previously proposed in R, such as packages hydromad, topmodel, dynatopmodel, RRAWFLOW or TUWmodel, to make available hydrological models such as IHACRES (Andrews et al., 2011), TopModel (Buytaert, 2011), dynamic TopModel (Metcalfe et al., 2016), RRAWFLOW (Long, 2015) or an HBV-based model (Viglione and Parajka, 2016), respectively. The airGR package (Coron et al., 2016) is now released to ease the implementation of the GR models, whose suitability has been shown for a variety of hydrological contexts (Andréassian et al., 2006, Smith et al., 2012). Since this R-package is proposed and maintained by the team developing the GR models, it fully benefits from the integration of the latest model versions. Also, airGR aims to help beginners on simple case studies by providing data preparation functions, a calibration algorithm, plotting and diagnostic tools. It has been designed to allow extensive testing schemes, with respect to computational efficiency and to offer the users the possibility to pair other external models, calibration routines or error criteria. While the computational efficiency of the original Fortran code is kept to a large extent, the input/output data management, calibration/simulation settings and graphical viewing are made within R. Altogether, these characteristics make the airGR package a useful tool for hydrological modelling serving educational purposes and scientific research, on a basis of a single-catchment analysis or large sample catchment experiments.

After shortly presenting the GR hydrological models and associated algorithms in section 2, the main functionalities of the package are detailed in section 3 and a basic implementation tutorial is given. Finally, an example of the application of the package for a French mountainous catchment is presented in section 4.

2. Models and algorithms included in airGR

The airGR package brings into R several of the GR hydrological models developed at Irstea in France. It also includes a snow-accounting model and a potential evapotranspiration (PE) module based on the Oudin formula, as well as an optimization scheme.

2.1. Hydrological models

The development of the GR models was launched by Claude Michel at the beginning of the 1980s. He adopted an empirical development approach, starting from very simple model structures and progressively increasing their complexity when better model performance was achieved. The same type of approach was earlier advocated by Nash and Sutcliffe (1970).

The GR models were developed for specific time steps, namely the annual, monthly, daily and hourly time steps. The idea behind is that to model the rainfall-runoff relationship, different levels of complexity are required (in terms of model structure or number of free parameters). The larger the time step, the lower the model complexity. This ended up in the models detailed in Table 1, which are included in the airGR package. The names of the models include the number of free parameters and a letter for the time step (A for annual, M for monthly, J for daily - Journalier in French - and H for hourly).

First, Claude Michel proposed a daily version based on two stores and a single free parameter (Michel, 1983) – not included in airGR. Then, the model was complexifed. Today, daily versions with 4, 5 and 6 free parameters exist. GR5J has an additional parameter in comparison to GR4J to account for more complex intercatchment groundwater exchanges, while GR6J has a more detailed routing scheme, which improves the simulation of low flows. More recently, GR4J was adapted to the hourly time step (GR4H).

Monthly and annual model versions were also developed. The production function of the current monthly version has strong similarities with the daily version, but has a simplified routing scheme. The annual version is drastically simple compared to the daily or monthly versions since it does not include any conceptual store and comes down to a simple water balance formula, very similar to the Turc formula (Turc, 1954). It is out of the scope of the article to give a full description of these models, but all mathematical details are provided in the references listed in Table 1.

The GR models have been extensively applied in a wide range of climate and flow river conditions, in France but also in other countries, for modelling objectives ranging from water resources assessment, flood and low-flow estimation and forecast, land use and climate change impact assessment. Examples of studies using the GR models are available on the developers website (http://webgr.irstea.fr).

2.2. Snow-accounting model

The daily GR models can be applied on snowy catchments with a snow-accounting model, called CemaNeige (Valéry et al., 2014). This snow-accumulation and melt model is based on a degree-day approach. Precipitation is split between liquid and solid precipitation depending on air temperature. Then, the evolution of the snowpack is a function of air temperature and is controlled by two parameters that have to be calibrated. The catchment is split into five elevation zones, each having their own snowpack and snow-melt. The elevation bands are determined using the hypsometric curve of the catchment to get five zones of equal area. The precipitation and temperature data of each zone are derived from the lumped data using predefined altitudinal gradients.

2.3. Calibration algorithm

The GR models have a limited number of parameters to calibrate. To some extent, this limits the classical problems of optimization (secondary optima or equifinality) and makes the models more robust. Relatively simple optimization algorithms (typically local search) can thus be used with the GR models. The default algorithm provided in the package was developed at Irstea (Michel, 1991). Two distinct steps are included in the procedure:

1.

a systematic inspection of the parameters space is performed to determine the most likely zone of convergence. This is done either by direct grid-screening or by constrained sampling based on empirical parameter databases;

2.

a steepest descent local search procedure is carried out to find an estimate of the optimum parameter set.

This approach was evaluated against more complex global search algorithms and proved to be equally efficient to locate a robust optimum while being more effective in terms of number of model runs for the GR models (Mathevet, 2005, Edijatno et al., 1999). Additional calibration algorithms can be easily plugged in by users such as Zambrano-Bigiarini and Rojas (2013).

2.4. Data requirements

The GR models have limited data requirements. As inputs, they only need continuous time series of mean precipitation and PE estimates over the catchment scale at the target time step. Mean precipitation can be computed based on ground raingauges or gridded products and should be processed outside the package. PE can be estimated using common formula such as Penman-Monteith (Monteith, 1965). However, the package also includes the PE formula proposed by Oudin et al. (2005), which is computed using temperature data and extraterrestrial radiation (which only depends on the catchment latitude). Additionally, temperature data are also needed when using the CemaNeige model.

Last, the model implementation requires streamflow observations for parameter calibration and model performance evaluation. Streamflow time series should be concomitant with the climate time series, but data do not necessarily need to be continuous since the calibration and the evaluation functions can cope with missing data.

3. The airGR package

The R statistical computing environment was used to develop the package. It was selected because it is open-source, widely used in the hydrological community, freely available on all major platforms and offers many possibilities in terms of time series processing and graphical output capabilities. All functions in the package have associated documentation and propose runnable examples, which can be accessed via the R-package help menu.

3.1. Main functions and objects structures

The airGR package was designed to fulfil two major requirements: facilitate the use of hydrological models by non-expert users and allow flexibility regarding the addition of external models, calibration algorithms and efficiency criteria by expert users. The names of the functions and their arguments were chosen to this end. The package is mostly based on three families of functions: RunModel(), ErrorCrit() and Calibration().

The RunModel*() functions are used to call a given model and perform a simulation given a parameter set. As inputs, they require three objects: InputsModel, RunOptions and Param, with predefined structures and classes. As outputs, they provide simulated flow series and time series of various model internal states. Examples of such functions are RunModel_GR1A() and RunModel_GR4J(), which can be called to run the annual and daily models respectively.

The ErrorCrit*() functions are used to compute a given efficiency criterion based on the observed and simulated flow series. As inputs, they require two objects: InputsCrit and OutputsModel, with predefined structures and classes. As outputs, they provide a value of efficiency criterion and additional information on the corresponding computation. Efficiency criteria can be used either for model calibration or for model evaluation. The package includes Root-Mean-Square Error (RMSE), Nash-Sutcliffe efficiency (Nash and Sutcliffe, 1970) and Kling-Gupta efficiency (Gupta et al., 2009), with the corresponding functions: ErrorCrit_RMSE(), ErrorCrit_NSE() and ErrorCrit_KGE().

The Calibration_Michel() function (see section 2.4) is used to calibrate a model with the selected algorithm and objective function. As inputs, it requires four objects: InputsModel, RunOptions, InputsCrit and CalibOptions, with predefined structures and classes. As outputs, they provide a set of optimum parameters and the corresponding value of the objective function, but also information on the calibration execution (number of model runs, evolution of the objective function).

The structure for most arguments of the functions was prescribed in order to limit the risk of misuse and to prevent from unexpected crashes during calibration and simulation. Advanced users wishing to interface and enrich the airGR package with their own external functions will need to comply with these imposed structures and refer to the package source codes to get all the specification requirements. However, for basic use, functions are included to prepare these specific objects, namely the functions CreateInputsModel(), CreateRunOptions(), CreateInputsCrit() and CreateCalibOptions(). The role of these functions is self-explicit and readers can refer to the airGR help menu for detailed information. One originality of the package is that it was designed to automatically run the models on one study case but also on a set of catchments. This can be very useful if one wants to generalize its findings by confronting it to many catchments.

3.2. How to get started with airGR?

The airGR package is provided with a few examples and datasets that demonstrate how to run each function of the package in an R console. These examples are directly accessible from the help menu of each function (see Appendix A for a preview). It is advised to users to execute the examples line by line to fully understand them.

As a starting tutorial, we recommend first-time users to follow these steps:

1

open R and install/load the airGR package; we remind that the help menu can be accessed with the command help(“FunctionName”);

2

refer to the help for RunModel_GR4J() and the associated example to assess how a simulation is called in airGR (here with the GR4J model);

3

refer to the help for CreateInputsModel() and the associated example to assess how the inputs of a model are prepared;

4

refer to the help for CreateRunOptions() and the associated example to assess how the simulation options are parametrised;

5

refer to the help for ErrorCrit_NSE(), CreateInputsCrit() and the associated examples to assess how the computation of an error criterion is done/prepared;

6

refer to the help for Calibration_Michel(), CreateCalibOptions() and the associated examples to assess how a model calibration is done/prepared.

By following these six steps, new users should be able to implement any of the models of the airGR package to perform model calibration and simulation tests on their own catchments. A tutorial for the airGR installation and use can be accessed via https://webgr.irstea.fr/airGR-website.

3.3. Download and installation

The package can be downloaded from the CRAN https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/airGR/index.html. Installation should be performed from R or RStudio with compatibility on most common platforms (Windows, Mac OS, Linux).

4. Example of the use of the airGR package

In this section, the package was used to simulate daily flows in a mountainous catchment in the French Alps, the River Ubaye at Barcelonette (549 km2).

The flow data were extracted from the national public HYDRO archive (http://www.hydro.eaufrance.fr), in which the catchment has the ID code X0434010. Data were checked for errors and erroneous data were considered as gaps. Total precipitation and air temperature series were extracted from the SAFRAN reanalysis from Météo-France (Vidal et al., 2010), which is available on 8 × 8 km2 cells at a daily time step. Finally, PE time series were computed using Oudin et al. (2005) formula, with air temperature data coming from the SAFRAN reanalysis. The catchment hypsometry ranges from 1100 m to 3300 m, and the forcing data were extrapolated over five elevations layers (this was automatically done via the CreateInputsModel() function included in the package).

A classical calibration-validation test was performed for GR4J, with the CemaNeige snow model activated. The model was calibrated over the period Oct. 1989 to Sept. 1999 using the KGE criterion. The efficiency scores obtained for this 10-year calibration period are KGE = 0.89 and NSE = 0.79. A simulation was then performed on another 10-year period (Oct. 1999 to Sept. 2009) and led to the following scores: KGE = 0.85 and NSE = 0.82.

Fig. 1 illustrates the graphical results that are produced by the airGR package. The graphs in this preview correspond to the evaluation period and include the precipitation observations, the simulated snow packs and discharge time series. The regime, the flow duration curve and a scatter plot showing simulated flows against observed flows are also represented in the bottom panel. This output visualisation provides a good overview of the model efficiency and confirms the good criteria that were obtained.

5. Summary

The airGR package was designed for easily implementing the GR hydrological models developed at Irstea within the R-platform. The package includes a series of functions for data preparation, model simulation, parameters optimisation and results preview. In addition, the package is flexible enough to include other models, calibration algorithms or efficiency criteria needed by the users. This makes the package a well-suited tool to carry out hydrological modelling on simple case studies (e.g. for educational purposes) as well as to extensively test modelling schemes (e.g. run large catchment samples for hydrological research projects).

The package will be maintained and further developed in the coming years, to include more options and functionalities. We also plan to develop a user-friendly interface and teaching-friendly functions to widen the possible applications of the package for research, operational and educational purposes. Feedbacks or contributions from users are welcome and can be sent to airGR@irstea.fr.

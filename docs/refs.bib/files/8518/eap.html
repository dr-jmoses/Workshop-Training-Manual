Abstract

Invertebrates are a major component of terrestrial ecosystems, however, estimating their biodiversity is challenging. We compiled an inventory of invertebrate biodiversity along an elevation gradient on the temperate forested island of Hauturu, New Zealand, by DNA barcoding of specimens obtained from leaf litter samples and pitfall traps. We compared the barcodes and biodiversity estimates from this data set with those from a parallel DNA metabarcoding analysis of soil from the same locations, and with pre‐existing sequences in reference databases, before exploring the use of combined data sets as a basis for estimating total invertebrate biodiversity. We obtained 1,282 28S and 1,610 COI barcodes from a total of 1,947 invertebrate specimens, which were clustered into 247 (28S) and 366 (COI) OTUs, of which ≤ 10% were represented in GenBank. Coleoptera were most abundant (730 sequenced specimens), followed by Hymenoptera, Diptera, Lepidoptera, and Amphipoda. The most abundant OTU from both the 28S (153 sequences) and COI (140 sequences) data sets was an undescribed beetle from the family Salpingidae. Based on the occurrences of COI OTUs along the elevation gradient, we estimated there are ~1,000 arthropod species (excluding mites) on Hauturu, including 770 insects, of which 344 are beetles. A DNA metabarcoding analysis of soil DNA from the same sites resulted in the identification of similar numbers of OTUs in most invertebrate groups compared with the DNA barcoding, but less than 10% of the DNA barcoding COI OTUs were also detected by the metabarcoding analysis of soil DNA. A mark–recapture analysis based on the overlap between these data sets estimated the presence of approximately 6,800 arthropod species (excluding mites) on the island, including ~3,900 insects. Estimates of New Zealand‐wide biodiversity for selected arthropod groups based on matching of the COI DNA barcodes with pre‐existing reference sequences suggested over 13,200 insect species are present, including 4,000 Coleoptera, 2,200 Diptera, and 2,700 Hymenoptera species, and 1,000 arachnid species (excluding mites). These results confirm that metabarcoding analyses of soil DNA tends to recover different components of terrestrial invertebrate biodiversity compared to traditional invertebrate sampling, but the combined methods provide a novel basis for estimating invertebrate biodiversity.

Introduction

Terrestrial invertebrates comprise a range of highly diverse phyla (especially the Arthropoda), which include many undiscovered and undescribed species, presenting a particular challenge for biodiversity analysis (Watt 1975, Hamilton et al. 2010, Macfarlane et al. 2010, Scheffers et al. 2012, Buckley et al. 2015). The number of invertebrate species that exist globally is uncertain (Scheffers et al. 2012), and it is difficult to characterize entire invertebrate communities using traditional methods, which require the examination of individual specimens by an expert taxonomist or diagnostician (Cook et al. 2010). In contrast, DNA barcoding can allow the identification of invertebrate specimens based on comparatively efficient molecular analysis protocols (Hebert et al. 2003). This has proven beneficial for biodiversity analyses (Smith et al. 2005), the discovery of cryptic species (Burns et al. 2008, Velasco‐Castrillón et al. 2014), and biosecurity applications (Armstrong and Ball 2005).

The efficiency of DNA barcoding has been surpassed by DNA metabarcoding approaches, which enable the simultaneous identification of multiple organisms and groups in mixed communities, leading to more efficient and comprehensive estimates of biodiversity (Bik et al. 2012, Taberlet et al. 2012a). DNA metabarcoding can be applied to bulk invertebrate specimens (Yu et al. 2012, Ji et al. 2013, Leray and Knowlton 2015) or samples of easily collected environmental materials such as soils or sediments (Schmidt et al. 2013, Drummond et al. 2015, Czechowski et al. 2017). Soils, in particular, are a promising substrate for metabarcoding analyses of terrestrial biodiversity, as they contain rich communities of organisms as well as biological debris including DNA molecules (Levy‐Booth et al. 2007, Pietramellara et al. 2008, Taberlet et al. 2012b, Czechowski et al. 2016), and knowledge of these communities lags behind that of aboveground organisms (Decaëns 2010). Broadly, the composition of invertebrate communities in soil differs from those in leaf litter and above‐ground, yet metabarcoding analyses of these communities may provide similar ecological insights (Yang et al. 2014). While DNA from large vertebrates can be detected in soil (Andersen et al. 2012), it remains unclear to what extent this is true of invertebrates on the forest floor.

DNA barcoding and metabarcoding approaches to invertebrate biodiversity analyses are still imperfect. It is difficult to design PCR primers that will amplify barcode genes from all invertebrate taxa, leading to species or groups being undetected by PCR‐based analyses. Furthermore, sequences are grouped into OTUs based on arbitrary sequence similarity thresholds, which may vary among taxa leading to imprecise species counts (Clare et al. 2016). Finally, successful identification of sequences and OTUs depends upon the existence of reliable reference DNA sequences to which new sequences can be compared; yet such databases contain sequences from only a small minority of invertebrate species (Ekrem et al. 2007, Kwong et al. 2012, Kvist 2013).

Hauturu (Little Barrier Island) is a densely forested, extinct volcanic island in northern New Zealand. The island is roughly circular with an area of 28 km2, and slopes steeply to a central range of peaks >700 m altitude in a series of deeply divided ridges, with regenerating scrub and temperate broadleaf forest at lower elevations and moist cloud forest at the top. Considered one of New Zealand's most intact natural ecosystems, Hauturu is home to a diverse assemblage of rare indigenous birds and reptiles, and the only remaining natural population of New Zealand's heaviest terrestrial invertebrate, the wetapunga (Deinacrida heteracantha; Watts and Thornburrow 2011). The invertebrate fauna of Hauturu has otherwise received little scrutiny (Buckley et al. 2011, Seldon and Leschen 2011).

The main objectives of this study were (1) to assess the biodiversity of terrestrial invertebrates on Hauturu using traditional DNA barcoding methods; (2) to determine the extent to which metabarcoding analysis of soil DNA from the same locations resulted in detection of comparable organisms and estimates of invertebrate biodiversity; (3) to explore whether the overlap of DNA barcodes from the different analysis methods, and with pre‐existing database sequences, can be used to estimate the total biodiversity of terrestrial invertebrate groups on Hauturu and throughout New Zealand.

Methods

Sampling, DNA extraction, PCR, and Sanger sequencing

Invertebrate specimens were collected within each of 10 plots sited along an elevation gradient on Hauturu (Little Barrier Island) using pitfall traps and leaf litter sifting, as described in Drummond et al. (2015). The specimens were morphologically identified where possible to Order or Family level. DNA was extracted from each specimen using a non‐destructive protocol also described in Drummond et al. (2015). PCR was used to amplify a 658 base‐pair region of the mitochondrial cytochrome c oxidase gene subunit 1 (COI), and the D2‐D3 region of the ribosomal 28S gene. The former is a fast‐evolving region providing high species resolution, whereas the latter is a slower evolving region providing better reconstruction of deep lineages. PCRs were carried out in 50‐μL volumes containing 1.5 U Invitrogen Platinum Taq (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), 200 mmol/L dNTPs, 2.5 mmol/L MgCl2, 0.2 μmol/L of each primer, and 5 μL of template DNA. The primers used for COI PCRs were LCOI490 (5′‐GGTCAACAAATCATAAAGATATTGG‐3′) and HCO2198 (5′‐TAAACTTCAGGGTGACCAAAAAATCA‐3′; Folmer et al. 1994) and for 28S PCRs were 500F (5′‐ACTTTGAAGAGAGAGTTCAAGAG‐3′) and 501R (5′‐TCGGAAGGAACCAGCTACTA‐3′; Nadler et al. 2000). The PCR cycles used were 94°C for 3 min, followed by 40 cycles of 94°C for 30 s, 48°C (COI) or 56°C (28S) for 30 s, and 72°C for 45 s, followed by a final extension phase of 72°C for 5 min. PCR amplicons were purified using ExoSAP‐IT (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and Sanger sequenced in both directions by Macrogen (Seoul, South Korea) using the primers with which they were amplified.

Analysis of DNA barcode sequences

Sanger sequence reads were imported into Geneious R6 (Kearse et al. 2012) and processed using the Biocode Laboratory Information Management System plugin (LIMS; Biomatters, Auckland, New Zealand). Chromatograms were trimmed of primers and regions of low sequence quality, before de novo assembly of pairs of sequences. Assembled sequences were sorted into bins based on the overall sequence quality. Chromatograms from low‐quality assemblies, and for which assembly failed, were manually trimmed and corrected to improve the sequence quality, if possible, before re‐attempting assembly. Assembled COI sequences were translated into protein sequences and checked for inconsistent reading frames or stop codons indicating probable basecall errors. Any such errors were manually corrected in the corresponding chromatograms, which were then reassembled.

All sequences were assigned a taxonomic identity at order level or above by BLASTn (Altschul et al. 1990) against the NCBI GenBank non redundant (nr) nucleotide database, followed by parsing of BLAST results using the lowest common ancestor (LCA) method implemented in MEGAN metagenome analyzer (Huson et al. 2011). BLAST results for any sequences that were assigned only to high‐level taxa (cellular organisms, Bilateria, or Eukaryota) or could not be assigned to a taxon were further examined to determine the likely sequence identity. Any sequences that were assigned to a non‐invertebrate taxon were discarded. Sequence‐derived and morphological identifications for each specimen were compared to detect discrepancies between the two identification methods, and any sequences with discrepancies that could not be resolved by re‐examination were excluded. If both 28S and COI sequences were obtained for a specimen, an agreed taxonomic assignment was determined by comparing both sequence identifications and taking the lowest taxon identity achieved by either gene. If the identifications conflicted, i.e., one was not a subset of the other, the lowest common ancestor was determined between the two identifications. A final taxonomic assignment for each sequence and specimen was determined by taking the lowest taxon identity achieved by either morphological analysis or the sequence assignment. The COI and 28S sequences are available from GenBank under the accession numbers KP420745–KP422464 and KT439401–KT440856, respectively.

The COI and 28S sequences were clustered into operational taxonomic units (OTUs) at the 97% identity level using the UCLUST algorithm within USEARCH version 7.0.1090 (Edgar 2010). The taxonomic identity of each OTU was inferred as the consensus of corresponding sequence and specimen identifications. The COI OTU sequences were also queried against the BOLD identification system (Ratnasingham and Hebert 2007) for species‐level identifications. Biodiversity analyses based on the resulting OTU tables were carried out in R Studio (R Studio Team 2015) using R 3.1.1 (R Core Team 2016). Nonmetric multidimensional scaling (MDS) ordinations, heatmaps, and PERMANOVA comparisons of community composition were carried out using the R packages vegan (Oksanen et al. 2017), phyloseq (McMurdie and Holmes 2013), and ggplot2 (Wickham 2009). Maximum likelihood trees were constructed from OTU sequences using a general time reversible plus gamma model in RAxML version 8 (Stamatakis 2014), following alignment of sequences within Geneious R7 (Kearse et al. 2012) using the MAFFT plugin version 1.3.1 (Katoh and Standley 2013). Trees of OTU centroid sequences were annotated and formatted using the Interactive Tree of Life (Letunic and Bork 2011).

Comparing invertebrate DNA barcodes with soil DNA metabarcoding results

The composition of COI OTUs that were also detected in a parallel soil DNA metabarcoding study was investigated, to assess the extent to which the two different approaches detect comparable organisms and produce similar biodiversity estimates. The DNA metabarcoding data set was obtained by extraction of DNA from 1.5‐g soil samples collected in the same plots as those included in the present study, followed by PCR and 454 sequencing of COI amplicons (Drummond et al. 2015). The 454 sequences were truncated to 300 bp and error‐filtered (exclusion of those with maximum expected errors > 3) using USEARCH (Edgar 2010), denoised with Acacia (Bragg et al. 2012), dereplicated, and clustered into OTUs at a 97% similarity threshold with simultaneous chimera filtering using the UPARSE algorithm (Edgar 2013). The OTUs were identified using BLASTn against the GenBank nr nucleotide database and MEGAN metagenome analyzer (Huson et al. 2011), as described in Drummond et al. (2015). USEARCH (Edgar 2010) was used to identify OTU sequences among the 454 data set that matched invertebrate specimen COI OTU sequences with ≥ 97% sequence identity.

Invertebrate biodiversity estimation

The total species richness on Hauturu of the most common invertebrate phyla, arthropod classes and insect orders detected in this study were estimated based on COI and 28S OTU occurrences using SPECRICH2 (available online).11 http://www.mbr-pwrc.usgs.gov/software/specrich2.shtml
This program estimates total species richness from the occurrence of species at multiple sites or sampling occasions using a jackknife estimator (White et al. 1978, Rexstad and Burnham 1991).

In addition, the total species richness of invertebrate groups on Hauturu, and arthropod groups New Zealand‐wide, were estimated using a mark–recapture approach (Nichols and Conroy 1996, Boulinier et al. 1998, Nichols et al. 1998). For the Hauturu estimates, the soil DNA metabarcoding OTUs from terrestrial invertebrate groups were considered a previously detected species pool (K); the COI OTUs detected in the present study were considered a newly detected species pool (n); and those that matched DNA metabarcoding OTUs were considered the recaptured species pool (k). The total biodiversity (N) of invertebrate groups on Hauturu was then calculated as follows:

N ≈ K + 1 n + 1 k + 1 − 1
(Chapman 1951).

For the estimation of New Zealand‐wide biodiversity of terrestrial arthropod groups, sets of COI sequences derived from New Zealand invertebrates and corresponding to taxonomic groups detected in the present study were downloaded from the BOLD database (Ratnasingham and Hebert 2007). The sets of downloaded COI sequences comprised (1) all New Zealand arthropod sequences (excluding mites, and the mainly aquatic groups Malacostraca, Ephemeroptera, Plecoptera, and Trichoptera), (2) New Zealand arachnid sequences (excluding mites), (3) New Zealand myriapods, (4) New Zealand insect sequences (excluding Ephemeroptera, Plecoptera, and Trichoptera), and (5) sequences from selected New Zealand insect orders. Malacostraca and Mollusca were not analysed, as these groups are dominated by aquatic species and it was not feasible to isolate barcodes from the terrestrial components of these groups.

Each downloaded set of sequences was clustered into OTUs at the 97% identity level using the UCLUST algorithm (Edgar 2010). The usearch_global command in USEARCH (Edgar 2010) was used to identify OTUs from the BOLD data set that matched invertebrate specimen COI OTU sequences with ≥97% sequence identity. The BOLD‐derived OTU sets were considered a previously detected species pool (K); the invertebrate specimen COI OTUs detected in the present study were considered a newly detected species pool (n); and those that matched any of the BOLD‐derived OTUs were considered the recaptured species pool (k). The New Zealand‐wide total biodiversity (N) of these taxonomic groups was then estimated as described above.

Results

Invertebrate specimens and barcodes collected

A total of 4,317 invertebrate specimens were collected, of which 2,483 (1,221 pitfall trap specimens and 1,262 leaf litter specimens) were subjected to DNA barcoding (Appendix S1: Table S1). This resulted in COI sequences for 1,610 specimens and 28S sequences for 1,282 specimens (after exclusion of 99 COI and 175 28S sequences with identification conflicts). Overall, at least one COI or 28S sequence was obtained from 1,948 specimens, and for 945 specimens both 28S and COI sequences were obtained.

The successfully barcoded invertebrates consisted mostly of arthropods (1,915 of 1,948 specimens), with 30 molluscs, two annelids, and a single onychophoran. Overall, the barcoded specimens included nine classes and 30 orders (including 13 insect orders, six myriapod orders, and four arachnid orders). Coleopterans were most abundant (730 specimens), followed by hymenopterans (233), dipterans (151), lepidopterans (149), and amphipods (139; Table 1). Twelve orders (including two myriapod orders and five insect orders) were each represented by three or fewer specimens.

There were differences in the taxonomic composition of specimens for which 28S and COI barcodes were successfully obtained (Table 1). Most notably, none of 162 attempts to obtain 28S barcodes from amphipods succeeded, whereas 66% of COI barcoding attempts succeeded (resulting in COI sequences from 139 amphipod specimens). Only 13% of dipteran 28S barcoding attempts succeeded (19 specimens), compared to 79% of COI barcoding attempts (147 specimens). Conversely, 99% of attempts to obtain 28S barcodes from polydesmid specimens succeeded (83 specimens), compared to only 43% of COI attempts (33 specimens). Similarly, 85% of gastropod 28S barcoding attempts succeeded (28 specimens), whereas only 27% of COI attempts succeeded (9 specimens).

The taxonomic composition of barcoded pitfall trap specimens contrasted with that of leaf litter specimens. Pitfall trap samples resulted in a greater number of Araneae (49), hymenopteran (190), and orthopteran (47) specimens than leaf litter samples (27, 43, and 1 specimen, respectively). In contrast, the leaf litter samples included many more polydesmid (77), lepidopteran (134), pseudoscorpion (38), isopod (43), and gastropod (30) specimens than pitfall trap samples (8, 15, 2, 5, and 2 specimens, respectively). Overall, leaf litter specimens included a somewhat higher number of orders (25) than pitfall trap specimens (21). A Pearson chi‐square test confirmed that the invertebrate assemblages detected were dependent on the collection method used (P < 0.001). Photographs were obtained for 143 invertebrate specimens, representative selections of which are illustrated in Fig. 1.

The COI sequences were clustered into 366 OTUs at 97% sequence identity, including 182 that were represented by only a single sequence (singletons) (Table 1; Appendix S1: Fig. S1). The 28S sequences were clustered into 247 OTUs at 97% sequence identity, including 114 singletons (Table 1; Appendix S1: Fig. S2). The most common OTU among both the COI and the 28S data (comprising 140 and 152 sequences respectively) was identified by morphology as a member of the Salpingidae (Coleoptera; e.g., NZAC03010562; Fig. 1).

Composition and distribution of invertebrate OTUs

The inferred community composition of invertebrate OTUs varied by collection method and with elevation (Fig. 2; Appendix S1: Figs. S3–S4). Few OTUs occurred in multiple samples. Forty‐three of the 28S OTUs and 47 of the COI OTUs were detected by both collection methods. Only 13 of the 28S OTUs and 12 of the COI OTUs occurred in more than one‐half of the sample plots (Appendix S1: Figs. S3–S4).

Multidimensional scaling ordinations based on Jaccard and unweighted UniFrac metrics for both COI and 28S OTUs showed consistent separation of pitfall and leaf litter samples. An elevation trend was evident in all cases except for 28S OTUs when the Unifrac metric was used (Fig. 2). PERMANOVA (Anderson 2001) detected significant community composition differences between collection methods and elevations for both COI and 28S OTUs based on Jaccard and UniFrac metrics, with the weakest elevation trend for the 28S OTUs based on the Unifrac metric (Appendix S1: Table S3). Procrustes analysis (Peres‐Neto and Jackson 2001) confirmed that the ordinations based on the COI Jaccard and COI UniFrac distances were most similar (with a correlation of 0.97), followed by those based on the COI Jaccard and 28S Jaccard distances (0.89), the 28S Jaccard and 28S UniFrac distances (0.67), and the COI UniFrac and 28S UniFrac distances (0.61; Appendix S1: Fig. S5).

How many sequences/OTUs are already present in sequence databases?

Very few of the 28S and COI sequences or OTUs were already represented in sequence databases (Fig. 3; Appendix S1: Table S2). Of the 1,282 28S sequences, 160 (12.5%) matched existing GenBank sequences with ≥97% similarity over ≥450 bp, as did 25 (10%) of the 247 28S OTU centroids. There were five 28S OTUs with 100% similarity to GenBank sequences (Formicidae: Huberia brounii; Siphonaptera: Ceratophyllus petrochelidoni; Opiliones: Rakaia granulosa; Coleoptera: Paracatops sp. and Cyloma sp.). Among the COI data only 41 of the 1,610 sequences (2.5%) and 10 of the 366 OTU centroid sequences (2.7%) had matches to GenBank sequences at ≥97% similarity over ≥450 bp. The BOLD system identified nine of the same 10 COI OTUs to species level, plus another 8 that were only identified to higher levels based on GenBank. Only a single COI OTU had a 100% match to an existing GenBank sequence (Coleoptera: Clambus simsoni). This plus another three OTUs had 100% matches based on the BOLD system (Hymenoptera: Pachycondyla castanea; Diptera: Bradysia impatiens; and Lepidoptera: Psychoda cinerea; Appendix S1: Table S2). The most abundant of the 28S and COI OTUs were only 91.4% and 88.5% identical to existing GenBank sequences, respectively. The mean identity of OTU centroid sequences to their closest GenBank matches was lower for COI OTUs (86.0%) than for 28S OTUs (90.4%).

Comparison of invertebrate specimen OTUs and soil DNA metabarcoding OTUs

DNA metabarcoding of soil samples resulted in 113,427 raw COI sequences, 50,832 unique filtered/denoised sequences, and 14,248 OTUs, of which 734 (representing 7,900 sequences) were identified as terrestrial invertebrates (Drummond et al. 2015). The numbers of invertebrate COI OTUs derived from DNA metabarcoding of soil samples were similar to the numbers of COI OTUs derived from DNA barcoded specimens for many invertebrate groups (Table 2). For example, there were 442 arthropod, 261 insect, and 25 lepidopteran OTUs in the soil DNA metabarcoding data, compared to 355 arthropod, 270 insect, and 28 lepidopteran OTUs among the DNA barcoded invertebrates. However, there were markedly higher numbers of annelid (207) and molluscan (81) OTUs among the DNA metabarcoding data than among the DNA barcoded invertebrates (one annelid and six molluscs). Conversely, there were many more coleopteran OTUs (121) among the DNA barcoded invertebrates than among the DNA metabarcoding data (24). Few OTUs were common to both data sets. For example, 22 arthropod and 17 insect OTUs among the invertebrate barcode data matched an arthropod or insect OTU among the soil DNA metabarcoding data set at ≥97% sequence identity. Among the insect groups, four lepidopteran OTUs matched between both data sets, whereas all of the coleopteran, dipteran, or hemipteran OTUs occurred in only one or other data set. When all 366 of the COI DNA barcode OTU sequences were compared to the full set of 14,248 soil DNA metabarcoding OTUs, 33 OTUs matched with ≥97% sequence identity between the two data sets (9% of the Sanger sequence COI OTUs, and 0.07% of the 454 sequence COI OTUs).

Estimation of Hauturu invertebrate biodiversity

The jackknife estimates (SPECRICH2) of total species richness of invertebrate groups on Hauturu based on COI DNA barcode OTU abundances included 1,024 arthropod species and seven mollusc species (Table 2). The estimated total insect species richness was 770, while the total species richness estimates for non‐mite arachnids, myriapods and malacostracans were all <100. Of the insect orders analysed, Coleoptera had the highest estimated species richness (344 species), followed by Diptera (113 species). Species richness estimates based on 28S barcode OTUs were ~25–40% lower than those based on COI data for most groups. The 28S‐based estimate of malacostracan species richness was less than half of the corresponding COI estimate, and both 28S and COI estimates of hymenopteran species richness were the same.

The species richness estimates resulting from the mark–recapture analysis of COI DNA barcode OTUs and soil DNA metabarcoding OTUs were higher for every invertebrate group considered than the corresponding SPECRICH2 estimates, and often markedly so (Table 2). Estimated richness for arthropods (excluding mites) was approximately 6,800 species, and for insects was approximately 3,900 species. Estimated species richness among the insect groups included 3,049 coleopteran species and 1,374 dipteran species, and between 20 (Orthoptera) and 340 (Hymenoptera) species for the other major insect groups. Of the non‐insect groups, the estimated species richness was lowest for myriapods (67 species) and onychophorans (seven species).

Estimation of New Zealand‐wide terrestrial arthropod biodiversity

The numbers of OTUs generated from BOLD sequences closely approximated the corresponding number of BOLD system‐defined BIN clusters (Ratnasingham and Hebert 2013) (Table 3). For example, 20,227 insect sequences corresponding to ~2,000 BINs were clustered into 2,132 OTUs. The numbers of BOLD‐derived OTUs exceeded the corresponding number of Hauturu‐derived COI DNA barcode OTUs (except for orthopterans). The relative OTU counts were most similar for coleopterans (165 BOLD OTUs and 121 Hauturu OTUs, between which there were only four matches of ≥97% sequence identity) and least similar for hymenopterans (782 BOLD OTUs and 30 Hauturu OTUs, between which there were eight matches of ≥97% sequence identity). Estimation of New Zealand‐wide terrestrial invertebrate diversity using mark–recapture analysis suggested approximately 18,100 arthropod species, 13,200 insect species, and 1.000 estimated non‐mite arachnid species (Table 3). Of the insect orders analysed, estimated coleopteran species richness was highest (4,000 species), followed by hymenopteran and dipteran species richness (2,700 and 2,200 species, respectively).

Discussion

The use of molecular techniques to inform biodiversity estimation, while showing much promise, remains to be developed into a robust procedure. Our study of invertebrate biodiversity on the small forested island of Hauturu demonstrates many of the pitfalls and promises of these methods.

The lack of close database matches for most barcode sequences in this study suggest that we have detected many previously unsequenced and likely undescribed invertebrates using conventional specimen collection and DNA barcoding methods. The resulting sequence data set represents a significant advance in the characterization of the invertebrate fauna of Hauturu, and has increased the number of New Zealand arthropod COI and 28S sequences present in GenBank by approximately 20% and 85%, respectively.

We attempted to obtain both 28S and COI barcodes from each specimen. We were unable to amplify either gene for ~5–10% of specimens in most arthropod classes/orders, but higher proportions of malacostracans and diplopods. This suggests that the non‐destructive DNA extraction protocol may have failed in these cases, perhaps due to exoskeleton characteristics of these groups. For a larger number of specimens, the success of sequencing 28S and COI loci varied among taxonomic groups, particularly for dipterans, amphipods, and myriapods. Given that the same invertebrate DNA extracts were used for both COI and 28S PCR attempts, these observations suggest probable differences in PCR primer binding specificity among these taxonomic groups (rather than differential success of DNA extractions). Differing rates of PCR success among arthropod taxa have previously been observed for the widely‐used COI primers (Geller et al. 2013, Gibson et al. 2014), but not for the 28S primers, which have not previously been applied to such a broad range of invertebrate taxa. Our results suggest that these 28S primers may need to be redesigned for PCR amplification of dipteran and amphipod sequences.

The two primer sets provided somewhat differing perspectives of invertebrate biodiversity. This is not only due to the varying PCR success rates of the primer sets described above, but likely also to the properties of the targeted loci. In particular, the lower estimates of 28S OTU richness compared to COI OTU richness are consistent with the slower evolution and lower taxonomic resolution of the 28S gene. For many taxa, the COI locus is thought to allow discrimination of genera and species (Hebert et al. 2003), suggesting that the COI OTU‐based species richness estimates may be more accurate than the 28S estimates. The slower evolutionary rate of the 28S gene also explains the higher proportion of close BLAST matches for 28S OTUs compared to COI OTUs, despite the lower number of pre‐existing 28S sequences in GenBank. Our results also imply that 28S sequence‐based identifications to species or genus level may be less reliable than those based on COI sequences.

Errors associated with the 454 sequencing technology have a propensity to inflate species richness estimates (Quince et al. 2009), but in this case, the richness of soil DNA metabarcoding OTUs was similar to that of invertebrate specimen barcodes for many invertebrate groups. For groups with contrasting richness between the two data sets, the observed differences are broadly consistent with expectations of the constituents of forest floor and belowground communities. For example, annelids OTUs were much more common among the soil DNA metabarcoding data set, whereas coleopteran OTUs were more common among the DNA barcodes from the forest floor. There was limited overlap of OTUs between the two data sets for all invertebrate groups, implying that different communities or habitats were sampled by each method, and that there is limited representation of forest floor invertebrate fauna in the soil DNA pool. This is perhaps not surprising, as the size of the soil samples analysed in this case was only 1.5 g, whereas the forest floor specimen collection methods gathered invertebrates from a considerably larger spatial area. However, that some OTUs were shared between both methods suggests that DNA metabarcoding analysis of soil material collected from a wider spatial scale may increase the detection of forest floor invertebrates.

The lack of OTUs common to both data sets resulted in elevated total Hauturu species richness estimates compared to those based on invertebrate specimen barcodes alone. It is reasonable to expect that the combined richness of invertebrate fauna in soil as well as on the forest floor will be higher than that for either substrate alone, however. The magnitude of species richness estimates resulting from this analysis should be taken as a rough guide. These mark–recapture estimates are sensitive to small changes in the number of OTUs shared between data sets. Furthermore, the treatment of results from two contrasting sampling protocols as equivalent for a mark–recapture analysis will tend to overestimate total species numbers, leading to a figure that may best be regarded as an upper limit. Together the jackknife and mark–recapture approaches probably provide a reasonable interval between which the truth may lie.

The mark–recapture estimation of New Zealand‐wide invertebrate biodiversity provided broadly concordant estimates of New Zealand‐wide biodiversity for arthropod groups compared to published terrestrial species richness estimates (Johns 2010, Macfarlane et al. 2010, Sirvid et al. 2010). New Zealand's total terrestrial invertebrate fauna has been estimated to include ~18,000 arachnid species (of which some 15,000 are mites), 500 myriapod species, and 20,000 hexapod species, of which a high proportion are endemic and many are undescribed or unknown (Johns 2010, Macfarlane et al. 2010, Sirvid et al. 2010). Our estimate of total insect OTU richness (13,218 OTUs) is 69.3% of published total insect richness estimates (19,076 species; Macfarlane et al. 2010). Our estimate of total hymenopteran richness (2,696 OTUs) is larger than conventionally estimated hymenopteran species richness (2,098 species), whereas our coleopteran, dipteran, hemipteran, and lepidopteran richness estimates are 47.5–61% of corresponding published estimates (Macfarlane et al. 2010). Our myriapod, non‐mite arachnid, and orthopteran estimates are only 34.1%, 28.3%, and 6.8% of published estimates, respectively (Johns 2010, Macfarlane et al. 2010, Sirvid et al. 2010). Considering that the specimens in this study were collected only from the forest floor on a small island ecosystem compared with pre‐existing data derived from a wider range of habitats and collection methods, such discrepancies are perhaps not unexpected. Indeed, sampling from a single island for “recapture” will bias toward estimation of the number of widespread species, and miss species that are confined to local regions with different environments to those found on Hauturu. Therefore, these results may actually be underestimates.

Despite these limitations, this research has significantly increased the number of New Zealand invertebrates that are represented in GenBank, and has raised the potential use of molecular techniques for estimating invertebrate biodiversity and mark–recapture methods for total biodiversity estimation based on different data sources.

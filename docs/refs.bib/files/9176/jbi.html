    Abstract

    In their recent critique, Qian et al. stated that the results of structural equation modeling analysis (SEM) in Chan et al. were flawed. Here, we show that the source of the difference, in their reanalysis, is that Qian et al. did not follow the standard, iterative strategy of SEM, which allows researchers to evaluate which model offers the best account of the data in both absolute and relative terms. Here, we provide step‐by‐step instructions to reproduce our published results. We address—and aim to resolve—Qian et al.'s concerns regarding SEM. Moreover, in our original paper, we used three distinct statistical methods—hierarchical partitioning, SEM and stationary bootstrap—to show that different temporal scales of environmental variability differentially impact the elevational range size (ERS) of species. We believe it is time to move on to probing the pressing issue of how and why climatic variability impacts ERS.

    1 INTRODUCTION

    In a critique of Chan et al. (2016), Qian, Field, Zhang, and Zhang (2017) concluded that the structural equation modeling (SEM) results in our Figure 1a (Chan et al., 2016) were flawed, because they could not repeat our results, “using the same dataset, analysis, and software”. In fact, they were unable to repeat most of our SEM results, stating, “…we cannot replicate the results reported for model 3 in Chan et al. (2016), nor those for many of the other models reported in their Table S2”. A close reading of Qian et al.'s Supporting Information explains how they arrived at this conclusion. Evidently, they did not follow the standard process that distinguishes SEM from traditional statistical tests. SEM consists of five consecutive, iterative steps: (a) model specification, (b) model identification, (c) estimation, (d) evaluation of fit and (e) model modification (Chou & Huh, 2012; Hoyle, 2012a,2012c). Thus, it is understandable that Qian et al. did not obtain the same results we did. In retrospect, we regret not having been more comprehensive in specifying the details of our SEM analyses in the Supplemental Online Material of Chan et al. (2016).

    To clarify every step of each procedure conducted in our published analysis, we have published Additional Supplemental Materials (Chan et al. 2018) for Chan et al. (2016), with full instructions for repeating and refining the statistical results reported in Chan et al. (2016). These materials include raw and derived data, all source code and the table of parameters for reproducing the results that were reported in Chan et al., 2016 (the data can be downloaded at http://science.sciencemag.org/content/351/6280/1437/tab-figures-data). By following our instructions, we expect that anyone with access to the appropriate software (ibm spss amos, Arbuckle, 2012) can easily apply our approach and reproduce our results. Here, we address and aim to resolve each of Qian et al.'s concerns regarding the reported coefficients and criteria for model‐fit. (We are puzzled by Qian et al.'s statement that they used the same software we did. While we used ibm spss amos, Qian et al. reported that they used lavaan in R, although we believe the correct use of different packages should give similar results.)

    2 SEM ANALYSIS

    Qian et al.'s commentary suggests a misunderstanding regarding the iterative nature of SEM analysis. Many researchers, including ecologists, have repeatedly emphasized that SEM analysis, correctly used, aims to assess and compare hypotheses (Chou & Huh, 2012; Grace, 2006; Hoyle, 2012c). Thus, we begin by building models that represent a group of theoretically meaningful, candidate hypotheses, based on the literature and our a priori subjective judgement about causes and processes among geographical factors, climatic factors and elevational range sizes (ERS). Subsequently, we use objective statistical criteria for model‐fit to develop one or more statistically adequate models. There are two steps in evaluating the alternative models. First, in an absolute sense, we evaluate whether each model has a statistically adequate fit to the observed data. Then, in a relative sense, we evaluate which of these adequate models—often after iterative model modification—offers the best fit to the data (Chou & Huh, 2012; Grace, 2006; Hoyle, 2012c).

    Mathematically, a SEM is represented by a set of simultaneous equations. If there are too many free variables (parameters, coefficients and/or intercepts) in a candidate model to produce a mathematically adequate solution to these simultaneous equations, or if the data are not sufficient to support a statistically adequate solution, the value of one or more of the variables can be constrained (specified) (Hoyle, 2012b). Thus, when we found that the fit of a candidate model was inadequate (by assessing the ratio of chi‐squared to degrees of freedom, which must be no greater than 5 [Jöreskog, 1969; Wheaton, Muthen, Alwin, & Summers, 1977; West, Taylor, & Wu, 2012]), we simplified (modified) the model, where possible and justifiable, by decreasing the number of free variables (parameters, path coefficients and/or intercepts) to be evaluated in the SEM analysis. We did this by specifying the value of one or more variables, thereby increasing the degrees of freedom of the model. In SEM, specifying the value of a variable in a model, instead of estimating it from the model, is a means of identifying the variable.

    Each alternative model structure that we initially proposed (table S2 in Chan et al., 2016) required simplification by the specification of variables to achieve acceptable degrees of freedom, under the chi‐squared ratio criterion. These simplifications are fully detailed in the Additional Supplemental Material (Chan et al. 2018), in which we describe additional technical details and reproduce in detail the key results in Chan et al. (2016). To fairly compare different alternative models that required specification of variables, we first explored the influences of different model identification strategies on model fit, for each model. Specifically, we compared models with (a) different numbers and (b) different positions (i.e., primary or secondary hierarchy) of identified (specified) parameters and path coefficients. We found that model fit values varied very little among different identification strategies (see Table S8 and S9 in Chan et al., 2018). In all cases of acceptable models (RMSEA <0.08 and CFI >0.95), SRMR ranged only between 0.0218 and 0.0243 for different numbers of identifications (Table S8) and between 0.0217 and 0.0241 for different positions of identifications (Table S9). Thus, we chose (Chan et al., 2016) to identify the same set of parameters and path coefficients in all models, whenever possible, while avoiding identifying parameters in the same hierarchy or path coefficients linked to the same parameter. Specifically, we specified the means and/or variances for a uniform set of parameters and path coefficients (the means of latitude = 0.497, MAP = 0.322 and DTR = 0.541; the multiple regression coefficients between latitude and STR = 0.830 and between DTR and range size = −0.233). These values can be easily computed from the model input data using any statistical tool (e.g., spss or R).

    By this procedure, we were able to decrease the number of free parameters and appropriately modify our models, guided by statistical evaluations of fit, and determine which candidate model was the best model (Chou & Huh, 2012; Hoyle, 2012a), as we reported in Chan et al. (2016). In contrast, without employing the steps of model specification, identification, estimation, evaluation of fit and model modification required for the proper and most effective use of SEM analysis, it is perhaps not surprising that Qian et al. concluded, “We are unable to meaningfully improve on the analysis of this dataset that was published by McCain (2009), so we do not attempt to provide a new ‘best model’.” In short, we used SEM to evaluate alternative hypotheses, which helped us identify the best among candidate hypotheses. We then performed further analyses, using stationary bootstrap, to validate our SEM findings. We found that mean annual precipitation (MAP) plays an important role in influencing the relative importance of the effects of DTR versus STR on the ERS of species.

    3 CONCERNS ABOUT NONINDEPENDENT OR UNBALANCED SAMPLES

    Many of Qian et al.'s (2017) other concerns have been adequately addressed in our original paper (Chan et al., 2016), including the most appropriate and powerful statistical approaches to deal with uneven or nonindependent sampling arising from aggregated geographical distributions or evolutionary relatedness among taxonomic groups. These are common issues in macroecological studies. Conventionally, rarefying samples or dichotomizing continuous predictors are widely adopted procedures, but often applied without considering the serious statistical and inferential perils of these approaches. Instead, to deal with these sampling issues, we conducted formal statistical analyses that use all the data and do not depend on arbitrary dichotomies.

    In our analysis, latitude and MAP emerged as the geographical and environmental factors that indirectly shape ERS, through their influence on climatic variability (diurnal temperature range or DTR, and seasonal temperature range, or STR). As Qian et al. correctly point out, mountain gradients in the McCain (2009) data set are not evenly distributed across Earth's continents. It is precisely for this reason that we used a stationary bootstrap method to assess whether STR and/or DTR is more explanatory than expected at random along gradients of latitude and MAP (representing the wet‐arid continuum). The issue of uneven sample distribution is commonly faced by most, if not all, meta‐analyses. Stationary bootstrap—a block‐resampling procedure that joins blocks of random length—is a formal statistical method for dealing with uneven sampling along a stationary gradient, such as a time series, latitude or an environmental variable (e.g., precipitation) (Politis & Romano, 1992, 1994). Using stationary bootstrap analysis, we showed that DTR is more important than STR in explaining ERS in regions with mid‐MAP (1,000–1,600 mm) and in midlatitude regions (25–35°N), whereas STR is more important than DTR in regions with higher MAP (between 1,600 and 2,700 m), regardless of latitude (fig. 2 of Chan et al., 2016).

    By reducing the data from the drier regions by half, Qian et al. (2017) rarefied the over‐represented “dry” mountain moiety of the data set to make the proportions of dry and wet samples fit the proportion of dry and wet gradients in the real world. Certainly, it is tempting to set up a qualitative dichotomy, that is, between “wet” and “dry” conditions, for a continuous variable, and evaluate results separately. Following McCain (2009), Qian et al. (2017) used a 50% humidity index to dichotomize the gradients—a criterion they refer to as “unbiased”, but which in fact is no less arbitrary than any other cut‐off point. The serious perils of dichotomizing continuous predictors—including the arbitrariness of the cut‐off point and inevitably smaller sample sizes—have been extensively discussed (Royston, Altman, & Sauerbrei, 2006). Thus, separating the data into “dry” versus “wet” gradients and performing the SEM separately for each group of gradients is statistically weak and potentially misleading. In contrast, we do not use the artificial 50% (or any other) cut‐off point. Instead, we consider the continuous global distribution of climatic conditions, based on land surface area. In fact, by surface area, low‐rainfall regions are actually under‐represented in our analysis (Figure 1). Thus, the role of DTR in influencing ERS of species might be even more important than suggested by our analysis.

    Overall, applying the stationary bootstrap procedure is a more repeatable, nonarbitrary, and rigorous approach to deal with effects of continuous variables, although the method is not familiar to most macroecologists. We suggest that macroecologists would do well to move away from discarding data and setting up arbitrary dichotomies, instead of using a more formal resampling method on a continuous variable to resolve the issue of the uneven sample distributions. We are not persuaded by the argument that any particular statistical procedure should be followed simply because it is familiar and commonly used, once manifestly better approaches have been developed.

    Qian et al. (2017) criticized our decision not to report R2 in our SEM analysis. Because a large number of variables and relationships were included in the SEM analysis (i.e., MAP, Latitude, DTR, STR, mean range size), modest values of R2 are expected for the overall fit of the model. It is precisely for this reason that many researchers emphasize AIC over R2 in model comparisons. As measures of fit appropriate to SEM, we reported RMSEA, SRMR and CFI in our SEM analysis. In our stationary bootstrap analysis, we provided a comprehensive analysis of R2 for the effects of DTR and STR (in our original fig. 2a), by comparing observed R2 to random expectation of R2. We consider this approach more rigorous than the approach that Qian et al. (2017) used in their critique.

    4 DATA SUBSETTING

    Qian et al. argued against conducting a single, taxonomically comprehensive analysis, stating:

    McCain (pers. comm.) strongly cautions against this, arguing in particular that including the rodent data in the analysis is inappropriate because rodents have the opposite elevational range size trend to the other vertebrate groups …. [O]ur final reanalysis started by excluding the rodent data and shows that removing a single, pseudoreplicated data‐point again overturns the key empirical conclusion of Chan et al. (2016).

    In addition to taxonomic subsetting, we note that McCain's (2009) key analyses were based on simple regression, with an additional form of data subsetting. Elevational distribution data were truncated at certain, taxon‐specific elevations, and only range sizes below these elevations were compared across latitude in a series of regressions—again, without considering the pitfalls of data subsetting. Moreover, McCain's (2009) analyses did not take into account the inherent collinearity among candidate predictors.

    In our paper, we formally examined taxon‐specific effects on range sizes (fig. S11 in Chan et al., 2016). We found that DTR has a relatively stronger effect on rodents, snakes and lizards than on other taxa. There is no a priori justification for removing any of these groups from the data set. In fact, in an alternative approach, we also statistically controlled for taxonomic effect by setting taxon as a variable in the model, as described in the Supplementary Material for our published paper (Chan et al., 2016, p. 3; L91–92). Following McCain's (2009) approach, Qian et al. also used simple regression to conclude that, “the DTR and mean ERS variables are not correlated with each other (r = 0.039, p = 0.651; Figure 1a).” Simple regressions on two‐variable scatterplots can completely miss important and significant relationships when confounding variables are not included in the analysis. It is straightforward to show that, if DTR and STR are both included in a multiple regression model, both DTR and STR have significant effects on the ERS of species (DTR: t = −2.19, p = 0.03; STR: t = 3.72, p < 0.01).

    Similarly, Qian et al. criticized the data set we used as biased and pseudoreplicated, because, “The 137 montane gradients in the full data set were located in only 82 sites”. Their solution was, again, to exclude data from the analysis. In this case, they excluded the rodent data, then identified two data points in the high DTR region as “influential” and “pseudo‐replicated” (although they were for different taxa, and thus carry different information). By removing all rodent data and one of the two “influential” points, they claimed that the DTR trend becomes non‐significant. Applying the same logic, why are the two points in the low DTR region in their fig. 3 not just as “influential”? Removing one of these two points makes the DTR trend even more significant (t = −2.26, p = 0.026). Because subjective data manipulation has no clear limits, we strongly caution against it. Data dependence is almost inherently unavoidable in studies of ecology and evolution. In fact, objective ways to deal with such nonindependent data structures have long been discussed and developed. In the present case, only a limited number of mountain ranges exist on Earth, and only a limited number of taxa have been sampled. Additional samples in the same sites (but for different taxa) and additional samples for the same taxa (but in different sites) help reduce the impact of stochasticity—for the same reason that experiments require replication (Oksanen, 2001). Mixed model analysis is a common approach to deal with data obtained in such nested or crossed experimental or sampling designs (Schielzeth & Nakagawa, 2013). Here, we found that, by setting mountain range as a random factor to control the nonindependence of sampling sites, the fixed effects of DTR and STR had negative and positive influence on ERS, respectively (DTR: t = −2.07, p = 0.038; STR: t = 2.40, p = 0.016). This analysis showed that controlling for multiple samples from the same mountain does not change the results of our original analysis.

    5 CONCLUSION

    For readers interested in whether daily climate variation impacts the ERS of species, we remain confident that the answer is, “Yes.” We used hierarchical partitioning to select the most influential candidate variables (Mac Nally, 1996). This analysis suggested that the explanatory power of daily temperature range (DTR) is, in general, greater that the explanatory power of STR—a factor widely viewed as representing climatic variation. (Specifically, DTR explains 13.8%, whereas STR explains only 2.9% of explainable variation in ERS; Supplementary Material of Chan et al., 2016). We then used structural equation modeling to infer the causal relationships among candidate variables. We found that MAP and latitude influence ERS indirectly through their influence on DTR and STR. Finally, we used stationary bootstrap to evaluate the relative importance of DTR and STR in explaining how ERS varies with MAP and latitude. Each of these analyses suggests that DTR and STR have a significantly negative and positive influence on ERS, respectively.

    Following Janzen's insightful climatic variability hypothesis (Janzen, 1967) and Gilchrist's simple but powerful model (Gilchrist, 1995), we offered evidence for the hypothesis that different temporal scales of environmental variability can differentially impact the ERS of species. Many interesting puzzles regarding the global pattern of ERS emerge from our studies, such as whether endotherms and ectotherms respond differently to DTR and STR. By what mechanism does greater DTR promote niche specialization, and, thus, smaller range size of species (other things being equal)? Finally, if climate change poses a greater threat to species with smaller range sizes, as many believe (e.g., Perez, Stroud, & Feeley, 2016; Tewksbury, Huey, & Deutsch, 2008), will species in regions with large DTR be more vulnerable, just as tropical species in regions with lower STR are believed to be especially vulnerable? More studies on the impacts of climatic variability on species range size are clearly needed.

Abstract

enThis link goes to a English sectionesThis link goes to a Spanish section

Litterfall and litter decomposition are key elements of nutrient cycling in tropical forests, a process in which decomposer communities such as macro‐arthropods play a critical role. Understanding the rate and extent to which ecosystem function and biodiversity recover during succession is useful to managing the growing area of tropical successional forest globally. Using a replicated chronosequence of forest succession (5–15, 15–30, 30–45 years, and primary forest) on abandoned pastures in lowland tropical wet forest, we examined litterfall, litter chemistry, and effects of macro‐arthropod exclusion on decomposition of two litter types (primary and 5‐ to 15‐years‐old secondary forest). Further, we assessed macro‐arthropod diversity and community composition across the chronosequence. Overstory cover, litterfall, and litter nutrients reached levels similar to primary forest within 15–30 years. Young secondary forest litter (5–15 years) had lower initial N and P content, higher C:N, and decayed 60 percent faster than primary forest litter. The presence of macro‐arthropods strongly mediated decomposition and nutrient release rates, increasing litter mass loss by 35–44 percent, N released by 53 percent, and P release by 84 percent. Forest age had no effect on soil nutrients, rates of litter decomposition, nutrient release, or macro‐arthropod influence. In contrast, abundance and community composition of macro‐arthropods remained significantly lower and distinct in all ages of secondary compared with primary forest. Order richness was lower in 5–15 years of secondary compared with primary forest. Our results suggest that in highly productive tropical wet forest, functional recovery of litter dynamics precedes recovery of decomposer community structure and biodiversity.

1 INTRODUCTION

Litterfall and litter decomposition are key ecosystem functions that release nutrients to forest soils and contribute a significant fraction of carbon (C) emissions into the atmosphere (Vitousek & Sanford, 1986; Berg & McClaugherty, 2014). Comparing the rate of these processes in successional forests to those in primary forests can be a useful indicator of recovery (Powers & Marín‐Spiotta, 2017; da Silva, Périco, Dalzochio, Santos, & Cajaiba, 2018) and informs modeling of global environmental change. Studies examining relationships between biodiversity and ecosystem functions in tropical secondary settings have shown that species richness of some taxonomic groups (mainly plants) and some ecosystem functions (biomass and productivity) are positively correlated (Lohbeck et al., 2012; Bu, Zang, & Ding, 2014; Lasky et al., 2014; Arroyo‐Rodriguez et al., 2017). Other lines of evidence suggest that reestablishment of ecosystem functions and biodiversity are unlikely to occur at similar rates (Connolly et al., 2013; Isbell et al., 2018). High levels of biodiversity in tropical wet forest lead to high functional redundancy, such that ecosystem processes like litterfall, decomposition, and nutrient release may recover ahead of aspects of community structure, including species diversity and the composition of plant and decomposer communities (e.g., Heemsbergen et al., 2004; Hooper et al., 2000; Wardle, 2006; Ostertag, Marín‐Spiotta, Silver, & Schulten, 2008; Huang, Ma, Niklaus, & Schmid, 2018). There have been very few measurements of litter processes in relation to biodiversity during succession in tropical wet forests. Existing studies focus on aboveground components (Ostertag et al., 2008; van der Putten et al., 2009), whereas linkages with belowground processes have received limited attention (Schilling, Waring, Schilling, & Powers, 2016). To address these gaps, we examined the rate and extent to which litter dynamics recovered following early stages of forest succession across a replicated chronosequence of tropical wet forest succession and quantified the influence of ground‐dwelling macro‐arthropods, a major belowground component of forest biodiversity associated with litter decomposition.

Although changes in aboveground biomass are the primary driver of biogeochemical processes in secondary tropical forests (Powers & Marín‐Spiotta, 2017), the rates of recovery of litter dynamics vary broadly across forest types. In secondary forests, litterfall rates have been found to return to, or even exceed, levels in primary forest within the first few decades (Ewel, 1976; Brown & Lugo, 1990; Barlow, Gardner, Ferreira, & Peres, 2007; Aryal, Jong, Ochoa‐Gaona, Mendoza‐Vega, & Esparza‐Olguin, 2015). Litter chemistry is also expected to change during forest succession due to differences in tree species composition, leaf longevity, nutrient acquisition, nutrient resorption, and defense strategy (Coley, 1983; Santiago & Wright, 2007; Cornwell et al., 2008; Wood, Lawrence, & Wells, 2011) although the evidence suggests few consistent trends (e.g., Dantas & Phillipson, 1989; Martius, Höfer, Garcia, Römbke, & Hanagarth, 2004; Huang et al., 2017). Rates of litter decay are also controlled by many factors at the ecosystem level, including leaf chemistry (Meentemeyer, 1978; Melillo, Aber, & Muratore, 1982; Swift & Anderson, 1989; Hobbie & Vitousek, 2000; Adair, Hobbie, & Hobbie, 2010), stand dynamics (Xuluc‐Tolosa, Vester, Ramirez‐Marcial, Castellanos‐Albores, & Lawrence, 2003; Welke & Hope, 2005; Remsburg & Turner, 2006), and shifts in species functional traits (Lohbeck, Poorter, Martínez‐Ramos, & Bongers, 2015). The variety in responses reported is not surprising as tropical forests are chemically heterogeneous (Townsend, Asner, & Cleveland, 2008), and measurements in successional forest are sparse relative to the complexity of the biome.

Soil and litter faunal communities strongly affect litter decomposition. Arthropods are known to enhance litter decomposition, particularly in the humid tropics (e.g., González & Seastedt, 2001; Hattenschwiler & Bretscher, 2001; Powers et al., 2009; Garcia‐Palacios, McKie, Handa, Frainer, & Hattenschwiler, 2016). Arthropod feeding activities in leaf litter accelerate litter mass loss (Wardle, Bonner, & Barker, 2002) and rates of nutrient cycling (Anderson, 1975; Carcamo, Abe, Prescott, Holl, & Chanway, 2000; De Deyn, Raaijmakers, & Putten, 2004; Meyer, Ostertag, & Cowie, 2011). These soil and litter communities are also the among most taxonomically diverse ecosystems on earth (Wolters, 2001; Decaens, Jimenez, Gioia, Measey, & Lavelle, 2006) constituting a significant proportion of tropical biodiversity. Although the diversity and density of these taxa play a large role in rates of litter decomposition in primary tropical forests (Lavelle, 1996; Wardle et al., 2002), less is known about recovery of community structure or role in litter decomposition during secondary succession.

We examined how a key ecosystem function (litter dynamics) and a key component of forest biodiversity related to litter decomposition (macro‐arthropods) recover during secondary succession using a replicated chronosequence on abandoned pastures in lowland tropical wet forest. We hypothesized that (a) rates of leaf litter decomposition and nutrient release are mediated by the presence of ground‐dwelling macro‐arthropods and (b) this ecosystem function would recover ahead of macro‐arthropod community composition and diversity. We assessed litter dynamics across the chronosequence by (a) measuring litterfall and litter chemistry; (b) testing the magnitude of macro‐arthropod effect on rates of decomposition and nutrient release from the two most distinct types of litter (primary and 5‐ to 15‐years‐old successional forest); and (c) quantifying macro‐arthropod abundance, diversity, and community composition.

2 METHODS

2.1 Study region

The study was conducted in 12 0.5‐ha lowland tropical wet forest sites representing a replicated chronosequence of forest secondary succession in southern Costa Rica near Piro Biological Station on the Osa Peninsula (8°240 N, 83°190 W) (Aguilar‐Arias, Ortiz‐Malavassi, Vílchez‐Alvarado, & Chazdon, 2012; Morales‐Salazar et al., 2012). The 12 sites ranged in elevation from 38 to 245m a.s.l., with mean annual precipitation ca. 3400–4000 mm and mean annual temperature of ~26°C. The regional climate can be divided into three seasons: dry season December to March; a moderate early wet season April to July; and heavy late wet season August to November. Soils in the region are classified as highly weathered, nutrient‐poor Ultisols and Inceptisols (Berrangé & Thorpe, 1988). The chronosequence included three replicates each of four age classes: 5–15; 15–30; 30–45 years; and primary forest. Sites used in this study were selected from a larger set of experimental forest plots based on similar site history and stand structure within age categories (see Morales‐Salazar et al., 2012 for details on site numbers 1, 2, 4–9, 12–14). Initial surveys showed that tree diversity, richness, and basal area increased during succession but were lower than in primary forest sites (Morales‐Salazar et al., 2012). Sites were separated by >300 m and are embedded within a complex matrix of agricultural land and remnant forests. In the center of each 0.5‐ha site, we established one 20 × 20 m subplot with all measurements conducted within those subplots.

2.2 Leaf litter decomposition

During peak litterfall in May 2011, we collected newly fallen, mixed species leaf litter from three 5‐ to 15‐years‐old secondary and three primary forests. The litter from each forest type was bulked and air‐dried for 3 days at ambient temperature. To evaluate litter decomposition, 5 ± 0.25 g of litter was placed in 12 × 12 cm litter bags with two different mesh treatments: litter bags with 1‐mm nylon mesh bottom and top (arthropods excluded) and litter bags with 1‐mm mesh on the bottom and 4‐cm mesh on the top (arthropods allowed). It was expected that most macro‐arthropods (>2 mm) would be excluded from the 1‐mm mesh bags, while meso‐ and micro‐fauna would move freely into both litter bag mesh treatments.

The decomposition experiment consisted of a full factorial litter bag mesh treatment (arthropods excluded vs. arthropods allowed), a litter source treatment (5‐ to 15‐years‐old secondary vs. primary forest) and four forest age categories (5–15; 15–30; 30–45 years; and primary forest). Litter bags were deployed at each of the 12 sites 2–5 June 2011 and collected after 30, 180, and 270 days. We deployed three replicate litterbags for each litter type × arthropod access within each of the three replicate forest stands at each successional stage (total of 432 litterbags). Immediately following placement in the field, an additional five bags of each litter type and mesh treatment were returned to the laboratory to establish mass loss during handling, dry mass relations, and for analysis of leaf chemistry.

Following collection, the litter was carefully washed to remove soil. Litter from each bag was oven‐dried to a constant weight at 60°C and weighed to the nearest 0.001 g. Subsamples of the litter in litterbags from the 30‐ and 180‐day collection, as well as samples of each litter type retained for initial nutrient content, were analyzed for total N, C, P, and K at Brookside Laboratories, New Bremen, OH (see Gavlak, Horneck, Miller, and Kotuby‐Amarcher (2003) for details on protocols). The samples from the 270‐day collection did not have enough remaining litter mass for nutrient analysis.

2.3 Arthropod sampling and identification

We collected macro‐arthropods (visible without magnification) from forest floor litter over two‐week periods in June 2011 (early wet season), October 2011 (late wet season), and February 2012 (dry season). Sampling was done at nine points systematically distributed throughout the 20 × 20 m area with different sampling points used at each seasonal collection. At each point, we placed a 50 × 50 cm metal frame on the ground and rapidly moved all litter inside the frame into a collecting bag. The litter samples from each plot were pooled, sieved through a 2‐cm mesh, and placed in Winkler extractors for 48 hr. All macro‐arthropods were quantified and identified to order or family level and broadly by functional group based on the most common feeding behaviors and physical characteristics of that group with assistance from A. Solis (INBio). Categories for functional groups include detritivores (saprophage), predator (carnivore), phytophage (herbivore), and mixed (all groups including omnivores). Identification to order or family was considered sufficient to give a broad overview of difference in richness and community structure since (a) the vast majority of tropical insects remain undescribed and (b) higher taxonomic levels are considered to act as reliable surrogates for patterns of species richness (Balmford, Jayasuriya, & Green, 1996; Báldi, 2003).

2.4 Litterfall, overhead cover, and soil nutrients

We quantified total annual fine litterfall by placing six 0.5‐m2 litter traps on 10‐m grid points within the 20 × 20 m plot at each site in June 2011 and collecting the litter twice monthly for one year. The litter was dried to a constant weight at 60°C and weighed to the nearest 0.001g. Bulked annual samples from each site were analyzed for percent N, P, and K following standard procedures at Brookside Laboratories, New Bremen, OH (Gavlak et al., 2003). Percent overstory cover was recorded in June 2011 (measured at 1 m above soil surface) using a spherical densiometer.

In June 2011, we collected nine 3‐cm‐diameter × 10‐cm‐deep soil cores from each site (108 total). Each soil core was homogenized and a portion immediately analyzed for extractable inorganic N. The remaining portions of the nine soil cores per site were bulked and analyzed for major nutrient elements. For extractable inorganic N, ~8 g fresh soil was added to 30 ml of 2 mol/L potassium chloride (KCl); then after 3 hr of repeated shaking and 1 hr of settling, solutions were filtered through 0.7‐lm Whatman glass fiber filters (GE Healthcare Bio‐Sciences) and frozen. Solutions were analyzed colorimetrically for ammonium (NH4+) using a Synergy HT microplate reader (BioTek) and nitrate (NO3−) and Alpkem autoanalyzer (OI Analytical) at University of Colorado Boulder. Bulked samples were analyzed for total C, N, K, C:N ratio, total P and other major cations following standard procedures at Brookside Laboratories, New Bremen, OH (see www.blinc.com/worksheet_pdf/SoilMethodologies.pdf and Gavlak et al., 2003).

Initial chemistry of secondary and primary forest litter was compared using an independent samples t test. We then ran three‐way analysis of variance (ANOVA) to determine whether litter decomposition rate (k) or the percentage of litter N, P, and K remaining after 180 days of decomposition varied significantly as a function of stand age, litterbag mesh size, litter type, or the interaction among any of these three predictor variables.

Arthropod abundances per season, total abundance, and abundance within orders were standardized to number of individuals per m2. We calculated individual sample‐based richness (Chao1) (Chao, 1987) using EstimateS (Colwell, 2013). We calculated Shannon index and Simpson's diversity index, D =[Σni(ni − 1)]/[N(N − 1)], where ni and N are the number of individuals at each site and total number of individual organisms of all species in each site. We initially compared arthropod abundance, measures of richness and diversity across seasons and forest types, and the interaction of these two variables using two‐way ANOVA. These preliminary analyses showed no significant interactions (p > .097 in all cases). Because the combined collections present a more complete view of arthropod richness and diversity over the time period of this study, we combined the seasonal data for all further analyses. Arthropod measures of abundance, richness and diversity, total annual fine litterfall, soil nutrients, and percent overstory cover were compared across the four ages of forest using one‐way ANOVA followed by Tukey's multiple comparison test or Kruskal–Wallis non‐parametric ANOVA when data did not meet assumptions of parametric statistics. We regressed total arthropod abundance and measures of richness against each individual soil and annual fine litterfall nutrients, and percent overstory cover (n = 12 in all cases). Differences in arthropod community composition based on abundance/m2 among the four habitat types were visualized with Non‐metric Multidimensional Scaling (NMS) followed by permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) based on Bray–Curtis dissimilarity. To evaluate the magnitude of the effect of arthropods on nutrient release, we estimated the difference in N, P, and K released from total annual litterfall during the first 180 days of decomposition using arthropods allowed and excluded scenarios for 5‐ to 15‐years‐old successional and primary forest.

Multivariate analyses were performed using PAST 3.05 statistical software (Hammer, Harper, & Ryan, 2001). All other statistical analyses were performed using the open‐source R software v. 2.13.0 (R Development Core Team). When necessary, data were transformed to meet assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variances. We consider ⍺ = 0.05 as significant and report means ± 1 SE throughout.

3 RESULTS

3.1 Leaf litter decomposition

Overall decomposition of litter was ~40% more rapid when macro‐arthropods were allowed access (F1,32 = 122.2, p = .001; Figure 1). Litter from the 5‐ to 15‐years‐old secondary forests decayed ~60% more rapidly than litter from primary forests (F1,32 = 237.5, p = .001; Figure 1). Forest age had no effect on rates of litter decomposition (F3,32 = 0.4, p = .750), and there were no significant interaction effects among arthropod access treatment, litter source (primary vs. secondary forest), and forest age (p > .1 for all potential interactions; see Table S1).

Primary forest litter had higher initial concentrations of N and P and lower initial C:N ratios than secondary forest litter (Table 1). Litter from primary forest retained a higher percentage of initial N, P, and K after 180 days of decomposition compared with secondary forest (Figure 2). Arthropod exclusion significantly increased the retention of N, P, and K in decomposing litter from both primary and secondary forests (Figure 2). There was no effect of forest age on the percentage of N, P, and K remaining in litter after 180 days of decomposition, and there were no significant interactions among litter sources, arthropod access, and forest age (p > .1 in all cases; Table S1).

3.2 Litter arthropods

We identified a total of 4,153 individual macro‐arthropods representing 29 orders. The most abundant taxa were Formicidae (Order Hymenoptera; 32.6%), Isopoda (16.8%), and Acari (13.4%). Taxa associated with mixed foraging strategies all groups including omnivores had the highest abundances (39.1%) followed by detritivores (22.6%), predators (8.3%), and phytophages (1.7%). The majority of arthropods were collected during the early wet season (64.7%) compared with the dry (16.6%) and late wet (18.7%) seasons (Figure 3).

Arthropod abundance increased with forest age and was significantly greater in primary forest compared with all ages of secondary forests (F3,8 = 20.3, p = .001) (Figure 3). The greater arthropod abundance in the primary forests was largely driven by high densities of beetles (Coleoptera) and ants (Formicidae) (Table 2). These taxa were associated with the mixed (including omnivores) functional group, and abundance within this functional group was also significantly greater in primary compared with all secondary forests (F3,8 = 11.6, p = .003) (Table 3). Arthropods in all other function groups identified in this study were found in similar densities across all forest ages (Table 3).

4 DISCUSSION

4.1 Overview

Our results suggest that components of an important ecosystem function, litter dynamics, recover more rapidly than a major component of ecosystem biodiversity, ground‐dwelling macro‐arthropods, during secondary succession in lowland, tropical wet forest. Across the chronosequence of forest succession, fine litterfall mass and litter nutrients recovered to primary forest levels within 30–45 years. Litter decomposition rates and soil nutrient levels showed no directional change over the chronosequence suggesting that either these functions are not affected by forest clearing or that they recover during the first 15 years. In contrast, abundance of macro‐arthropods increased slowly during forest succession but remained significantly lower than in primary forest. Order richness returned to primary forest levels within the first 30 years. However, the community composition of these arthropods remained markedly different, even after 45 years of succession. Despite the slow recovery of the arthropod communities, they exerted a uniform and strong influence on rates of litter mass loss and nutrient release across all forest ages. These results support our hypotheses that macro‐arthropods strongly influence litter decomposition and that this ecosystem function, along with other components of litter dynamics, reaches primary forest levels ahead of some aspects of biodiversity during tropical forest succession.

4.2 Litter decomposition

Litter decomposition occurred very rapidly in this lowland tropical wet forest with a mean litter residence time of ~270 days and >70 percent mass loss in the first nine months. Secondary forest litter decayed 60 percent more rapidly than primary forest litter although based on the initial chemistry, these differences were not as expected. The young secondary forest litter had comparatively lower initial N and P, and higher C:N, factors that are commonly associated with slower decay rates across multiple biomes (e.g., Gholz, Wedin, Smitherman, Harmon, & Parton, 2000; Berg & Laskowski, 2006; Parton et al., 2007; Szefer et al., 2017). However, litter nutrient content is not always a good predictor of decay rates, particularly in humid tropical forests where nutrient concentrations are spatially heterogeneous (Townsend et al., 2008) and leaf litter from diverse tree species has a large range of litter quality (Hattenschwiler & Jorgensen, 2010). Several other studies comparing decomposition of high‐ and low‐quality litter (Powers et al., 2009) as well as tropical primary and secondary forest litter show regionally specific results (Ewel, 1976; Xuluc‐Tolosa et al., 2003; Vasconcelos & Laurance, 2005). Relevant to our study, a review of patterns of litter decomposition in Amazonian lowland rain forests found little correlation between litter nutrients and decomposition; rather, decomposition rates were driven by non‐lignin, labile C compounds, and soil fauna (Haettenschwiler, Coq, Barantal, & Handa, 2011).

Arthropods strongly mediated rates of litter decomposition regardless of forest age or litter type. Decomposition occurred 35 percent more rapidly for secondary and 44 percent more rapidly for primary forest litter in treatments where arthropods had access. Soil and litter fauna exert substantial control over litter decomposition across the humid tropics (Seastedt, 1984; Wall et al., 2008; Powers et al., 2009; Chen et al., 2014; Stone, Shoo, Stork, Shieldon, & Catterall, 2019). Gonzales, Ley, Schmidt, Zou, and Seastedt (2001) showed that soil and litter fauna increased litter mass loss up to 67 percent in wet Neotropical forests but had no significant effect in dry tropical and temperate forests. Yang and Chen (2009) found that soil and litter fauna contribution to litter decomposition was greatest in mature tropical forest (41% increased mass loss) where litter C:N was highest compared with older secondary forest (20% increased mass loss) in China. Across the tropics, meso‐fauna exclusion can decrease decomposition rates by half, with faunal effects ranking higher and operating independently from other major explanatory variables including precipitation and litter quality (Powers et al., 2009).

Nutrient release from litter occurred in step with patterns in mass loss and arthropod access. Primary forest litter retained significantly higher percentages of initial N, P, and K compared with secondary forest litter, and overall nutrient loss was strongly magnified by the presence of arthropods. Mechanistically, arthropod feeding activities in leaf litter are known to considerably increase N mineralization as shown in both microcosm (Scheu, 1993; Kaneko & Salamanca, 1999; Hattenschwiler & Bretscher, 2001; Frouz, Lobinske, Kalcík, & Ali, 2008; Pieper & Weigmann, 2008) and in situ experiments (Gonzales et al., 2001). Our estimates of arthropod effect on litter nutrient release points to the magnitude of their importance in this process. The presence of macro‐arthropods increases mean N released by 53 percent and P release by 84 percent comprising 34.3 kg/ha and 1.4 kg/ha over the 6‐month decomposition period, respectively. By comparison, one of the few other estimates of macro‐arthropod influence on nutrient release from litterfall shows a 33.2 percent increase in N release (3.32 kg ha−1 year−1) in Hawaiian wet forest, an amount of N greater than annual inputs from other atmospheric and biological sources in this ecosystem (Meyer et al., 2011).

Across the chronosequence, forest age had no effect on rates of litter decomposition, arthropod effects, or rates of nutrient release. These results suggest that either these functions are not affected by forest clearing or that they recover quite quickly, within the first 5–15 years of succession. Ewel (1976) found that litter decomposition was significantly slower on sites cleared of vegetation compared with sites having 3 to 14 years of successional growth or primary forest (which all had similar decomposition rates). In tropical forest in SW China, litter mass loss increased along a gradient of forest disturbance from pasture to primary forest and the negative effect of arthropod exclusion increased with forest degradation (Paudel et al. 2015). In moist forest in Costa Rica, litter decomposition in forest fragments was slower compared with intact forest due to reduced influence of arthropods (Cuke & Srivastava, 2016).

4.3 Litterfall and litter and soil nutrients

Litterfall and litter N content increased rapidly during forest succession, reaching primary forest levels within 15–30 years. This finding agrees with other measurements in tropical wet forests showing that litterfall and litter chemical diversity can recover within the first two decades of succession (Ostertag et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2013). Litterfall was also exceptionally high supporting other evidence that the Osa Peninsula has some of the highest measured rates of net primary production in the tropics (Wanek et al. 2008, Taylor et al., 2017). Litterfall was 14.9 ± 0.9 Mg ha−1 year−1 in the primary forest and 9.7 ± 1.6 Mg ha−1 year−1 in the youngest secondary forest. By comparison, mean litterfall across multiple sites in old growth tropical forests in South America has been estimated to be ~ 8.6 ± 1.9 Mg ha−1 year−1 in primary and 8.01 ± 3.4 Mg ha−1 year−1 in secondary forests (Chave et al., 2010). Rainfall seasonality caused variation in litter inputs (Zhang, Yuan, Dong, & Liu, 2014) and suggests differences based on forest age. During the late wet season to dry season transition, litterfall was markedly lower in the younger secondary forests (5–15 and 15–30 years‐old) compared with older forests.

4.4 Recovery of macro‐arthropod communities

Forest age was an important driver of arthropod community structure. Arthropod abundance increased during secondary succession, but primary forests had significantly greater macro‐arthropod abundance than all of the secondary forests. We also found that order richness recovered more quickly than abundance, within the first 15–30 years of succession. A caveat is merited here: Our classification to order level is very general relative to the richness and diversity of arthropods and provides only a high‐level view of community structure and biodiversity. Several factors may explain the substantial differences in abundance and richness during forest secondary succession. First, the greater structural complexity of primary forests compared with young forests provides more niches and opportunities for exploiting environmental resources, thereby increasing species diversity (e.g., Tews et al., 2004). Structural complexity has been strongly correlated with arthropod richness and diversity in tropical forests (Gibson et al., 2011; Basset et al., 2015). Additional studies of arthropod communities in transformed tropical landscapes in Colombia (Cabra‐García, Bermúdez‐Rivas, Osorio, & Chacón, 2012), agricultural landscapes in Ethiopia (Lemessa, Hambäck, & Hylander, 2015), and restored forests in Costa Rica (Cole, Holl, Zahawi, Wickey, & Townsend, 2016) have reported strong associations between habitat heterogeneity and greater arthropod richness. Second, greater litter biomass, which we found to increase with forest age, has often been associated with greater arthropod abundance (Kaspari & Yanoviak, 2008; Sayer, Sutcliffe, Ross, & Tanner, 2010; Ashford et al., 2013).

Arthropod community composition in primary forest differed significantly from the composition in secondary successional forests. The slow recovery of the high‐level community structure after ~45 of succession is not overly surprising given that several meta‐analyses indicate that while species richness can recover relatively quickly, recovery of species composition takes centuries (Benayas, Newton, Diaz, & Bullock, 2009, Jones & Schmitz, 2009, Curran, Hellweg, & Beck, 2014, Rosendaal et al. 2019). However, abundances within broad functional groups were similar across forest ages with the exception of taxa classified as having mixed foraging strategies which were significantly higher in primary compared with all successional forests. Arthropod richness and abundance within these functional groups appear to recover more quickly than community composition, consistent with other studies across forest succession (Ottermanns et al., 2011; Curran et al., 2014; Cole et al., 2016). Although the impact of arthropods on litter decomposition was similar across all the ages of forest, arthropod abundance and community composition showed much slower trends of recovery. One possible explanation for this discrepancy is a high degree of functional redundancy within litter‐dwelling organisms. For example, our results show similar abundances of detritivores across all forest ages. A large proportion of litter arthropod is thought to have a low degree of specialization (Donoso, Johnston, & Kaspari, 2010; Makkonen et al., 2012). Such communities are often highly dynamic, and when one component is removed, another can compensate (Anderson & Swift, 1983).

Taken together, our results show that several key ecosystem functions, including litterfall and litter chemistry, can recover within the first two decades of forest secondary succession in highly productive and diverse tropical wet forest. Rates of litter decomposition and nutrient release were similar across all ages of forest suggesting that these functions can recover quite early during forest regrowth. We also found that ground‐dwelling macro‐arthropods exert very strong controls over litter decomposition and nutrient release but their abundance and richness remain lower and community composition greatly altered compared with primary forests even after 45 years of forest succession. This study underscores the promising role of secondary forests in recovery of key ecosystem functions in altered landscapes but also emphasizes the importance of protecting remaining old growth forest in order to conserve a full complement of biodiversity.

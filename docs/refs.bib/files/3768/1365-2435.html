    Summary

    1. Physiological and energetic mechanisms have been proposed to constrain body sizes of organisms along climatic gradients; however, these provide contrasting predictions. While Bergmann's rule predicts increases in body sizes in cooler climates resulting from physiological constraints, energy‐based community assembly rules suggest declines in the mean body size of species caused by increased extinction probabilities for large‐bodied species in low‐energy habitats.
    2. We tested these contrasting hypotheses by quantifying trait distributions in bee communities along a 3·6‐km elevational gradient at Mt. Kilimanjaro. Traditionally, intra‐ and interspecific trait shifts along environmental gradients have been investigated in isolation. However, a surge of theoretical approaches and studies on plants demonstrated that the explicit integration of trait variation among and within species can be essential for identifying the mechanisms that shape traits and related ecosystem functions along environmental gradients. We therefore studied variation in body size and related morphological traits at both the intra‐ and interspecific level.
    3. We found support for both physiological constraints and energy‐based community assembly rules as drivers of trait distribution in bee communities along elevational gradients, which, however, affected different levels of biotic organization, that is the population and community level. While the number of bee species with large body sizes declined with elevation, individuals within species became on average larger, resulting in contrasting trends in morphometric parameters at the community versus population level.
    4. Furthermore, body size within bee communities became less variable at higher elevations, largely as a result of a non‐random, directive loss of species, but paralleled by a decline in intraspecific variance, suggesting intensified filtering effects with increasing elevation. Similar patterns were found for other functional traits related to the foraging ecology of bees (tongue length, relative forewing length).
    5. We conclude that along climatic gradients both physiological and energetic constraints shape trait distributions of pollinators, but at different levels of biological organization.

    Introduction

    Understanding the causes of morphological variation in organisms along climatic gradients is one of the most enduring challenges in ecology (Bergmann 1847; Allen 1877; McNab 1971; Brown & Maurer 1989; Brown 1995; Ashton 2002) and essential for predicting the impact of climate changes on species, species interactions and ecosystem functions (Mouillot et al. 2013; Ohlberger 2013; Soudzilovskaia et al. 2013; Violle et al. 2014; Hoiss, Krauss & Steffan‐Dewenter 2015). Mechanisms regarding physiological constraints of species and mechanisms characterizing the availability and allocation of energy among species in local species assemblages are used to explain the distribution of organismic traits along gradients of latitude and elevation [e.g. Bergmann's rule (Bergmann 1847), Allen's rule (Allen 1877) or species‐energy theory (Brown & Maurer 1989) and character displacement (Brown & Wilson 1956; Winkelmann et al. 2014), respectively]. Even though these mechanisms are non‐exclusive, they make different predictions concerning trends in organismic traits (Fig. 1) and find conflicting evidence from the field (Blanckenhorn 2004; Feldman & Meiri 2014; Jellyman, McHugh & McIntosh 2014).

    Physiology‐based theories, such as those proposed to explain Bergmann's rule, assume that changes in the physical environment and physiological constraints determine the distribution and evolution of organisms, with consequences for morphological traits distribution. Larger average size with decreasing temperatures (Bergmann 1847) has been suggested to result, inter alia, from lower developmental temperatures in thermoconformers (Van Voorhies 1996), or increased heat conservation through lower surface‐to‐volume ratios in thermoregulators (Blackburn, Gaston & Loder 1999). The rule (and its exceptions) has been verified for numerous taxa, including invertebrates (Chown & Gaston 2010; Entling et al. 2010), along elevational and latitudinal gradients, and on different levels of biotic organization [i.e. within species (e.g. Ashton 2002; Blanckenhorn 2004) or across species (e.g. Cruz et al. 2005)]. Conformity to Bergmann's rule has been suggested to be pronounced in higher latitudes (McNab 1971; Rodríguez, Olalla‐Tárraga & Hawkins 2008), in species with large geographical ranges and latitudinal extent (Meiri, Yom‐Tov & Geffen 2007) and/or in large‐bodied species (Freckleton, Harvey & Pagel 2003), but generalizations of rule refinements might be hampered by the inflation of spatial and phylogenetic signals in most data (e.g. Ashton 2004).

    In contrast, community assembly rules considering the allocation of resources among species in size‐structured communities predict mean body sizes on average to decrease with elevation, due to higher extinction risks of large compared with small organisms in energy‐limited habitats (Brown & Maurer 1989). Everything else being equal, large‐bodied species have higher per capita energy requirements and consequently lower population sizes when having access to the same amount of resources (Damuth 1981; Brown et al. 2004). With extinction probabilities on average increasing with decreasing population sizes, in energy‐ and area‐restricted high elevational habitats, the extinction probability of large‐bodied species is predicted to be higher than that of small‐bodied species (Brown & Maurer 1989), resulting in an overall decline in the mean and variation in body sizes within species communities. Closely related to this ‘energy constraints hypothesis’ are the ‘habitat availability hypothesis’, which was taken to explain the graduate loss of large‐bodied mammals along elevational gradients in the neotropical Andes (Rodríguez, Olalla‐Tárraga & Hawkins 2008), and the ‘size dependence hypothesis’, which predicts a size threshold in thermoconformers above which the longer heating time of larger animals restricts their activity window so drastically that it exceeds all advantages related to large body sizes (e.g. reduced heat loss) (Olalla‐Tarraga, Rodriguez & Hawkins 2006; Olalla‐Tárraga & Rodríguez 2007).

    Although theories are not mutually exclusive, the predicted impact of physiological and energy‐based constraints on species trait distributions appears to be contradicting. However, the hierarchical organization of biodiversity may allow both mechanisms to simultaneously operate, that is by affecting trait distributions at the population or community level (Laughlin et al. 2012; Violle et al. 2012), which has been empirically studied in plant (Ackerly & Cornwell 2007; Jung et al. 2010; Albert et al. 2011), but rarely in animal communities (Muñoz, Wegener & Algar 2014; Peters et al. 2016).

    Here, we used the extensive elevational gradient on Mount Kilimanjaro to analyse how the mean and variation in body size of wild bees (Hymenoptera: Apoidea: Apiformes) shift along climatic gradients. Many bee species are thermoregulators (Stabentheiner & Kovac 2014). Therefore, adaptation tempering heat loss, for example an increase in body size, is expected to be under high selection pressure at high elevations (Hoiss et al. 2012; Peters et al. 2016). Simultaneously, large‐bodied bee species require more food resources for their offspring than small species (Müller et al. 2006), which could favour small‐bodied species when resources become a limiting factor. Indeed, large bee species have been shown to decline faster in body sizes, population sizes and distributions than small bee species over the last century, which has mostly been explained by food shortages and habitat degradation (Bartomeus et al. 2013; Scheper et al. 2014; Oliveira et al. 2016). Therefore, we analysed elevational changes in body size and two additional morphological traits strongly related to the foraging ecology of bees: glossa length (GL), a trait that could be associated with the floral diet spectrum, and forewing length, which is correlated with bees’ flying capacity at different air densities (Dillon, Frazier & Dudley 2006). The selected traits are of high ecological importance due to their connectedness to metabolism, resource use, dispersal capacity and pollination function, and have been shown to be sensitive to abiotic and biotic changes (Hoiss et al. 2012; Bartomeus et al. 2013; Peters et al. 2016).

    Materials and methods

    Study Site and Study Design

    The study was conducted on the southern and south‐eastern slopes of Mount Kilimanjaro (Tanzania, East Africa, 2°45′–3°25′S, 37°00′–37°43′E) between 2011 and 2012. We selected thirty‐five 0·25‐ha study sites covering natural and semi‐natural habitats in seven elevational belts ranging from savanna (871–1130 m a.s.l.), homegarden (1150–1840 m a.s.l.), submontane and lower montane forest (1620–2040 m a.s.l.), Ocotea forest (2260–2750 m a.s.l.), Podocarpus forest (2720–2800 m a.s.l.) and Erica forest (3510–3900 m a.s.l.) to the alpine Helichrysum zone (3880–4550 m a.s.l.) (Fig. 2). In each habitat type, we established five study sites in a way to achieve a within‐habitat type elevation gradient. Spatial distances among study sites were at the minimum 300 m and 97% of all distances between study site pairs were >2 km. Study sites were placed within larger areas of the respective habitat type in order to minimize edge effects. We recorded temperature and calculated elevational‐belt resource availability for each study site. See Appendix S1 (Supporting Information) for more details.

    Bee Sampling and Trait Measuring

    Bees were collected using UV‐bright coloured pan traps (Westphal et al. 2008). A total of eight pan trap clusters, each consisting of one blue, one yellow and one white pan, were installed along two 50‐m transects on each study site with a minimal distance of 15 m between clusters, and in two corners of the study site. We sampled bees in different vegetation heights, that is c. 35 cm (herbal layer) and c. 120 cm (shrub layer) above the ground. In forest sites, we installed three additional trap clusters in the canopy (up to a mid‐canopy layer of 25 m). However, for all sites, we used data of eight randomly chosen trap clusters per sampling round to guarantee equal sample intensity on all study sites. Pan traps were filled with water and a drop of liquid soap to break water surface tension, and were recollected after 48 h. Three sampling rounds were conducted summing up to a total of 24 pant trap clusters per study site. We pooled species of all sampling rounds per study site. All individuals were identified to genera level and then sorted to morphospecies by taxonomic experts [Connel Eardley (Eardley, Kuhlmann & Pauly 2010; Eardley & Urban 2010), Mary Gikungu (Gikungu et al. 2011)]. Especially for rarely collected species, we supplemented individuals of the same morphospecies that were caught during transect walks on the same study sites. Four study sites had to be excluded from statistical analyses, because we did not catch a single bee there (see Table S2 in Appendix S2). For all individuals, we measured the maximal distance between the wing bases [=intertegular distance (ITD)], which is a good measure for the body size of bees (Cane 1987), GL and relative forewing length (rFL) (=forewing length/ITD) by using a binocular microscope with a calibrated ocular micrometer. We measured all individuals on all study sites. However, in case of more than 10 individuals of a species at a study site, we randomly sampled the remaining individuals from a Gaussian probability distribution of trait values of the respective population, which was defined by the mean and standard deviation of the ten measured individuals (Albert et al. 2011).

    Statistical Analysis of Community Trait Mean Shifts

    Generalized least‐squares (gls) models were used to examine shifts of abundance‐weighted and species‐weighted trait means along the elevational gradient. Whereas the former is calculated by averaging trait values across all individuals within a community, and is therefore more heavily affected by dominant species, the latter is calculated by averaging across the species within a community independent of their abundance. Because results remained qualitatively similar, here we report analyses employing abundance‐weighted mean estimates. In case of ITD and rFL, trait means were ln‐transformed to improve normality of residuals. To control for spatial autocorrelation, we added different types of spatial correlation functions (‘exponential’, ‘gaussian’, ‘rationale quadratic’, ‘spherical’) to our trait models (Zuur et al. 2009) as implemented in the r packages ‘nlme’ (Pinheiro et al. 2016) and ‘ncf’ (Bjornstad 2016). We extracted Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) values from each model and selected the correlation function with the lowest AIC value. We compared the selected models to the original model without spatial correlation structure using the AIC and by calculating likelihood ratio tests. As models incorporating data on the spatial correlation structure were generally better supported by the data (see Table S3 in Appendix S3), we consistently accounted for spatial autocorrelation by adding a Gaussian correlation function (i.e. the best supported correlation function) to gls models. Identical analyses were used to investigate the shifts of maximal and minimal community trait values along elevation.

    Statistical Analysis of Intraspecific Trait Shifts

    The impact of elevation on the mean trait values within species was analysed with generalized linear mixed‐effects models with Gaussian error distribution (fixed effect: elevation, crossed random effects: species, study site) (Bates, Maechler & Bolker 2013). Species that occurred only on a single study site were excluded from analyses. P‐values were calculated based on Satterthwaite's approximations (summary function within the r‐package lmerTest (Kuznetsova, Brockhoff & Christensen, 2016). Alternatively, we used linear mixed‐effects models with species and elevation as fixed effect variables and study site as single random effect, but results were not different from those of the generalized mixed‐effects models and are thus not presented here.

    We quantified spatial and phylogenetic autocorrelation on model residuals employing Moran's I tests [phylogenetic topology based on taxonomic information (Michener 2007)]. Results of these tests suggested that the inclusion of study site and species as random factors adequately controlled for spatial and phylogenetic autocorrelation (not shown), and we therefore always included these random factors in intraspecific analyses (Pagel 1999; Paradis, Claude & Strimmer 2004; Paradis 2012).

    Statistical Analysis of Trait Variances

    An anova‐based approach was used to separate within‐ and between‐group variance components (Violle et al. 2012). Trait measures of singleton species were excluded from these analyses as these were assumed to be transient species and intraspecific variation cannot be calculated.     We checked for spatial autocorrelation, following the protocol described above. Generalized least‐squares models that did not include spatial random structures had significantly lower AIC values than gls models that included spatial random structures. Therefore, we used ordinary linear models to investigate the impact of elevation on standardized inter‐ and intraspecific variances.

    We also used linear mixed‐effects models with intraspecific variances of single traits (averaged for each species on a study site level) as response variable, elevation as a predictor variable and species as random term. With this, we tested whether the effects of elevation on intraspecific variances are caused by variance declines within species (only in this case, the potential effect of elevation on intraspecific variance would stay significant), or by shifts in species composition (as, e.g., lowland communities might hold more species with higher phenotypic variability than highland communities).

    All statistical analyses were conducted with the statistics program r (R Development Core Team 2014).

    Results

    In total, we caught 3991 bee individuals and between one and 17 morphospecies per study site. Species richness declined with elevation (glm with a poisson error distribution, z = −9·01, P < 0·001). Species trait means shifted conditionally on the level of biological organization.

    Community Trait Shifts

    At the community level, body size decreased with increasing elevation (Table 1, Fig. 3a). The decrease in mean body size was shaped by the directed loss of large‐bodied species from communities along the elevational (Figs 2c and 3a) and not by intraspecific declines of body size. The decline of mean body size in bee communities was strongly correlated with the decline in the availability of floral resources from lower to higher elevations (Table 1). Besides body size, GL decreased with increasing elevation (Table 1, Fig. 3b). In contrast, rFLs increased with elevation (Table 1, Fig. 3c).

    In addition to trait means, trait variance also changed with elevation: interspecific variation in all traits decreased with increasing elevation (σ2 ITD: R2 = 0·45, d.f. = 29, t = −4·90, P < 0·001; σ2 GL: R2 = 0·53, d.f. = 29, t = −5·76, P < 0·001; rFL: R2 = 0·13, d.f. = 29, t = −2·06, P = 0·048) (Fig. 4a–c). In case of ITD and GL, this pattern was mainly explained by an increasing absence of large‐bodied (Figs 2c and 3a) and long‐tongued species (Fig. 3b) along the elevational gradient.

    Intraspecific Trait Shifts

    On average, individuals within species were larger in colder habitats, as predicted by physiology‐based theory (e.g. Bergmann's rule) (Table 1, Fig. 3d). This pattern stayed consistent, when using mean annual temperature as explaining variable (Table 1). GLs decreased with elevation within species (Table 1, Fig. 3e), while rFLs did not significantly change with elevation (Table 1, Fig. 3f).

    Similar to interspecific trait variance, intraspecific trait variance of body size and GL decreased with increasing elevation (α2 ITD: R2 = 0.13, d.f. = 29, t = −2.07, P = 0.047; σ2 GL: R2 = 0.28, d.f. = 29, t = −3.35, P = 0.002) (Figs 4d, 4e). This pattern was shaped by changes in the species composition of communities (i.e. in lower elevations more species with broad intraspecific variances occurred than in higher elevations), but not by the decline of intraspecific variation within species (lme, ITD: denDF = 58, F = 0·43, P = 0·516; GL: denDF = 57, F = 0·01, P = 0·915). Intraspecific variation in rFLs increased with increasing elevation (Fig. 4f) (σ2 rFL: R2 = 0·27, d.f. = 29, t = 3·26, P = 0·003), which was at least partly explained by the increase in intraspecific trait variances within single species along the elevational gradient (lme, rFL, denDF = 58, F = 7·37, P = 0·009).

    Discussion

    The distribution of morphological traits in wild bee communities along elevational gradients at Mount Kilimanjaro gives evidence for contrasting trends in the size and shape of organisms at different levels of biotic organization, that is for individuals within populations and for species within communities. The depicted patterns are in agreement with major theories explaining the variation in organisms’ body size either by physiological constrains, that is Bergmann's rule, or by community assembly rules based on differential energy requirements among size‐structured populations of co‐occurring species.

    We found that the body size of individuals within bee species followed on average predictions of Bergmann's rule, that is individuals were overall larger in cooler environments. Due to reduced surface‐to‐volume ratios, larger organisms lose less heat through conduction than smaller organisms and are thus energetically favoured. This explanation for Bergmann's rule in endotherms appears also to hold true for semi‐homoeothermic bees (Bishop & Armbruster 1999; Peat et al. 2005), which elevate their thoracic flying temperature above ambient temperature by muscular activity in cold environments, and are subject to heat stress in warm environments (Heinrich 1974). In cold environments, larger bees are capable to forage at lower temperatures and forage over a more broad thermal range, which may explain the benefit of larger body sizes with elevational gradient (Peters et al. 2016). Alternatively, physical constraints operating on cellular and molecular levels, or adaptive plasticity mechanisms that account for the benefits and costs of prolonged developmental times and delayed maturity that are associated with larger body sizes in cool environments, might explain the detected pattern (Angilletta, Steury & Sears 2004).

    Although on average bee species followed Bergmann's rule, species responses to elevation varied, with many species showing no significant body size trend along the elevational gradient. Even though some of the variation might be due to the lack of statistical power for single species, we assume that species evolved, apart from the increase in body size, different behavioural and/or physiological strategies, and morphological adaptations, respectively (Peters et al. 2016), to cope with lower temperatures which we did not consider in this study.

    While at the population level, bees became on average larger in cool environments, large‐bodied species increasingly disappeared from high elevations, as predicted by energy‐based community assembly rules. This was reflected in a decrease in the abundance‐weighted community trait mean and paralleled by a decline of the interspecific trait variance. Due to the limited area on mountain tops offering less space for flower resources (Körner 2000), and lower resource harvesting rates under cool temperatures (Classen et al. 2015), the energy supply for bees is sharply restricted towards the highlands. This aggravates the maintenance of more and larger populations in high elevations and increases the risk of local species extinction. As their absolute energy requirements are higher (Kleiber 1932; West, Brown & Enquist 1997), larger organisms can only sustain smaller population sizes when having access to the same amount of resources (Damuth 1987). Under energy limitations, populations of large organisms are therefore more prone to local extinction (Smith et al. 2010). Our data suggest that the advantages of being large (higher dispersal capacities, higher foraging ranges, reduced heat loss) cannot balance the increased energy requirements on a species level here. Interestingly, the pattern of an increasing loss of large species along elevational gradients is not ubiquitous: a recent study from the German Alps revealed opposite trends, that is increasing bee body sizes on a community level with increasing elevations (Hoiss et al. 2012; Peters et al. 2016). We suggest that the isolated nature of Mount Kilimanjaro, which is related to a steep decline of high‐elevated land area, is mainly responsible for the stronger effects of energy constraints on bee body sizes found in our study. In the mountain chain of the Alps, alpine habitats are distributed over extensive land area and resource availability may not be limiting here (Bertuzzo et al. 2016). Although we cannot fully exclude other potential causes for the observed stepwise exclusion of large species from low to high elevations, we regard energetic constraints operating at the species level to be the most likely explanation. Declines in the variation of available nesting sites may be another important filter of bee body sizes (potentially favouring small soil‐nesting bees in the rocky high‐elevation habitats); however, in this case, we would expect the highest variation in body size in the complex mid‐elevation tropical rain forests and not in the open, less complex grassland systems. Besides the gradual loss of large species from species pools, patterns in relative wing and GLs support the view that energy limitations play a major role in structuring bee communities along elevational gradients at Mt. Kilimanjaro. For example, increase in rFLs maintains flight capacities under dropping air density (Dillon, Frazier & Dudley 2006) or might be associated with larger foraging ranges, counteracting energetic constraints. Similarly, the decrease in GLs could signal that bees tend to become less specialized in their food choice (Miller‐Struttmann et al. 2015) or that they become adapted to the dominance of short‐tubed composites in high elevations, allowing optimal foraging in energy‐limited habitats.

    The opposing intra‐ and interspecific trends observed for bees at Mt. Kilimanjaro exemplify that different mechanisms may simultaneously shape the traits of organisms along climatic gradients by affecting different levels of biotic organization (Vellend et al. 2014). This contradicts the idea that a common mechanism links the size structure and thermal energy use across multiple biological scales (Cornwell & Ackerly 2009; Vellend et al. 2014). While the intraspecific increase in body size along the elevational gradient can be well explained by selection for larger individuals in higher elevations or phenotypic plasticity, the mechanisms causing a gradual loss of large species are linked to emergent species‐level properties such as population size or elevational range, that is traits not reducible to a single organism. The hierarchical structure of diversity provides the potential for identifying multiple mechanisms to operate in shaping the distribution of traits along environmental gradients (Jablonski 2008). This potential is largely neglected in community ecology and animal physiology due to their focus on either inter‐ or intraspecific trait variation, respectively, and may explain some of the conflicting results in the past (Shelomi 2012).

    Interspecific trait variance decreased with increasing elevation, as expected under directive, trait‐based species loss and species turnover. For the traits body size and GL, interspecific trait variance declines were paralleled by declines in intraspecific trait variance. Weaker energetic and physiological constraints and higher spatial and temporal habitat heterogeneity might allow multiple phenotypes to coexist in the warmer lowlands (Tews et al. 2004). However, along the elevational gradient, phenotypes at the extreme sides of the trait density distributions are more and more filtered due to increasingly intensified physiological or energetic constraints. The decrease in intraspecific trait variances signals that such filtering seems to have a higher impact on trait dynamics than, for example, competition. If competition would drive trait evolution, one could expect an increase in intraspecific trait variance (‘character release’) in high elevations, due to the successive extinction of competing species or due to lowered temperature‐dependent species interaction rates (Lomolino et al. 2012).

    The investigation of trait variances is of ecological relevance, as trait distribution patterns hint at species’ and communities’ capacity to react to environmental changes. High trait variance within species or communities increases the chance that at least part of the population or community is pre‐adapted to new environmental conditions (Kühsel & Blüthgen 2015). Our results may therefore imply that highland communities and associated ecosystem functions are more sensitive towards environmental changes than lowland communities. However, such interpretation needs to be handled with care, as other responses to environmental change (e.g. species range shifts, altered species interaction rates) might obliterate the benefits of phenotypic variance within populations and communities. Therefore, it is not surprising that empirical studies often fail to link trait variances and resilience (Angilletta, Steury & Sears 2004; Macheriotou et al. 2015).

    Conclusions

    Morphological traits of bee communities at Mt. Kilimanjaro showed parallel, but also contrasting patterns at the community and population level giving support to both physiological‐ and energy‐based constraints structuring trait expression simultaneously at different levels of biological organization. Further work will have to determine the transferability to other taxa and mountain regions, but the parallel analysis of population and community traits may sharpen our understanding about trait‐related species and community responses to climatic change.

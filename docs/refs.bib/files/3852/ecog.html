    Abstract

    Declines or mid‐elevation peaks in invertebrate diversity with elevation are often attributed to climate and geometric constraints. However, vegetation structure may also drive diversity patterns, especially for tree‐dwelling species, via its effects on microhabitat use and competitive interactions. Here we investigate these effects on the diversity and community structure of tree‐nesting ants over elevation. We exhaustively sampled ant nests in 1254 trees within continuous plots of primary rainforest at low (200 m a.s.l.), mid (900 m a.s.l.) and high (1800 m a.s.l.) elevation in Papua New Guinea. Ant diversity, nest abundance and tree occupancy peaked at mid‐elevation. Although host tree diversity also peaked at mid‐elevation, there was low specialisation of ant species to tree species at all elevations. Mid‐elevation trees hosted more species, more nests and a greater diversity of nest types than trees of a similar size at low or high elevation. Tree size and nest microhabitat use were the strongest predictors of species composition, explaining twice as much of the variability in the communities than elevation. At mid to high elevation there were proportionally fewer large nests than in the lowlands, with an increase in smaller nests in live hollow twigs and epiphytes. There was high species turnover between elevations, and between trees within elevations. Species co‐occurrence patterns within trees differed with tree size, and with elevation. In large trees species tended to co‐occur at random at low and high elevation, but co‐occurred more often than expected by chance at mid elevation, indicating an elevational shift in competitive interactions. We conclude that the more extreme diurnal temperatures at higher elevations, combined with increased epiphyte availability, drive ants to nest in more insulated microhabitats. This results in smaller colony sizes and a decrease in interspecific competition, thereby boosting species co‐existence at mid elevation.

    Introduction

    Examining changes in species diversity and composition along elevation gradients can generate powerful insights into the ecological processes that shape communities. For most taxa, species richness declines monotonically or peaks at mid elevation, and turnover of species composition with elevation is well documented (Rahbek 1995, 2005). Climate is a key driver of these large‐scale patterns and the elevational distribution of species is largely determined by their ability to thrive in the abiotic environment (Machac et al. 2011). This ability is also important at the local scale, where organisms use microhabitats which have suitable microclimates. However, the use of microhabitats may also be mediated by biotic factors such as vegetation structure and competitive interactions (Rosenzweig 1992, Szewczyk and McCain 2019).

    Structurally diverse plant communities have more niches, which can increase the capacity of animals to co‐exist and thereby increase diversity (Williams et al. 2002, Potts et al. 2004). Nowhere is this more evident than in tropical rainforests. There, trees of different sizes, adorned with epiphytes, aerial deadwood and trapped soil and leaf litter, provide a complex habitat with niches for many of the estimated six million species of arthropods living in tropical forests (Hamilton et al. 2013). In rainforest trees, few arthropods are as abundant and ecologically important as the ants (Dejean et al. 2007). Arboreal ant species have diverse nesting habits (Powell et al. 2011, Klimes 2017), and the structural diversity provided by trees of different sizes could increase ant species richness by increasing the number and variety of nesting niches. Nest microhabitats are exploited from the tree trunk to the tree crown, including cavities excavated by other insects, aerial deadwood or ant‐built structures made of carton or leaves (Klimes 2017). Some ants nest inside the hollow stems of myrmecophytic trees and the chambers of epiphytic bulb plants (Chomicki and Renner 2015, Plowman et al. 2017), and others opportunistically nest in and under epiphytes and their root systems (Ellwood et al. 2002). The presence of such epiphytes in tree canopies is influenced by vegetation structure and climate (Kreft et al. 2004, Fayle et al. 2009), and can significantly increase arboreal ant richness (Stuntz et al. 2003, Yanoviak et al. 2003). Nest microhabitat use in arboreal ants has been investigated in the lowlands (Powell et al. 2011, Klimes 2017), but its importance to ant community assembly has not been explored at other elevations.

    Ants are widely used in elevational studies, as they are species rich but relatively easy to identify, and they are sensitive to changes in temperature (Warren and Chick 2013). Behavioural shifts in microhabitat use can create important refugia from the climatic extremes associated with high elevation (Sunday et al. 2014). In New Guinea forests, ants have been recorded from the lowlands to 2200 m a.s.l. in the rainforest understorey (Orivel et al. 2018), but it is unclear whether they shift their nesting behaviours with elevation. There is evidence from Mexican coffee landscapes that ant occupancy of dry hollow twigs peaks at mid‐elevation (Gillette et al. 2015), suggesting that microhabitat use could change with elevation in response to climate. This has not been investigated in more structurally complex rainforest habitats, which host a more diverse arboreal ant fauna. Moreover, while elevational richness patterns of tropical ground‐dwelling ants are relatively well understood (Longino and Branstetter 2019), due to the impracticality of sampling remote rainforest canopies, these patterns have not been explored for canopy ant communities.

    In addition to climate, competition and the influence of numerically and behaviourally dominant species are thought to be one of the most important factors shaping large‐scale arboreal ant community composition (Blüthgen and Stork 2007). According to ant mosaic theory (Majer 1976), the most numerous and aggressive species will exclude each other from their territories in canopies (e.g. Oecophylla smaragdina and Anonychomyrma gilberti in Daintree, Australia; Blüthgen et al. 2004), and this in turn may shape the spatial distribution of less dominant species. However, as different sampling methods obtain different results (Floren and Linsenmair 2000, Fayle et al. 2013, Dejean et al. 2015), the general patterns and drivers of species co‐existence in whole communities are still under debate. We argue that we should complement our understanding of these large‐scale community patterns by studying how species co‐exist within trees. Dissecting whole trees and all their various nesting microhabitats in whole forest plots (Klimes et al. 2012) can provide a rare glimpse into how vegetation structure can shape the diversity and composition of whole communities of tree‐dwelling ant species.

    Here we evaluate the role of elevation, tree size and nest microhabitat use on the diversity, composition and structure of arboreal ant communities. Whole communities of tree nesting ants were censused by dissecting all trees in 0.2 ha plots of primary rainforests at 200, 900 and 1800 m a.s.l. in Papua New Guinea (PNG), to examine 1) how elevation and tree size affect species richness and turnover, nest abundance and nest diversity, 2) the relative contribution of elevation, tree size and nest microhabitats to variability in ant species composition and 3) if species co‐occurrence within trees is non‐random and if this changes with elevation. Based on previous studies in the region (Plowman et al. 2017, Orivel et al. 2018), we expected tree nesting ant richness and abundance to decline with elevation. Furthermore, we expected nest microhabitat use to shift from having a more diverse range of nest types in the lowlands, to more insulated nests at high elevation due to harsher climatic conditions. We also expected elevational differences in the vegetation structure to be a major driver of species richness and composition, due to differences in the carrying capacity and nest microhabitat diversity of trees of different sizes and changes in competitive interactions. Finally, we predicted that ant species would be over‐dispersed at low elevation due to competition, and increasingly under‐dispersed towards high elevation due to the increasing importance of environmental constraints (Machac et al. 2011).

    Material and methods

    Study sites and sampling method

    The study was conducted in lowland, mid‐elevation and high elevation primary rainforest at three different sites in north‐eastern PNG (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Fig. A1), as part of a wider study on rainforest food webs (Klimes et al. 2012, Whitfeld et al. 2012, Redmond et al. 2019). These sites were near Mt Wilhelm (4509 m), in the Central Range of PNG. There is low seasonality, an average annual rainfall of 3288 mm in lowlands, rising to 4400 mm at the timberline, and mean annual temperature decreases of 0.54°C per 100 m increase in elevation (Sam et al. 2019). We sampled lowland forest near Wanang village (~200 m a.s.l.; 05°140S, 145°110E), mid‐elevation forest near Numba village, Bismarck Range (~900 m a.s.l.; 5°43′18″S, 145°16′2″E) and high elevation forest near Yawan village, Saruwaged Range (~1800 m a.s.l.; 6°09′46″S, 146°50′22″E). All plots were at least 50 yr old growth and surrounding forests were relatively pristine due to the distance from large human populations. At all sites the local communities practice shifting slash and burn agriculture, so we worked in plots allocated by the community leaders as the next food gardens. The study thus did not contribute to net deforestation. Community members were employed to collect insect samples from felled trees, giving a net income greater than what would have been earned from garden produce alone. This additional income and the active participation of local people in our research helped to establish and protect nearby forest conservation areas (Wanang Conservation Area and YUS Conservation Area; Novotny 2010).

    Overall, we sampled 1254 trees for ant nests in a total of 1 ha of forest (two lowland plots, one mid‐elevation plot and two high elevation plots, 0.2 ha each; Table 1). High elevation plots were ~80 m apart. In the lowlands a continuous 0.4 ha (100 × 40 m) plot was censused for all ants between October 2006 and November 2007 (Klimes 2017). To allow comparison of this larger plot with our smaller plots at mid and high elevation (each 0.2 ha measuring 44.5 × 44.5 m), we divided the 0.4 ha plot to two plots of 0.2 ha (50 × 40 m). One mid‐elevation plot was felled in May–August 2013. Two high elevation plots were felled in September–November 2010 and March–April 2012.

    In all plots, trees ≥ 5 cm diameter at breast height (DBH) were felled and exhaustively sampled for arboreal ants and their nests. We recorded tree species, tree height and DBH. Immediately after felling, each tree was searched intensively for ant nests from the tree base to the tree crown. From every nest, representative adults (i.e. wingless workers, winged sexual castes when present, and queens) and brood (larvae and pupae) were collected and stored in ethanol. Destructive sampling ensured the discovery of even cryptic nests in interstitial microhabitats and aerial soil that would be otherwise under‐sampled (Yanoviak et al. 2003). We considered ant nests associated with epiphytes (e.g. inside myrmecophytic bulb plants such as Hydnophytum and Myrmecodia, or under the roots of mosses, orchids and ferns) as present on the supporting tree. Nests were categorized into 12 types based on nest locations (microhabitat sites), where they were found within each tree: ‘on trunk base’, ‘under epiphyte roots’, ‘cavity/gallery in trunk’, ‘carton/soil/silk on leaves’, ‘under bark’, ‘on bark’, ‘in/under liana’, ‘myrmecophytic epiphyte’, ‘cavity/gallery in live branch’, ‘cavity/gallery in live twig’, ‘in dead/rotten branch’ and ‘in dead/rotten twig’ (Klimes et al. 2012, Mottl et al. 2019). We recorded the distance in metres of the nest from ground level (nest height hereafter), and estimated nest size on an ordinal scale (1: ≤ 100 workers, 2: 100–500 workers, 3: 500–1000 workers 4: > 1000 workers). In trees where there were multiple nests of the same species within the same continuous habitat (e.g. liana, branch), likely belonging to the same colony, we considered nests closer than 1 m to each other as the same nest. For further details see Klimes et al. (2015), Klimes (2017) and Volf et al. (2019).

    Ant identification

    We identified ants to species/morphospecies (hereafter species) using our reference collections and those of the Museum of Comparative Zoology, as well as online databases (<www.newguineants.org>), available keys (Bolton 1994, Andersen 2000, Schmidt and Shattuck 2014), and the assistance of specialist taxonomists (see Acknowledgements). Species boundaries were defined by morphological differences, and for more taxonomically difficult genera, DNA barcoding (cytochrome oxidase CO1, 659 bp; see Klimes et al. 2012 and Plowman et al. 2017). Ant vouchers are deposited at the Institute of Entomology, Biology Centre of Czech Academy of Sciences, České Budějovice. A full species list with accession numbers and reference codes is available in Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A1.

    Analysis

    How do elevation and tree size affect species richness and turnover, nest abundance and nest diversity?

    For each elevation we created species‐by‐tree abundance matrices, and input this data to iNext to construct species accumulation curves (q = 0, datatype = ‘incidence_raw’, 999 iterations; R Core Team, Hsieh et al. 2016). To assess species turnover we quantified species dissimilarity for all pairs of trees within each elevation with the Sørensen dissimilarity index. The index ranges from 0 to 1, where zero indicates all species are shared, and 1 indicates no common species (vegdist in the R package vegan; Oksanen et al. 2007). We then tested if Sørensen dissimilarity values (n = 63 407) differed with elevation using GLM with quasibinomial distribution (lme4). In addition, we used the index to calculate turnover among individual plots within and across elevations, plotting the difference between elevations using the Euler diagram app <http://eulerr.co/> (based on R package eulerr).

    We used generalised linear mixed models (zero inflation models; R package glmmTMB) to test the effect of elevation, tree size and their interaction on species richness (n ant species per tree), nest abundance (n nests per tree) and nest diversity (n nest types per tree). Independent variables were modelled individually, and together with their interactions (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A8–A10). The significance of each variable was determined by likelihood ratio testing (e.g. anova (mod0, mod1, test = chisq)).

    What is the relative contribution of elevation, tree size and nest microhabitats to variability in ant species composition?

    The effect of nest microhabitats (nest types and nest height) and tree size on ant species composition at each elevation was quantified with canonical correspondence analysis (CCA) and variation partitioning in Canoco v5.0, with trees as sampling units. Type I error was minimised by analysing only species and nest types that were present in at least three trees in each forest. This reduced the dataset to 98% of occupied trees (n = 754) and 61% of ant species (n = 102 of 168; Table 1). To identify the factors with a significant effect on community composition, we conducted ‘Interactive Forward Selection’ analysis with the following variables: elevation (as a continuous variable), DBH (diameter at breast height, i.e. 1.3 m), nest height (distance in m from the ground) and nest types (12 categories as defined above, each as a categorical variable; Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A2). Where multiple nests occurred in a tree, nest height was calculated as mean and nest types scored as their proportion per tree. We then quantified the relative contribution of elevation versus the other predictors that were significant in the above analysis (i.e. DBH, nest height and nest type levels) to variability in ant species composition with variation partitioning (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A3). In addition to testing this across elevations, we also tested the relative contribution of these factors to ant species composition within elevation (999 permutations, p adj. < 0.05; Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A4). For all these analyses, we present adjusted explained variation and adjusted p‐value, as recommended for datasets with many species and variables (Šmilauer and Lepš 2014). We did not test for the effect of tree species in the CCA due to the high proportion of rare species. However, previous analyses of the lowland forest found a very limited effect of tree species on ant communities (Klimes 2017), and to assess this at other elevations we calculated the network specialisation (h2′) of ants and host trees at each elevation with the R package bipartite (Dormann et al. 2009).

    Is species co‐occurrence within trees non‐random and does this change with elevation?

    To investigate the role of competition in structuring community composition with elevation we used the checkerboard score method (C‐score; Stone and Roberts 1990, Gotelli 2000), a statistic that quantifies the distribution of species and checks for non‐randomness in their occurrences. We tested, using the presence/absence of ant species within trees, whether species co‐occur non‐randomly. First, to account for the effect of tree size on species richness, we split the DBH data for each elevation into three quantiles, in order to categorise trees into three size classes of similar sample size (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A5). The DBH ranges for small, medium and large trees respectively were as follows: low elevation (5–8, 9–13, 13–100 cm), mid‐elevation (5–8, 8.1–14, 14–68 cm), high elevation (5–11, 12–19, 19–96 cm). We then calculated C‐scores and standardised effect sizes (SES) of the trees in each plot and size class (i.e. all trees, small trees, medium trees and large trees) with the function cooc_null_model in the R package EcoSimR. Negative SES indicates species clustering (co‐occurring more often than expected), positive SES indicates overdispersion (co‐occurring less often than expected).

    We ran two C‐score models with different assumptions, a ‘Fixed’ model and a ‘Relaxed’ model (sim9 and sim2 algorithms respectively, 50 000 randomisations). For both models all singleton ant species were excluded to reduce risk of type I error. The ‘Fixed’ model was constrained to have the same total number of occurrences of a species, and the same number of species per tree, i.e. row sums and column sums were fixed. The effects of interspecific interactions would be obscured by including trees hosting a single species, so we excluded them, reducing our sample size by 30–80% (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A5). However, in the ‘Relaxed’ model, i.e. row sums fixed but occurrence in columns equiprobable, we included trees with only one ant species since our assumption was to allow trees to contain the maximum number of species observed in its size category. This assumes that the carrying capacity of each tree can be greater than the observed number of species, e.g. if the presence of one species leads to the competitive exclusion of additional species. Since allowing a fully randomised species richness in trees of all sizes is not biologically realistic, we did not conduct the ‘Relaxed’ model for the ‘all trees’ category. Finally, to assess if there were any negative or positive associations between pairs of species (Ellwood et al. 2016), we ran an additional C‐score model (sim2 algorithm, 100 000 randomisations), using the ten most abundant species at each elevation, without splitting the trees by size category. In pairwise tests we applied Šidák correction in significance testing to minimise type I error associated with multiple comparisons.

    Results

    How do elevation and tree size affect species richness and turnover, nest abundance and nest diversity?

    We collected 168 species of ants from a total of 2245 nests found in 769 trees of the 1254 trees sampled. More than a third of all ant species were singletons or doubletons. We found a strong mid‐elevation peak for nest abundance and species richness (Fig. 1, Table 1). Since our data was from a full census, not random sampling, the maximum observed values of the accumulation curves (Fig. 1A) are the total richness of ants collected from all trees in the sample area. The predicted values for a sample size of 600 trees (90, 120 and 24 species for low, mid and high elevation respectively) indicate we would still have found significant differences in species richness with elevation if we had sampled an equal number of trees at each elevation. There were more ant species per 0.2 ha at mid‐elevation (102) than at low (65 ± 7) and high (17.5 ± 3.5) elevations (Fig. 1A). A similar pattern was found for species richness per tree (Fig. 1B). At all elevations, species turnover was high between trees (mean Sørensen dissimilarity = 0.82–0.92), with significantly lower turnover at high elevations (Fig. 1C, Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A6, A7). Only one species, Anonychomyrma minuta, was found at all three sites (Fig. 2A), and it strongly varied in abundance and nest size with elevation (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Fig. A2, Table A1). Sørensen dissimilarity calculated between pairs of 0.2 ha plots were as follows: low‐mid = 0.62 ± 0.02, mid‐high = 0.97 ± 0.001, low‐high = 0.97 ± 0.003. Between plots dissimilarity was low at both low and high elevation (0.31 and 0.25 respectively).

    There was a significant effect of DBH, elevation and their interaction on species richness (GLMM; p < 0.001, Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A8, Fig. 3), a significant effect of DBH and elevation on nest abundance (p < 0.001, Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A9) and a significant effect of DBH and elevation on nest diversity, with an interaction at high elevation (p < 0.01, Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A10). Ant nests were twice as abundant at mid‐elevation than at low elevation, and three times more than at high elevation (Table 1). Tree occupancy and mean number of nests per tree was highest at mid‐elevation (86%, 3.2), followed by low (69%, 1.9) and high (43%, 1.1) elevations. The proportion of large nests (with > 1000 workers) was greatest at low elevation, while mid and high elevation had an increased proportion of smaller nests (< 500 workers, Chi‐square test; χ2 = 297.64, df = 6, p < 0.001; Supplementary material Appendix 1 Fig. A2).

    What is the relative contribution of elevation, tree size and nest microhabitats to variability in ant species composition?

    Our explanatory variables accounted for 6.5% of all variation in ant communities on individual trees and the efficiency of the variability possible to explain in our dataset was high, i.e. 90% in the first axis (Fig. 2B). Of the explained fraction, elevation accounted for 26.4%, nest height 5.3%, tree size 3.4% and nest types 59.1%. The most important nesting types ‘carton/soil/silk on leaves’, ‘carton/soil on bark’ and ‘under epiphyte roots’ accounted for 14.9, 13.7 and 11.0%.

    The use of these nest types differed with elevation. Nests on leaves and on bark were common in lowland forest but rare at mid and high elevation (Fig. 4). In highland and mid‐elevation forest most nests were under epiphytes, and inside live twigs. Nests under epiphytes tended to have small colonies of Ponerinae and Myrmicinae species. The numerically dominant species of Anonychomyrma, Camponotus and Colobopsis typically nested in live twigs and live branches. Ants also used these nest types in lowland forest, but in the lowlands the number of nests was more evenly distributed among the categories (Fig. 4).

    Nest microhabitats (i.e. nest types and nest height), explained most of the species variability in all elevations when compared to effect of tree size (individual effect > 79%, Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A4). The individual effect of tree size was much smaller in the lowlands than at higher elevations (~2% versus ~17%), because in the lowlands the effect of tree size was more correlated with nest microhabitats. However, tree size positively correlated with species richness, number of nests per tree and nest type diversity at all elevations (GLMM; df = 8, p < 0.001), and for a given tree size, trees had more nests, nest types and species at mid‐elevation (Fig. 3). While the analysis did not include the effect of tree species, network analysis indicated low specialisation of ant species to tree species at all elevations (h2′ < 0.33). Mid‐elevation had the lowest h2′ (0.19) despite having the greatest tree and ant species richness (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Fig. A3, Table 1).

    Is species co‐occurrence within trees non‐random and does this change with elevation?

    ‘Fixed’ model: we found that only the categories ‘All trees’ at mid‐elevation, ‘Large trees’ in one plot, and ‘Small trees’ in the other plot at low elevation had significantly non‐random co‐occurrence patterns (SES > 2.0). At mid‐elevation, species in the community as whole were overdispersed (all trees SES = 2.95), but within different tree size classes species co‐occurred at random (SES between 1 and −1). At low elevation, species also co‐occurred randomly in the whole community, but there was a larger range of SES values within size categories, tending from more random to marginally overdispersed (SES range −0.49 to 2.24; Fig. 5A). At high elevation, species in medium and large trees co‐occurred randomly (SES between 1 and −1). C‐scores could not be calculated for the small tree category due to the low sample size after excluding trees with only one species (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Table A5).

    ‘Relaxed’ model: co‐occurrence patterns were less random when we included trees with only one species and relaxed our assumptions about tree capacity. In small trees at all elevations, species co‐occurred less often than expected at random. In medium sized trees, species co‐occurrence was overdispersed at low elevation, marginally so at mid‐elevation and in one plot of high elevation. In large trees species co‐occurrence tended to be random at low and high elevations, but at mid‐elevation species co‐occurred in them significantly more often than random (Fig. 5B).

    Species pairs: our co‐occurrence model using pairs of the ten most abundant species at each elevation found non‐random associations only at low elevation, where Anonychomyrma cf. scrutator was negatively associated with Crematogaster polita and Colobopsis vitrea (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Fig. A4).

    Discussion

    Our study has demonstrated that vegetation structure and its differential use by ant species can contribute to the elevational patterns in tree‐nesting ant richness, abundance and community structure. We comprehensively sampled all nests in all trees in forest plots, an approach previously used to explore the effects of forest succession, but not a forest elevational gradient (Klimes et al. 2012, Mottl et al. 2019). We found a strong mid‐elevation peak in nest abundance and species richness, and there, in the largest trees species co‐occurred more than expected by chance. While our study had limited plot replication and only one replicate at mid‐elevation, predicted species richness values indicate that even with increased sampling effort elevational differences in richness would remain significant. Our three forest sites differed rather little in tree density and their sizes, so the effect was not driven simply by large changes in vegetation structure with elevation, unlike in successional gradients (Klimes et al. 2012). Indeed the significant interaction of DBH and elevation on species richness and nest diversity implies that species richness does not increase consistently with tree size, and is likely complicated by elevational changes in climate. While there are as yet no comparable studies of arboreal ant richness with elevation, our findings contrast with other studies from the region which found a linear decline of understorey ant species richness, and a much weaker mid‐elevation peak in terrestrial ant species richness (Colwell et al. 2016, Plowman et al. 2017, Orivel et al. 2018). This in itself suggests that the rainforest canopy environment has unique effects on biodiversity and assembly of communities with elevation.

    Within elevation, the high turnover of ant species observed between trees may partly be driven by the high proportion of rare species, even in trees hosting common or dominant species, and the lower turnover at high elevation is likely explained by the smaller species pool. The relatively small overlap in ant species among elevations in our study (Fig. 2) was unsurprising given that tropical mountains are rich in endemic species, and most tropical ants species have narrow elevational ranges (Brühl et al. 1999, Merckx et al. 2015). Since most species on Mt Wilhelm range less than 500 m in elevation (Colwell et al. 2016, Orivel et al. 2018), it was not possible to assess if individual species change their nest microhabitat use with elevation.

    However, elevation, tree size and nest microhabitats (i.e. nest types and their vertical height) strongly influenced community composition, perhaps contributing to elevational diversity patterns. Our study reinforces previous findings from the lowlands: tree size, nest height and nest types accounted for more of the variation in ant communities than elevation itself. We observed low specificity of ant species to trees species (Supplementary material Appendix 1 Fig. A3), thus the increase in tree diversity cannot explain the increased ant diversity in mid‐elevational canopies. While other studies have used tree species richness as surrogate of habitat heterogeneity (Ribas et al. 2003), this may not capture the variability in forest structure and the resources they provide. As with birds (Macarthur and Macarthur 1961), it seems that the vertical structure of the vegetation and the diversity and turnover of nesting types is more important in structuring arboreal ant diversity than plant species composition itself (Klimes et al. 2012, Klimes 2017). Use of epiphytes and live hollow twigs as nest microhabitats increased with elevation, while carton nests on bark and foliage, and nests in dead wood were less important with elevation.

    Nest microhabitat availability could be responsible for the observed patterns: the diversity and abundance of epiphytes also peaks at mid‐elevation due to increased humidity in the condensation zone or orographic cloud layer (Krömer et al. 2005, Cardelús et al. 2006). In lowland rainforest, epiphytic bryophytes are typically restricted to thick horizontal branches in the inner canopy, whereas in lower montane forest, like our mid‐elevation site, bryophyte load can reach up to 50% coverage (Frahm and Gradstein 1991). Both epiphytic cover on trees and the species richness of epiphytes also increase towards the higher elevations at Mt Wilhelm (Colwell et al. 2016). While epiphyte availability likely contributed to an increase in their use as nesting habitats, it remains unclear if the same is true for live hollow twigs. To resolve this, one would need an independent measure of available cavities (i.e. including the empty branches), that was not feasible to conduct in the scope of our study. It could be that at high elevations there is less dead wood available, and as a result more ants nested in live stems. But an alternative explanation is that turnover in the use of nest types also had a behavioural component, and live twigs provide better insulation from the cooler climate, and a refuge to farm sap‐sucking scale insects for honeydew, reducing the reliance on external food‐sources.

    As well as an elevation climate gradient, arboreal ants experience considerable variability of temperature within elevation. There is a strong vertical stratification of climate from the ground to the canopy (Kaspari et al. 2015), and these vertical differences in temperature could mediate the competitive effects of species limited by their thermal tolerances (Wittman et al. 2010). This would explain why nest height was an important factor in explaining community composition, and why ant co‐occurrence tended to be more overdispersed in whole plots than for tree size categories (Fig. 5A). At mid‐elevation, increased humidity from clouds could make high canopy habitats more stable in climate than in the lowlands that have more extreme diurnal fluctuations (Nowrouzi et al. 2016). These elevational shifts in vertical climate are thought to explain the increased arboreality of tropical frogs and some butterfly taxa with elevation (Pyrcz and Wojtusiak 2002, Scheffers et al. 2013), and could act as an additional mechanism for increasing ant richness at mid‐elevation.

    In addition, co‐occurrence patterns within trees suggested a decreased role of competitive exclusion at mid and high elevation. Although separating trees by size resulted in smaller matrices, our novel approach to C‐score analysis controlled for the effect of tree size on species richness. As our three forest sites did not differ much in size distribution (mean and max dbh) and stem density per plot, our size classes are well comparable between elevations. Our ‘Fixed’ co‐occurrence model, which limited tree capacity to the observed number of species occurrences, revealed that co‐occurrence patterns in our observed communities, for the most part, were random. There were two interesting exceptions: the first was at mid‐elevation, where species across ‘All trees’ were overdispersed, while within tree size categories they co‐occurred at random. This suggests that at mid‐elevation there was some segregation of species by tree size, but that within tree size classes, communities were not strongly structured by competitive interactions. This may also have been true to some extent at high elevation, although the effect size did not pass our criteria for statistical significance. This segregation of species could reflect competitive exclusion, but that may also interact with climate and ant thermal tolerance since the temperatures experiences by ants in high canopy trees can vastly exceed those in the lower strata (Kaspari et al. 2015).

    The second exception was at low elevation, where ‘Large trees’ in one plot had overdispersed species while the other tree size classes were random. One explanation for this could be the presence of highly territorial dominant species in large trees. Pair‐wise co‐occurrence analysis revealed that at low elevation the dominant species Anonychomyrma cf. scrutator had negative associations with the dominant species Crematogaster polita and the species Colobopsis vitrea which was positively associated with C. polita. Although this positive association was not statistically significant, the two species are very often found foraging together and the relationship is most likely a parabiosis similar to previously described Crematogaster–Camponotus associations in Borneo (Menzel et al. 2008).

    Our relaxed model suggested a stronger effect of interspecific competitive exclusion on smaller trees, where species were more overdispersed, likely due to reduced resource availability and space. At mid‐elevation however, species co‐occurrence on large trees was significantly clustered. This indicates the importance of nest site availability, since we did not obtain this result when single species trees were excluded. Decreased nest size at mid‐elevation (i.e. smaller colonies), may also facilitate the co‐existence of species in the largest trees. Yet, even though nest size decreased most at the highest elevation, we did not see species aggregation; in one plot species tended to be overdispersed, the other randomly distributed. We speculate that rather than evidence of direct competition, this is due to tighter microhabitat requirements to withstand the climate at high elevation, thus reducing the carrying capacity of trees without suitable nesting sites.

    The influence of dominant species decreased with elevation. Dominant species in the region usually have territories larger than our 0.2 ha plots (e.g. Crematogaster polita in the lowlands; Klimes unpubl.), so our dataset cannot elucidate large‐scale spatial patterns driven by dominants. However, the modular carton nests built by C. polita, or the woven leaf nests of Oecophylla smaragdina are perhaps less suited to high elevation habitats, since diurnal fluctuations in climate are more extreme than in low elevations, with potential consequences for brood growth and development in less insulated nests. Dominants may also be disproportionately affected due to their preferences for sugar feeding, as elevation may have negative effects on their external homopteran symbionts. Mapping the territories of dominant species at a larger scale would be very informative, however it is clear from our study that a diverse community can still co‐exist with dominant species, by taking advantage of the complex arboreal habitat and differing tree sizes to escape competition.

    Conclusions

    This study provides the first strong evidence of a mid‐elevation peak in diversity and nesting abundance of tree‐dwelling ants. We show that tree size and nest microhabitat use, likely interact with climatic changes to drive these diversity patterns. The effects of variability in tree sizes and canopy microhabitats have no parallel on the ground, and limited effect in the understorey, so can potentially explain differences in diversity patterns between the strata.

    The differences in species co‐occurrence patterns between elevations highlighted the reduced importance of dominant species at higher elevations, suggesting that competitive interactions are not the key ecological mechanism structuring montane canopy communities. As temperatures cool and humidity increases with elevation, ant communities largely retreat to more insulated nests inside live twigs and under epiphytes. This results in smaller colonies, thereby reducing competition and boosting species co‐existence.

    Importantly, co‐occurrence patterns also differed between tree size classes suggesting another key mechanism by which species can avoid interspecific competition, vertical stratification. Thus, having the full range of tree strata can further promote co‐existence in diverse communities. This has strong conservation implications: modern logging practices typically target the largest trees for extraction, but larger trees support more species, potentially distinct from the species found on smaller trees. These findings, along with the high levels of endemism along the elevational gradient, further demonstrate the conservation value of tropical montane rainforests.

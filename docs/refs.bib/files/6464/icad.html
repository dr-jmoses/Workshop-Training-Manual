    Abstract

    1. Despite their ubiquity, ant assemblages in the sub‐boreal forests of British Columbia, Canada, are largely unknown.
    2. Ant assemblages and area densities of species colonies were characterised in lodgepole pine‐leading forests through strip‐plot hand sampling of coarse woody debris (CWD), with species richness supplementation through pitfall trapping and mini‐Winkler sampling of forest floor litter, over five seral ages.
    3. Seventeen species of ants were identified across all seral ages. Ant assemblage dissimilarity (non‐metric multidimensional scaling) determined from CWD (i.e. ordination separation between sites) decreased through early seral ages then began to increase in later seral ages.
    4. Despite a species richness curve superficially consistent with competitive exclusion developing in later seral ages, the overall decline in all species abundances with advancing seral age, the low colony densities, and the persistence of only an ecologically subordinate species in the oldest seral age, argued against competitive exclusion and for a thermally based hypothesis of evolved species tolerance.
    5. Successional facilitation and examples of the role of individual life history characteristics were also noted with seral progression.

    Introduction

    Ants are known to be a thermophilic group (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990), which is evident by a decline in species richness with increasing latitude (Kusnezov, 1957). Although a recent taxonomic catalogue provides a global total of 11 477 extant species of ants (Bolton et al., 2006), only some 82 species are reported for British Columbia (BC), Canada (Higgins & Lindgren, 2009). Despite this relative paucity of species, ants are very common throughout most of BC, especially in the south (Naumann et al., 1999), and are rare only under cool and closed forest canopies (Higgins & Lindgren, 2006).

    Global forest ecosystems are increasingly fragmented by timber harvesting (Gustafson et al., 2000) that may now be interacting with climate change (Li et al., 2013). In west‐central BC, timber harvesting (typically clear‐cut) is creating a mosaic landscape with a greater variety of patch sizes at varying seral ages than has occurred historically (Steventon, 2002). The BC Ministry of the Environment report on climate change (MOE, 2007) indicates that summer temperatures close to the focal point of this study, have been increasing 0.14 °C per decade since 1950. This suggests an increase in almost 1 °C in mean summer temperatures since 1950, and the report estimates additional temperature increases of 2–3 °C by 2050. For a thermophilic group, such change is important and understanding the response of ant communities in forests of differing seral age (and likely temperature regimes) may be illustrative of responses more broadly. Examinations of ant assemblages with seral changes have been rare in Canada. The only example followed ant succession in the lichen‐spruce forests of Quebec (Lafleur et al., 2006). The first objective of this study, therefore, was to examine ant assemblages at differing seral ages in the sub‐boreal forests of BC. The process by which these seral assemblages formed may then be considered by taking into account what is known of the autecology of these species (Vepsäläinen & Pisarski, 1982).

    Our second objective was to follow the successional changes observationally in the ant assemblages of varying seral age to determine if the rise of dominant species is associated with a reduction in subdominant and/or subordinate species (Cerdá et al., 2013), indicative of competitive exclusion. In forests ecologically related to those of this study, competitive dominance was determined to be a critical factor structuring ant assemblages, particularly in late successional ages where polydomous territorial species were reported to strongly influence overall ant assemblages (Savolainen & Vepsäläinen, 1988; Punttila et al., 1994). This was supported in subsequent work where dominant ant species were associated with an overall decrease in ant species richness (Savolainen & Vepsäläinen, 1989; Savolainen et al., 1989; Punttila et al., 1996). Other research, however, has questioned the significance of competition. Neuvonen et al. (2012) did not see any impact on other ant species by red wood ants (five species within Formica rufa group) in two seral ages of forest in Finland. A lack of structure imposed by competition was also reported in a large scale study in tropical forest (Baccaro et al., 2012). Studies where dominant species of ants have been excluded have also raised questions about an almost exclusive role for competition in structuring ant assemblages. Gibb and Johansson (2011) removed the dominant territorial ant species, Formica aquilonia, from 80‐ to 100‐year‐old forests in Sweden and found no effect the following year on the ant assemblage, although it should be noted that an effect may not be evident in a short time period. In a related study, however, Gibb (2011) found that the removal of this species from three differing seral ages did have an impact in younger ages suggesting a role for competition at earlier ages.

    Methods

    We used records from Houston Forest Products (HFP, Houston, BC, Canada), to identify non‐harvested and post‐harvested sites (clear‐cut) (five seral ages: 2–3, 8–10, 13–15, 23–25 year, and non‐harvested) in the sub‐boreal spruce biogeoclimatic zone and moist‐cold subzone, variant 2 (SBSmc2) (Meidinger & Pojar, 1991). These stands were lodgepole pine‐leading (Pinus contorta Dougl. var. latifolia Engelm). Common shrubs in these stands are black huckleberry (Vaccinium membrananceum Douglas ex. Torr.), Sitka alder [Alnus viridis ssp. sinuata (Chiax) D.C.], and black twinberry (Lonicera involucrata Richardson Banks ex. Spreng.); common herbs include kinnikinnick [Arctostaphylos uva‐ursi (L.) Spreng.] and five leaf bramble (Rubus pedatus Banks & Sol. ex. Lowe); and common mosses include red stemmed feathermoss [Pleurozium schreberi (Brid.) Mitt.] and Cladina lichens (Cladina spp. P. Browne) (DeLong et al., 1993).

    We identified a minimum of three replicate sites from each seral age within a 100‐km radius of Houston, BC (54°24′N, 126°40′W) (Fig. 2). For non‐harvested sites and 8–10 year post‐harvest sites, four replicate sites were established. Replicate sites varied in distance from 5 to 110 km from each other. Following identification, we positioned a single 1‐ha sampling plot within each site such that natural and artificial boundaries (e.g. streams, road, slopes) were at least 50 m from plot edges.

    Our study was conducted between 2003 and 2005 with all sampling for ants occurring between late June and mid‐August. Non‐harvested plots and 8–10 year post‐harvest plots were sampled in 2003, 13–15 and 23–25 year post‐harvest plots in 2004, and 2–3 year post‐harvest plots in 2005. Sampling emphasised hatchet assisted searching of coarse woody debris (CWD) in strip plots (unit area) for two purposes. First, the ant fauna appeared to be heavily dependent upon CWD for nesting. Second, it permitted estimates of absolute colony densities of each species on sites. Pitfall trapping and mini‐Winkler sampling of forest floor litter were also performed in early (June/July) and late (July/August) summer to improve sampling for total species richness (Fig. 1). CWD was comprised of both downed woody debris (DWD) and stumps. Downed woody debris was defined as pieces of downed wood of any length with a large end diameter ≥10 cm within the strip plot (either cut or simply the largest diameter where it intercepted the strip‐plot boundary). The threshold for inclusion of a stump was a cut end diameter of 10 cm. Downed woody debris and stumps were sampled on each 1‐ha plot within two sets of 4 × 25‐m sampling strips centred on random locations (Fig. 1). Each strip was positioned at right angles to each other and shared a 10‐m offset centre to avoid overlap. For sampling purposes, each strip was halved resulting in 16 sampling units of 4 × 12.5‐m (50 m2) per site (Fig. 1). Data were also analysed using plot (a 1‐ha plot per site) as sampling unit. As the number of plot replicates was low (n = 3 or 4 plots per seral age), test power decreased with this approach but approximately 50% of significant findings from the analysis based on the 50‐m² sampling units remained significant at that scale and the overall pattern and interpretation of the data were not affected. As a result, we only present here the results obtained using the 50‐m² sampling units.

    Within each sampling strip, we opened CWD by hand and/or hatchet to locate and sample ant colonies. When a species was found within a piece of woody debris only once, a simple localised cluster of 15 or more ants was considered a colony. Where multiple clusters of the same species were located within the same piece of woody debris, the wood was examined for connecting tunnels to determine if they were distinct colonies. Statistical differences for each of the five most frequently captured species as well as all species pooled (i.e. total colonies), between seral ages, were assessed by a Kruskal–Wallis test (α = 0.05). Where this was significant, post hoc pairwise Mann–Whitney tests were performed with a Bonferroni adjustment (α = 0.01) to account for multiple tests (STATA 9.2, StataCorp LP, College Station, TX, USA, © 1985–2007).

    We used Nordlander pitfall traps constructed from 237 ml plastic cups with a lid diameter of 8 cm (Nordlander, 1987; Higgins & Lindgren, 2012a) to which approximately 80 ml of 25% propylene glycol/water was added. Four 80 m long transects were randomly positioned within each site, constrained only by avoiding overlapping transects. Pitfall traps were positioned every 20 m along each transect (i.e. five pitfall traps per transect, 20 pitfall traps per site) (Fig. 1). These were left in place to sample ants for 2 weeks after which samples were collected and the pitfalls closed but left in place. Approximately 1 month later the pitfalls were reactivated for a second 2 week sampling period. Data from the two samples taken from each pitfall were pooled.

    We performed forest floor litter sampling for ants using mini‐Winkler litter sample bag extraction (Agosti et al., 2000). We collected two sets of 10 randomly placed surface litter samples (0.5 m2) with at least 1 month between sets (i.e. 20 litter samples per site) (Fig. 1). Surface litter was scraped loose by hand and trowel then sifted through a screen with an approximately 2.5 cm mesh. Large debris was broken up by hand, as extensively as possible, and also sifted. We then placed each sample in a mini‐Winkler extraction bag, and the bags were hung for approximately 48 h in a utility shed at ambient temperatures to protect them from the weather and from being physically disturbed (Agosti et al., 2000).

    We followed ant species nomenclature as revised by Bolton et al. (2006) except where more recent revisions have been made. We identified ants to species using the keys of Wheeler and Wheeler (1963), Francoeur (1973), Wheeler and Wheeler (1986), Naumann et al. (1999), and Hansen and Klotz (2005). Ants of the genus Myrmica were identified using an unpublished key provided by A. Francoeur (Centre de données sur la biodiversité du Québec, Chicoutimi, Québec). Voucher specimens will be deposited at the Royal British Columbia Museum.

    We monitored site temperatures using iButton® 1 Wire Thermocron® DS1921G dataloggers (Maxim Technologies, Dallas, TX, USA). During the summer of 2005, one iButton temperature datalogger was randomly positioned within each 1‐ha plot approximately 1 cm underneath the surface forest duff layer. We chose this depth to best measure the temperature which epigaeic foraging ants would be experiencing, while blocking direct insolation that might result in high radiative temperature readings. Temperatures were recorded on the dataloggers (time synchronised) every 30 min between 8 July and 18 August. Means were then calculated for every time‐synchronised temperature record across replicates within each seral age. The resulting mean data were then sine modelled to derive daily temperature and amplitude parameters for each seral age (SigmaPlot 11.0, Systat Software Inc., San Jose, CA, USA, © 2008; non‐linear regression, sine, 4 parameters). These parameters were then used with the standard error and relevant t‐statistic (d.f. = 2, 1987, α = 0.05) to determine 95% confidence intervals, allowing parameters to be assessed for significant differences.

    We compared the ordination of ant assemblages in study sites by non‐metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) using PC‐ORD (Version 5, McCune & Mefford, 1999). We used only CWD samples for this analysis (total colonies of each species in both strip plots (800 m2)) per site as these data were founded on identified ant colonies (absolute abundances) rather than captured individuals in pitfall or litter samples (relative abundances) (Higgins & Lindgren, 2012a). This also eliminated the need for data transformations that are often applied to the latter data sets. NMDS separations, using the Bray–Curtis distance measure, were performed three times independently to assess for local minima. Each separation was run 250 times and compared to 250 runs of a randomised data set (Monte Carlo). Following an analysis of the scree plot associated with this run the appropriate solution dimension was determined. This solution dimension was then iterated 500 times to improve the separation. PC‐ORD measures stress by calculating Kruskal's stress formula one, then rescaling between 0 and 100. The axes correlation coefficient (r) for each of the five most common species was determined.

    Results

    Overall, 17 species of ants were collected when all sampling techniques were pooled across all sites (Table 1). Of these, 14 were found during CWD sampling, 15 by pitfall trapping, and 11 by mini‐Winkler soil/litter sampling. No ants were observed nesting in soil by incidental observation or during random litter collection for mini‐Winkler extraction. The species missed by each technique were uncommon except for Myrmica fracticornis Forel which was commonly captured by pitfall trapping and mini‐Winkler litter extractions, but not found within CWD (Table 1). One species, Formica dakotensis Emery, captured by a pitfall trap, is a new record for BC although its presence is not surprising as it is known to occur in northwestern Alberta (Wu & Wong, 1987) and in the Yukon (Francoeur, 1997).

    A two‐dimensional solution for the NMDS ordination was appropriate for this data set given observations of the initial solution scree plot. NMDS mean stress (Kruskal stress formula 1 multiplied by 100) over the initial 250 runs was 12.583 as compared to the equivalent randomised Monte Carlo mean stress of 17.165 (P = 0.0040). Using the two‐dimensional solution derived from the first run, the data set was reiterated 500 times to achieve a final stress value of 8.363, which is considered a ‘good’ to ‘fair’ NMDS solution (McCune & Grace, 2002). Axis 1 accounted for 48% of the proportion of variance while axis 2 accounted for 36.9%. All the five most common ant species demonstrated a negative correlation coefficient on Axis 1 [Leptothorax muscorum (Nylander) (r = −0.771), Myrmica alaskensis Wheeler (r = −0.704), Camponotus herculeanus (L.) (r = −0.636), Formica neorufibarbis Emery (r = −0.494) and F. aserva Forel (r = −0.962)] and Axis 2 [(L. muscorum (r = −0.593), M. alaskensis (r = −0.073), C. herculeanus (r = −0.258), F. neorufibarbis (r = −0.308), and F. aserva (r = −0.454)].

    Clear ordinal separation between seral ages, with the exception of 8–10 and 13–15 year post‐harvest sites, was observed from NMDS of species distributions between study sites, derived from the hand sampling of CWD in strip plots (Fig. 3). The dissimilarity among replicates within each seral age class, as determined from the plotted variability between sites in the NMDS analysis, decreased between the earliest (i.e. 2–3 year post‐harvest) and mid‐seral age of this study (13–15 year post‐harvest) and then increased in the two later seral ages (Fig. 3).

    Datalogger failures occurred in one of the three 2–3 year post‐harvest plots, as well as in two of the four non‐harvested plots. All daily mean temperatures and mean temperature amplitudes differed significantly between seral ages (Table 2). The highest daily mean soil temperatures were recorded in the earliest seral age (2–3 year post‐harvest) with temperatures just over 14 °C between July and August of 2005. Mean temperatures declined steadily with seral age and were below 10 °C in non‐harvested plots. Although daily mean soil temperatures dropped approximately 5 °C over the five seral ages, daily mean temperature amplitudes dropped by approximately 6 °C, indicating increasingly stable, but lower temperatures with increasing seral age.

    Ant colony abundance in CWD varied significantly with seral age (Kruskal–Wallis; P = 0.0001) (Fig. 4a) with each seral age significantly differing from adjacent ages (Mann–Witney; α = 0.01; Bonferroni adjustment, n = 5). CWD volume, however, did not differ significantly between ages (Mixed model anova; P = 0.05) (Fig. 4b). Ant colonies were most abundant in the 13–15 year post‐harvest plots where colony densities reached approximately 10 per 50 m2. Ant colonies were rare in non‐harvested plots, with approximately 0.3 colonies per 50 m2. In one non‐harvested plot, no ant colonies were located.

    Both the relative and absolute abundances of the five most common ant species (Fig. 5) in CWD varied with seral age. In the earliest seral age (2–3 year post‐harvest), L. muscorum was the most common species but the relative abundance of this species declined after the 13–15 year post‐harvest seral age. Leptothorax muscorum colony densities differed significantly (P < 0.01) between all seral ages except between 8–10 and 13–15 year post‐harvest (P = 0.53). That decline is contrasted by an increase in the relative abundance of M. alaskensis, which was the most common species in non‐harvested plots. Myrmica alaskensis colony densities differed significantly between 2–3 and 8–10 year post‐harvest sites (P = 0.0001) and 23–25 years post‐harvest and non‐harvested sites (P < 0.0001). These two species, combined, always accounted for greater than 60% of identified nests in CWD within each seral age. In the last seral age (non‐harvested plots), M. alaskensis alone was responsible for over 90% of the total colonies identified (Fig. 5).

    Discussion

    Ordination of ant assemblages

    In viewing the NMDS figure (Fig. 3) in the context of specific ant assemblage data (Fig. 5), total ant colony abundances (Fig. 4a), and negative correlation coefficients for all the five most common species, it is possible to interpret Axis 1 as reflecting an increase in ant colony abundance towards the left as early seral development establishes. This pattern likely also reflects the increasing ant species richness (Table 3). Axis 2 may reflect a shift in relative species abundance, with L. muscorum, a pioneer species, relatively abundant in assemblages towards the bottom and M. alaskensis, a more common mature forest species, more abundant in assemblages towards the top. This is supported by the weaker negative correlation coefficient for M. alaskensis as compared to L. muscorum on Axis 2. Ant assemblages were more homogeneous (i.e. lower ordination separation) within seral ages than between them, despite the physical separation of some replicate sites by approximately 100 km (Fig. 2). The only exceptions were the 8–10 year post‐harvest and 13–15 year post‐harvest sites, where one 8–10 year post‐harvest site was similar to the 13–15 year post‐harvest sites. This observation may be explained as a consequence of a similar species assemblage differing largely in abundance, with most 13–15 year post‐harvest sites having a greater number of colonies (Fig. 4a).

    The pattern of site relatedness in species assemblages (i.e. separation in ordination) (Fig. 3) may reflect some stochastic attributes in how ant assemblages establish within newly available areas, compounded by low numbers of both colonies and species, that becomes more deterministic as most common colonisers arrive and establish within the first 13–15 years. In seral ages later than 13–15 year post‐harvest, as thermal conditions begin to become less favourable (see below), the assemblages show more variation suggesting that each site has followed a unique path. This pattern contrasts with the NMDS analysis of Wike et al. (2010) and correspondence analysis of Zorilla et al. (1986) who found high ordination separation within seral classes and overlap of ant assemblages between seral classes. This argues for a more stochastic process throughout succession in these other studies, likely the result of the much higher habitat species richness associated with the humid subtropical climate of South Carolina (Wike et al., 2010) or Mediterranean climate of Spain (Zorilla et al., 1986).

    Successional changes in ant assemblages

    It has been argued that multiple paths can lead to any given temporally static ant assemblage structure (Simberloff, 1978; Vepsäläinen & Pisarski, 1982). Only when the context of the environment and the autecologies of the component ant species are considered can a likely path be postulated (Vepsäläinen & Pisarski, 1982). While relatively little is known about the autecology of most North American boreal and sub‐boreal ant species, as compared to the European fauna, some basic autecological foundations can provide guidance in examining the process of seral ant assemblage changes.

    Given the depauperate ant assemblage present in the non‐harvested sites of this study, the damage to soil and woody debris during clear‐cut harvesting (Higgins & Lindgren, 2006), and the tendency of mated queens to select open areas for nest founding (Wilson & Hunt, 1966), it is likely that ants found in early seral ages (i.e. 2–3 year post‐harvest) have arrived through queen flight dispersal from source areas in the surrounding landscape. In our study this consisted of a mosaic of managed forest, shaped mostly by clearcut blocks of varying seral age, but not varying significantly in the volume of CWD (Fig. 4b). It has been argued that most species that come to define later seral stages arrive early (Drury & Nisbet, 1973). Of the five most common species of ants identified in this study, four (C. herculeanus, M. alaskensis, L. muscorum, and F. neorufibarbis) were located in the 2–3 year post‐harvest sites. Only F. aserva, a species that normally establishes through nest parasitism, may have required facilitation (Connell & Slayter, 1977) as it was first detected in the 8–10 year post‐harvest stands.

    Camponotus herculeanus was present as a pioneer species in the earliest seral stages, showing strong dispersal ability. Solitary queens were frequently encountered in these sites although they were not included in the final tabulated data because their success in initiating a colony could not be verified. The pattern of change in absolute abundance (i.e. the number of colonies per 50 m2) is typical of most of the species of ants followed in this study with the exception of F. neorufibaribis (see later), that is, a steady rise in absolute abundance, peaking in 13–15 year post‐harvest sites followed by a decline (Fig. 5). McColl (2010), examining carabid ecology in these forests, reported that canopy development followed a sigmoidal curve with canopy closure at approximately 50% in 16 year post‐harvest sites, 80% in 25 year post‐harvest sites, and 100% in non‐harvested forests. Mean summer litter temperatures (Table 2) were less than 10 °C in non‐harvested sites, conditions under which C. herculeanus was never found within our study plots despite an abundance of large CWD that should be suitable as a nesting substrate. Despite this observation, this species may have some cold tolerance as one mature colony was located in non‐harvested forest outside the study area.

    The absence of F. aserva in the earliest seral age may be explained by a need for a facilitated establishment via parasitic dependent nest founding (Connell & Slayter, 1977). This species is a facultative slave taker (Francoeur, 1983), usually initiating colonies as a parasite of a Formica fusca species group colony. In Finnish boreal forests, it was noted that fully one‐third of all species of ants used parasitic dependent nest founding (Punttila et al., 1991). Of the five most common ant species in our study, only F. aserva is known to initiate nests in this manner (Wheeler & Wheeler, 1963; Francoeur, 1983). The most common potential slave within the F. fusca species group was F. neorufibarbis, which was present as a pioneer species in the earliest seral age and likely became a host to many F. aserva queens and subsequent colonies. Formica neorufibarbis slaves were twice identified within F. aserva nests during the course of our study. Furthermore, the growing population of F. aserva in the 8–10 and 13–15 year post‐harvest sites, may explain the depression in the F. neorufibarbis population in 13–15 year post‐harvest sites, a seral age where other species of ants were still increasing in abundance (Fig. 5).

    Evolved environmental tolerances (Pickett, 1976) may explain the dramatic decline in absolute abundance of F. aserva between 13 and 15 year post‐harvest sites and 23–25 year post‐harvest sites (Fig. 5). This was also reflected in pitfall traps and mini‐Winkler captures although the decline was not as strong. Experimental manipulation of F. aserva nest temperatures in 13–15 year post‐harvest sites by shading established nests to emulate canopy closure (Higgins & Lindgren, 2012b), resulted in more colonies of F. aserva abandoning their nest sites as compared to unshaded controls. Leptothorax muscorum colonies treated in a similar manner also negatively responded to shading but not to the degree observed for F. aserva. This finding is also supported by Punttila et al. (1996) who report that F. sanguinea, a member of the same species group as F. aserva, with similar ecology (dependent nest founding), avoided shaded areas but was abundant in forest openings with high insolation.

    The most abundant species in this study were L. muscorum and M. alaskensis, both in the subfamily Myrmicinae. Both species, especially L. muscorum (Heinze, 1993), are small, do not form large colonies, and forage within approximately 2 m of their nest sites (Brian et al., 1965). Thus, there is extensive space available in harvested sites for nest establishment and foraging. Both were pioneer species although L. muscorum was most common in the earliest seral ages (2–3 to 13–15 year post‐harvest) (Fig. 5). The opposite was true for M. alaskensis which increased in relative abundance with seral age and was the only ant regularly found in non‐harvested sites, despite a reduction in its absolute abundance in latest seral ages (Fig. 5). Myrmica species were present in the oldest forests in both Fennoscandia (Punttila et al., 1991, 1994) and Russia (Dlusskii & Putyatina, 2004), indicating that some species in this genus are tolerant of cool conditions. In both of these cases, however, they were not the only ant species capable of living in this habitat. Similarly, Myrmica species were the most common ants in the oldest lichen‐spruce forests of Quebec (Lafleur et al., 2006), and M. alaskensis has been identified near the treeline in northern Canada (Francoeur, 1983). Thus, species of Myrmica, and M. alaskensis specifically, appear to be particularly cold tolerant. Despite this, M. alaskensis was still rarely encountered in non‐harvested sites, where daily mean litter temperatures were below 10 °C (Table 2). On one occasion its presence was clearly associated with a small gap in the non‐harvested forest canopy.

    It should be noted that while three species of ants captured in pitfall traps or mini‐Winklers were not found in CWD, two (F. dakotensis and Polyergus breviceps) were recorded only as a single specimen (Table 1) and both are known to nest in CWD. The third species, M. fracticornis, however, appeared frequently in pitfall traps and mini‐Winklers. It is possible that this species was nesting in the ground, unobserved, but it is likely that this species was nesting in pieces of wood that were below the 10 cm diameter cut‐off for our study. This is supported by Lindgren and MacIsaac (2002) who report finding this ant nesting in wood below our 10 cm diameter threshold.

    No ants were incidentally observed nesting directly in the soil, under rocks, or in thatched mounds nor were they encountered during litter sampling for mini‐Winkler extractions. Soil temperature monitoring in a related study within 15 year post‐harvest stands in these forests (10 cm depth), indicated an average temperature of 11 °C with daily fluctuations of 1 °C (Higgins & Lindgren, 2012b). Elmes and Wardlaw (1983) found that Myrmica larvae required a minimum temperature of 15 °C to develop, supporting the hypothesis that these soils are too cold for ant nesting. It is possible that almost all ants in these sub‐boreal forests are CWD associated. This dependence may be a conservation concern. Following the mountain pine beetle (Dendroctonus ponderosae) epidemic in BC, the provincial government has encouraged the development of the bioenergy industry. This includes targeting, ‘forest residues from logging practices’ (BC Ministry of Energy, Mines & Petroleum Resources, nd).

    Competition

    Authors have structured boreal ant species into a hierarchy based on behavioural dominance as a foundation for examining competition (Vepsäläinen & Pisarski, 1982; Savolainen & Vepsäläinen, 1988; Cerdá et al., 2013). Following Cerdá et al. (2013) species were classified as ‘dominants’ if they would defend their territory and food, ‘subdominants’ if they defend only food, and ‘subordinates’ if they defend only their nest. These studies, and others (Savolainen & Vepsäläinen, 1989; Savolainen et al., 1989; Punttila et al., 1994, 1996) found that dominants were important in structuring the ant assemblage, especially in later seral ages when dominants were well established. In our study the primary dominant was F. aserva, whereas C. herculeanus and possibly F. neorufibarbis were subdominants, and L. muscorum and M. alaskensis were subordinates.

    Recognising the limitations of an observational study such as ours, there is still little evidence to support a role for competition in shaping the ant assemblage. First, the near loss of the only dominant ant, F. aserva, in stands older than 13–15 years, removes the primary species capable of structuring the ant assemblage. Second, the decline in absolute abundances of both subdominants (C. herculeanus and F. neorufibarbis) after 13–15 years (P < 0.01) (Fig. 5) further impoverishes the assemblage of species exerting an action on other ant species away from their nest. Third, the decline in colony abundances for all species of ants in later seral ages (23–25 years and non‐harvested) suggests a shaping factor other than the ants themselves. Fourth, attempts at examining ant interactions at baits (not reported here) in the 13–15 year seral sites failed to attract any ants over 2 h observation periods, indicative of the low density of foraging ants that could compete. Finally, the observation that un‐harvested forests support only submissive species, M. alaskensis, at very low densities is not indicative of an assemblage shaped by competition.

    While this study is consistent with a number of recent publications (Gibb, 2011; Gibb & Johansson, 2011; Baccaro et al., 2012; Neuvonen et al., 2012) that have failed to document competition, it is likely that the reason competition is not evident in these sub‐boreal forests is because the thermally adequate period for colony development is too ephemeral during seral development. Even during the peak in ant colony densities, nests of the dominant F. aserva, never exceeded one colony per 50 m2 (Fig. 5).

    Thus, the initial assemblage in 2–3 year post‐harvest sites, through succession, would then have been shaped by the individual life history strategies (e.g. colony life cycles, colony sizes) (Gleason, 1917; Drury & Nisbet, 1973) and the evolved environmental tolerances of each species (Pickett, 1976). The attribute most likely to play a critical role in shaping the assemblage structure by interacting with life history strategies and evolved tolerances is temperature, which has been supported experimentally in these forests (Higgins & Lindgren, 2012b).

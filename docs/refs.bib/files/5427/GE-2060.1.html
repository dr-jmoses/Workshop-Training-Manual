Abstract

Assessing parasite specificity to vector is crucial to understanding the emergence of vector-borne diseases and the evolution of parasite diversity. Avian malaria parasites have a cosmopolitan distribution and broad avian host range, which together predict they are vector generalists, but little is known about parasite–vector associations in the wild. We tested this prediction by asking if 5 different mosquito species, known to feed on birds and abundant in the northeastern United States, were naturally infected in the field with identical avian Plasmodium spp. lineages. Mosquitoes were not pooled but rather analyzed individually, and, possibly as a result, lineage diversity was higher than reported in previous avian malaria vector studies. Plasmodium spp. lineages were rare in Aedes canadensis and absent in Aedes aurifer and Culiseta melanura. We sequenced a standard Plasmodium cytochrome b marker from Culex pipiens pipiens, Culex restuans, and Ae. canadensis. Most Plasmodium clades were shared by Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans. In addition, 4 individual lineages were shared by both mosquito species, including the most common lineage. One Plasmodium clade, however, was only found in Cx. restuans. We therefore found limited support for our prediction that avian Plasmodium spp. vector breadth accompanies host breadth. The association of both Culex species with most Plasmodium clades, and the presence of a single parasite lineage in 3 mosquito species representing 2 genera, suggests that avian Plasmodium species are not tightly coevolved with vector species.

Vector-borne parasites present a major challenge to understanding parasite evolution because parasite interactions with both host and vector must be considered. Many vertebrate parasites rely on blood-feeding insect vectors for transmission to new hosts. A parasite ingested in a blood meal must evade the insect's immune system and complete its development while the insect remains alive long enough to feed on another host (Marquardt et al., 2005). Malaria vector-parasite associations are particularly important because sexual reproduction of the parasite occurs in the mosquito vector (Diptera: Culicidae), making the vector the definitive host (Valkiūnas, 2005). The importance of the vector in the Plasmodium spp. life cycles suggests that interactions with vectors would be important in the evolution of the parasite.

Malaria is a particularly well-studied vector-borne disease in humans, but also is important to the health of bird populations. For example, the inadvertent introduction to the Hawaiian Islands in the early 1800s of the vector Culex quinquefasciatus led to the subsequent emergence of malaria (Plasmodium relictum) into the previously unexposed native avifauna. This emergence has been implicated in the extinction of many bird species in that archipelago (Atkinson et al., 1995). In zoos, naïve birds become vulnerable to avian malaria when they are brought into captivity in close proximity to infected birds and local mosquito vectors (Fix et al., 1988; Schrenzel et al., 2003).

The interactions between malaria parasites and their mosquito vectors are complex, and can be affected by both genetic and environmental factors (Tripet et al., 2008). Crucial to understanding the potential role of vectors in influencing the dynamics of vector-borne diseases is assessing parasite specificity to the vector as well as to the host. Parasites able to exploit a wider range of vector species could gain access to a higher diversity of potential hosts, and thus could be expected to emerge into new host populations or new host species more readily than vector specialists. The relative scarcity of data on the ecology of vectors of avian malaria and other related blood parasites seems particularly acute given the importance of these parasites in the ecology and evolution of birds (Hamilton and Zuk, 1982; Ricklefs, 1992; Martin et al., 2001; MacDougall-Shackleton et al., 2002; Waldenström et al., 2002; Marzal et al., 2005; Møller and Nielsen, 2007; Marzal et al., 2008). A few studies have begun to use molecular markers to examine the distribution of avian blood parasite lineages in wild-caught arthropod vectors (Gager et al., 2008; Ishtiaq et al., 2008), but parasite–vector associations remain an understudied aspect of avian malaria ecology.

Vector ecology could be a particularly important factor in the dynamics of avian blood parasites in their hosts if parasite species exhibited vector specificity. For example, avian Plasmodium spp. lineages form geographic patterns in single avian host species, at both large and small geographic scales (Beadell et al., 2006; Kimura et al., 2006; Wood et al., 2007), suggesting that such patterns could be driven by variation in vector ecology across geographic regions. Two recently published long-term studies of avian host populations have revealed temporally fluctuating patterns of prevalence in different Plasmodium spp. lineages that may be tied to variation in vector phenology (Bensch et al., 2007; Cosgrove et al., 2008), but data on vectors are needed to evaluate their importance. Spatial variation in vector community composition could result from variation among mosquito species in the specificity of their oviposition sites (Eldridge and Edman, 2000), resting sites, canopy height (Lothrop and Reisen, 2001), passive aggregation, e.g., via wind, or spatial patterns in adult mortality (Ellis, 2008). Patterns of mosquito aggregation among microhabitats could lead to highly localized differences in the transmission of different Plasmodium spp. lineages. Furthermore, mosquito species exhibit variation in phenology, with some species maintaining high population densities throughout the season and others showing a peak and decline (Barker et al., 2003). If Plasmodium spp. lineages were vector-specific, then temporal patterns of their prevalence in avian hosts could likewise be driven by differences in mosquito phenology.

Avian malaria parasites in the genus Plasmodium (Haemosporida: Plasmodiidae) are represented by about 200 morphospecies, which have a cosmopolitan distribution and occur naturally in nearly all avian taxa (Valkiūnas, 2005). Molecular markers have become a popular method of characterizing avian malaria parasites as genetic lineages rather than species, especially where morphological data are not available. We do not know whether genetic lineage diversity represents inter- or intraspecific diversity, though some evidence suggests that mtDNA lineages may be reproductively isolated (Bensch et al., 2004). Many Plasmodium spp. lineages show little specificity to avian species, genus, family, or order (Ricklefs and Fallon, 2002; Beadell et al., 2004; Fallon et al., 2005; Beadell et al., 2006), suggesting that even if avian host specificity had originally facilitated the diversification of parasites via cospeciation, subsequent host switching and sharing have obscured most coevolutionary associations (Ricklefs et al., 2004). Evidence exists, however, for the coevolution of avian blood parasites with their vectors at higher taxonomic levels. A recent multigene phylogeny of many diverse avian and saurian haemosporidian taxa showed that major deeper nodes in the tree correspond to switches of parasites into families of dipteran vectors (Martinsen et al., 2008).

Two recent studies, both in tropical systems, have examined the distribution of avian haemosporidian lineages in their respective mosquito communities. Gager et al. (2008) found unique avian Plasmodium spp. lineages isolated from 2 mosquito species, representing different genera, in their study area in Panama. Ishtiaq et al. (2008) conducted a broader geographic survey across an island system and found 4 Plasmodium spp. lineages in 4 mosquito species representing 2 genera, with 1 lineage shared between 2 congeneric mosquito species. Both of these studies recovered a limited number of lineages from mosquitoes, and sampled all the common mosquito species found at their respective study sites. A more targeted approach, focusing on host-seeking mosquitoes of documented ornithophilic species, might be expected to uncover a greater richness of Plasmodium spp. lineages.

The broad avian host breadth and widespread geographic distribution of many Plasmodium spp. lineages could be mediated by a broad vector range, where parasites could exploit multiple vector species and ultimately gain access to diverse avian hosts in different habitats. The high lineage diversity found in avian Plasmodium spp. lineages coupled with low avian host specificity (Beadell et al., 2004), however, leads to the opposite prediction, i.e., that parasite–vector specificity reflects a close coevolutionary history between parasite and vector. In the present study, we tested these opposing predictions by asking if, and to what extent, different mosquito species are naturally infected with identical avian Plasmodium spp. lineages. Our effort represents a step towards understanding the evolution of avian malaria parasites across a natural transmission system by explicitly asking whether avian malaria parasites in the genus Plasmodium, some of which are known to be avian host generalists, are shared among vector species.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Mosquito collection, species, and identification

Mosquitoes were collected in bird-baited traps in 2004 and resting on vegetation in aspirator collections from 2002–2004, in Ithaca, New York. Two mosquito species, Culex pipiens and Culex restuans, were caught in bird-baited traps from June to September 2004 in the same study area, with Gallus domesticus (domestic chicken) or Passer domesticus (house sparrow), as described by Darbro and Harrington (2006). There was no evidence for differences between chicken-baited and sparrow-baited traps in mosquito species or the number of mosquito species collected (Darbro and Harringon, 2006). Only mated adult Cx. pipiens females will seek a blood meal; consequently, we increased the probability of sampling avian host-seeking Plasmodium spp.–infected mosquitoes by utilizing bird-baited traps. In contrast, standard collection techniques such as carbon-dioxide–baited Centers for Disease Control light traps collect host-seeking mosquitoes that may feed on a variety of animals. Moreover, mosquito resting collection techniques, which involve aspirating mosquitoes from their resting habitat, produce high numbers of mosquitoes, including males (that do not feed on blood) and virgin teneral females (that would not yet be seeking a blood meal). Females that have taken at least 1 previous blood meal represent the proportion capable of transmission in subsequent blood meals.

Individual mosquitoes were identified to species with the use of standard taxonomic keys (Darsie and Ward, 2004). The identity of Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans, which cannot be reliably distinguished by morphology alone, was confirmed with molecular methods (for details, see Darbro and Harrington, 2006; Harrington and Poulson, 2008). The 2 focal mosquito species, Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans, were chosen because they represented over 90% of the individuals captured in the bird-baited traps throughout the sampling season (Darbro and Harrington, 2006). We also included 3 other abundant mosquito species, Aedes aurifer, Aedes canadensis, and Culiseta melanura, which were collected by aspirating resting individuals on vegetation. These additional species were readily identified by their morphology. We also included a smaller number of resting Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans individuals in our sample (n  =  30 for both species). All 5 mosquito species used in this study occupy similar forested habitat and utilize woodland pools or artificial containers for breeding, so we expected that they would encounter a similar suite of avian host species. The life cycles are different for each mosquito species with respect to overwintering life stages and timing of peak abundance. However, ongoing surveys conducted for multiple years in this study area indicate that mosquitoes used in this study were collected through the representative period for all species when females would have been mated and seeking blood meals.

Culex pipiens is one of the best-studied mosquito vector species of avian Plasmodium spp., in part because of its suspected role in West Nile virus transmission (Turell et al., 2000), and because of its amenability to rearing in the laboratory. Early laboratory avian malaria transmission experiments used Cx. pipiens successfully as a vector for P. relictum (Huff, 1929; Tate and Vincent, 1934) and Plasmodium cathemerium (Huff, 1931). Both Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans were previously found to be competent in the laboratory for Plasmodium elongatum in captive penguins (Beier and Trpis, 1981), and Cx. restuans was found to be competent for Plasmodium forresteri, which infects raptors (Telford et al., 1997). Blood meal analyses have shown Cs. melanura to be a strongly ornithophilic species, only occasionally feeding on mammals (Magnarelli, 1977; Molaei and Andreadis, 2006; Molaei et al., 2006). Little is known about the host feeding preferences of Ae. aurifer or Ae. canadensis, although the latter is known to feed on both birds and mammals (LeDuc et al., 1972).

In mosquitoes, Plasmodium species life stages develop after infected erythrocytes are ingested from an avian host. The parasite gametes fuse to form a zygote within the mosquito gut lumen. Zygotes eventually form oocysts that penetrate the mosquito midgut wall, giving rise to sporozoites. Once sporozoites infect the salivary glands, they can be transmitted to a new host during mosquito blood feeding (Garnham, 1966). In the present study, we attempted to include only those parasites that had successfully reached the oocyst or sporozoite stage, rather than those parasites ingested in a blood meal from a chicken or sparrow inside the traps, by sampling whole individual mosquitoes that were physically excluded from feeding on birds in the trap by a mesh barrier or did not contain blood in the abdomen upon visual inspection. For resting site collections, we only included whole bodies of mosquitoes where no blood was seen in the abdomen or the blooded abdomen had been removed. Because DNA was extracted from whole bodies of mosquitoes for other purposes (for a previous study, Darbro and Harrington, 2006), we were not able to separate the head and thorax from the abdomen for the bird-baited individuals.

Molecular identification of Plasmodium spp. from mosquitoes

Because malaria taxonomy is based mainly on morphology in the vertebrate blood stages, molecular methods are the most reliable way to distinguish parasite taxa in their mosquito stages. Genomic DNA extracted from individual mosquitoes was screened for Plasmodium spp. infection using primers R2 (Beadell et al., 2004) and HaemF2 (GGATGGTGTTTTAGATATATGCATG), modified from Bensch et al. (2000), which amplify 359 base pairs (bp) of Plasmodium spp. cytochrome b, and where possible we sequenced an overlapping fragment with the use of primers F2.Plas.2 (modified from Beadell et al., 2004) and H15725 (Ricklefs and Fallon, 2002), to obtain a total of 726 bp. Primers HaemF2 and F2.Plas.2 were designed in regions of Plasmodium cytochrome b that are highly conserved among parasites that infect birds and those that infect rodents (Plasmodium chabaudi; Perkins et al., 2007). The fact that the primers we used in our study readily amplify Plasmodium spp. from infected bird blood samples suggests that these primers are robust enough to detect infection in a broad range of background host DNA, and a consistent inability to amplify a particular parasite lineage from any given mosquito species most likely represents the true absence of that lineage, not simple PCR failure. We employed a conservative standard for scoring individual mosquitoes as infected; we only included infections in analyses if we were able to obtain readable sequence. Infection rates among mosquito species were compared with a 2-tailed z test for independent proportions (Zar, 1984). To test for the presence of traces of avian blood, we also conducted a PCR assay for avian cytochrome b with the use of universal avian primers cytb2.RC and cytb.wow (Dumbacher et al., 2003).

Sequences were uploaded to GenBank (GQ471939–GQ471956) and aligned with the use of Sequencher 4.2 (GeneCodes Corp., Ann Arbor, Michigan). Diagnostic taxonomic characters are rare in the mosquito stages of Plasmodium spp. Consequently, where possible, we inferred taxonomic identity by assessing the phylogenetic affinities of mosquito-isolated Plasmodium spp. lineages with published sequences from GenBank that were reliably identified to morphological species. To explore phylogenetic relationships among Plasmodium spp. lineages isolated from mosquitoes, we used MrModeltest v.2 (Nylander, 2004) to determine the appropriate model of molecular evolution using hierarchical likelihood ratio tests. We then conducted a Bayesian phylogenetic analysis using MrBayes v.3.1.2 (Huelsenbeck and Ronquist, 2001), with 2 simultaneous runs of 10 million generations, with 4 incrementally heated chains, sampling trees every 2,000 generations, and discarding the first 5 million generations as burn-in. We used 5 outgroup taxa: 1 avian haematozoan parasite species, Haemoproteus balmorali, and 4 Plasmodium spp. infecting mammals, i.e., Plasmodium reichenowi, Plasmodium falciparum, Plasmodium malariae, and P. chabaudi.

Finally, we compared our mosquito-isolated Plasmodium spp. lineages to previously published avian Plasmodium spp. cytochrome b sequences with the use of MalAvi, the avian malaria database (Bensch et al., 2009). The explosion of interest in sequencing avian haemosporidians with little coordination among researchers has resulted in the publication to GenBank of sequences of many disparate, nonoverlapping fragments of cytochrome b. The new database streamlines comparative analysis because it contains only haemosporidian cytochrome b sequences fully overlapping with the 478-bp reference sequence used in Bensch et al. (2000), and, therefore, greatly facilitates the identification of shared avian hosts. The Plasmodium spp. sequences in this study aligned completely with the database reference sequence for those samples that yielded 726 bp. Samples for which shorter sequence was obtained overlapped by 359 bp. In either case, we scored the sequences as matches if our sequences were identical to the MalAvi reference sequences.

RESULTS

Plasmodium spp. prevalence in mosquitoes

The overall proportions of Cx. pipiens (14.2%) and Cx. restuans (11.1%) yielding readable Plasmodium spp. sequence did not differ significantly (z  =  0.832, 2-tailed test). Of the resting individuals caught by aspiration, Plasmodium spp. were found in 4 Cx. pipiens and no Cx. restuans. Plasmodium spp. were detected in only 1 of the 161 Ae. canadensis screened. We did not detect Plasmodium spp. in any of the Ae. aurifer or Cs. melanura individuals screened (Table I). The results from the avian cytochrome b assay on a subset of samples twice amplified a fragment that a BLAST search revealed to be only slightly similar to Aedes aegypti, a tropical mosquito species not found in our study area, but not at all similar to any known avian species.

Phylogenetic analysis and patterns of lineage diversity

The MrModelTest analysis indicated that GTR + I + G (general time-reversible + gamma + proportion invariant) was the most appropriate model of evolution for this analysis. The resulting Bayesian phylogenetic tree showed 3 well-supported clades (99–100% posterior probability, Clades A–C, Fig. 1). Comparison of our sequences to those in MalAvi (Bensch et al., 2009) showed that 5 of our mosquito-isolated Plasmodium spp. lineages were previously found in avian hosts: SYAT05 (GenBank DQ847271), TUMIG3 (source: MalAvi), LINN1 (DQ847270), PADOM11 (MalAvi) and SEIAUR01 (MalAvi, synonymous with lineage F1, AY640130). Moreover, all clades contained at least 1 lineage previously found in birds. These 2 results together strongly suggest that the mosquito-isolated Plasmodium spp. lineages in this study indeed infect birds.

Figure 1

Bayesian phylogenetic analysis of mosquito-isolated Plasmodium spp. cytochrome b (726 bp). Lineages isolated from mosquitoes are indicated with a mosquito icon, with filled triangles representing Cx. pipiens, open squares Cx. restuans, and grey circle Ae. canadensis. Lineages previously isolated from birds are indicated with a bird icon, and those isolated from birds and mosquitoes are in bold text. Major clades are indicated by letters A–C. Numbers at nodes indicate percentages of mean posterior probabilities, with values under 50 omitted. Scale bar indicates number of nucleotide substitutions per site.

Lineage diversity was high, particularly in Clade C. This clade consisted of lineage TUMIG3, which is sister to a subclade consisting of mostly lineages isolated from individual mosquitoes. Clade C also included lineage SYAT05, the most common lineage found in both mosquito species (Table I).

Plasmodium spp. associations with mosquito vectors and avian hosts

In an ongoing effort to integrate molecular and microscopic taxonomic data on avian blood parasites, recent studies have linked morphospecies to mtDNA cytochrome b sequence. Because multiple infections are common in wild-caught birds, single parasite lineages obtained from wild bird blood cannot be as reliably linked to morphospecies as by subinoculating naïve birds with parasites of known morphospecies (Palinauskas et al., 2007; Valkiūnas et al., 2007, 2008). We, therefore, included only these 3 morphospecies from these studies in the phylogeny. Our analysis suggests that lineage E1 corresponds to P. elongatum because both cluster in Clade B, and they differ by a single nucleotide change. Neither P. relictum nor Plasmodium circumflexum showed a strong enough phylogenetic affinity to the mosquito-isolated Plasmodium spp. lineages to reliably assign morphospecies.

Of the Plasmodium spp. lineages isolated from mosquitoes, 2 (E1 and F1) were identical to those previously isolated from wild birds at our study site. Lineage E1, found in Cx. restuans only, was previously found in local bird species representing several avian families (Szymanski and Lovette, 2005; also reported in migratory and resident bird species throughout North America and the Lesser Antilles as “PA” by Fallon et al. 2005). Lineage F1 was found in 3 mosquito species, i.e., Cx. pipiens, Cx. restuans, and Ae. canadensis, and in 3 bird species, Carpodacus mexicanus (house finch), Melospiza georgiana (swamp sparrow), and Tachycineta bicolor (tree swallow), from previous studies conducted in the same study area (Szymanski and Lovette, 2005; Kimura et al. 2006).

DISCUSSION

Sharing of Plasmodium spp. lineages by mosquito species

The high Plasmodium spp. prevalence in Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans relative to other abundant mosquito species we examined suggests that they are important vectors in our study area. A similar proportion of adult female Cx. quinquefasciatus (12%) was infected with P. relictum in Hawaii, where they are known to be the dominant vector species (Reiter and LaPointe, 2007). We found moderate support for our prediction that host breadth accompanies vector breadth, with 1 of the most common Plasmodium spp. lineages infecting birds (F1, also called SEIAUR01) also infecting 3 mosquito species, but another lineage (E1, probably P. elongatum, widespread among bird taxa and geographic areas) infecting just 1 mosquito species and rarely. A previous study (Kimura et al., 2006) found E1 to be the most common lineage occurring in C. mexicanus in Ithaca, New York and at several other sampling sites across North America. It was also isolated from passerine hosts (T. bicolor, Melospiza melodia, Dendroica petechia, and C. mexicanus, Szymanski and Lovette, 2005), and a nonpasserine host (Megascops asio; M. Kimura, unpubl. obs.) in the same study area. Fallon et al. (2005) found the same lineage (PA) in both resident and migratory birds on several islands across the Lesser Antilles. The broad geographic and host range of lineage E1 would lead us to predict that it would be similarly widespread in vector species. It is, therefore, curious that we isolated E1 just once from Cx. restuans and not at all from Cx. pipiens.

The high level of sharing of Plasmodium spp. lineages between Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans might be due to the similar ecology of these 2 mosquito species. Both species feed primarily on birds (Magnarelli, 1977), and compete with each other in their larval stages (Reiskind and Wilson, 2008). Their frequent capture in the same bird-baited traps indicates that they have similar foraging habits (Darbro and Harrington, 2006) and, therefore, may encounter the same suite of potential hosts. Our finding of shared Plasmodium spp. lineages in 2 Culex species is consistent with the finding of Ishtiaq et al. (2008), who reported that 2 Culex species shared 1 Plasmodium lineage. On the other hand, Gager et al. (2008) found no evidence for vector sharing among Plasmodium spp. lineages.

The evolution of major clades of haemosporidian parasites correlates with vector shifts into different dipteran families, presumably by giving parasites access to new hosts (Martinsen et al., 2008). This correlation has been shown on a broad phylogenetic scale, and evidence exists for partitioning of lineages among divergent mosquito genera (Gager et al., 2008). Our data demonstrate that some Plasmodium spp. lineages are found across these congeneric and locally sympatric mosquito species. The same Plasmodium spp. lineage, F1, was shared by 3 mosquito species (Cx. pipiens, Cx. restuans, and Ae. canadensis) representing 2 genera, showing that Plasmodium spp. vector sharing also occurs at the mosquito genus level. This result confirms previous laboratory experiments showing that infective sporozoites of the same Plasmodium gallinaceum (Alavi et al., 2003) and P. relictum (Hunninen, 1953) strains could form infective sporozoites in mosquito species representing different genera. Vector phylogenetic relatedness may not be a reliable index of their ability to become infected with the same Plasmodium spp. lineage, and our result provides further evidence for extensive vector switching and/or sharing over the evolution of avian Plasmodium species. More sampling of divergent mosquito taxa needs to be added to the growing data set of vector-isolated parasites to understand patterns of dipteran host sharing. Although robust molecular phylogenies for the Culex and Aedes genera are presently not available, 1 phylogenetic hypothesis based on morphological characters suggests that Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans are not sister taxa (Miller et al., 1996). The position of Aedes species relative to Culex species is not known.

Differences in Plasmodium spp. prevalence among mosquito species and evidence for local transmission

Only a few of the 78 described species in Culex have documented competence for avian Plasmodium species, most commonly Cx. quinquefasciatus, closely related to Cx. pipiens (LaPointe et al., 2005). We were, therefore, not expecting to find equal levels of prevalence of Plasmodium spp. in all the mosquito species we tested. Our results are consistent with a general finding from other studies (LaPointe et al., 2005; Gager et al., 2008) that avian malaria vector competence, and by extension the probability of avian Plasmodium spp. infection, varies among mosquito species. The apparent absence of Plasmodium spp. infection in Ae. aurifer and Cs. melanura might be due to these species not being as important as Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans as competent vectors at our study site, or that resting collections do not yield as many infected mosquitoes as bird-baited traps. A similar proportion of Cx. pipiens was found in samples from both collection methods, but infected Cx. restuans were absent from aspirated resting collections.

Our finding that most Plasmodium spp. lineages were found in 2 mosquito species, Cx. pipiens and Cx. restuans, both previously demonstrated to be competent vectors in laboratory studies, suggests that it is reasonable that they are indeed competent vectors in the wild. However, the PCR-based approach is limited by its inability to specifically target salivary gland sporozoites, the only Plasmodium spp. life stage that would indicate incipient transmission, a point discussed by Ishtiaq et al. (2008). PCR amplifies genomic DNA regardless of life stage; therefore, Plasmodium spp. DNA could have originated from a few undigested infected avian erythrocytes hidden in the mosquito's digestive system, or undeveloped oocyst or ookinete stages. It is unlikely that parasite DNA originated from blood for the following reasons. Laboratory experiments on blood-fed mosquitoes killed at different times after feeding demonstrate that 48 hr after feeding, only a very short fragment (136 bp) of host DNA is detectable by PCR, presumably due to the denaturing of host DNA by mosquito digestive enzymes (Kent and Norris, 2005). Given that host DNA is far more abundant than parasite DNA, even if avian blood remnants were so faint as to be undetected visually, then parasitemia would need to be extremely high for such a small amount of blood to yield readable parasite sequences of 345–726 bp eventually, and our samples would have had to be collected within 48 hr of feeding. Our avian PCR assay was not conclusive, as the primers amplified mosquito DNA, and in any case it is difficult to determine absence of host blood by PCR failure. Documenting infective sporozoites in salivary glands via experiments with naïve avian hosts would be the only definitive way to score vector competence, but dissections require time and expertise and the sporozoite staining process could compromise the quality of the sample for genetic analyses. Future studies might employ RNA-based methods, such as reverse-transcription PCR, to examine the expression of sporozoite-specific proteins (Waters et al., 1989). Such techniques have been developed to target gametocytes in their vertebrate hosts (Wargo et al., 2006).

The occurrence of so many Plasmodium cytochrome b lineages in the 2 most abundant ornithophilic mosquito species, and the co-occurrence of some of those lineages in diverse avian host species at our study site, support the hypothesis that transmission occurs locally. This result supports the conclusion of Szymanski and Lovette (2005) that transmission occurs on the breeding grounds because infections of both Haemoproteus and Plasmodium spp. lineages were found in recently fledged birds still in their natal area. However, this finding contrasts with the conclusion of Waldenström et al. (2002) that transmission of avian Plasmodium and Haemoproteus spp. to migratory birds occurs largely on the wintering grounds. More sampling of Plasmodium spp. vectors in both breeding and wintering areas of migratory birds would help to elucidate transmission dynamics over the annual cycle of migratory birds.

Patterns of Plasmodium spp. lineage diversity

The elevated lineage diversity in our sample relative to that found by Gager et al. (2008) or Ishtiaq et al. (2008) could reflect differences found in the different study areas, e.g., temperate versus tropical. Another possible explanation, however, is that by avoiding pooling of multiple mosquitoes during DNA extraction and instead extracting parasite DNA from individual mosquitoes, we increased the probability of isolating unique parasite lineages. A drawback to PCR-based approaches in blood parasite studies is that they can underestimate diversity in mixed infections, which are common in natural hosts, and can be detected with the use of microscopy in concert with molecular methods (Valkiūnas et al., 2006). A single lineage may be preferentially amplified in a pool of many lineages where primer bias exists, or where a single lineage is more abundant than others. Because there are relatively few taxonomic characters in the mosquito stages of Plasmodium spp. (ookinete, oocyst, and sporozoite), molecular methods are the only way to identify parasite taxa isolated from mosquitoes in the field. To avoid underestimating Plasmodium spp. lineage diversity in mosquitoes, it is therefore especially important to analyze mosquitoes individually rather than in pools.

The high Plasmodium spp. lineage diversity in our mosquito-isolated samples suggests that avian malaria is even more diverse than previously realized. The presence of 12 unique Plasmodium spp. lineages recovered from mosquitoes and not previously seen in avian hosts in this, or any other, study may reflect our limited sampling of avian hosts to date. Most studies on avian malaria parasite genetic diversity are biased toward small, common passerine birds that are easily captured in mist nets, and our results suggest that more hosts need to be sampled to realize the high diversity of parasites in avian communities.

The dynamics of vector-borne diseases depend upon a complex interaction among host, vector, and parasite. Because many birds regularly migrate vast geographic distances, the ability to infect different vector species could further facilitate a parasite's colonization of new hosts inhabiting different regions. For example, a broad vector range has been hypothesized to facilitate the radiation of primate malarias in southeast Asia, which may have led to the emergence of Plasmodium spp. in humans (Escalante et al., 1998). Our results do not completely support the hypothesis that vector breadth accompanies host breadth, i.e., 1 of the Plasmodium spp. lineages most commonly found in hosts was isolated from 3 mosquito species in our study area, but the other common lineage was found in just 1 mosquito species. We have presented definitive evidence that mosquito species share Plasmodium spp. lineages, and that mosquitoes harbor a high diversity of these lineages.

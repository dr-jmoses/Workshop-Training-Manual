    •

    Abstract

    Forest dieback of mountainous areas is a common problem in almost all regions of the world. This process is characterized by high variability and is independent of whether the process is caused by climate changes, insect pests or human impact on the environment, particularly environmental pollution. This state of affairs is probably influenced not only by unusual atmospheric conditions, unusual stressors or species composition but also by many factors that have not been identified to date.

    Even in the case of relatively small mountainous areas in Central Europe with similar species composition, climatic conditions, and stressors, the process of tree dieback might have different characteristics and causes. During the analyses of such phenomena, remote sensing technologies, such as LiDAR and aerial imagery, which have become increasingly popular and precise, are helpful. Additionally, access to geographic information systems has become common by forest owners.

    Using previous research, as well as airborne laser scanning data, aerial imagery and Polish State Forests IT system (SILP) data, it was possible to analyze almost 200,000 dead trees inventoried in the areas of the Western Sudety, Eastern Sudety, Beskid Śląski and Beskid Żywiecki mountain ranges. Despite the fact these areas are located relatively close to each other, they differ in terms of forest structure and in terms of period of mass deforerstation. The impact of detailed landform, age, and species composition were considered, and complemented many other factors, which eventually enabled the development of three different models for each of the analyzed areas. The models depict the influence of the respective factors of the environment on Norway spruce dieback in the Central Europe mountains.

    The obtained results show that there are certain factors, such as e.g. age or stocking, which increase the probability of damage in all three areas, but certain ones are significant exclusively for one or two areas, such as e.g. altitude, which has an influence on the likelihood of damage only in the Western Sudety and the Western Beskidy Mountains. What is interesting, the altitude does not impact unequivocally on the share of dead spruces itself, since in the case of the Western and Eastern Sudety, the increase of altitude is positively correlated with the share, whereas in the case of the Western Beskidy Mountains, the correlation is negative. An important fact is that the increase of deciduous species’ share impacts positively on the forest dieback of spruces on all analyzed areas.

    1. Introduction

    Forest dieback in Central Europe has occurred since the 1970s, when air pollution (especially NOx and SOx) reached its highest level and resulted in the decline of forest stands. Concurrently, since the late 1990s, forest degradation and mortality of spruce stands have also reportedly increased in the Western Carpathians (Kozak, 1996, Badea et al., 2004, Grodzki et al., 2004). Dieback of forests in this part of Europe is attributed to various factors, such as heavy metals deposition (Guderian, 1977, Shparyk and Parpan, 2004), air pollution (Baltensweiler, 1985, Christiansen, 1989, Vacek et al., 2015), damage from secondary pests, particularly bark beetles (Schelhaas et al., 2003, Grodzki, 2007, Svoboda et al., 2010, Svoboda et al., 2012, Zhao et al., 2011), climate warming (Althoff, 1985, Hais, 2003), atmospheric conditions (Sobik et al., 1998, Błaś, 2000) and drought (Grodzki, 1998a). However, ambient ozone is also increasingly recognized as the primary cause of the damage (Bytnerowicz et al., 2004, Šrámek et al., 2012). In the 1980s, multiple factors that caused the massive dieback of spruce (Picea abies (L.) H. Karst) were recognized and described as a “spiral disease” (Manion, 1981). Many studies have analyzed only a single disturbance agent, while only a small number of studies have examined the interactions between different disturbance agents (Klopčič et al., 2009). There is still no complete agreement as to which of those were initiating factors and which were participating or predisposing. The role of individual factors can be discussed, but it is most frequently accepted that the initiating factors were air pollutants, which had a direct impact on the condition of tree stands (Althoff, 1985, Christiansen, 1989, Grodzki, 1992, Makkonen et al., 2016). The participating factors included insect outbreaks. The predisposing role was assigned to human activities in previous years, particularly creating monocultures (single-species stands based on seedlings from foreign provenance), excessive stand density, and overly high cutting age (Modrzyński, 1984). Multiple publications related to the impact of pollution and its accumulation in the soil and conifer needles indicate the relation among land topography, the decreased annual growth, and dieback of the stands (Dore et al., 2000, Sobik et al., 1998, Sobik and Błaś, 2008). The research carried out in the Izery Mountains indicates that the contamination of soil and conifer needles with sulfur and heavy metals is still considerable, even though it has been over 30 years since the beginning of the ecological disaster (Gleba and Bogacz, 2013, Gamrat and Ligocka, 2018). Regardless of which factors are considered to be the most significant and how their mutual interactions are described, there is still no satisfactory answer to the question of why certain parts of tree stands survived, while other suffered of forest dieback. There are still very few works that thoroughly investigate the issue of how the forest dieback process occurred; whether the altitude above sea level, aspect, or slope contributed to the problem, and how significant were their impacts. Although the relationship between forest dieback and terrain topography in this area has previously been examined (Poławski and Zawiła-Niedźwiecki, 1987, Zawiła-Niedźwiecki and Glasenapp, 1994, Ciołkosz and Zawiła-Niedźwiecki, 1995, Poławski and Zawiła-Niedźwiecki, 1995), only in recent years the detailed terrain models have become available. These models have been developed based on airborne laser scanning (ALS), which enables more detailed analysis. Additionally, works developed for the Western Beskidy Mountains (Main-Knorn et al., 2009), in which the impact of topography on the forest dieback process was analyzed, were based on much less precise terrain models. The accuracy of topographic data has increased from several dozen/hundred meters to several dozen centimeters in the last several years (Reutebuch et al., 2003, Hyyppä et al., 2005), which directly influences the quality of analyses. Analyzing local variability and the variability of whole mountain chains according to Tien et al. (2008) means that this type of data is the most reliable due to its accuracy, and its analysis may help to elucidate the existing research problems.

    The purpose of this study is to assess the impact of terrain topography, including altitude above sea level, aspect and slope, as well as selected stand characteristics, on the dieback of tree stands. This constitutes an attempt to summarize processes occurring in three different areas and to determine similarities and differences, like influence of forest dieback on its structure and finally on current health condition. Answering this questions may result in a better understanding of the mechanisms related to forest dieback in the mountains of Central Europe.

    2. Materials and methods

    2.1. Study area

    In this project, three mountain areas located in south and south-west Poland (Fig. 1) were analyzed: Western Sudety Mountains (Area “A”), Eastern Sudety Mountains (Area “B”), and Western Beskidy Mountains (Area “C”). The total study area was approximately 364 kha, of which the Western Sudety Mountains extend for 70.5 kha (19.4%), the Eastern Sudety Mountains extend for 118 kha (32.4%), and the Western Beskidy Mountains extend for 175.5 kha (48.2%). The percentage of dead and weakened trees in individual areas was 0.7% (Area “A”), 0.9% (Area “B”) and 1.5% (Area “C”) (Fig. 2). It should be noted that in 2012 (the same year when data about dead trees were collected) all three analyzed forest areas were in various growth stages and generally they differed in terms of stand structures. Western Sudety (Area “A”) is an area which was afflicted by the deforestation the most in the eighties of last century (Fig. 3). The main reason for this was considerable air pollution causing acid rains and primal species composition change, especially in the lower parts of the mountains with deciduous species (mainly beech) and spruce of foreign provenance. Together with these factors insect pests outbreak has occurred. In the beginning nun moth (Lymantria monacha L.), and afterwards bark beetle (Ips typographus L.) were reported (Capecki et al., 1989, Zawiła-Niedźwiecki and Glasenapp, 1994, Grodzki, 1995, Sobik and Błaś, 1999, Bałazy, 2002). Currently, the area is regenerated using spruce of the local provenance, and conversion of spruce stands in favor of deciduous species takes part in the lower parts of the area.

    Easter Sudety (Area “B”) is an area, where the existing stands were also considerably rebuilt, the harvested areas are regenerated almost solely with spruce. Even though the region did not suffer so much as Western Sudety, the drought occurring there in the recent years caused the tree stands weakness and considerable rise in the insect pests occurrence. Right now there is a rise in wood harvest due to sanitary reasons recorded and the progress of the process is expected (Grodzki, 2018).

    Beskid Śląski and Beskid Żywiecki (Area “C”) are the areas where also foreign spruce provenances were introduced. However, unlike Western Sudety, the air pollution did not play such significant role. Besides the insect pests, the more significant contribution to spruce dieback seems to come from drought in 2006 and inappropriate silviculture methods (Niemtur et al., 2005, Astel et al., 2008, Astel et al., 2009, Barszcz et al., 2007, Małek et al., 2010, Małek et al., 2012, Małek et al., 2014, Szabla and Małek, 2017), which also contributed to the development of fungi pathogens infestation. Among the analyzed areas, it is the one which suffered the most due to mass deforestation in 2012.

    Spruce was and still is the main species at all three areas. The Holding maintains an integrated database for the forests (State Forests’ Information System – SILP), and updates not only the area changes, but also all species composition and many other parameters on an annual basis.

    2.2. Data description

    In the study, data were acquired in July and August 2012 via a DMCII digital airborne camera and LMS-Q680i laser scanner. DMCII cameras take pictures in the range of four spectral channels – red (R), green (G), blue (B) and near infrared (IR), as well as panchromatic images. Orthophotomaps were developed out of the pictures in RGB and CIR color models with 0.5 m spatial resolution and 8-bit radiometric resolution. ALS data had an average density of approximately 6 points per m2, height error up to 15 cm and an X, Y positioning error less than 20 cm.

    As the basis for surface analyses, vector layers from the central SILP database were used. Each polygon representing a sub-compartment contained the assigned information regarding the surface area, species composition, age of trees, dominant species, performed operations, etc.

    2.3. GIS and remote sensing data analysis

    Because data originating from various sources have been used, the data processing and analysis was performed in several parallel stages (Fig. 4).

    2.3.1. Digital terrain models and derivative product generation

    A digital terrain model (DTM) was generated using the triangulated irregular network (TIN) model active algorithm implemented in the TerraScan environment (Axelsson, 2000). The spatial resolution was set to 0.5 m. The height accuracy of the model according to a study by Stereńczak et al. (2016) was estimated to be 0.22 cm (RMSE) and 0.14 cm (BIAS). Based on the digital terrain model, the following rasters were generated in the ArcGIS 10.3 environment: slope (spatial resolution 0.5 m) and aspect (spatial resolution 100 m). It was necessary to resample the aspect raster from 0.5 m to 100 m resolution in order to acquire gradient information about the whole slope.

    2.3.2. Single treetop detection

    To generate treetops, Impact Lidar software was applied. Impact Lidar is a software package developed by Joanneum Research in Graz, Austria (Hirschmugl, 2008). This package is a set of tools that enables data processing of airborne laser scans. In the first steps, the digital terrain model was generated by normalized-convolution interpolation based on the points classified as ground. Due to gaps occurring in the model, in the regions where points were classified as water bodies or buildings, fill-hole interpolation was applied several times until all of the no-data gaps were filled. Next, based on the first reflected laser points classified as vegetation, a digital surface model was created. Subtraction of these two models resulted in a normalized crown height model (nDSM); additionally, a nDSM smoothing algorithm was applied. The spatial resolution of all models was 0.5 m.

    Using the nDSM as input files, treetop detection was performed. To this end, a combination of the Laplacian and the Gauss filter (Gonzalez and Woods, 2002, Schardt et al., 2015) was applied and was further described as the Laplacian of Gauss filter (LoG). The treetop detection process consists of several steps. The LoG filter is used to blur the image and the degree of blurring is determined by the value of the standard deviation (σ). To detect trees of different sizes, three degrees of filtering were involved, depending on the σ value (2, 3, 4) (Hirschmugl, 2008). Then, the original nDSM is processed using a simple Local Maxima Approach method (LMA). Next, the LoG images are weighted according to their respective levels and then stacked. Intensity maxima are detected using LMA in the image produced in the previous step. The intensity maxima are dragged to their nearest height maximum (Hirschmugl, 2008, Schardt et al., 2015). An example result of treetop generation is presented in Fig. 5.

    The accuracy of single tree detection was depicted using 16 test areas. Single test area size was 100x100 meters. The areas were located in forest stands with a Norway spruce species share ≥0.8. In the forest stands with age up to 20, between 20 and 60 and above 60 years −4, 8 and 4 test areas were placed, respectively. For each area, the number of treetops was calculated using LMA. Next, four GIS operators independently, and not knowing the detection results, indicated the identified treetops in the test areas. The results of the manual (reference) and automatic detection were compared. The average automatic single treetops detection result was estimated to be 77%. The accuracy varied with respective age groups. In the age class group of up to 20 years, it was 75%; in the group ranging from 20 to 60 years it was 83%; and for ages above 60 years, it was 80%. In the case of younger age class groups, the LMA algorithm showed underestimation error and in the case of the age class above 60 years, the algorithm overestimated the amount of trees. Altogether in three study areas, the LMA algorithm detected 38.7 million trees, most of which could be identified as forest stand first layer trees.

    2.3.3. Dead tree classification

    The first stage of classification consisted of the orthophotomap range limited to the territory belonging to the State Forests National Forest Holding. Second, a binary raster was generated based on the nDSM. The binary raster was classified as follows: a NoData value was assigned to the terrain with height below 1.0 m, and a value of one was assigned to the terrain with a height above 1.0 m. The binary raster was used to mask large forest plantations, public and forest roads, meadows and gaps in forest fragments with lower crown coverage out of the forest areas.

    Next, the training polygons were selected based on which spectral characteristics and threshold values of the respective classes were depicted. Altogether, 1800 training polygons for two classes, dead and weakened trees, and healthy trees were indicated in every study area using the Classification Workflow in the ENVI 5.0 environment. Supervised classification was performed using the Maximum Likelihood Algorithm. In the maximum likelihood method, the probability of affiliation to the defined class was calculated for each pixel. Pixels were classified to the class of the highest probability of affiliation. The algorithm also allowed the value of the threshold probability to be defined, below which the analyzed pixel remained unclassified. The layer of control points was clipped with a polygon layer of dead and weakened trees obtained from the first stage. The verification has shown that dead and weakened trees are classified with an total accuracy of 95% (Area A), 97% (Area B) and 96% (Area C).     2.3.4. Database creation

    Spatial information from layers acquired during earlier stages was added to the forest compartments shapefile polygon layer. The following information was added to every forest compartment:

    •

    an area, site type, dominant species, dominant species share, stocking, age, DBH (based on SILP database);

    •

    an average altitude above sea level, average gradient value and dominant aspect in the compartment (data from DTM and derived products);

    •

    a height class, slope class, and aspect class based on the average and dominant values: 100 m altitude intervals (200–300, 300–400, …, 1200–1300 m a.s.l.), 5° slope intervals (0–5, 5–10, 10–15, 15–20, 20–25, >25°), and 8 aspects (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW, N);

    •

    the fraction of dead trees in the compartment (data from orthophotomap);

    •

    the number of trees in the compartment (data from tree detection).

    Analyses were performed using data from 28,966 forest stands divided into 3 regions marked as A, B and C (consisting of 8374, 13,998 and 6594 forest stands, respectively).

    2.3.5. Statistical analyses

    The degree of damage of each stand in the area of interest was defined as a ratio of the number of dead and significantly weakened trees, and all trees identified within particular stand. Dead and weakened trees have been classified based on the data from CIR orthophotomaps.

    The basic analysis was based on differences between the share of damaged trees in individual classes counted in relation to the total number of trees. The significance of differences in the percent share of damaged trees was examined using the proportion test (Newcombe, 1998, Wilson, 1927), and differences between the distributions of features were examined with the chi-square test (Agresti, 2007). All the analyses were performed using the prop.test function of the stats package in the R software environment (R Core Team, 2012) at a 0.05 significance level.

    Apart from the overall characteristics of the damaged trees, an attempt was made to build models allowing for calculation of the share of damaged trees based on the terrain topography and selected stand characteristics. Because of the distinct differences among analyzed areas (different geographical characteristics, significantly different average degree of damage and the nature of influence of particular factors on the stand damage), analyses were done separately for each of the distinguished regions (A, B and C, respectively).

    All individual variables characterizing individual forest stands have been converted or transformed to be suitable for the regression analysis. The share of dead trees was determined as a ratio of the number of dead trees to the overall number of trees in each particular forest compartment. A plot aspect, initially expressed as an azimuth (A) in degrees, was transformed to numerical values according to the approach proposed by Beers et al., 1966, Socha, 2010, assuming an azimuth of 45°, i.e., NE and SW aspects, as a reference point indicating extreme climatic values:

    (2)aspect=sinA+45+1

    Site types (a total of 23) were combined into broader groups, considering the areas occupied by each site type and their similarity in fertility. In region A, sites were merged into vegetation types according to the site fertility and location (upland mixed coniferous, upland mixed broadleaved, upland broadleaved, mountain mixed coniferous, mountain mixed broadleaved, and mountain broadleaved forest vegetation type). In regions B and C, only the site fertility itself was used for further classification (coniferous, mixed coniferous, mixed broadleaved, and broadleaved forest vegetation type). Site types have been divided according to the Polish site classification used for the forest management planning (Siedliskowe podstawy hodowli lasu, Site bases of silviculture 2004). Resulting site types were included in the model using dummy variables.

    The dependent variable in the model is a share of dead trees in the stand, i.e., the probability of finding a dead tree within the compartment. In the case of such problems, the logistic regression is frequently used and assumes the binomial distribution of the predicted variable. However, in the analyzed case, such response variable can be problematic when using a classic generalized regression approach, as it contain many “zero” values and the vast majority of dependent probability variable values do not exceed 0.05. In such a situation it was necessary to modify the procedure and use the approach designed for “data with excess zeros” or “zero-inflated data” (Welsh et al., 1996, Ridout et al., 1998, Barry and Welsh, 2002), even though in some literature cases the simplest approach was applied and ignored the abundance of zeros, e.g., when modeling the probability of mountain pine beetle red-attack damage (White et al., 2006, Wulder et al., 2006).

    For modeling purposes a double-step approach has been applied, as it seems to be the most robust and logical, and thus relatively easy for interpretation. It has been used, among others, in studies on species distribution (Barry and Welsh, 2002), mapping percent tree mortality due to mountain pine beetle damage (Long and Lawrence, 2016) or modeling of regeneration processes in natural forests (Drozdowski, 2013). In the first step, a presence-absence model is created using a re-coded response variable. During the transformation, the dependent variable gets a value equal to 0 if the count of dead trees on the plot equals 0, and a value of 1 otherwise. This model can be treated as a typical one for the logistic regression analysis. In the second step, a model of abundance or probability is developed for cases with a response variable greater than 0.

    Logistics regression models do not use the probability of an event directly as a response variable. The most frequently used expression to substitute probability, p, is called odds, and it is calculated as

    (3)Odds=p1-p

    Due to this transformation, the probability 0 < p < 1 forms values from 0 to +∞ and its logarithm from −∞ to +∞. In such a case the logarithm of odds (called logit) is represented by

    (4)logit(p)=lnp1-p

    It can be modeled using a linear regression approach, usually a generalized linear model (GLM):

    (5)logit(p)=lnp1-p=α+β1·x1+β2·x2+...+βn·xn

    In both stages, models were built using a forward stepwise regression approach, i.e. by gradually including variables with the strongest influence on the variable of interest, as long as the model can be considered “full”, with all the parameters significant at a 0.05 significance level. To avoid redundancy and multicollinearity of predictors, the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) coefficient was calculated for all independent variables (Fox, 1991, O’Brien, 2007, Socha, 2010, Zurr et al., 2010, Drozdowski, 2013). VIF coefficient is calculated based on the multiple R2j (coefficient of determination) between a given variable j and all other covariates already included in the model:

    (6)VIFj=11-Rj2

    Values of VIF in excess of a certain threshold value indicate the presence of multicollinearity that can cause model over-parametrization. The most frequently used threshold value for VIF analysis equals 4 (Fox, 1991, Larose, 2006, Socha, 2010, Drozdowski, 2013). However, various authors mention both higher, e.g., 10 or more (Montgomery et al., 2012), and lower limit values, e.g., 2 or 3 (Zurr et al., 2010). In this study, parameters with calculated VIF values above 2 were excluded from the model. The overall goodness-of-fit was determined using the Aikaike Information Criterion (AIC) value.

    In the second stage of the analysis, models describing magnitude of damage were created based exclusively on the plots with observed dead trees. The extent of damage (dependent variable) was expressed by the share (percentage) of dead trees in the compartment. Such data are frequently modeled using beta regression (Long and Lawrence, 2016), which is based on the assumption that the variable of interest follows beta distribution and that its mean is linearly related to a set of predictors with unknown coefficients and link functions. This approach is suitable for large data sets and addresses issues of heteroskedasticity, or skewness, that are commonly observed in data expressed as shares, percentages or proportions (Cribari-Neto and Zeileis, 2010).

    All calculations were performed using the glm function of R software (R Core Team, 2012). VIF analysis was performed using the vif function from the car R package (Fox and Weisberg, 2011).

    3. Results

    Generalized linear models were built to assess the influence of various factors on the logarithm of the share of dead trees in the stand. Due to the use of the stepwise regression and elimination of strongly related predictors, all of the included independent variables have a significant influence on the variable of interest. In all of the obtained models, coefficients marked as “b” are slope coefficients. They are interpreted as a change (increase or decrease – depending on the coefficient sign) of the dependent variable (in our case logit(y) or log odds of the dead tree to be found) if a given predictor changes by one unit or one class. For example, if the slope b equals 0.0239114 as a predictor for spruce share, it means that if the share of the species increases by 1 unit (here: 1 class, i.e., 10%), the log odds of the damaged tree share increases by 0.0239114. This can be interpreted as a higher percentage of damaged trees for stands with a higher share of spruce in the species composition. If the slope equals −0.0003 for altitude class as a predictor, it means that if the altitude of the stand increases by 1 unit (here: 1 class, i.e., 100 m), the log odds of the damaged tree share decreases by 0.0003. This can be interpreted as a decreased percentage of damaged trees for stands located in higher altitudes, or an increased share of damaged trees for lower altitudes.

    4. Discussion

    During the course of our research, various models were built, which bring us closer to the description of the dependencies between the selected topographic and environmental properties and Norway spruce dieback in the Sudety and the Carpathian Mountains. Even though the applied remote sensing techniques allowed for the identification of almost all of the trees in the upper layer of the stand in the area of interest (altogether approximately 40,000,000), including more than tens of thousands of dead trees, this should still be considered as information about the effects of dieback, not the reason. Therefore, it is impossible to state what is the subject sample share of the spruce damaged by the most significant factors in Central Europe, such as wind or bark beetles (Schelhaas et al., 2003, Svoboda et al., 2010, Svoboda et al., 2012).

    The winds blowing from the Atlantic have a strong impact on the analyzed areas, not only in their dominant aspect (in this area W and SW – Wallen, 1977), but also with regard to the deposition of pollutants influencing spruce forest dieback (Feliksik, 1995, Moravćik and Ćernŷ, 1995). Research carried out in 1989 and 1993 in the Beskidy Mountains (Zawada, 1995), i.e. prior to the disaster in this region, indicated that the content of particular elements in the surface layers of soil was higher at heights of 800–1100 m above sea level than the limit values adopted for sulfur, nitrogen, and iron in the soil, in the context of the forest dieback. Topographic aspects of sulfur variability on particular slopes analyzed at that time have shown that the areas of the northern aspect were characterized by a greater content of this element in the surface soil layer. Later, forest dieback in the Beskidy Mountains dominated in the northern aspect, i.e. in the areas of increased quantity of sulfur in the soil. Furthermore, in the Beskidy Mountains, the differences are visible between the direction of the dieback processes today and in the past. In addition, the latest climate and dendrochronological research (Błaś, 2000; Godek et al., 2012), as well as research based on remote sensing techniques (Main-Knorn et al., 2009), confirms the strong relation between the aspect, winds, and the reduced growth or mortality. Although in many publications a clear relation: aspect-wind and direction-defoliation was proven, within the existing research no significant dependency between spruce dieback and aspect was found, except for the Eastern Sudety, where aspect was a factor that was positively correlated with the probability of damage occurrence. It is difficult to explain such results of the analysis unequivocally, bearing in mind the fact that it is the Eastern Sudety and not the Western Sudety, which constitutes the first orographic barrier for air masses flowing from the west Europe. However, it should be noted that out of all three analyzed areas, it is the Eastern Sudetes that currently has the largest number of old stands and they are the ones most exposed to insect gradation. The impact by aspect on the forest dieback process (caused by bark beetles) in Beskid Żywiecki was also proven by Grodzki (2014), who highlighted at the same time, that the year 2007 was exceptional, as substantially greater forest dieback was observed on eastern slopes in that period (Grodzki, 2009). It may explain partially the role of aspect in the Eastern Sudetes in 2012. Such a situation may also means, that pollutants transported by the winds no longer plays an important role because comparing to the 70s or 80s they generally decreased (NOx and SOx). Another reason for the lack of this relationship has been provided by Błaś et al. (2012), who suggest that the largest deforestation was observed at sites with the highest frequency of severely acidified fog connected with low-lying cloud base of orographic origin. This concerns the western and north-western edges of the Sudety Mountains (Jizera, Karkonosze) as well as the Beskids (Moravian-Silesian Beskid). However, in the second and following orographic ranges, where the cloud base rarely covers the subalpine spruce forest, deforestation processes were limited. These spatial regularities are closely connected with the atmospheric circulation and transformation of the flowing air masses. This is related to their gradual depletion and partial removal of pollutants by air masses infiltrating into the mountain along the WNW-ESE profile. This results in a systematic reduction of wet deposition efficiency. It also shows that the aspect-wind and direction-defoliation relationships vary according to the position in relation to the whole mountain range and prevailing wind conditions.

    Godek et al. (2015) specifies that deteriorated forest condition is observed mainly on the ridges and summits, where the most important role in atmospheric tree stand damage play wind and rime, and also the processes affecting the intensity of the deposition of pollutants. In the studied area, the decline of influence of processes stated above was observed in the range of 600–900 m a.s.l. According to the authors, this is the safest area for the functioning of forest ecosystem with spruce as a dominant species.

    Grodzki (1998a) demonstrated that there is a relation between the contents of sulfur in spruce needles and the altitude and aspect. This is important since the content of this element in needles in the Western Sudety Mountains, as well as in the Beskidy Mountains, exceeded generally adopted norms according to Bonneau (1991), as well as Austrian norms (Kwapis, 1990). The altitude also plays an important role as a factor in all phases of forest dieback processes in the Central European mountains, as was proven by Grodzki, 2007, Klopčič et al., 2009, Mezei et al., 2014a, Mezei et al., 2014b. Influence of altitude may also change in the future depending on the future combination of temperature and precipitation (Seidl et al., 2017).

    Our analyses confirmed that log odds of the occurence of dead trees increases with increasing altitude in areas A and C (first modeling stage, parameters b1 in Eqs. (8), (10)). However, it is interesting that as it was demonstrated by the statistical analyses performed in the second stage of modeling, in the areas already classified as damaged, the altitude above sea level has an impact on the increase of the share of dead trees exclusively in the case of the Beskidy Mountains (area C). In the case of the Western and Eastern Sudety, along with the increase in altitude, the share of dead trees in damaged areas decreases. Among all three areas, only the results observed in the Beskidy are consistent with the results acquired by the authors mentioned above. It should be noted that in the Sudety, the forest dieback process ended two decades earlier, and in the case of the Beskidy, the year 2012 (though significantly better than the previous ones) was still under the pressure of bark beetle gradation, which was gradually moving up to the higher areas (Grodzki, 2010). The natural consequence of the above was the number of examined dead trees, which was substantially lower in the Sudety than in the Beskidy. Partly, this theory is confirmed by the research carried out by Lausch et al. (2011), who have proven, in the context of bark beetle mass occurrence, that in the epidemic phase, mortality was more severe in trees showing lower slenderness ratios at higher elevations. The negative response of the magnitude of damage to altitude in disturbed stands could be also related to current climate changes and their impact on forests, as described by Seidl et al. (2017). Higher temperature in lower altitudes may also cause a higher risk of bark beetle outbreaks. The answer to the question of whether this situation is non-recurring or perhaps a change in existing trends, could be only provided by research based on re-scanning and the reanalysis of dead trees in this area, additional analysis of stocking or analysis of previous salvage cutting.

    The slope, though mentioned by Mezei et al. (2014a) as one of the elements which influence the probability of damage to spruce stands (the higher the slope, the greater probability of damage occurrence), was determined to be significant only in the case of the Western Sudety. For the Eastern Sudety and the Beskidy, the slope did not have any impact on the probability of damage to tree stands. This situation might be explained by the fact that out of the three investigated areas, it is the Western Sudety that constitutes the first orographic barrier in the way of flowing air masses, and the role of negative factors is additionally reinforced there. At the same time, our analyses showed that the slope does not significantly influence the share of dead trees in damaged areas in the Western Sudety. Features, which have an impact on the increase of probability of damage occurrence in all three areas, are also the age of tree stands and their volume is also correlated with stand DBH, as well as stand high, which are mentioned as important factors in the work by Mezei et al. (2014b).

    Statistical analyses performed during the second stage enabled the assessment of factors that significantly influence the size of damage in areas already classified as damaged. In all three areas, the size of damage grows along with the increase in the share of spruce in forest compartments, which is quite an obvious conclusion and is also indicated by other authors (Grodzki, 1992, Grodzki, 2007, Klopčič et al., 2009, Mezei et al., 2014a, Mezei et al., 2014b). Interestingly, at the same time, the size of damage in forest compartments where dead trees were observed decreases along with the altitude in areas A and B. It is important that in all three areas, along with the growth of altitude, the stocking of stands decreases, which may partially explain the obtained results. As far as factors that influence the size of damage in all three areas concerned, no significant impact of slope or aspect was stated. The results differ from the observations made in the 1980s in the Western Sudety by Zawiła-Niedźwiecki (1994), who proved the impact of aspect on forest dieback. Since pollution by sulfur and nitrogen oxides is currently not a problem in this area (Adamski and Wawrzoniak, 1998, European Environment Agency, 2016), the reasons for the tree dieback have also changed, and this may be a probable explanation of occurring differences. The research carried out by Zawiła-Niedźwiecki et al., confirms (1994) that the aspect affected the forest stands dieback in Western Sudety precisely, when the stands of the more advanced age were dominant. Similar situation can be observed now in the Eastern Sudety Mountains, where the older stands are dominant, and especially there the aspect affects significantly the forest dieback. Although the increase of stocking positively influences the probability of damage occurrence in all three areas (which was discussed above), in case of the Western Sudety the size of damage itself is limited by stocking. This finding is likely due to a completely different distribution of stands stocking in the Western Sudety, where forest dieback in the 1980s affected large areas of the upper parts of the forest, distorting the age class structure and thus the stocking. In all three areas, the size of damage falls down along with the increase of deciduous species, which is another equally obvious observation.

    5. Conclusions

    •

    The obtained results clearly show that there is no one universal answer to the question which factors have a significant influence on forest dieback processes in the mountains. The impact of various climate and topography characteristics on tree mortality have to be analyzed in connection with the local conditions and the historical development of the process.

    •

    The age of tree stands is negatively correlated with the probability of damage occurrence in all three areas, and the increase of altitude above sea level increases the probability of damage occurrence exclusively in the case of the Western Sudety and the Beskidy. The influence on such a situation might be caused by the fact that both the Western Sudety and the Beskidy have larger share of forest stands of lower age class that are more susceptible to changing weather conditions along with height growth. Altitude is of no importance in the case of the Eastern Sudety which, in turn, is the only area where the probability of damage occurrence is correlated with the aspect. It is also the only region which consists of more overmature stands. The only one out of the investigated areas, where the impact of slope on the probability of damage occurrence was proven, is the Western Sudety. Stocking increases the probability of damage occurrence in the Eastern Sudety and the Beskidy, but it has no impact in the Western Sudety.

    •

    The size of damage in all three areas is influenced by the growth of the share of spruce in forest compartments; however, it is interesting that along with the increase in altitude, the damage in area A and B decreases. The Western Sudety is the exclusive region where the size of damage grows along with an increase in age of the stands, and this is the only area where the growing altitude above sea level has an impact on the decrease of damage. As far as the other areas are concerned, both abovementioned features were of no significance in the context of damage size; however, significant elements there included the share of deciduous habitats (mixed conifer site group, mixed broadleaved site group, broadleaved site group), which was negatively correlated with the size of damage.

    •

    The carried out studies confirm that in order to acquire smaller mortality of the Norway spruce it is recommended to strive for greater diversity of species composition preferring broad-leaved species (Capecki, 1997). The negative factor that uniformly affects the mortality on all areas is the age of the stands. The factor is mentioned as one of the important reasons of the stands forest die-back in West Beskidy Mountains and as the present studies confirm, on all three areas, it should be an issue of foresters special attention. As the species structure shows, soon the mass deforestation might also affect the area of Eastern Sudety Mountains, on which we have very large share of the old age class tree stands.

    •

    Similarly the increase of wood stocking negatively affects the health of the spruce stands. Consequently it is important to prevent too high stand density by shaping it in a proper way since the youngest age. Willing to improve the monitoring of the stands health it is required to focus on the most endangered stands in the first place. Ergo, in the case of all three analyzed areas, the focus should be on the older stands and those which have larger share of spruce in species composition

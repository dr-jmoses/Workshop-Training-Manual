    Abstract

    Aim

    Similar to species richness, ecological interactions can vary across latitudinal and environmental gradients. Knowing the patterns and drivers of such variation could help us to better understand the role of species interactions in maintaining biodiversity. In this study, we analysed the macroecological patterns of the structure and interaction beta diversity of interaction networks involving trees and ants.

    Location

    Twenty‐nine sites encompassing 20 degrees of latitude throughout the Neotropical savanna.

    Time period

    2010–2015.

    Major taxa studied

    Trees and arboreal nesting ants.

    Methods

    For each site, we built an interaction network and calculated network size, interaction diversity (Shannon diversity of interactions), specialization, modularity, nestedness, and interaction dissimilarity (contribution of each network to the regional pool of possible interactions). We also determined how interaction beta diversity varied among all sampling sites. Net primary productivity (NPP), temperature and rainfall were evaluated as potential correlates of the observed changes in network descriptors and interaction beta diversity.

    Results

    We found no latitudinal gradient in network specialization, nestedness or modularity. However, sites at higher latitudes had larger networks, higher interaction diversity and higher interaction dissimilarity, and this was correlated mainly with the latitudinal variation in NPP. Interaction rewiring generated by the reassembly of the interactions between the same species in different sites was the main contributor to the total interaction beta diversity. However, the level of interaction rewiring was independent of the geographical and environmental distance between sampling sites.

    Main conclusions

    Ant–tree network structure remained relatively invariant across the latitudinal and environmental gradient possibly due to high interaction rewiring among the partners. Moreover, our findings show that more productive sites, located at higher latitudes, have high dissimilarity to the regional pool of possible interactions (i.e., strong interaction filtering), indicating that these sites significantly contribute to the maintenance of interaction biodiversity in Neotropical savannas.

    1 INTRODUCTION

    A widespread pattern in ecology is that the similarity in the structure (e.g., in the number and identity of the component species) of biological communities decreases as the geographical and environmental distance among them increases (Hubbell, 2001; Nekola & White, 1999). Deterministic (i.e., niche‐based theory, response to environmental variations) and stochastic (i.e., neutral theory, geographical barriers and dispersal abilities) processes underlying the distance decay of community similarity have been evaluated for a wide range of taxa (Dallas & Poisot, 2018; Guisan & Thuiller, 2005). However, despite the increasing number of studies on distance decay of similarity, there is still little knowledge about how and why biotic interactions vary across ecological gradients.

    One issue of current debate is that the ecological and evolutionary mechanisms shaping species richness and composition seem to be different from those underlying biotic interactions (Poisot, Canard, Mouillot, Mouquet, & Gravel, 2012; Poisot, Guéveneux‐Julien, Fortin, Gravel, & Legendre, 2017). This is because two species can co‐occur and not necessarily interact with each other due to differences in species abundances or phenological mismatches (Vázquez, Blüthgen, Cagnolo, & Chacoff, 2009), making biotic interactions among species potentially more variable over ecological gradients than species composition (Poisot et al., 2017; Poisot, Stouffer, & Gravel, 2015). Therefore, knowing the mechanisms of such changes in species interactions could improve our understanding of the dynamics of ecological interactions and their role in maintaining biodiversity.

    Studies dealing with species‐rich interaction networks have provided new insights into the ecological and evolutionary processes structuring the organization of biotic interactions (Dáttilo & Rico‐Gray, 2018). This is because these complex networks exhibit non‐random patterns of species interactions that do not emerge when we study interactions only between pairs of species. Two structural properties frequently reported in interaction networks are nestedness and modularity. In nested networks, specialist species engaged in few interactions are connected to a subset of generalist species with more interactions (Bascompte, Jordano, Melián, & Olesen, 2003). Modularity describes a pattern where there are subgroups of species of one trophic level that interact more frequently with a group of species of another trophic level (Olesen, Bascompte, Dupont, & Jordano, 2007). Both nestedness and modularity are present in different types of networks, from mutualistic to antagonistic interactions. In addition, other structural descriptors also help us to describe the organization of interaction networks. Two of these descriptors are interaction diversity and network specialization. The first estimates the degree to which a community is rich in species and ecological interactions (and how these interactions are distributed among all species), whereas the latter indicates the exclusiveness of interactions and is related to the fragility of co‐extinctions of a community, as communities with low specialization levels are more robust to co‐extinction processes (Blüthgen, 2010).

    Macroecological research dealing with interaction networks has advanced rapidly during recent years (Dalsgaard et al., 2013; Pauw & Stanway, 2015; Schleuning et al., 2012, 2014; Trøjelsgaard & Olesen, 2013). These studies have shown, for instance, that plant–pollinator networks show latitudinal trends in network structure, associated with current and/or historical variations in climate (Dalsgaard et al., 2013). In general, mutualistic networks tend to be more modular and specialized in tropical species‐rich communities due to warmer temperatures as well as greater historical temperature stability in those regions, favouring niche differentiation and specialization (Martín‐González et al., 2015; Trøjelsgaard & Olesen, 2013). However, some studies have observed that networks involving both mutualistic (Sebástián‐González, Dalsgaard, Sandel, & Guimarães, 2015) and antagonistic (Morris, Gripenber, Lewis, & Roslin, 2014) interactions are structured independently of geographical variations in climate (Morris et al., 2014) or exhibit reversed latitudinal patterns (Dalsgaard et al., 2017; Schleuning et al., 2012). Thus, there is still much debate on how interaction networks are structured over broad spatial scales, and this topic remains to be elucidated in detail (Moles & Ollerton, 2016).

    In addition to evaluating how the structural organization of ecological networks varies along geographical and environmental gradients, we also need to understand how the interactions between pairs of species vary within different ecological contexts. In recent years, an encouraging framework to study the dissimilarity of interactions between species has attracted the attention of ecologists (CaraDonna et al., 2017; Carstensen, Sabatino, Trøjelsgaard, & Morellato, 2014; Poisot et al., 2012). This framework considers that beta diversity can be interpreted not only in terms of the changes in species composition among communities, but also highlights those sites that most contribute to the regional pool of species interactions (CaraDonna et al., 2017; Carstensen et al., 2014; Novotny, 2009; Poisot et al., 2017, 2015). Despite the potential use of interaction beta diversity to evaluate the ecological and evolutionary dynamics of species interactions, the amount of theoretical and empirical information available is still limited (but see Poisot et al., 2015).

    Here, we evaluated the macroecological patterns of the structure and beta diversity of interaction networks involving trees and arboreal nesting ants, an extremely common ecological interaction in the Neotropical Cerrado savannas. In this ant–tree interaction, ants nest in pre‐formed tree cavities (such as those made by wood‐boring insects) or occasionally on the ground at the base of the tree, and while foraging on the host tree, they can reduce herbivory (Rico‐Gray & Oliveira, 2007). Arboreal ants are a numerically and behaviourally dominant group of arthropods on the foliage of Cerrado trees, where they feed on resources provided directly (e.g., extrafloral nectar) or indirectly (insect exudates and prey) by their host trees (Schoereder, Sobrinho, Madureira, Ribas, & Oliveira, 2010). Therefore, the Cerrado savannas provide us with an excellent system to study ant–tree interaction networks. Although the number and identity of the ant species can vary with both the size and identity of the host tree, most arboreal ants are generalists and thus can nest in a wide variety of tree species (Camarota, Powell, Vasconcelos, Priest, & Marquis, 2015; Powell, Costa, Lopes, & Vasconcelos, 2011, and unpublished field observations). Ant exclusion experiments, on the other hand, have shown that ants often reduce the amount of herbivory on their host trees, but that the occurrence and magnitude of this effect depend both on the overall ant abundance and the composition of the ant species community, which in turn can be influenced by both the size and identity of the host tree (Del‐Claro et al. 2016; Schoereder et al., 2010). Therefore, the biotic interaction studied here is highly facultative and can range from commensal (where plants provide shelter for ants) to mutualistic (where ants receive shelter, whereas plants receive some degree of protection against herbivory).

    We tackled the following questions: (a) Is the organization of ant–tree interaction networks structured according to latitude in terms of network size, interaction diversity, specialization, nestedness and modularity? (b) Are current environmental conditions correlated with the organization of ant–tree interaction networks? (c) What is the relative influence of geographical and environmental distances between sites on interaction beta diversity? In our study region, the savannas located at mid to high latitudes are often the most productive ones (Vasconcelos et al., 2018, 2019), and such latitudes tend to support a higher diversity of trees (i.e., higher variety of food and nesting resources) (Françoso, Haidar, & Machado, 2016; Ratter, Bridgewater, & Ribeiro, 2003) and ant species (Vasconcelos et al., 2018, 2019). Thus, we expected to find greater network size and interaction diversity at higher latitudes. Moreover, we expected to find greater specialization and modularity and lower nestedness in species‐richer networks as a consequence of mechanisms minimizing interspecific competition (Bastolla et al., 2009). In addition, we also postulated that the organization of ant–tree interaction networks would be influenced by geographical variations in plant productivity and rainfall. Finally, we postulated that the greater the differences in both geographical distance and environmental conditions between sites, the greater the dissimilarity in interaction beta diversity.

    2 METHODS

    2.1 Study sites and sampling

    Our study region spanned 20 degrees of latitude (from 04°05′ S to 24°11′ S), encompassing most of the Cerrado savanna biome in central Brazil. We recorded the arboreal ants associated with each host tree in 29 sites that were spread across this region (Figure 1). We restricted our sampling to the most typical Cerrado savanna physiognomy, known as cerrado sensu stricto. The latter has an open canopy of trees 3–8 m tall and a dense ground layer composed of grasses, herbs and small shrubs (Oliveira‐Filho & Ratter, 2002).

    Ant and plant collections were performed once at each of the 29 sampling sites. The timing of sampling varied depending on the availability of personnel, funding and access authorization, which was granted in all cases. However, we found that sampling completeness was not correlated with latitude (R2 = 0.29; p = 0.12), indicating that there was no latitudinal bias in the sampling completeness. Moreover, as ant colonies tend to be long‐term occupants of the trees, seasonal variation in the structure of arboreal ant assemblages associated with individual trees is extremely limited (Camarota et al., 2015).

    At each site, we established three line transects (360 m long), with at least 1 km distance from each other, and along each transect, the nearest tree (> 2.5 m in height) found every 40 m was selected (n = 30 trees randomly sampled per site). It is important to mention that none of the tree species sampled in this study had specialized structures for sheltering ant nests (i.e., domatia). Therefore, our networks did not involve any extremely specialized interactions, such as those between ants and myrmecophytes. All sampled trees were identified with the aid of an expert of the Cerrado flora (see Acknowledgments). For those few tree species whose taxonomic identification was not possible, we assigned a unique morphospecies code.

    To collect ants, four pitfall traps were fixed on the branches of each tree, according to the methodology described in Vasconcelos et al. (2018). The traps remained in operation for 48 hr. Collected ants were sorted to morphospecies (subsequently referred to as “species”) and, whenever possible, identified using available taxonomic keys.

    We classified the collected ant species according to their main nesting and foraging strata as “arboreal” or “ground‐dwellers”, and only the former ones were included in the analyses presented here. Several ant genera have strict nesting habits (e.g., all species of Azteca, Cephalotes, Nesomyrmex and Myrmelachista are arboreal), and in this case, the classification of the individual species (and morphospecies) was straightforward. For species from other genera (including those in which most, but not all members are arboreal; e.g., Camponotus and Pseudomyrmex), our classification was based on the relative frequency with which each species (or morphospecies) was collected in the arboreal traps versus those placed on the ground (cf. Vasconcelos et al., 2018). In addition, we used information from previous studies of our research group on arboreal ants (Camarota et al., 2016) to classify some of the collected ant specimens (including especially those that have not been named yet, such as Solenopsis sp1 and Tapinoma sp1). Most arboreal ants nest in pre‐formed tree cavities. The most notable exceptions are Ectatomma tuberculatum and Paraponera clavata, which nevertheless were also classified as arboreal as their ground nests are always built at the base of the tree on which they predominantly forage. Although non‐arboreal species represented 24.8 ± 9.8% (mean ± SD) of the ant species collected in our traps, they accounted for only 12.4 ± 7.2% of the species records and, therefore, were rarely found on trees.

    2.2 Network descriptors

    We used each tree species and their associated ants in each of the 29 sites sampled as independent ant–tree networks. Each ant–tree network was built by an interaction matrix A, where Aij = number of times in which the plant species j interacted with the ant species i within the transects and zero otherwise (Fagundes et al. 2016). In ants, the individuals are represented by the colony, not by the workers. Therefore, we used as frequency the number of occurrences of each specific combination of interactions to minimize the possibility of overestimating abundances of ant species with higher recruitment of workers (Fagundes et al. 2017; Del‐Claro et al. 2018). For each ant–plant network, we calculated the following network descriptors: network size (plant richness × ant richness), modularity, nestedness, specialization, and interaction diversity. We used only weighted network descriptors, mainly because recent studies have shown that binary networks tend to be more sensitive to sampling bias (Vizentin‐Bugoni et al., 2016).

    Initially, we calculated weighted modularity (Q) computed by the QuanBiMo algorithm (Dormann & Strauss, 2014). This algorithm repeatedly divides a network into modules (we set to 107 swaps) and recalculates modularity until reaching an optimal Q value, which ranges from 0 (no more links within modules than expected by chance) to 1 (maximum possible modularity). Due to the stochasticity of this algorithm, in which similar starting conditions may lead to different results (i.e., an increase in modularity is always accepted), we used the highest Q value after 50 independent runs. We estimated the quantitative nestedness analysis using wNODF (Almeida‐Neto & Ulrich, 2011), a nestedness descriptor that varies from zero (not nested) to 100 (perfectly nested). Network specialization was quantified by the H2' index derived from the Shannon entropy, which is based on the deviation from the expected probability distribution of chance interactions. Values of H2' range from 0 (extreme generalization) to 1 (extreme specialization) (Blüthgen, Menzel, & Blüthgen, 2006). To estimate interaction diversity (ID), we used a descriptor based on the exponential value of the Shannon index to calculate the number of true or equivalent interactions in a network. Therefore, such values represent the interaction patterns based on both richness and frequency of interactions among species (Dáttilo & Dyer, 2014).

    In general, studies dealing with macroecology and interaction networks take into account a potential sampling bias. Here, despite our standardized design, networks also differed in network dimensions (i.e., species richness and number of interactions), which could affect network descriptors. Therefore, we used null model corrections (z‐transformations) to standardize the differences in species richness, connectance, and heterogeneity of interactions between the sampling sites to allow cross‐network comparisons (Dalsgaard et al., 2017; Schleuning et al., 2012; Sebastián‐Gonzalez et al., 2015). Both values of nestedness and modularity were standardized as Z‐scores, defined as Zscore = (x – μ)/σ, where x is the observed value (Q or wNODF), μ is the mean value of randomized matrices and σ is the standard deviation of the randomized matrices. We used a Monte Carlo procedure to generate 1,000 random matrices for each network descriptor, using a null model that fixes both marginal totals and connectance (swap.web null model) implemented in the “bipartite” package in R (Dormann, Fründ, Blüthgen, & Gruber, 2009). Note that we did not use any standardization in H2' because a standardization is already performed when this network descriptor is calculated (Blüthgen et al., 2006). Finally, we also found no evidence that nestedness could be an artefact of the shape of our networks as network asymmetry (i.e., simply the ratio of matrix dimensions) was not correlated with nestedness (r2 = 0.01; p > 0.05). Web asymmetry was computed as follows: (A – P)/ (A + P), where A = number of ant species and P = number of plant species.

    2.3 Interaction beta diversity

    We evaluated the interaction beta diversity (βWN) for all pairwise combination of sites, using the framework proposed by Poisot et al. (2012). Interaction beta diversity is derived from Whittaker's (1960) beta diversity and can be partitioned into two additive components: species turnover (βST; i.e., changes in identity of the interactions due to spatial changes in species composition) and interaction rewiring (βIR; i.e., changes in identity of the interactions generated by the reassembly of interactions between the same species in different sampling sites). Note that our notation for interaction rewiring is equivalent to βOS used by Poisot et al. (2012). Values of each component range from 0 to 1, where higher values of βST represent higher interaction turnover generated by the loss or gain of species, and higher values of βIR represent greater changes in who is interacting with whom (i.e., interactions are reassembled).

    In addition, we also used a network descriptor based on the interaction beta diversity to identify those sites with high dissimilarity to the regional pool of possible interactions throughout the Neotropical savanna (Poisot et al., 2015, 2017). For this, we measured interaction dissimilarity using the βOS' estimator for each network A. Subsequently, we first took all networks in the dataset, and for each plant with each ant, we determined if they interact at least once. These results are represented in a meta‐web A' involving all ants, plants and interactions recorded across all sampling sites (i.e., a biogeographical pool of species and potential interactions). The value of βOS' simply represents the interaction composition distance between A and A'. Values of βOS' close to 0 indicate that local sites tend to keep a majority of the regional interactions (i.e., weak interaction filtering), whereas values close to 1 indicate that most potential interactions have been filtered out (i.e., strong interaction filtering). In other words, this descriptor estimates the contribution of each network to the interaction dissimilarity of the meta‐web involving all ants, plants and interactions recorded across all sampling sites. We used the R package “betalink” (Poisot, 2016) to perform all analyses of interaction beta diversity and interaction dissimilarity.

    2.4 Predictor variables

    We evaluated the relative importance of net primary productivity, mean annual temperature, temperature range (i.e., the difference between the maximum temperature in the warmest quarter and the minimum temperature in the coldest quarter), annual rainfall and rainfall seasonality (i.e., the coefficient of variation in monthly rainfall) as potential correlates of ant–tree interaction network structure. These variables have commonly been used in studies evaluating spatial patterns of species richness (e.g., Oliveira & Diniz‐Filho, 2010) and found to be of importance in shaping some interaction networks (Dalsgaard et al., 2011; Martín‐González et al., 2015; Rico‐Gray, Díaz‐Castelazo, Ramírez‐Hernández, Guimarães, & Holland, 2012; Sebastián‐González, Dalsgaard, Sandel, & Guimarães, 2015; Vermaat, Dunne, & Gilbert, 2009). The parameters mean annual temperature and annual rainfall reflect, respectively, the availability of pure energy (heat) and water in our sampling sites, net primary productivity reflects the balance between water and energy, whereas temperature range and rainfall seasonality are surrogates of environmental stability (Oliveira & Diniz‐Filho, 2010). In our study region, both mean annual temperature and rainfall seasonality decrease as latitude increases, whereas net primary productivity and temperature range are positively correlated with latitude; annual rainfall was not correlated with any variable (Supporting Information Table S1).

    Climatic data for each of the three transects per site were obtained from WorldClim (Hijmans, Cameron, Parra, Jones, Jarvis, 2005) at a resolution of 30 arc‐seconds (c. 1 km2). The net primary productivity values were extracted from NASA (National Aeronautics and Space Administration) Earth Observation and MOD17 (an algorithm used for processing data from the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS), in a global map of net primary productivity (g C/m2/year) averaged over the years 2000–2015. The data extracted for each transect were averaged to obtain a single value for each site (n = 29 sites).

    2.5 Statistical analyses

    The relationships between network descriptors and environmental predictors (net primary productivity, mean annual temperature, temperature range, annual rainfall and rainfall seasonality) were evaluated using a model averaging and selection approach (Burnham & Anderson, 2002) (all response and predictor variables are available at http://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.6998486). A separate analysis was performed  for each response variable estimated at the network level (i.e., network size, interaction diversity, specialization, nestedness, modularity and interaction dissimilarity). We built linear models with a Gaussian distribution and an “identity” link for all response variables except network size, for which we built a generalized linear model assuming a negative binomial error distribution and a logarithmic link as appropriate for overdispersed count data. We checked for normality of the response variables and residuals of the full models using the Shapiro test. The assumption of normality was met in all cases, except for the variable interaction dissimilarity, for which the logit transformation (Warton & Hui, 2011) of the original data was necessary. We checked if there was collinearity among the predictor variables using the variance inflation factor (VIF) and found that VIF values were low (< 2.5); therefore, collinearity was not a problem in our models (Supporting Information Table S2). We used the VIF statistic instead of correlations among pairs of independent variables, as small pairwise correlations do not necessarily indicate linear independence among the predictor variables.

    The relationships between network descriptors and latitude were evaluated using simple linear regressions. Latitude was significantly correlated with most of our predictor variables (Supporting Information Table S1). Consequently, models in which latitude was a predictor presented high VIF values. For this reason, latitude was not included as predictor in our models. We tested for spatial autocorrelation in the residuals of our models using global Moran's I and found no evidence of positive spatial autocorrelation (Supporting Information Tables S2 and S3).

    Model selection was based on the Akaike information criterion corrected for small sample sizes (AICc). As the best‐supported models (those with a ΔAICc < 2) presented a low support value (as indicated by the Akaike weight; wi) (Supporting Information Table S2), we report the model averaging results. For this, the standardized regression coefficients of each predictor variable were averaged across all models in which the variable was present, and the average was weighted according to the wi value of the model (Burnham & Anderson, 2002). The relative importance of each variable was determined by summing the wi values across all models that included that particular variable (Burnham & Anderson, 2002). Model selection was done using the R package “MuMIn”, whereas global Moran's I and VIF were performed using the packages “ape” and “usdm”, respectively.

    Based on all comparisons between sampling sites, we generated 406 distinct pairwise values of species turnover (βST) and interaction rewiring (βIR). We compared the mean values of each of these two components of interaction beta diversity (βWN) across all sites using a paired t test. The relative influences of geographical distance and environmental dissimilarity on the total interaction beta diversity (βWN), and those generated by interaction rewiring (βIR) and species turnover (βST) among our survey sites, were assessed using generalized dissimilarity modelling (GDM; Ferrier, Manion, Elith, & Richardson, 2007). A separate model was fitted for each response variable (i.e., βWN, βIR and βST) using the default options of the R package “gdm” (Manion et al., 2017). Predictor variables included in these models were geographical distance, net primary productivity, mean annual temperature, temperature range, annual rainfall and rainfall seasonality. With these predictors, GDM derives sets of I‐splines, as described below, and calculates the distances between all possible pairs of sites (Ferrier et al., 2007). The GDM is a nonlinear and multivariate extension of the more traditional, linear matrix regression approach (i.e., the Mantel test) and takes into account the fact that the relationship between environmental or geographical distance and interaction beta diversity among sampling sites is often curvilinear. In addition, and in contrast to linear matrix regression, GDM does not assume that the rate of change in the response variable remains constant across the entire range of the environmental or geographical gradients (Ferrier et al., 2007). The GDM uses maximum‐likelihood estimation and a set (three is the default) of monotonically increasing I‐spline basis functions to calculate the distances between all possible pairs of sites in relation to each geographical and environmental predictor variable and to find the best fit between interaction beta diversity and inter‐site environmental–geographical distances. The height of the I‐splines basis functions provides an indication of the relative importance of each variable in predicting the response variable (Fitzpatrick et al., 2013).

    3 RESULTS

    In total, we recorded 3,900 independent interactions involving 100 ant species and 177 tree species (Supporting Information: https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.5950306). Within each sampling site, we recorded from 9 to 21 tree species (mean ± SD = 15.1 ± 3.6) and from 14 to 44 species of arboreal ants (mean ± SD=27.7 ± 7.3). Our sampling retrieved 75.8 ± 13.5% (mean ± SD) of the ant species expected to be found in each site, according to the estimator Chao 1.

    We observed that sites with larger network dimensions presented greater diversity of interactions, greater interaction dissimilarity, lower specialization and lower nestedness, whereas greater modularity was associated with greater specialization (available at Table S4). Overall, we did not find a latitudinal gradient in network specialization, nestedness or modularity. However, sites located at higher latitudes presented larger network sizes, as well as higher interaction diversity and interaction dissimilarity (βOS'; Figures 1 and 2). The latitudinal changes in network size, interaction diversity and interaction dissimilarity (βOS') were associated more with differences in net primary productivity than with differences in climatic stability or the availability of energy or water among our sampling sites (Supporting Information Figure S1). In fact, net primary productivity showed a much higher importance value in the models describing the observed changes in network size, interaction diversity and interaction dissimilarity compared to the variables of temperature and rainfall (Table 1). In addition, net primary productivity was included as a predictor in all best‐supported models, showing a positive influence on network size, interaction diversity and interaction dissimilarity (Table 1; Supporting Information Figure S1). In contrast, mean annual temperature was included as a predictor only in the model describing changes in network size. The best‐supported model for specialization, nestedness and modularity was the null model (Table 1), indicating that climatic factors and net primary productivity have a negligible influence on these network descriptors.

    4 DISCUSSION

    Previous studies evaluating macroecological patterns at global spatial scales have shown that plant–pollinator interaction networks can be structured according to latitude (e.g., Dalsgaard et al., 2017; Trøjelsgaard & Olesen, 2013; Zanata et al., 2017). Here, we used a standardized sampling protocol and network descriptors controlled by differences in species richness, connectance, and heterogeneity of interactions to evaluate the extent to which the structure of the interaction networks of arboreal ants on trees vary across a latitudinal gradient in a savanna‐dominated region. Although the spatial extent of our study is much smaller than that of previous studies, we found little evidence of latitudinal differences in the organization of ant–tree interaction networks. In fact, sites located more than 2,000 km apart from each other presented ant–tree interaction networks with similar levels of specialization, nestedness and modularity.

    Studies involving the facultative interactions between ants and plants bearing extrafloral nectaries have shown that nestedness is relatively invariant at small spatial scales (about 6 km; Dáttilo, Guimarães, & Izzo, 2013). Our study, which involved facultative interactions between savanna trees and arboreal nesting ants, indicates that this is also the case at medium to large spatial scales. Although some network descriptors (i.e., specialization, nestedness and modularity) remained relatively invariant across the latitudinal gradient we studied, network dimensions (i.e., network size and interaction diversity) and interaction dissimilarity (βOS') both increased as latitude increased. This finding indicates that more complex measurements – such as network analysis – are not needed to detect some geographical patterns of interaction between ants and trees, given that network size (i.e., species richness) is a good predictor of their interaction diversity and dissimilarity.

    The second aim of our study was to identify the main environmental correlates of the observed changes in the structure of ant–tree interaction networks. We analysed five potential correlates of the structure of these networks and found little evidence that they have an influence on the degree of specialization, nestedness or modularity of our networks. However, network size, interaction diversity and interaction dissimilarity increased at sites with higher productivity, indicating that the observed changes in network dimensions are mediated at least in part by the relationship between diversity and productivity (Lange, Dáttilo, & Del‐Claro, 2013; Rico‐Gray et al., 2012). In our study region, sites located at mid to high latitudes are more productive and often support a higher number of trees (Françoso et al., 2016; Ratter et al., 2003) and ant species (Vasconcelos et al., 2018, 2019), and consequently, network size and interaction diversity tend to be greater at these sites than at those located at lower latitudes. Interspecific competition is likely to be less intense in more productive sites, thus favouring species coexistence (Sebastián‐González et al., 2015; Trøjelsgaard & Olesen, 2013). More productive and floristically diverse sites may sustain a greater variety of nesting resources for arboreal ants, and this can also increase the number of locally coexisting species (Powell et al., 2011). The greater diversity of species and interactions in more productive sites, in turn, appears to generate greater interaction dissimilarity. In fact, we found a moderate and positive correlation between network size and interaction dissimilarity, indicating that as productivity and, consequently, network size increases, the dissimilarity of each network to the regional pool of possible interactions also increases.

    The third aim of our study was to evaluate the relative influence of geographical and environmental distances between sites on total interaction beta diversity as well as on its interaction rewiring and species turnover. Our results indicate that while current environmental conditions are relatively good predictors of network dimensions, they are poor predictors of interaction beta diversity (βWN). In fact, most of the observed changes in interaction beta diversity were related to the geographical distance between sampling sites rather than to variations in temperature, rainfall or net primary productivity. As also shown by CaraDonna et al. (2017) for plant–pollinator networks, we found that the contribution of interaction rewiring (βIR) to the interaction beta diversity between any two sites was higher than that generated by species turnover (βST), indicating that the same species tend to interact differently in different sites. This may be because the ecological interaction studied by us is highly facultative and opportunistic, as virtually any ant species can interact with any tree species (Camarota et al., 2015, 2016), generating low fidelity among the partners. Alternatively or in addition, the high rewiring in our interaction networks may well be associated with neutral abundance‐based processes, where species are ecologically equivalent (e.g., without the morphological matching of interacting species) and interact randomly and proportionally to their relative abundances (Krishna, Guimarães, Jordano, Bascompte, 2008; Tylianakis & Morris, 2017; Vázquez et al., 2009). Therefore, if the same species are found in different sites, but differ locally in their abundances, they may change the identity and quantity of their interactions. Previous studies have shown that neutrality is a key factor structuring a variety of interaction networks, including seed‐dispersal (Jordano, 1987), pollination (Vázquez et al., 2007) and protective ant–plant networks (Dáttilo et al., 2014). The high rewiring in species interaction found here seems to explain why specialization, nestedness and modularity remained relatively invariant across the latitudinal gradient we studied. In other words, we show that while the identity of species interactions changes between sites (high interaction rewiring), the organization of their interactions does not change. It is important to mention that although interaction rewiring was the main component of total interaction beta diversity, we found that rewiring was independent of the geographical or environmental distances between sampling sites. Therefore, it seems that the lack of trait matching of interacting species and abundance‐based processes across populations is constant along the spatial and environmental scales studied.

    In summary, we empirically showed that the dimension of interaction networks between arboreal ants and savanna trees increases as latitude and productivity increase. Sites at higher latitudes also had a high dissimilarity to the regional pool of possible interactions (i.e., strong interaction filtering), indicating that these regions significantly contribute to the maintenance of interactive biodiversity in our study region. Network structure, however, remained relatively invariant across the latitudinal and environmental gradients, possibly because interaction rewiring was the major component of interaction beta diversity. Overall, our findings offer an advance in the knowledge of the structure and dissimilarities of species interaction networks and their correlates over broad spatial scales. Therefore, we hope that our contribution will stimulate new studies of macroecological patterns of interaction networks, evaluating to what extent our results can be generalized to networks involving other types of interactions and biodiversity hotspots.

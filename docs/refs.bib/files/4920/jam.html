    Abstract

    Aims

    Variation in microbiota of the coffee berry borer (CBB) Hypothenemus hampei was studied. Diversity, structure and function of bacterial communities were compared between eggs vs adults, CBBs from shade coffee vs sun coffee, CBBs from the field vs raised in the laboratory, and CBBs with and without the antibiotic tetracycline.

    Methods and Results

    We sequenced the region V4 of the gene 16 S rRNA. Pseudomonadaceae and Enterobacteriaceae, particularly Pseudomonas and Pantoea, dominated microbiotas of the CBB. Comparative functional inferences with PICRUSt suggested that samples from the field were enriched for genes involved in carbohydrate and protein digestion and absorption, while laboratory‐reared samples were higher in genes for melanization and caffeine metabolism.

    Conclusions

    Microbiotas of the CBB were diverse and dominated by the genus Pseudomonas, several species of which have been previously associated with caffeine degradation in this insect. Wolbachia was the only endosymbiont detected with known ability to manipulate host reproduction.

    Significance and Impact of the Study

    This study demonstrates that stage of development and origin of samples affected the structure and function of the CBB's bacterial communities. This is the first attempt to predict functional significance of the CBB microbiota in nutrition, reproduction and defence.

    Introduction

    The coffee berry borer Hypothenemus hampei Ferrari (Coleoptera: Curculionidae) is the most devastating pest of coffee worldwide (Damon 2000; Jaramillo et al. 2006; Vega et al. 2009). The coffee berry borer (CBB) depends exclusively on coffee fruits for food and reproduction (Damon 2000). The entire life cycle occurs sheltered inside the fruit, thus protecting the CBB against control measures (Damon 2000; Vega et al. 2002, 2009).

    The economic and social impacts of the CBB in coffee‐producing areas have motivated research on its distribution, natural enemies and control (De la Rosa et al. 1997; Silva et al. 2006; Armbrecht and Gallego 2007; Cárdenas et al. 2007; Góngora et al. 2013; Mariño et al. 2017b). Past technological limitations had limited the research in the microbial community associated with the CBB, its role in nutrition, defence and reproduction, and its potential manipulation for biological control.

    Insects have a range of symbioses with micro‐organisms, including obligate, facultative, pathogenic and parasitic relationships (Baumann 2005; Moran et al. 2008; Brownlie and Johnson 2009). Symbionts play many important and complex roles in the biology of their insect hosts, including nutritional adaptations (Baumann 2005; Douglas 2009), heat tolerance (Russell and Moran 2006; Moran et al. 2008), protection against parasites and pathogens (Haine 2008; Brownlie and Johnson 2009; Indiragandhi et al. 2011; Shao et al. 2017) and reproductive manipulations (Zchori‐Fein and Perlman 2004; Perotti et al. 2006; Werren et al. 2008).

    Wolbachia has been reported in adult CBB females, and proposed as the cause of CBB's skewed sex ratio (about 9:1 female: male) (Vega et al. 2002). Wolbachia may also be necessary to assure the reproductive success of the CBB, since it is a reproductive manipulator in many insects (Mariño et al. 2017a). However, to our knowledge, the effects of environmental conditions and host developmental stage on the CBB microbiota and reproductive parasites such as Wolbachia have not been tested. Understanding these effects may lead to strategies for management of the CBB.

    A recent survey on the CBB microbiota in the gut of adult females showed that the genus Pseudomonas was the most common group and P. fulva was important in caffeine degradation (Ceja‐Navarro et al. 2015). It was a pioneering study that raised many questions to be addressed.

    We assessed the bacterial communities from eggs to adults collected from fruits growing in shade and sun coffee and reared on artificial diet Cenibroca (Portilla 1999) with and without the antibiotic tetracycline (0·1% w/v), an antibiotic used to cure Wolbachia infections in insects (Werren 1997; Stouthamer et al. 1999; Dedeine et al. 2001). We addressed the following questions about bacterial community composition, diversity and function: (i) Do the microbiotas of the CBB differ between eggs and adults? We hypothesized that microbiota of eggs will be less complex than that of adult females, but with some core taxa maintained across life history stages. (ii) Do the microbiotas of the CBB from sun coffee vs shade‐grown coffee differ? Shade coffee is characterized by more stable environmental conditions than sun; these two cultivation systems also differ in susceptibility to insect pests and diseases. We, therefore, hypothesized that CBB from shade coffee will have richer and more diverse microbiotas than CBB from sun coffee, given that environmental homogeneity is often associated with diversity. (iii) Are these hypotheses reflected in the predicted gene content of samples, as determined by PICRUSt analyses? We hypothesized that stage of development, environment and origin of samples (field‐collected vs laboratory‐reared CBBs) will also affect the relative abundance of genes involved in certain metabolic pathways as a consequence of changes in composition in the microbiota of the CBB. Understanding factors that influence structure and function of the microbiota of the CBB, and particularly Wolbachia, can provide information for management decisions.

    Materials and methods

    Collection and laboratory rearing of coffee berry borers

    We compared microbiota of the CBB from: (i) eggs vs adult females, (ii) field‐collected individuals from infested fruits growing in sun vs shade, (iii) field‐collected CBB vs laboratory‐reared on the artificial diet Cenibroca, and (iv) individuals reared on the artificial diet with tetracycline 0·1% vs control diet. One of our main goals was to estimate the relative abundance of Wolbachia in the CBB microbiota and to look for other endosymbionts that are reproductive manipulators of insects, like Rickettsia which has been reported in other Curculionidae (Zchori‐Fein et al. 2006). Only adult females and eggs were sampled because these endosymbionts are maternally inherited (Werren 1997; Duron et al. 2008).

    Field CBBs were removed from infested coffee fruits (Coffea arabica) growing under sun and shade in two farms in Adjuntas, Puerto Rico: Farm 1 (18°10′42·4″N, 66°44′36″W, 527 m a.s.l) and Farm 2 (18°10′45·5″N, 66°46′23·6″W, 624 m a.s.l). Fruits were dissected to extract adult females and eggs. Environmental variables for plots sampled in this study were previously measured by Mariño et al. (2016). Sun vs shade coffee differed significantly in temperature and relative humidity; shade plots had lower maximum temperatures, more stable daily temperature fluctuations and higher relative humidity (Table 1).

    CBBs originally collected from Farm 1 were reared for 11 consecutive generations in the artificial diet Cenibroca, which contains coffee bean meal, yeast, casein, benzoic acid, benomyl, vitamins, salts, formaldehyde, ethanol and agar (Portilla 1999). To reduce Wolbachia infections, tetracycline hydrochloride (0·1% w/v) (Fisher Bioreagents) was added to the diet for direct and continuous treatment of adult females. CBB were reared in the dark in a growth chamber at 25°C and 80–96% relative humidity. After the F11 generation, vials were carefully examined to collect adult females and eggs. Mean generation time was approx. 34·5 days for control and 35·2 days for diet with tetracycline (Mariño et al. 2017a).

    DNA extraction and sequencing

    Insects were stored dry at −20°C until DNA was extracted. Total DNA was extracted from 20 eggs and the entire bodies of five females (with four replicates per treatment, for a total of 320 eggs and 80 adult females) using the DNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen Sciences). Pooling of CBB eggs and females was done to maximize the representation of bacterial diversity from a CBB population sample rather than an individual. About 290 bp from the hypervariable V4 region of the bacterial 16S rRNA gene were amplified using the primers 515‐F: 5′ TGCCAGCMGCCGCGGTAA 3′and 806‐R: 5′GGACTACHVGGGTWTCTAAT 3′ (Caporaso et al. 2012) with the addition of Illumina adapters. The PCR amplification program included an initial denaturation at 94°C for 3′ followed by 35 cycles of denaturation at 94°C for 30″, annealing at 54°C for 45″, extension at 72°C for 1′ and final extension at 72°C for 10′. All PCRs included negative controls, from which no products were obtained. Amplicons were sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq platform at the University of Puerto Rico Sequencing and Genotyping Facility (UPR‐SGF).

    Data processing

    Illumina sequence data were processed using scripts in MacQIIME ver. 1·8·0 (Caporaso et al. 2010). The initial step joined the demultiplexed files containing the paired‐end reads using QIIME's ‘join_paired_ends.py’ script. The assembled reads were divided into fasta and qual files with the script ‘convert_fastaqual_fastaq.py’. The resulting files were used as input for the ‘split_libraries.py’ script, where the forward and reverse primers were removed using the ‘truncate only’ argument. At this point, sequences with average Quality Score <30 and a length <200 bp and >300 bp were also discarded.

    Operational Taxonomic Units (OTUs) were selected using the ‘pick_de_novo_otus.py’ script, which made clusters based on 99% sequence similarity. From each cluster a representative sequence was selected and chimeras were identified using the ChimeraSlayer algorithm and the Greengenes template alignment as implemented by PyNAST (Caporaso et al. 2009). The chimeric sequences were eliminated from OTU tables using the ‘filter_otus_from_otu_table.py’ script. The resulting filtered set was assigned taxonomy using the Ribosomal Database Project (RDP) Classifier (Wang et al. 2007), and the 99% Greengenes taxonomy (McDonald et al. 2012). Some OTUs with more than 100 reads assigned to Pseudomonadaceae and Enterobacteriaceae could not be identified to genus or species using the Greengenes database; they were blasted to identify the most similar sequences in GenBank.

    Data accessibility

    Raw sequence reads from the hypervariable region V4 of 16S rRNA generated from this study were deposited in GenBank Sequence Read Archive (accession number PRJNA378359).

    Data analysis

    Alpha and beta diversity

    For estimation of alpha diversity, data were rarefied to 1,500,000 sequences per sample. The rarefied data set was used to calculate the indices of Shannon or community diversity (Shannon 1948) and Chao1 or species richness (Chao 1984). One‐way anovas followed by post hoc Tukey tests (significance level P = 0·05) were performed to determine significance of differences in diversity indices between origin of samples (field vs laboratory‐reared), environment (sun vs shade coffee) and stage of development (eggs vs adults). anovas were done in r (R Core Development Team, 2017).

    Nonmetric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) plots were created to visualize the variation in bacterial community structures among the samples based on Bray–Curtis dissimilarity index matrices. NMDS ordination analysis was achieved by the metaMDS function from the vegan package in r (Dixon 2003). Additionally, the Bray–Curtis distance matrix was used in R to perform an analysis of similarities (ANOSIM) (Clarke 1993) with 999 permutations to test significant differences in community composition. NDMS ordination plots were done using the ggplot2 package in r (Wickham 2016).

    Prediction of potential microbial function

    Predicted functions of CBB bacterial communities were inferred using the pipeline Phylogenetic Investigation of Communities by Reconstruction of Unobserved States (PICRUSt). PICRUSt links 16S rRNA data with a database of reference genomes and Orthology annotation to predict the presence of gene families (Langille et al. 2013).

    As a first step for this analysis, an OTU table was generated using the pick_closed_references_otus.py script in QIIME. The table was normalized to equalize 16S rRNA gene copy numbers, and the normalized table was used to predict metagenome function using the command PICRUSt's ‘predict_metagenomes.py’. The accuracy of metagenome predictions was measured by estimating the Nearest Sequenced Taxon Index (NSTI). Finally, the predicted functions were categorized based on KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) annotation (Kanehisa and Goto 2000) by using the script ‘categorize_by_function.py’.

    Statistical Analysis of Taxonomic and Functional Profiles (STAMP) (Parks et al. 2014) was used to perform Welch's t tests with Bonferroni correction to determine significant differences in the relative abundance of four selected KEEG pathways (level 3). Additionally, differences in the relative abundance of the most common genera and Wolbachia between the different comparisons were tested in STAMP using a G‐test (with Yates' correction) + Fischer's exact test with Bonferroni correction, both with a significance level of 0·05.

    Results

    General composition of the microbiota of the CBB as revealed by 16S rRNA sequencing

    After quality filtering and elimination of chimeras, the data set contained 15,523,532 sequences from the V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene, or an average of 485 110 ± 26 346 reads for each of the 32 samples. Sequences were clustered into 1 150 OTUs.

    Microbiotas of the Coffee Berry Borer were mostly comprised of the phyla Proteobacteria (90%), Bacterioidetes (6%) and Firmicutes (3%) were also detected. Within the Proteobacteria, regardless of stage of development, environment and origin of samples, microbiotas were dominated by the families Pseudomonadaceae (ranging from 21·8 to 91·4% among samples) and Enterobacteriaceae (0·7–51·3%). Within the Pseudomonadaceae, the genus Pseudomonas represented 48·5% of the microbiota of the CBB and its relative abundance among samples varied from 16·5 to 63·7%, Pseudomonas was particularly common in samples from artificial diets (Fig. 1). In the Enterobacteriaceae, Pantoea was the most abundant genus and represented 10·2% of the microbiota of the CBB; Pantoea varied from 0·6 to 27% and was most abundant in CBB collected from infested fruits in the field (Fig. 1). Laboratory‐reared adults on diet without tetracycline contained a high proportion (21%) of Firmicutes.

    A total of 392 genera were detected. 13·7% (n = 53) of genera were shared among all samples. In descending order of abundance, they were: Pseudomonas, Pantoea, Stenotrophomonas, Acinetobacter, Erwinia, Sphingobacterium, Agrobacterium, Variovorax, Pedobacter, Enterobacter, Ochrobactrum, Novosphingobium, Bosea, Salmonella, Rhodanobacter, Delftia, Wolbachia, Klebsiella, Hylemonella, Curtobacterium, Brachybacterium, Sphingomonas, Rhizobium, Rhodococcus and Methylobacterium. Each of these genera represented >0·1% of the bacterial microbiota of the CBB; they may represent its core microbiota, as discussed below.

    blast searches on sequences assigned to Pseudomonadaceae showed that the most abundant OTU (denovo1064164), which represented 32% of all reads, had 100% identity to a sequence from P. chlororaphis (GenBank KU877656). The other two most abundant OTUs in Pseudomonas (13% combined, denovo297355 and denovo377458) were similar to P. putida (GeneBank KY038314 and KY038310 respectively). The most abundant OTU from Enterobacteriaceae (10%) had 100% identity to Pantoea cypripedii (GeneBank KT253981), a common facultative plant‐ and insect‐associated symbiont (Lo et al. 2016) (Table S1).

    The total relative abundance of Wolbachia in the microbiota of the CBB was 0·2%, ranging from 0·03% in adult females reared in the laboratory with tetracycline to 0·37% in adult females reared in the laboratory without tetracycline (Fig. 2a). Wolbachia was the only endosymbiotic bacterium detected with known ability to manipulate host reproduction: Rickettsia, Cardinium and Spiroplasma (Moran et al. 2008; Kikuchi 2009) were not detected.

    Significant differences were observed among samples in both indices of α diversity (Shannon: anova, F = 32·8, P = 0·0002 and Chao 1: anova, F = 26·7, P = 0·0005) (Table 2). Overall, samples collected from the field were more diverse and species‐rich than samples from individuals reared on artificial diet; the most diverse and species‐rich samples were those collected in the field from fruits growing in sun (Table 2). Rarefaction curves of observed OTUs showed that 1·4 × 106 reads per sample are not enough to achieve a complete assessment of the bacterial diversity in CBB (Fig. 3a). Differences in the rarefaction curves agreed with the results from diversity indices.

    Factors shaping the bacterial community structure

    Bacterial communities from different origins were distinct, according to NMDS analyses based on Bray–Curtis dissimilarity indices (Table 3). Field‐collected samples were more similar to each other than to laboratory‐reared samples (Fig. 3b), and these differences were significant (ANOSIM, R = 0·60, P = 0·0001; R = 1 in ANOSIM means maximum dissimilarity). Eggs vs adults showed a considerable separation (Fig. 3c), and differences were significant (ANOSIM, R = 0·30, P = 0·002). Samples from sun vs shade coffee were not significantly different (R = −0·007, P = 0·463).

    CBB eggs vs adults

    Adult females harboured significantly more OTUs than eggs (379 ± 31·3 vs 309 ± 12·5 OTUs per replicate respectively; t‐test, t = 2·09, p = 0·04). However, no significant differences were observed in α diversity indices (Table 2). Sixty percent of genera were shared, 27% were specific to adults and 13% specific to eggs (Fig. 4a). Pseudomonas, Stenotrophomonas, Pantoea, Sphingobacterium and Acinetobacter were the most abundant genera shared between eggs and adults. The relative abundance of Pseudomonas, Pantoea and Erwinia was significantly higher in adult females, while Stenotrophomonas, Acinetobacter and Wolbachia were significantly higher in eggs (Table S2).

    CBB from sun vs shade coffee

    The number of OTUs was similar in CBBs from sun and shade coffee (361 ± 29·2 vs 327 ± 23·6 OTUs per replicate respectively; t‐test, t = −0·89, p = 0·387) and no significant differences were observed in α diversity indices (Table 2). Samples from these two environments shared most genera (66%); 20% of genera were only found in CBB from sun coffee and 14% only in CBB from shade coffee (Fig. 4b). Pseudomonas, Pantoea, Erwinia, Enterobacter, Stenotrophomonas and Sphingobacterium were the dominant genera in both environments. Pseudomonas, Erwinia, Sphingobacterium and Wolbachia were significantly more abundant in shade coffee (Table S2) and Pantoea, Enterobacter and Stenotrophomonas were significantly higher in sun coffee (Table S2).

    Field vs laboratory‐reared CBBs

    Field samples harboured significantly more OTUs than laboratory‐reared samples (344 ± 18·6 and 208 ± 13·9 OTUs per replicate respectively; t‐test, t = 5·85, P < 0·0001). Samples from field‐collected CBBs were also more diverse than samples from laboratory‐reared CBBs (Table 2). These two groups were significantly different, with only 53% of genera shared (Fig. 4c). Microbiotas from laboratory‐reared samples were dominated by Pseudomonas (74·6%), in particular P. chlororaphis (Table 4), while in samples from the field Pseudomonas was far less common (25·2%) and P. putida was the most common species (Table 4). Pantoea, Stenotrophomonas, Erwinia and Acinetobacter were also more common in field‐collected CBBs; only Pseudomonas, Pedobacter, Rhodanobacter and Wolbachia were significantly more frequent in laboratory‐reared samples (Fig. 1; Table S2).

    Laboratory‐reared CBBs with vs without tetracycline

    Adult females reared on diet with and without tetracycline harboured a similar number of OTUs (158 ± 23·9 and 170 ± 12·5 OTUs respectively; t‐test, t = 0·47, P = 0·653). α diversity was also not affected by tetracycline: no significant differences were observed in Shannon and Chao 1 indices (Table 2). However, only 49% of genera were shared (Fig. 4d). Treatment with tetracycline significantly reduced the relative abundance of Wolbachia, Pantoea, Erwinia, Enterobacter, Stenotrophomonas, Sphingobacterium, Ochrobactrum and Acinetobacter, and increased the abundance of Pseudomonas, Rhodanobacter and Pedobacter (Table S2). The genus Pseudomonas represented 63·7% of OTUs in CBBs fed with tetracycline. The relative abundance of P. putida, P. viridiflava and P. fulva was significantly reduced by tetracycline, while the abundance of P. chlororaphis was significantly increased (Table 4).

    Functional inferences of the metagenome

    A total of 287 putative metabolic functions at level 3 were predicted using the PICRUSt pipeline. The accuracy of the prediction was evaluated by computing the NSTI (Nearest Sequenced Taxon Index), which sums the phylogenetic distances for each organism in the OTU table to a nearest available sequenced reference genome; the average NSTI for the 32 samples was 0·04 ± 0·001 SE. This low value suggests that PICRUSt performed well in the functional predictions of bacterial communities in the CBB. Four selected metabolic functions differed significantly between laboratory‐reared and field‐collected CBBs. Microbiotas from field‐collected CBBs were significantly enriched for genes involved in carbohydrate and protein digestion and absorption, whereas microbiotas from laboratory samples were enriched for genes involved in melanization and caffeine metabolism (Fig. 5a). Comparisons between eggs and adults showed that bacterial communities from eggs contained more genes involved in protein digestion and absorption, while those from adults contained more genes involved in carbohydrate digestion and absorption (Fig. 5b). Putative functions for the other two comparisons (sun vs shade coffee, and artificial diets with and without tetracycline) were not significantly different.

    Discussion

    Core microbiota of the CBB (Hypothenemus hampei)

    Based on next‐generation 16S rRNA analysis, all bacterial communities from eggs to adult females of the CBB were dominated by Proteobacteria, and to a lesser extent by Bacteriodetes and Firmicutes. These phyla also dominated the microbiota of other insects (Vasanthakumar et al. 2008; Moran et al. 2012; Jones et al. 2013; Wong et al. 2013), and have been proposed as components of the core microbiome of arthropods (Jones et al. 2013). Proteobacteria includes many known insect symbionts (Jones et al. 2013), some of which influence host nutrition (Douglas 2009; Aksoy et al. 2014), immune response (Oliver et al. 2005; Teixeira et al. 2008) and reproduction (Dedeine et al. 2001; Dong et al. 2006; Zchori‐Fein et al. 2006; Son et al. 2008).

    Fifty‐three genera were shared among all samples, and 25 of these genera were present at >1% relative abundance in all samples. Ceja‐Navarro et al. (2015) reported 10 of these genera (Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter, Pantoea, Erwinia, Agrobacterium, Ochrobactrum, Wolbachia, Brachybacterium, Sphingomonas and Methylobacterium) in adult female CBBs from several coffee‐growing regions including Puerto Rico. These genera can, therefore, be considered the core microbiota of H. hampei (Fig. 1).

    The majority of core genera were more abundant in field‐collected samples, except Pseudomonas and Wolbachia, which were enriched in laboratory‐reared CBB. Some of these genera have previously been associated with coffee; Pantoea, Methylobacterium and Pseudomonas have been isolated as endophytes from coffee fruits (Vega et al. 2005), while Acinetobacter has been isolated from coffee fruits during fermentation (Silva et al. 2008) and found in diverse habitats. The CBB presumably acquires them from coffee fruits while feeding.

    Wolbachia is the best known reproductive manipulator of insects (Werren 1997; Stouthamer et al. 1999). Double infections of Wolbachia and Rickettsia were detected in the CBB (Ceja‐Navarro et al. 2015). However, in this study, we only detected Wolbachia, and in low relative abundance (<1%, Fig. 2a,b). Treatment with tetracycline did not eliminate Wolbachia completely, but reduced almost 10× the proportion of Wolbachia in adult females (Fig. 2a). Infection with Wolbachia may augment the reproductive success of the CBB; previous experiments showed that when Wolbachia was reduced by tetracycline, CBBs had significantly fewer progeny, lower fecundity and fewer eggs per female. Tetracycline also reduced the population growth rate, net reproductive rate and mean generation time of the CBB (Mariño et al. 2017a).

    Factors that affected the CBB's microbiota

    The CBB has limited genetic variability worldwide (Gauthier 2010). Populations in Puerto Rico may be even more homogenous genetically, since they presumably result from one or a few introductions. Variation in the CBB's microbiota depending on environment and life cycle stage may help individuals adapt to local conditions, a hologenome that is more flexible than the genome and can change over an individual's lifetime (Rosenberg and Zilber‐Rosengerg 2014).

    Bacterial communities in adult female CBBs were more diverse and different than those in eggs (Table 2 and Fig. 3c). Changes in diversity, composition and structure of bacterial communities have been observed across life stages in several insect species (Vasanthakumar et al. 2008; Wang et al. 2011; Martinson et al. 2012). Microbial communities in eggs are mainly heritable symbionts (like Wolbachia) and microbes of maternal origin that adhere during oviposition; whereas, in adults, additional bacteria are acquired from the environment during feeding and this results in more complex bacterial communities.

    The environment exerts selective pressures favouring certain micro‐organisms over others (Fuhrman 2009); moreover, environmental variability may enhance diversity (D'Odorico et al. 2008); even in protected environments such as an arthropod host inside a fruit. Sun coffee plots sampled in this study varied more than shade coffee plots; for example, daily range in temperature was greater in sun plots (Table 1). The most diverse and species‐rich samples were from sun coffee, probably because of high variability in temperature. However, shade plots were also diverse; differences in α diversity between sun and shade coffee were not significant (Table 2).

    The differences in environmental conditions (and resulting differences in fruit composition) between sun and shade coffee were reflected in the relative abundance of some bacterial genera. Sun coffee plots sampled for this study had higher maximum temperatures than shade coffee plots, 38·2°C vs 30·7°C (Table 1). These higher temperatures may have inhibited growth of Pseudomonas and Wolbachia, and favoured the presence of Pantoea, Enterobacter, Ochrobactrum and Stenotrophomonas (Figs 1 and 2b; Table S2). Several studies have demonstrated that high temperatures can eliminate or reduce Wolbachia infection (Van Opijnen and Breeuwer 1999; Hurst et al. 2000; Gotoh et al. 2007). For example, the abundance of Wolbachia in the mite Tetranychus urticae exposed to continuous temperatures of 32°C was gradually reduced (Van Opijnen and Breeuwer 1999). In terms of Wolbachia, sun coffee appears to have advantages over shade coffee, because the lower infection rate should lead to slower CBB reproduction. However, we did not observe this pattern in the field (Mariño et al. 2016).

    Sun coffee has higher sucrose content than shade coffee (Vaast et al. 2006). The relative abundance of Pantoea was higher in CBB from sun coffee; Pantoea species can degrade cellulose, hemicellulose and simple sugars (Suen et al. 2010; Adams et al. 2011). In contrast, shade coffee has higher caffeine concentration than sun coffee (Vaast et al. 2006). The relative abundance of Pseudomonas was higher in shade coffee, perhaps reflecting the fact that some Pseudomonas species can degrade caffeine (Mazzafera 2002; Gokulakrishnan et al. 2005; Ibrahim et al. 2014; Ceja‐Navarro et al. 2015; Vilanova et al. 2015).

    The term ‘origin of samples’ is used here to differentiate between field‐collected and laboratory‐reared CBBs. Bacterial communities from field‐collected CBBs were more diverse and species‐rich than those of CBBs reared on artificial diets. Also, these two groups were significantly different in composition and relative abundance of the predominant genera. This difference was expected since CBBs in the field are exposed to a wider range of micro‐organisms, while individuals on an artificial diet are exposed to fewer, as observed in other studies (Geib et al. 2009; Rani et al. 2009; Staubach et al. 2013). Differences in diet and environmental conditions (especially temperature and relative humidity) can also explain the differences between these communities. Moreover, the artificial diet used in the laboratory contains benzoic acid, which is included to moderate microbial growth.

    Host diet has been previously suggested as a major determinant factor of bacterial community composition in insects (Kelley and Dobler 2011; Colman et al. 2012; Staubach et al. 2013; Aksoy et al. 2014; Montagna et al. 2015). Alterations in hosts' diet can completely change community composition and structure (Dillon and Dillon 2004; Colman et al. 2012; Staubach et al. 2013). Similarly, a previous study found that bacterial communities of CBBs on artificial diet were different in composition from those of CBBs from the field, although diversity was not estimated (Ceja‐Navarro et al. 2015). The diet also contains preservatives such as benzoic acid, as we mentioned before this is an inhibitor of bacterial growth (Brul and Coote 1999), and the fungicide benomyl which is also known to alter bacterial growth (Rousk et al. 2009). These compounds probably favoured some taxa over others. For example, benomyl can favour the presence of some Pseudomonas strains that are able to use it as a source of carbon (Fuchs and De Vries 1978), indicating a potential swift of Pseudomonas population species on CBBs from field to diet.

    The most striking difference among treatments in terms of Pseudomonas was in P. chlororaphis, which was the predominant taxon in the microbiota of adult females exposed to tetracycline (Table 4). P. chlororaphis induces tolerance to drought and salinity in the plant Arabidopsis thaliana (Cho et al. 2008, 2012). In the case of the CBB, P. chlororaphis could have contributed to resistance or mitigated the stress of tetracycline or other compounds in the artificial diet.

    Functional inferences of the microbiota of the CBB

    Are differences in relative abundance of taxa among samples, especially between field and laboratory‐reared CBBs, reflected in functional roles of the microbiota? Overall, the predicted relative abundance of genes involved in carbohydrate and protein digestion and absorption was significantly higher in samples collected from the field vs samples reared on artificial diet in the laboratory (Fig. 5). In contrast, the predicted relative abundance of genes involved in caffeine metabolism and melanization were significantly higher in samples reared on artificial diets in the laboratory. However, the predicted abundance of genes involved in these four selected metabolic pathways did not differ significantly between sun and shade coffee samples, or between samples reared in the laboratory with and without tetracycline. The fact that some predicted pathways remained stable among treatments even though the microbiota changed suggests that there is functional redundancy or resilience among different groups of bacteria.

    The predicted relative frequency of genes involved in carbohydrate digestion and absorption was significantly higher in samples collected from the field (Fig. 5a). Many members of the family Enterobacteriaceae produce enzymes that degrade major structural components in plant materials (Chen et al. 2016). Enterobacteriaceae were most abundant in samples collected from field, especially Pantoea and Klebsiella; previous studies have demonstrated the capacity of these two genera to degrade cellulose in the leaf‐cutter ant Atta columbica (Suen et al. 2010). The genome of Pantoea isolated from the wood‐feeding wasp Sirex noctilio includes genes putatively encoding carbohydrate‐active enzymes (CAZyme) that metabolize hemicellulose and simple sugars (Adams et al. 2011). These two genera may help the CBB digest carbohydrates in coffee fruits, as discussed by Ceja‐Navarro et al. (2015).

    Caffeine degradation is important for the CBB since caffeine is toxic to insects and its concentration in coffee fruits is high (Ceja‐Navarro et al. 2015). The predicted relative abundance of genes involved in caffeine metabolism was significantly higher in samples reared on artificial diets in the laboratory. Members of the genus Pseudomonas are considered one of the most important caffeine‐degrading micro‐organisms (Mazzafera 2002; Gokulakrishnan et al. 2005; Ibrahim et al. 2014). The genus Pseudomonas was most abundant in samples reared on artificial diets.

    Recently, a strain of P. fulva able to degrade caffeine was isolated from the CBB (Ceja‐Navarro et al. 2015). However, P. fulva was not detected in this study. blast searches of OTUs of Pseudomonadaceae and Pseudomonas with >100 sequences showed that two of these OTUs had 99% similarity to P. fulva, but they represented only 0·001–0·002% of the microbiota of the CBB (Table S1). P. putida was one of the most abundant species of Pseudomonas in our samples (Table 4). P. putida has been previously associated in caffeine degradation (Blecher and Lingens 1977; Hohnloser et al. 1980; Ogunseitan 2002; Dash and Gummadi 2006; Yu et al. 2009). There may be a consortium of Pseudomonas species involved in detoxification of caffeine in the CBB. Caffeine degradation has also been demonstrated in other genera that were abundant in the CBB, for example, Klebsiella, Rhodococcus, Acinetobacter, Sphingobacterium, Enterobacter, Ochrobactrum, Brachybacterium and Stenotrophomonas (Madyastha and Sridhar 1998; Yamaoka‐Yano and Mazzafera 1998; Divine 2014; Ceja‐Navarro et al. 2015).

    Melanization is an important immune response of insects that includes the production and deposition of melanin pigments on invading structures of pathogens including bacteria, fungi and viruses (Gillespie and et al. 1997; Christensen et al. 2005; Yassine et al. 2012). Normally, melanization requires the activation of phenoloxidases (PO), and the participation of humoral and cellular factors (Eleftherianos and Revenis 2011; González‐Santoyo and Córdoba‐Aguilar 2012; Shao et al. 2012). However, several studies have demonstrated the importance of the insect microbiota in the activation of melanization (Dong et al. 2009; Thomas et al. 2011; Weiss et al. 2011). For example, melanization by Wolbachia may protect its hosts against viruses (Rancès et al. 2012).

    Our analyses predicted that the CBB microbiota contains genes involved in melanization (Fig. 5). Some reports have linked melanization to insect defence against the fungus Beauveria bassiana (Prasad and Syed 2010; Yassine et al. 2012); for example, adult females of the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster infected with Wolbachia were more resistant to B. bassiana than uninfected females, partly due to melanization (Panteleev et al. 2007). We suggest that in addition to the important role of Wolbachia in CBB reproduction (Mariño et al. 2017a), Wolbachia may also protect the CBB against B. bassiana (De la Rosa et al. 1997; Cárdenas et al. 2007; Vega et al. 2008; Góngora et al. 2013) via melanization.

    In summary, we found that the relative abundance of taxa, community structure and predicted function in the microbiota of the CBB were influenced by host stage of development, environment and diet. In all treatments, the microbiota of the CBB was dominated by the genus Pseudomonas, several species of which have been previously associated with caffeine degradation in this insect. Other abundant genera (Acinetobacter, Sphingobacterium and Stenotrophomonas) have also been demonstrated to be important in caffeine and cellulose degradation capabilities in other insect species. The genus Pantoea, a facultative insect symbiont, was the second most abundant taxa in the microbiota of the CBB, and may contribute to the CBB's ability to digest and absorb carbohydrates. The CBB harboured low densities of the endosymbiotic bacterium Wolbachia, which has been previously reported contribute to its reproduction. The description of the microbiota of the CBB and the implication of its roles in important functions such as nutrition, reproduction and protection against pathogens may provide novel opportunities to develop management strategies for this economically important pest. For example, inhibiting the growth of some pseudomonads, Pantoea or Wolbachia with probiotics or chemical or cultural control could help control growth or reproduction of the CBB. However, the apparent functional redundancy among taxa may mean that other species may be able to fill the niche.

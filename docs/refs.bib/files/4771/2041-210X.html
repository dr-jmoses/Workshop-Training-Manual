Summary

  1. Model selection is difficult. Even in the apparently straightforward case of choosing between standard linear regression models, there does not yet appear to be consensus in the statistical ecology literature as to the right approach.
  2. We review recent works on model selection in ecology and subsequently focus on one aspect in particular: the use of the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) or its small‐sample equivalent, . We create a novel framework for simulation studies and use this to study model selection from simulated data sets with a range of properties, which differ in terms of degree of unobserved heterogeneity. We use the results of the simulation study to suggest an approach for model selection based on ideas from information criteria but requiring simulation.
  3. We find that the relative predictive performance of model selection by different information criteria is heavily dependent on the degree of unobserved heterogeneity between data sets. When heterogeneity is small, AIC or are likely to perform well, but if heterogeneity is large, the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) will often perform better, due to the stronger penalty afforded.
  4. Our conclusion is that the choice of information criterion (or more broadly, the strength of likelihood penalty) should ideally be based upon hypothesized (or estimated from previous data) properties of the population of data sets from which a given data set could have arisen. Relying on a single form of information criterion is unlikely to be universally successful.

Introduction

The topic of model selection continues to receive considerable attention in statistical ecology. This work will present a brief review of the topic before examining the most widely used current approach, and developing a novel simulation framework for assessing the performance of a set of model selection strategies. We then suggest how to use this framework as a practical, data‐based model selection strategy. The focus throughout will be on linear models and generalized linear models (GLMs), although the general principles extend to all classes of model; in this context, the term ‘model selection’ will be interchangeable with ‘variable selection’, although more generally the former term is more broad as it can involve choosing between different classes or types of models, for example.

Review

Much current debate and discussion on model selection in ecology stems from the well‐known text Burnham & Anderson (2002), which recommended the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC; Akaike 1973) be used for model selection, ideally in a multimodel context. Theoretically, AIC is optimal in the sense of minimizing the mean square error of predictions, whereas a competing metric, the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC; Schwarz 1978), is consistent – that is, it will tend to select the ‘true’ model as the sample size increases to infinity. The concept of a true model is only useful if: (a) it exists and contains only a finite number of explanatory variables; and (b) the full set of explanatory variables is available as data. If (b) is not satisfied, Burnham & Anderson (2002, §6.3.4) suggest BIC will in practice tend to find the ‘quasi‐true’ model, one which contains (only) the subset of explanatory variables from the true model which are in the data set. A number of authors have argued that (a) is unlikely to ever be true in biological applications: for a recent discussion, see Aho, Derryberry & Peterson (2014). The standard argument (expressed also in Buckland, Burnham & Augustin 1997) is that the true model is too complex to model exactly (with number of parameters p greater than the sample size n), and that effect sizes will tend to taper: there will be a few large, important effects, followed by very many decreasing effects. This led Burnham & Anderson (2004) to state that one should use ‘AIC for tapering effects and BIC for when there are no effects at all or a few big effects and all others are zero effects (no intermediate effects, no tapering effects)’, although this is not a universally held view: Raffalovich et al. (2008) claimed via simulation experiments that BIC was generally preferable in all cases. We examine this issue closely in a simulation study.

Finding a ‘Best’ Model

In their classic regression text, Draper & Smith (1966) included a section on model selection and called it Selecting the ‘Best’ Regression Equation; the quotation marks reflect the difficulty in determining what makes a fitted model ‘best’, and also how to find it; this has been a long‐standing issue in statistics. In the third edition (Draper & Smith 1998), the section had become a whole chapter (15), with a section on the need to exercise caution when selecting models based on observational data. Potential problems include omitted variable bias (‘latent or lurking variables’; Draper & Smith 1998) observed (p. 328) that an ‘observed false effect of a visible variable’ may be due to an omitted variable, itself correlated with the visible variable. Since the values of the omitted variable are not part of the selected model, substantial changes between data sets can cause predictions from the model to ‘become unreliable’.

This is an aspect of model selection which is typically ignored (but was recognized by Wenger & Olden 2012, for example). Theoretical justifications for AIC's optimality assume that the sampling regime remains constant across repeated data sets. Shibata (1981) proved the optimality of AIC with respect to ‘[t]he expectation, with respect to future observations, of the sum of squared errors of prediction’, under the assumption for sample size n that ‘the regression function is specified by infinitely many nonzero parameters or by an increasing number of variables as n ∞’. An unwritten but necessary additional assumption is that the underlying regression model and data processes are constant across repeated sampling. It is this assumption which can be difficult to justify or verify in practice; we suspect that it is often not realistic. For example, it is common to attempt to use a model fitted at one sampling location (or ‘site’) to predict responses at another, and spatial variability may worsen predictive accuracy; temporal variation may also make prediction difficult at the same site but at different times. One question we might ask is: What effects do different kinds of between‐data set heterogeneity have on relative model prediction performance of models selected by different information criteria? We address this question in the simulation study, where we simulate data sets according to different heterogeneity regimes and examine RMSE performance of AIC, and BIC in terms of prediction quality from models they select.

A useful observation at this point is that, had we data sets from multiple sites or time points, we might naturally tend to fit a hierarchical (or random effect) model; in other words, we model explicitly the variability between sites or times, allowing each to have (in essence) a different model. In practice, however, we often do not have this luxury, being restricted instead to a single data set from a single time or place. In that circumstance, we are not able to estimate the degree of heterogeneity between data sets, but it may have an impact on our modelling; just how much is the topic of the following section.

Simulation study

Some authors have criticized past attempts at conducting simulation studies. While recognizing the importance of simulation, Burnham & Anderson (2002) argued that in practice, studies are often too simplistic or biased against AIC, because:
  1. a simple generating model is used, with no tapering, and often only few variables;
  2. the set of models considered contains the generating model; and
  3. the aim of the simulation study is to select the generating model.

Burnham & Anderson (2002) noted justification for the routine use of AIC via theoretical considerations; however, as the theory is largely based on asymptotic arguments, they also acknowledged that uncovering practical implications for ‘small’ (which technically means ‘not infinite’) data sets requires simulation experiments. Later, Burnham & Anderson (2004) suggested that the ‘proper way to compare AIC‐ and BIC‐based model selection is in terms of achieved performance, especially prediction …’. Murtaugh (2009) argued that ‘Simulation, in which the ‘true’ model is known, would seem the only definitive way to compare model‐building techniques’. Hence, we adopt this approach here.

We establish a novel simulation framework for linear models with the express aim of studying the effects of different levels and types of unobserved heterogeneity across data sets on model selection. Within the framework we can allow data sets to vary in a number of different ways, and we can use the results to compare the predictive performance of different model selection strategies. We allow any kind and any number (within computational constraints) of effect sizes, variables from the generating model can be omitted from the data set, and other, ‘spurious’ variables can be included. In this way, we avoid favouring (either deliberately or inadvertently) BIC over AIC, a particular concern of Buckland, Burnham & Augustin (1997).

Simulation in Practice

We provide an r package ICsims (https://github.com/MarkJBrewer/ICsims/) for working with our sampling framework. The user specifies sample sizes for each simulation, the number of simulations, and which of the x covariates are to be supplied as data to the model (to avoid the criticism that the generating model is in the candidate set). The code works by simulating the covariates x and z and residuals r, calculating the response y, and fitting all possible models (using the dredge function in the MuMIn package, Barton 2015). Then, a second, ‘out‐of‐sample’ data set is simulated, and the predictions using the fitted models (i.e. the models obtained from the first data set) are compared with the second set of responses; we use root mean square error (RMSE) as our metric of prediction quality. The process just described counts as one simulation in our framework; in the examples below, we run 1000 simulations.

The user supplies values for , , , , and either or the parameters for the Hardin, Garcia & Golan (2013) method (i.e. ρ, and ) if the covariance matrix is to be generated. This is perhaps a little surprising; we might have expected either AIC or to perform better here, given the theory. In the second row of plots, we see that BIC is often the best choice of criterion, even for larger sample sizes. One caveat on these comparisons is that for any given sample size, the differences in average RMSE are very small between the different criteria (except perhaps for the smallest samples); sample size itself is a bigger driver of RMSE value, as would be expected.

The reason for these results should be clear; the difference between AIC and BIC is that AIC will more often select the very weak effects in a taper. For smaller sample sizes, those effects will be estimated very poorly in relative terms – estimates of tiny positive numbers in this context can go negative, for example; in that case, zero may be a better estimate for the coefficient, which is equivalent to not selecting the variable in the first place. This is why BIC has better predictive performance for the smaller sample sizes; for larger samples, all methods perform equally well, since then, even when spurious variables are selected, the coefficients are likely to be tiny and thus the predictions are not affected greatly. corrects AIC for smaller sample sizes, but even then there is a slight edge in favour of BIC for all but the smallest.

This finding is in conflict with the earlier statement from Burnham & Anderson (2004) on the optimality and performance of AIC; it turns out that it is not the tapering effects which are favouring AIC (or ) after all.

Summary of Results

According to our simulations, the key aspect of any data set or real data situation with respect to a choice of information criterion is clearly heterogeneity, relative to effect size. With only large effects, the choice of metric is unimportant. With small effects and moderate sample sizes, and accepting that with almost all data sets in ecology there will be some form of non‐trivial correlation among candidate explanatory variables, BIC provided lower prediction error than AIC or if either the correlations or the effect sizes themselves are highly variable between data sets. It is this heterogeneity which causes the assumptions behind the derivation of AIC's supposed optimality to fail.

We stress that the simulations from our framework can account for between‐data set heterogeneity, but the modelling process does not. As noted earlier, often in practice we do not have replication – we may only have data from one time or place. It is clear from the results above that data set heterogeneity is important, but earlier work – including Burnham & Anderson (2002) – did not allow for it. This is why our findings with respect to BIC in terms of selecting models for prediction are novel.

Other than making a general statement about variability, it would appear difficult to forecast with any certainty which information criterion will perform best in any particular modelling exercise. In fact, given that there is no reason to believe that BIC is optimal any more than AIC, we could broaden the question as to what level of penalty is appropriate for any analysis. We address these issues in the following section.

We did not complicate the issue here by introducing any form of between‐data set heterogeneity; we will do that in the next section, where we consider a real data application and how our simulation framework might assist model selection.

Discussion

Submitting papers to ecological journals, we have had personal experience of reviewers insisting that AIC be used, preferably in the manner espoused by Burnham & Anderson (2002), when we have not felt it necessary or even appropriate. This ‘one size fits all’ world view is not supported by the results in this paper; as long ago as 2005, Stephens et al. (2005) were calling for ‘pluralism’, the notion that there might be different, valid ways of performing model selection. We have focussed on the use of information criteria – other aspects (especially the role of hypothesis testing) being dealt with elsewhere (e.g. Murtaugh 2014a,b) – and shown how in practice AIC (or ) is not necessarily optimal in all cases, especially when there is a high degree of unobserved between‐sample heterogeneity. That theoretical optimality only extends to situations where the data‐generating mechanisms and relationships are relatively stable, something borne out by our simulation study. Wenger & Olden (2012) found that models transferred poorly from one site to another unless model complexity was constrained; we believe the method in the section exploring the link between penalty and predictive performance and the practical data exploration suggested in the freshwater pearl mussels application could be seen as a ‘transferability assessment’, recommended by Wenger & Olden (2012).

This study has focussed on whether the claims made about AIC (or ) held in practice, and hence, our simulation study looked at RMSE exclusively. There are other criteria which could be used for model selection and comparison. For example, in some applications accuracy may be a more important concern than precision, and hence, a measure of bias of predictions might be preferred to RMSE. Rather than just assuming that RMSE is the ‘correct’ criterion, how to evaluate the quality of predictions should be considered on a case‐by‐case basis. This may be especially relevant in the case of non‐normal responses, where correct allocations to classes or groupings may be more important than simple numerical accuracy or precision. We have left aside considerations of model assessments not related to prediction, for example identifying effects and estimating effect sizes, as claims for AIC are less relevant in such cases.

There are two further issues raised by Burnham & Anderson (2002): first, null hypothesis significance testing (NHST) for model selection is ‘often poor’; and secondly, rather than focussing on a single ‘best’ model, inference should be made on the basis of multiple models, ranked and/or weighted using calculated AIC values. We discuss these issues only briefly here.

Information Criteria vs. Hypothesis Testing

We are aware, as are others (for example, Whittingham et al. 2006), of problems associated with stepwise regression using traditional F‐tests (or likelihood ratio tests, LRTs) for choosing between models. Freedman (1983) showed how and why P‐values of final models are not valid for inference – they are conditional results from multiple testing. While we share the concerns about stepwise procedures, we argue that NHST is useful when comparing a small set of models (in a pairwise fashion) when examining a particular hypothesis. These expressions differ only in a term equal to twice the difference in the numbers of parameters in the two models; when comparing two models, therefore, the two approaches are very similar. Murtaugh (2014a) notes that in a comparison of two models which differ by one parameter, preferring one model over another on the basis of AIC is equivalent to using a P‐value significance level of 15·7% in a LRT, and so AIC is more liberal in terms of including variables. Burnham & Anderson (2002, §6.9.3) give an example which they claim illustrates the theoretical finding that when comparing two models, AIC has a ‘sound theoretical basis’ whereas LRTs do not. They consider a set of models, all with the same AIC, which differ by j parameters from a null model. From the equations earlier, we can see that the LRT statistics are equal to 2j; as j increases from 1 to 30, the LRTs produce P‐values which go from 0·157 to 0·001 – that is, they change, and produce different inferences for different j, unlike AIC. It is the constancy of AIC here which supposedly provides the ‘sound’ basis; however, this reasoning is flawed, as the simple explanation is that the two approaches are testing very different things. Consider when j = 30, so that we are comparing a null model with another containing an extra 30 explanatory variables; AIC is asking ‘is it worth including all of the 30 variables?’ whereas the LRT is asking ‘is it worth including any of the 30 variables?’ Another way of looking at this is that AIC requires an improvement in log‐likelihood sufficient to justify the inclusion of all 30 variables, whereas the LRT in theory needs only an improvement sufficient to justify even a single extra variable. Either way, these are fundamentally different questions, so of course the answers are different.

Multimodel Inference

We agree that model selection should not, ideally, present only a single model as ‘best’; there are likely to be competing models, none of which can be concluded as categorically ‘better’ than the rest. Rather, the set of models should be presented and compared, and the model uncertainty noted. However, the AIC‐based approach to multimodel inference put forward by Burnham & Anderson (2002) has recently been criticized by Cade (2015), who noted, for example, that AIC is a statistic relating to models, so that any form of model averaging done at the level of the explanatory variables will be dependent on the set of candidate models, and ignores the effect of multicollinearity; Murray & Conner (2009) found that AIC was a poor indicator of a variable's ‘importance’. In addition, the use of AIC to calculate model ‘weights’ (often loosely, but incorrectly, referred to as ‘probabilities’, which strictly would require a Bayesian approach) suffers from the ‘add a spurious variable’ problem; if you take the best model by AIC, and add a randomly generated explanatory variable to form a new model, the ΔAIC will necessarily be less than 2 (as the log‐likelihood can only increase, and p increases by 1). Thus, any model which is the best model plus any other variable will automatically have a relatively large weight. Burnham & Anderson (2002) discuss this issue in their text box on p. 131; they state in this situation we should see whether the two models have ‘essentially the same’ log‐likelihood values, to assess whether the addition of the extra variable was warranted. But how are we to judge what is ‘essentially the same’? Ironically, the standard technique in statistics for determining whether the difference in log‐likelihood between two models is ‘enough’ to justify the larger model is a LRT, the very thing Burnham & Anderson (2002) are trying to avoid.

The ‘add a spurious variable’ problem was addressed by Buckland, Burnham & Augustin (1997, p. 607). Consider two competing models which differ only by inclusion/exclusion of one, spurious, variable. AIC weights will weight both models highly, in effect (almost) doubling the weight for the smaller model. A nonparametric bootstrap method, which involves selecting a single ‘best’ model from each of a number of bootstrap resamples, will only ever select one of the models for each resample; this helps avoid the ‘double weighting’ issue.

There have been a number of other studies looking at the performance of different model selection strategies. Murtaugh (1998) and Murtaugh (2009) both concluded after simulation studies that the choice of model selection approach seems to have little bearing on results, either in terms of identifying important variables or in terms of prediction quality – with the possible exception that regression tree methods seem to perform relatively poorly. We have not discussed Bayesian model selection approaches, but for a recent review see Hooten & Hobbs (2015). Further developments are being made, and new forms of information criteria have been proposed. The Watanabe–Akaike (or Widely Applicable) Information Criterion (WAIC; Watanabe 2010a,b, 2013) is an attempt from Bayesian statistics to emulate a cross‐validation analysis; while preferred to DIC, a direct leave‐one‐out criterion is now recommended (Vehtari, Gelman & Gabry 2015). There have been attempts to generalize or modify AIC and BIC in various ways; a particularly interesting example is found in Lv & Liu (2014), who decompose one of their criteria () into three parts: goodness‐of‐fit; model complexity; and model misspecification. Most information criteria address only the first two parts, and as Lv & Liu (2014) found, estimating the third part is difficult – it requires knowledge of the true model, generally unknown; the authors acknowledge that further research is needed in this area.

Applicability of Findings

As a final comment, we note that throughout this paper we have referred to data sets recorded at a single location or at a single point in time. This will be a very common scenario, but more generally, our findings hold in any situation where all of the observations have come from a single sampling unit (whether that is site, year, region, individual, species, etc.) but where there is an interest in generalizing beyond that unit; while this situation may be regarded as undesirable, in practice it may be unavoidable. Our findings are also relevant where there is only limited replication, insufficient to allow the variability between sampling units to be estimated as part of the modelling (via, e.g., random effect or hierarchical models).

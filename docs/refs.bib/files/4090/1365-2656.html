    Abstract

    1. Ecological disturbance is fundamental to the dynamics of biological communities, yet a conceptual framework for understanding the responses of faunal communities to disturbance remains elusive. Here, I propose five principles for understanding the disturbance dynamics of ants—a globally dominant faunal group that is widely used as bioindicators in land management, which appear to have wide applicability to other taxa.

    2. These principles are as follows: (1) The most important effects of habitat disturbance on ants are typically indirect, through its effects on habitat structure, microclimate, resource availability and competitive interactions; (2) habitat openness is a key driver of variation in ant communities; (3) ant species responses to disturbance are to a large degree determined by their responses to habitat openness; (4) the same disturbance will have different effects on ants in different habitats, because of different impacts on habitat openness; and (5) ant community responses to the same disturbance will vary according to ant functional composition and biogeographical history in relation to habitat openness.

    3. I illustrate these principles using results primarily from studies of ant responses to fire, a dominant agent of disturbance globally, to provide a common disturbance currency for comparative analysis.

    4. I argue that many of the principles also apply to other faunal groups and so can be considered as general ecological “laws.” As is the case for ants, many impacts of habitat disturbance on other faunal groups are fundamentally related to habitat openness, the effects of disturbance on it and the functional composition of species in relation to it.

    1 INTRODUCTION

    Ecological disturbance is fundamental to the maintenance of biodiversity in natural ecosystems (Sousa, 1984). Of the primary drivers of ecosystem dynamics, only those relating to disturbance (such as fire, herbivory and resource extraction) are widely amenable to management at the landscape scale; ecosystem management (Brussard, Reed, & Richard, 1998; Szaro, Sexton, & Malone, 1998) is therefore to a large extent about the management of disturbance (Chapin, Kofinas, & Floke, 2009). An understanding of ecosystems, and the effective management of them, therefore requires an understanding of disturbance dynamics. It is particularly important to understand the disturbance dynamics of ecologically dominant taxa, because these can have cascading effects on other biota that might not be apparent from the direct impacts of disturbance.

    Ants are a globally dominant faunal group, contributing a large proportion of terrestrial faunal biomass and playing key ecological roles as soil engineers, predators, nutrient cyclers and regulators of plant growth and reproduction (Del Toro, Ribbons, & Pelini, 2012; Folgarait, 1998; Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990). In addition, ants are widely used as bioindicators of the ecological impacts of land management (Andersen, 1999; Andersen & Majer, 2004; Majer, 1983; Underwood & Fisher, 2006). As such, ants are a priority taxon for having a well‐developed understanding of responses to disturbance, both for understanding the disturbance dynamics of an ecologically dominant faunal group whose responses are likely to have a significant effect on ecosystem function and also for maximising their bioindicator value through a mechanistic understanding of the ecological impacts of land management.

    There is an extensive literature on responses of ant communities to disturbance (Philpott, Perfecto, Armbrecht, & Parr, 2010), covering all major land management activities such as agriculture (Dalle Laste, Durigan, & Andersen, 2018; Frizzo & Vasconcelos, 2013; Kuate, Hanna, Tindo, Nanga, & Nagel, 2015; Pacheco, Vasconcelos, Groc, Camacho, & Frizzo, 2013), agroforestry (Kone, Konate, Yeo, Kouassi, & Linsenmair, 2012; Philpott et al., 2008; Wang & Foster, 2015), native forest management (Andersen, Penman, Debas, & Houadria, 2009; Arnan, Gracia, Comas, & Retana, 2009; Bihn, Gebauer, & Brandl, 2010; Nakamura, Catterall, House, Kitching, & Burwell, 2007; Vasconcelos, Vilhena, & Caliri, 2000), mining (Hoffmann, Griffiths, & Andersen, 2000; Majer, Day, Kabay, & Perriman, 1984; van Hamburg, Andersen, Meyer, & Robertson, 2004), pastoralism (Andersen, Fisher, Hoffmann, Read, & Richards, 2004; Arcoverde, Andersen, & Setterfield, 2017; Franklin, 2012; Hoffmann, 2010; Moranz et al., 2013), fire (Andersen, Woinarski, & Parr, 2012; Arnan, Rodrigo, & Retana, 2006; Vasconcelos, Maravalhas, & Cornelissen, 2017) and ecosystem restoration (Andersen, 1993; Lawes, Moore, Andersen, Preece, & Franklin, 2017; Piper, Catterall, Kanowski, & Proctor, 2009), along with associated processes such as habitat fragmentation (Debuse, King, & House, 2007; Leal, Filgueiras, Gomes, & Andersen, 2012; Vasconcelos, Vilhena, Magnusson, & Albernaz, 2006) and weed invasion (Lenda, Witek, Skórka, Moron, & Woyciechowski, 2013; Osunkoya, Polo, & Andersen, 2011; Parr, Ryan, & Setterfield, 2010). Given the great variation in the type and intensity of disturbance, and in study locations, it is unsurprising that a wide range of disturbance responses have been reported (Gibb et al., 2015; Hoffmann & Andersen, 2003; Philpott et al., 2010). However, a conceptual framework is lacking for making sense of such variable responses to disturbance, and so results from different studies can appear inexplicably inconsistent: it can be unclear why different types of disturbance have different impacts, why different studies report different responses to the same disturbance, why the same species can vary in its response to the same disturbance and why ant communities differ in their apparent resilience. For example, a recent global review of effects of fire on ant communities (Vasconcelos et al., 2017) concluded: “Overall, the results of our study indicate that habitat type is an important predictor of ant community responses to fire. However, even within a given habitat, reported effects were quite variable among the studies reviewed, evidencing the idiosyncratic nature of fire effects on ants.”

    All this can give an impression of idiosyncratic variability and chaotic unpredictability, as is the case for other faunal groups (e.g. Lindenmayer et al., 2008; Nimmo, Kelly, Farnsworth, Watson, & Bennett, 2014; Pulsford, Lindenmayer, & Driscoll, 2016). However, at least for ant communities, I believe that this is a false impression and that predictable patterns of responses to disturbance can be identified. Here, I propose five principles that provide a conceptual framework underpinning a predictive understanding (Houlahan, McKinney, Anderson, & McGill, 2017) of the disturbance dynamics of ant communities. I follow Grime (1979) in defining disturbance as any factor that removes biomass, as distinguished from environmental stress, which relates to factors that limit biomass production. I illustrate the five principles using results primarily from studies of ant responses to fire: fire is a dominant agent of disturbance in many ecosystems throughout the world (Bond & van Wilgen, 1996; Bowman et al., 2009; Whelan, 1995), it is a particularly common focus of ant community studies (Vasconcelos et al., 2017), and it provides a common disturbance currency for comparative analysis. I believe that the principles provide the foundation for a mechanistic understanding of the disturbance dynamics of this ecologically dominant faunal group and for an enhanced capacity to interpret ant responses in the context of bio‐indication. I also contend that the principles are widely applicable to many other faunal taxa.

    2 PRINCIPLE 1. THE MOST IMPORTANT EFFECTS OF HABITAT DISTURBANCE ON ANTS ARE TYPICALLY INDIRECT, THROUGH ITS EFFECTS ON HABITAT STRUCTURE, MICROCLIMATE, RESOURCE AVAILABILITY AND COMPETITIVE INTERACTIONS

    From an ant's perspective, habitat disturbance is only a direct disturbance (sensu Grime, 1979) to the extent that it removes ant biomass. Ants nesting in above‐ground structures are most susceptible to direct impacts of disturbance. For example, leaf‐nesting Oecophylla smaragdina is especially sensitive to fire in Australian tropical savannas: fire directly consumes nests occurring close to the ground, and leaf‐scorching can result in significant mortality of nests in the canopy (Andersen, Parr, Lowe, & Müller, 2007; Parr & Andersen, 2008). In Kenyan savannas, mortality during fire of ant colonies nesting in swollen acacia thorns is directly related to nest height: most colonies located lower than 2 m are killed by fire, whereas there is >80% survivorship of colonies higher than 5 m (Figure 1). Notably, frequent fire appears to have led to an evolutionary response by some of the swollen thorn‐nesting ant species, with species of Crematogaster using smoke as a cue to evacuate their nests in advance of the fire front, taking refuge in cracks within the soil (Kimuyu, Sensenig, Riginos, Veblen, & Young, 2014; Sensenig et al., 2017). These species have far higher survivorship than does Tetraponera penzigi, which remains in its swollen thorn nests (Kimuyu et al., 2014). Fire favours the subordinate species Crematogaster nigriceps over the behaviourally dominant and stronger mutualist C. mimosae, which has important implications for the defence services provided by ants against mammalian herbivores (Sensenig et al., 2017). Fire can also cause high colony mortality of tree‐nesting Cephalotes pusilus in the Neotropics (Morais & Benson, 1988), and ant species that nest in dead wood or other structures on the ground, such as acorn‐nesting species of Temnothorax (Arnan, Cerdá, Rodrigo, & Retana, 2013), can also suffer substantial direct mortality during fire.

    However, given that most ants nest in the soil, disturbance typically has a significant direct impact on ants only if it is severe enough to cause major soil disruption, such as through mining (Hoffmann et al., 2000; Majer et al., 1984; van Hamburg et al., 2004) or ploughing (Evans, Dawes, Ward, & Lo, 2011). The major effects of disturbance on ant communities are more typically indirect, through modifications to habitat structure, microclimate, resource availability and competitive interactions (Andersen et al., 2012; Fagundes, Anjos, Carvalho, & Del‐Claro, 2015; Frizzo, Campos, & Vasconcelos, 2012; Silveira et al., 2016; Vasconcelos et al., 2017). This can be caused by a single disturbance event, such as through a fire forcing species to forage on the ground when they would normally forage on trees (Andersen, 1988; Andersen & Yen, 1985) or a fire changing ground‐layer structure and therefore habitat suitability, especially through changes in microclimate (Andersen, 1988; Anjos, Campos, & Ribeiro, 2015; Arnan et al., 2013; Parr, Andersen, Chastagnol, & Duffaud, 2007). Such effects of even a single moderate disturbance event can persist for many years in some habitats (Arnan et al., 2009; Silveira et al., 2013). However, the strongest impacts of moderate disturbance are when they are chronic, such as through changes in long‐term fire regimes, where systematic variation in fire patterns can result in marked and persistent variation in habitat structure, with consequent impacts on ant communities (Andersen, 1991; Andersen et al., 2009; Maravalhas & Vasconcelos, 2014; Parr, Robertson, Biggs, & Chown, 2004; York, 2000).

    3 PRINCIPLE 2. HABITAT OPENNESS IS A KEY DRIVER OF VARIATION IN ANT COMMUNITIES

    Habitat openness (i.e. level of vegetation cover) is a fundamental driver of biological communities, and there is often a striking dichotomy between species based on their responses to openness. This is exemplified by the contrasting functional ecology of the two dominant biomes in the tropics, forest and savanna, which often coexist in areas of high but highly seasonal rainfall, with such coexistence mediated by fire (Bond & Parr, 2010; Veldman et al., 2015). The shade‐intolerant plants occurring in savanna are functionally very different from the shade‐tolerant plants occurring in forest, and relatively few species occur in both habitat types (Ratnam et al., 2011). Forest and savanna also typically have highly contrasting ant faunas that differ fundamentally in their preferences for shady or open habitats. The contrast is especially strong in Australia, where the savanna ant fauna is dominated by autochthonous, arid‐adapted elements that are missing entirely from forest faunas, and forest faunas include Indo‐Malayan taxa that never occur in savanna (Andersen, van Ingen, & Campos, 2007; Reichel & Andersen, 1996; Taylor, 1972; van Ingen, Campos, & Andersen, 2008; Figure 2).

    Habitat openness is more generally a key driver of variation in the composition of ant communities (Crist & Campbell, 2017; Dalle Laste et al., 2018; Solar et al., 2016; Underwood & Fisher, 2006; Yusah & Foster, 2016). The importance of canopy openness can pervade all aspects of ant life history. For example, founding queens of Atta leaf‐cutting ants are attracted to environments with a higher incidence of light (Forys, Allen, & Wojcik, 2002) and prefer exposed soils as nesting sites (Vasconcelos, Vieira‐Neto, Mundim, & Bruna, 2006). The establishment of Atta colonies is more successful, and established colonies are more productive, in areas exposed to sunlight (Vieira‐Neto, Vasconcelos, & Bruna, 2016; Weber, 1972). Such a preference for open areas sees species of Atta proliferate in disturbed habitats (Leal, Wirth, & Tabarelli, 2014; Siqueira et al., 2017).

    Even within ant faunas of generally open habitats, there is marked variation in habitat preferences related to degree of openness. For example, in oak canopies of northern Bavaria, some ant species strongly prefer the more open conditions of coppice regrowth, while others prefer the shadier conditions of mature stands; in this system, canopy cover is the best predictor of variation in ant composition associated with forests subject to different management practices (Dolek et al., 2009). In mesic (>1,000 mm/year) savannas of northern Australia, some ant species prefer relatively high canopy cover, others are characteristic of typical savanna, and still others prefer very open savanna (Andersen, Hertog, & Woinarski, 2006).

    Habitat openness can affect the ecological services provided by ants independently of effects on ant species composition. For example, the production of extra‐floral nectar by the perennial legume Senna chapmanii in pine rockland habitats of south Florida, designed to attract ants for defence against herbivores, is strongly influenced by light intensity such that ant defence services are provided only in open (sunny) patches (Jones et al., 2017). In open patches, ants tending extra‐floral nectaries have a major impact on herbivory rates and consequently plant reproductive fitness, but they have no such effects under shade. Increased canopy closure due to fire suppression has resulted in lower overall levels of plant protection by ants (Jones et al., 2017).

    4 PRINCIPLE 3. ANT SPECIES RESPONSES TO DISTURBANCE ARE TO A LARGE DEGREE DETERMINED BY THEIR RESPONSES TO HABITAT OPENNESS

    A primary effect of disturbance on habitat structure is to increase habitat openness, and this is of critical importance to an understanding of ant responses to disturbance. Disturbance often leads to predictable patterns of “winner–loser” replacement in ecological communities more generally that is mediated by changes in habitat openness. For example, increasing disturbance in tropical forests sees a progressive loss of shade‐tolerant plants and increased domination by relatively shade‐intolerant pioneer plants and species with generalised habitat requirements (Tabarelli, Peres, & Melo, 2012). For ant communities, increasing disturbance leads to similarly predictable changes in composition based on species preferences in relation to habitat openness. This can apply to arboreal as well as epigaeic communities, such as the case for isolated trees following deforestation (Gove, Majer, & Rico‐Gray, 2009). Species that prefer open habitats, such as those belonging to the functional groups (sensu Andersen, 1995) dominant Dolichoderinae and hot‐climate specialists, along with generalists such as opportunists and generalised Myrmicinae, are often favoured by disturbance (Hoffmann & Andersen, 2003; Kuate et al., 2015). Conversely, groups that favour closed habitats, such as cryptic species, cold‐climate specialists and many specialist predators, are typically disturbance “losers” (Hoffmann & Andersen, 2003; Kuate et al., 2015; Leal et al., 2012; Solar et al., 2016). For example, repeated burning of Amazonian forest leads to the elimination of specialist closed‐forest species and an influx of open‐habitat specialists in a “savannisation” process that mirrors vegetation structural change (Paolucci, Schoereder, & Andersen, 2017). Prescribed burning in eucalypt forest of South Australia favours opportunists and generalised Myrmicinae at the expense of cold‐climate specialists and specialist predators (Beaumont, Mackay, & Whalen, 2012), and in blackbutt forests of coastal New South Wales, frequent burning eliminates cryptic species of Hypoponera, Lordomyrma, Amblyopone, Discothyrea, Cryptopone and Ponera (York, 2000). Similarly, the foraging abundances of species of Rhytidoponera (opportunists) and Monomorium (generalised Myrmicinae) increased dramatically following fire in heathland and woodland elsewhere in south‐eastern Australia (Andersen, 1988).

    Changes in ant community composition in relation to fire‐mediated habitat openness have been extensively documented in mesic savannas of Australia's Northern Territory. With increasing time since fire, there is a progressive increase in abundance of generalised taxa associated with higher canopy cover (such as species of Nylanderia, Tetramorium, Pheidole and Odontomachus) and concomitant decline in highly thermophilic taxa characteristic of very open habitats, such as species of Iridomyrmex (dominant Dolichoderinae) and Melophorus (hot‐climate specialists; Andersen et al., 2012). For example, at Solar Village where fire had been excluded for 23 years, species associated with higher canopy cover represented 51% of all species records, compared with only 19% at an adjacent site that had been frequently burned (Andersen et al., 2006). Similarly, species of Iridomyrmex accounted for more than half of total ants collected in pitfall traps in annually burnt plots at Munmarlary, compared with <20% in plots with 14 years of fire exclusion (Andersen, 1991). The positive response of Iridomyrmex and other thermophilic taxa to fire‐mediated habitat openness in mesic Australian savannas is highly nonlinear, such that ant communities tend to occur in one of two alternative states featuring high and low abundances of thermophilic taxa, respectively, rather than in a gradient of relative abundance (Andersen et al., 2012).

    Species of Iridomyrmex and other dominant Dolichoderinae are “winners” throughout Australia due to disturbance‐mediated increases in habitat openness (Hoffmann & Andersen, 2003). Behaviourally dominant ants (sensu Arnan, Andersen et al., 2018 more generally favour well‐insolated foraging environments (Andersen, 1995), and a positive response to disturbance‐mediated increases in habitat openness appears to be typical for such species. For example, nest densities of territorial species of Formica are higher in young than old forests and, in old forests, are far higher in forest edges than in forest interiors (Punttila, 1996). Competitive interactions within ant communities in the forests therefore vary predictably with forest succession (Gibb, 2011). The positive response of behaviourally dominant species to disturbance‐induced changes to habitat openness has important implications for ant community dynamics given the importance of competition as a structuring force in ant communities (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990; Parr & Gibb, 2010), potentially making increased competition a major driver of longer‐term effects of fire (Moranz et al., 2013). The positive response of behaviourally dominant species to disturbance also has important implications for broader ecological function given the high levels of biomass sustained by dominant ants and their prominence as providers of defensive services in ant–plant interactions (e.g. Eastwood & Fraser, 1999 for ant‐lycaenid associations; Blüthgen & Fiedler, 2004 for nectar‐feeding ants; Huxley, 1982 for myrmecophytes). Fire can more generally induce major changes in the functional traits of ant communities that might have important consequences for ecosystem function (Arnan et al., 2013).

    Unsurprisingly, taxa with contrasting openness preferences often have contrasting geographical distributions, with closed‐habitat specialists restricted to regions of higher rainfall and open‐habitat specialists extending to more arid regions. The corollary of this is that by favouring open‐habitat taxa disturbance acts to “aridify” the biota. This occurs when recurrent fire in Amazonian forest leads to invasion by savanna ant species (Paolucci et al., 2017), given that savanna extends into much drier regions than does forest. In mesic savannas of northern Australia, frequent fire leads to an increased dominance by taxa having affinities with Australia's arid zone, with such Eyrean taxa having by far the strongest responses to frequent experimental fire following long‐term fire exclusion (Andersen, Ribbons, Pettit, & Parr, 2014). Similarly, scrubland fire in north‐western Patagonia leads to an increased dominance of xeric species and reduced abundances of humid species (Farji‐Brener, Corley, & Bettinelli, 2002).

    5 PRINCIPLE 4. THE SAME DISTURBANCE WILL HAVE DIFFERENT EFFECTS ON ANTS IN DIFFERENT HABITATS, BECAUSE OF DIFFERENT IMPACTS ON HABITAT OPENNESS

    The same disturbance can be expected to have different impacts on different habitats because of inherent differences in habitat openness (McCoy & Bell, 1991; Figure 3). The removal of moderate amounts of plant biomass will have little if any impact on vegetation structure in very open habitats, where openings like those caused by disturbance already occur due to environmental stress. Similarly, where habitats are open because of recurring disturbance, such as through frequent fire, then moderate levels of additional disturbance will have little impact on habitat structure. Conversely, even moderate levels of habitat disturbance can have a marked impact on vegetation structure in closed habitats, because it creates open spaces that did not previously occur. The contrasting impact of disturbance in closed compared with open habitats is epitomised by forest gap dynamics, where treefall gaps create conditions for light‐demanding plant species that cannot establish under heavy shade, and this plays a key role in maintaining species diversity (Denslow & Spies, 1990; McCarthy, 2001; Yamamoto, 2000). Such gap dynamics obviously do not apply in open habitats, where sunlight is not limiting.

    The variable effects of disturbance on habitat openness due to differences in vegetation structure have two important implications for understanding disturbance impacts on ant communities. First, it means that disturbance will tend to have a greater impact on ant communities occurring in closed compared with open habitats. The most dramatic illustration of this is the contrasting impacts of fire on tropical forest (closed‐habitat) and savanna (open‐habitat) ant communities. Ant communities of tropical forest are highly sensitive to even single fires (Paolucci et al., 2016; Silveira et al., 2013), and recurrent fire has a dramatic impact, leading to major changes in functional composition as it reflects the major changes in vegetation structure (Paolucci et al., 2017). In contrast, highly frequent fire typically has relatively little impact on savanna ant communities, because it maintains rather than transforms vegetation structure (Andersen et al., 2014; Frizzo et al., 2012; Hoffmann, 2003; Parr & Andersen, 2008; Parr et al., 2004). There is extensive evidence that the effects of disturbance such as fire on ant communities vary more generally with habitat structure (Vasconcelos et al., 2017). For example, fire in Western Australia's Purnululu National Park had a far greater impact on ants in spinifex habitat, where there were pronounced differences between burnt and unburnt sites in vegetation structure, than it did in sandplain habitat, where vegetation change was less persistent (Barrow, Parr, & Kohen, 2007). Similarly, the resilience of Mediterranean ant communities to fire varies with vegetation type (Arnan et al., 2006), as is the case for ant communities in north‐western Patagonia (Farji‐Brener et al., 2002).

    A corollary of the principle that disturbance has a greater impact on closed compared with open habitats is that disturbance will tend to have a greater impact with increasing annual rainfall, given that vegetation cover and structural complexity tend to increase with rainfall. This is strongly confounded by variation in productivity with rainfall, such that the higher immediate impacts of disturbance on structurally complex vegetation at higher rainfall sites are at least partly counterbalanced by a higher rate of recovery from disturbance. The importance of rates of vegetation recovery as a moderator of disturbance impacts on ants is illustrated by the effects of fire on Mediterranean ant communities. The cover of Quercus suber forests recovers rapidly after fire due to epicormic re‐sprouting, and this appears to confer high resilience of ant communities in these forests (Arnan et al., 2006). Despite such confounding factors, there is evidence that the impacts of disturbance on ant communities increase with rainfall. For example, results from long‐term experimental burn plots in Kruger National Park show that the greatest effects of fire on ant communities occurred in the region of the highest rainfall (Parr et al., 2004). Similarly, Mediterranean ant communities tend to be more resilient in drier areas (Arnan et al., 2006), and greatest declines in ant phylogenetic and functional diversity due to livestock grazing in Brazilian Caatinga occur in the wettest areas (Arnan, Andersen et al., 2018; Arnan, Arcoverde, Pie, Ribeiro‐Neto, & Leal, 2018).

    The second important implication of disturbance having variable effects on habitat openness depending on vegetation structure is that any given ant species will inevitably show habitat‐contingent responses to disturbance, varying according to how disturbance affects openness‐related habitat favourability for it. If habitat structure is optimal for a species prior to disturbance, then any impact of disturbance will be to reduce that species abundance, by increasing habitat openness beyond its preference. If habitat openness is currently higher than a species prefers, such that its abundance is lower than that under optimal conditions, then any impact of disturbance will be to reduce its abundance further. Conversely, when the species occurs where habitat openness is lower than it prefers, disturbance can lead to an increase in its abundance because it increases habitat favourability. The same disturbance can therefore lead to either a decrease or an increase in abundance of any given species depending on the habitat, or it can have no impact if it causes no substantial change in habitat structure. Unfortunately, there are very limited data available to test this proposition, because it requires many studies involving the same species across multiple habitats. There is support from the finding that the responses of two common ant species (Anonychomyrma sp. and Crematogaster laeviceps) to fire in a eucalypt forest of south‐eastern Australia depended on whether sites had been selectively logged (Andersen et al., 2009). However, the relative importance of habitat openness as a driver of habitat‐contingent responses to disturbance, compared with other factors relating to vegetation structure, complexity and composition, remains to be established.

    6 PRINCIPLE 5. ANT COMMUNITY RESPONSES TO THE SAME DISTURBANCE WILL VARY ACCORDING TO ANT FUNCTIONAL COMPOSITION AND BIOGEOGRAPHICAL HISTORY IN RELATION TO HABITAT OPENNESS

    The effects of disturbance on ant communities are not just determined by the extent of habitat change, but also by the resilience of the resident ant species (Osunkoya et al., 2011). In particular, arid‐adapted species that favour open habitats will generally be more resilient than shade‐adapted species that cannot tolerate open habitats. The contrasting impacts of fire on tropical forest and savanna described above are due not just to differential effects of fire on vegetation structure, but also to functionally contrasting ant faunas in relation to habitat openness. Forest ant faunas are dominated by specialist shade‐tolerant taxa that cannot tolerate the open habitats produced by fire; in contrast, savanna ant species prefer the open habitats maintained by frequent fire and so are not negatively impacted by it (Andersen et al., 2012).

    The contrasting impacts of fire on shade‐tolerant and open‐adapted ant taxa are illustrated by the contrasting responses of the behaviourally dominant weaver ant Oecophylla smaragdina and meat ants Iridomyrmex sanguineus and I. reburrus in high‐rainfall savannas of northern Australia. Oecophylla smaragdina occurs in forest habitats throughout the South‐east Asian region (Hölldobler & Wilson, 1990), whereas meat ants belong to an arid‐adapted complex from inland Australia (Shattuck, 1993). High intensity, late‐season fires at Kapalga in the Northern Territory led to marked declines in the abundance of O. smaragdina, while promoting the abundance of I. sanguineus (Figure 4; Andersen, Parr et al., 2007). Similarly, the first experimental fires at the Territory Wildlife Park near Darwin after 20 years of active fire exclusion caused dramatic declines in O. smaragdina, and they resulted in I. reburrus immediately foraging into previously unoccupied burnt plots from nests located in nearby open patches. One factor contributing to the high sensitivity of O. smaragdina to fire is direct mortality due to its leaf‐nesting habit (Andersen, Parr et al., 2007; Andersen, van Ingen et al., 2007; Parr & Andersen, 2008), and such leaf‐nesting reflects its biogeographical origins in tropical forest, where leaf‐nesting by ants almost exclusively occurs. Lower vegetative cover also contributes to the sensitivity of O. smaragdina to fire, and this likewise reflects its biogeographical origins in forest. The direct mortality suffered by O. smaradgina means that it is affected by single fires, whereas effects on I. sanguineus rely on longer‐term changes in vegetation openness due to repeated burning (Andersen, Parr et al., 2007; Andersen, van Ingen et al., 2007).

    Given that arid‐adapted species tend to be more resilient to disturbance than are shade‐adapted species because of different tolerances of habitat openness, one would expect a strong biogeographical signal in the differential responses of ant species to disturbance. This is indeed the case, as shown by the contrasting positive and negative impacts, respectively, of scrubland fire on xeric and humid species in north‐western Patagonia (Farji‐Brener et al., 2002). Importantly, such a biogeographical influence can apply to ant faunas occurring in comparable environments on different continents, which are functionally different because of historical processes. This is graphically illustrated by the contrasting biogeographical histories of the Australian and Brazilian savanna ant faunas that have led to contrasting responses to habitat openness. The Australian savanna biome has been biogeographically associated with Australia's arid zone throughout its evolutionary history, and the savanna ant fauna is therefore dominated by arid‐adapted elements that strongly prefer open habitats (Andersen, 2000b). In contrast, the biogeographical history of Brazilian savannas is associated with expanding and contracting rainforest, and its ant fauna is dominated by forest genera, with few arid‐adapted elements and many species that also occur in forest (Vasconcelos et al., 2018). These contrasting origins are reflected by contrasting responses of species richness to increasing aridity: in Australia, species richness remains constantly high throughout the rainfall gradient from >1,500 to 500 mm/year (Andersen, Del Toro, & Parr, 2015), whereas in Brazil, it declines with decreasing rainfall (Vasconcelos et al., 2018). One would predict that such contrasting origins leading to functionally different responses to habitat openness would also lead to contrasting responses to disturbance, and this is indeed the case. The Australian savanna ant fauna is highly resilient to frequent fire (Andersen et al., 2012): effects of fire are only apparent between strongly contrasting regimes, and even then there is substantial overlap of sites experiencing different regimes (Andersen et al., 2014). In contrast, highly distinctive ant communities were associated with each of three experimental fire regimes in Brazilian savanna, with no overlap in species composition at sites subject to different regimes (Maravalhas & Vasconcelos, 2014; Figure 5).

    7 CONCLUSIONS

    I have proposed five principles as a basis for a predictive understanding of the responses of ant communities to disturbance, addressing the community dynamics of a globally dominant faunal group that is commonly used as ecological indicators of disturbance impacts. The principles recognise that responses to disturbance are a function of both the extent of impact on habitat and the functional composition of the biota (Figure 6). The first principle recognises that ant responses to disturbance are primarily indirect, through modifications to habitat structure, resource availability and competitive interactions. The second principle highlights the importance of habitat openness as a driver of variation in ant communities. The remaining principles then use habitat openness as a basis for understanding variation in responses to disturbance among ant species, between habitats representing different vegetation structural types and in relation to the functional composition of ant communities. I have highlighted the importance of both contemporary and historical biogeographical processes as factors influencing community resilience through their effects on functional responses to habitat openness. These principles obviously do not encapsulate the full myriad of factors influencing the disturbance dynamics of ant communities; however, I believe that they are fundamental to an understanding of ant community responses to disturbance.

    I also believe that the five principles proposed here for ants provide a useful foundation for understanding the disturbance dynamics of terrestrial fauna more generally. The primary effects of disturbance on fauna are typically indirect, through modification of habitat (Kelly et al., 2011; Lindenmayer et al., 2016; Monamy & Fox, 2000; Nimmo et al., 2014; Whelan, 1995; Principle 1). Canopy openness is a widespread driver of variation in faunal composition (Kato, Fukasawa, & Seiwa, 2017; Muiruri & Koricheva, 2017; Principle 2) and mediator of faunal responses to disturbance (Principle 3), as illustrated by its role in the pervasive edge effects in forest fragments (Broadbent et al., 2008; Laurance et al., 2007; Zurita, Peer, Bellocq, & Hansbauer, 2012). More specifically, changes in canopy openness are often a fundamental factor driving faunal responses to fire (Ferreira, Mateus, & Santos, 2016; Harper, Ford, Lashley, Moorman, & Stambaugh, 2016). Disturbance does not just lead to the aridification of ant communities, but other taxa from drier environments tend to be less affected by disturbance, such that disturbance leads to widespread biotic aridification (Frishkoff et al., 2016; corollary of Principle 3). This includes plants: for example, grazing by livestock in grassy woodlands of southern Australia favours plant species characteristic of more arid regions (Prober & Thiele, 2004).

    Faunal responses to disturbance are characteristically habitat contingent (Lindenmayer et al., 2008; Westgate, MacGregor, Scheele, Driscoll, & Lindenmayer, 2018), having different effects on different habitats because of vegetation‐specific impacts on habitat structure (Nimmo et al., 2014). This is often related directly to differential effects on habitat openness (Principle 4). For example, disturbance has a greater impact on arthropod communities in more complex compared with structurally simple habitats (Broza & Izhaki, 1997; Gardner, Cabido, Valladares, & Diaz, 1995), and livestock grazing can have contrasting effects on reptile assemblages in arid and mesic habitats due to differential effects on habitat openness (Rotem et al., 2016). Finally, both contemporary biogeography and historical biogeography are often important factors mediating faunal responses to disturbance by determining functional responses of the biota to habitat openness (Principle 5). For example, fire favours Mediterranean over non‐Mediterranean reptile species due to their preferences for open habitats (Ferreira et al., 2016). Biogeographical origin affects termite responses to fire in African savannas, with genera having a forest rather than savanna origin being more strongly impacted because of their intolerance of open habitats (Davies, Parr, & Rensburg, 2010). Biogeographical history is an important determinant of beetle responses to disturbance in mountains of the Mexican Transition Zone, where taxa from different biogeographical origins are differentially affected by disturbance due to different preferences for open habitats (Moctezuma, Halfter, & Escobar, 2016).

    In conclusion, there is strong evidence that the principles I have proposed for understanding the disturbance dynamics of ant communities are widely applicable to other taxa—many impacts of habitat disturbance on fauna are fundamentally related to habitat openness, the effects of disturbance on it and the functional composition of species in relation to it. The five principles therefore seem to represent ecological “laws” in the sense of “widespread, repeatable patterns in nature” that apply to a wide range of taxa. They may indeed be the sort of “weak, fuzzy generalisation” (Lawton, 1999) that characterises much of community ecology. However, I believe that they nevertheless represent a significant advance in disturbance ecology in terms of a “growing mechanistic understanding of causes, patterns and processes” (Simberloff, 2004).

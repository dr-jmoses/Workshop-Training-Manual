    Abstract

    1. Behavioural responses to varying macronutrient availability are increasingly studied in highly invasive ant species to better understand their ecological success. However, such work is lacking in relation to native ant species confronted with biological invaders.

    2. Here the link between diet and behaviour was examined in Prolasius advenus, a native ant from New Zealand facing intense competition for food with invasive insects, including social wasps. A long‐term laboratory experiment was conducted to assess the impact of protein and carbohydrate restriction on several behavioural parameters.

    3. Ants shifted their food‐collecting activity towards the least available macronutrient in both protein‐limited and carbohydrate‐limited colonies. But when lacking carbohydrate, they also strongly increased their efforts to discover resources. After 10 weeks, the proportion of the colony patrolling outside of the nest was eight times higher than at the initiation of the experiment. This high patrolling activity was then maintained for several weeks and resulted in a higher efficiency to explore a novel territory. Moreover, ants fed a carbohydrate‐limited diet engaged in longer aggressive acts towards conspecifics.

    4. These behavioural responses to carbohydrate scarcity may, in part, enhance the ability of P. advenus to resist a competing invader under natural conditions. While much of the previous research has linked diet and behavioural dominance in invasive ants, the present study shows that conducting similar investigations in the native species to which they are confronted may shed light on the mechanisms behind biotic resistance and the ability of some native species to coexist with highly abundant invaders.

    Introduction

    Macronutrient availability influences foraging behaviour in a wide array of arthropods, including insect herbivores and predators (Mayntz et al., 2005; Behmer, 2009). In ants, individual and collective decision‐making allow workers to adjust their foraging efforts to the available food resources so that the colony performance is optimised (Detrain et al., 1999; Portha et al., 2002; Kay, 2004; Dussutour & Simpson, 2008). Nutrient imbalances can also induce changes in the way ants behave towards competitors or mutualists (Offenberg, 2001; Horn et al., 2013). In recent years, these topics have received particular attention in the case of highly invasive ant species, with the aim of better understanding their ecological success and associated effects on native ecosystems. A number of studies have examined how different contexts of nutrient availability and their effects at the individual, colony, population and community levels may favor or prevent the ability of an introduced ant species to maintain and expand in a new environment. For example, increased carbohydrate availability was found to increase aggression towards prey in the yellow crazy ants, Anoplolepis gracilipes (Savage & Whitney, 2011), but to decrease aggressiveness towards ant competitors in the Caribbean crazy ants, Nylanderia fulva (Horn et al., 2013). Nutrient imbalances were shown to affect colony growth and induce dietary manipulation of hoarded food in fire ants, Solenopsis invicta (Cook et al., 2010; Wilder et al., 2011), and to influence foraging activity and competitive performance in the Argentine ant Linepithema humile (Grover et al., 2007; Sagata & Lester, 2009; Kay et al., 2010). An important result emerging from this growing body of work is that ants' invasiveness may depend, in part, on their ability and tendency to respond strongly to carbohydrate availability (Rowles & Silverman, 2009; Savage & Whitney, 2011; Savage et al., 2011; Shik & Silverman, 2013).

    Conversely, there is little or no such detailed work on the influence of diet manipulation on native ant species confronted with biological invaders. Yet, direct and indirect evidence for food competition between native ants and invasive species is plentiful (e.g. Human & Gordon, 1996; Holway, 1999; Morrison, 2000; Le Breton et al., 2007; Rowles & O'Dowd, 2007; Tillberg et al., 2007). Thus, if nutritional ecology is crucial in explaining the success of invasive species, one can assume it to be equally important to understand why the degree of biotic resistance from native ants appears so variable (see Holway et al., 2002). In the present study, long‐term responses of the formicine ant Prolasius advenus (Fr. Smith, 1862) to variation in macronutrient availability were examined. Native to New Zealand, this common species has been confronted with several invasive species that have altered its access to food resources. First, field sampling in different parts of North Island showed P. advenus to co‐occur with several introduced ant species, including with the highly invasive Argentine ant L. humile (Ward, 2009; Habtom, 2012). These invasive Argentine ants have been observed to discover and exploit resources more efficiently than P. advenus regardless of temperature conditions (McGrannachan & Lester, 2012). Secondly, P. advenus aggressively compete for food with invasive Vespula wasps. Both insects engage in direct, aggressive interactions over prey carcasses which are likely to be an important source of protein for both social insects (Grangier & Lester, 2011, 2012). In addition, invasive wasps severely deplete honeydew resources in beech forests (Beggs & Wilson, 1991; Beggs, 2001) so that the ants' access to carbohydrate is likely to be reduced. The ant P. advenus may thus suffer from food competition from introduced species in a wide range of situations, making it an ideal model species to study native ants' response to variations in nutrient availability.

    Here, laboratory experiments were conducted to determine the impact of protein and carbohydrate restriction on several behavioural parameters in P. advenus workers. First, ant foraging was examined by quantifying food‐collecting and food‐searching activities over several months, while most previous studies have focused on experimental periods of only a few weeks. Exploratory activity of a novel, unmarked territory was measured as well. Secondly, ant aggressiveness was assessed during intra‐ and interspecific encounters, by taking into account both the level and duration of aggressive acts.

    Materials and methods

    Study model

    Endemic to and widespread in New Zealand, the small brown bush ant P. advenus is the most abundant and dominant ant species in much of native forest (Don, 2007; Grangier & Lester, 2011; Duthie & Lester, 2013). There are frequently several hundreds of colonies ha–1 in Nothofagus beech forests, generally nesting in soil and rotting logs (Don, 2007; Burne et al., 2014). Workers forage and scavenge on small arthropods. They also collect honeydew from the Ultracoelostoma spp. scale insects that live in abundance on beech trees (J. Grangier pers. obs.).

    Experimental design

    Fifteen P. advenus colonies were collected from beech forests of the northern South Island of New Zealand in March and April 2009, and maintained at ∼60% RH, 20 °C and LD 16 : 8 h. Three groups of five colonies each were formed, with each randomly assigned to one control and two treatment groups. Natural colony sizes were kept for experiments, because P. advenus had never been reared in captivity before and very little is known about its social organisation. We ensured, however, that the composition of the three groups did not differ significantly: there were a mean ± SE of 615.2 ± 172.9, 579.4 ± 152.1 and 535.4 ± 166.8 workers per colony (range ∼200–1200; anova, F2,12 = 0.06, df = 14; P = 0.94) and 4.2 ± 1.0, 7.2 ± 2.2 and 7.2 ± 5.7 queens per colony (range 1–30; Kruskal–Wallis test, H = 2.06, P  = 0.36). A very small amount of brood was present in each colony at the start of the experiment. Colonies were all placed in artificial plaster nests, each connected to a separate 27 × 17 × 12 cm3 foraging area with Fluon® (ethylene tetrafluoroethylene)‐coated walls to prevent ant escape.

    A 300‐day experiment was run in three periods from October 2009 until July 2010: (i) a pre‐experimental period (60 days) – all colonies were fed ad libitum with one freshly killed mealworm (average wet weight = 0.16 ± 0.02 g), and 7 ml of a 20% v/v honey water solution three times week–1; food uptake was quantified and found to be uncorrelated with colony size, confirming that this initial diet was not limiting; (ii) an experimental period (225 days) – the ‘control group’ received the same diet as during the pre‐experimental period, the ‘low‐carbohydrate group’ received the same quantity of mealworms but a low‐concentrated 2% v/v honey water solution only twice a month to ensure a severe carbohydrate limitation, and the ‘low‐protein group’ received the same quantity of honey as during the pre‐experimental period but only half a mealworm twice a month; and (iii) a post‐experimental period (15 days) – the pre‐experimental diet was used again for all colonies, to determine if the effects of the experiment on ant behaviour were reversible.

    Measurement of foraging activities

    Three different aspects of the ant foraging behaviour were examined. First, the ‘food collecting activity’ was measured 1 h after feeding the colonies by counting the number of workers that were in contact with fresh mealworms and honey. This count was undertaken seven times during the pre‐experimental period, four times during the experimental period (only during bi‐monthly occasions when both honey and mealworms were given to food‐deprived colonies), and twice during the post‐experimental period. Secondly, the ‘food‐searching activity’ was assessed by counting the total number of workers in the foraging area 24–36 h after fresh food was supplied. The counts were always done early in the afternoon, 18 times during the pre‐experimental period, 38 times during the experimental period, and eight times during the post‐experimental period (see Figure S1 for a detailed sequence of recording events throughout the experiment). Thirdly, the ‘exploratory activity’ was evaluated once before and once after diet modification (Figure S1) by examining how ants visited a wooden structure placed in the foraging area. One structure was built for each colony. Each one was composed of a central vertical piece to which three horizontal sticks were attached. Five distinct areas were defined on the structure (Figure S2). During 1 h, the progression of ants along the structure was continuously observed to record how many of the five areas they reached and how long it took the ants to do so (hereafter, ‘exploration time’). For statistical comparison, an index of exploration efficiency was calculated by dividing the total number of visited areas by the exploration time. The initial and final numbers of ants present in the foraging area were also noted, to verify if the mere presence of a resource‐free novel territory could be enough to trigger recruitment of nestmates. Finally, the total number of workers per colony was recorded at the end of the experimental period and compared with the initial populations, in order to examine whether differences in foraging activities could be linked to differences in mortality rates among groups over the 300 day period.

    Measurement of aggressiveness

    Behavioural tests were conducted to quantify both intra‐ and interspecific aggressiveness, once before and once ∼100 days after diet modification (Figure S1). To quantify intraspecific aggressiveness, all possible pairs of nests within each of the three experimental groups were tested (i.e. 10 pairs in each group). Intra‐colony aggressiveness was also measured in seven nests as a control. To obtain a comparative estimate of interspecific aggressiveness, we used the endemic and frequently sympatric myrmicine ants Huberia striata (Fr. Smith) and Huberia brounii Forel. One H. brounii and three H. striata colonies were collected from the same locations as the P. advenus and were maintained in the laboratory as for control P. advenus colonies.

    Ants used in the trials were active foragers collected from the foraging area. Aggression tests consisted of one‐to‐one interactions in a neutral circular arena with fluon‐coated walls. All interactions were observed under a stereoscopic microscope during 5 min and scored using the following index of aggression: 1, antennation (tapping the antennae somewhere on the other ant); 2, threat (opening mandibles); 3, biting; 4, fighting, usually with use of venom or formic acid. Aggressive acts (levels 2, 3 and 4) were timed. For each assessment, we calculated the average aggression index as well as the average duration of aggressive acts. Four replicate assessments were performed for each pair of nests (i.e., 120 replicate trials in total for each of the two testing periods).

    Data analyses

    To analyse food‐collecting and food‐searching activities, the relative abundances of foragers (percentage of the colony foraging out of the nest) were calculated based on raw counts, in order to account for differences in colony sizes. These data were averaged for each colony over each of the three experimental periods, and arcsine‐transformed for statistical analysis. Repeated‐measures two‐way anovas were used to examine the effect of time (before, during or after diet modification), diet (control, low carbohydrate, low protein) and their interaction on the percentage of the colony actively foraging. The Greenhouse–Geisser correction was applied to the degrees of freedom to account for violation of the sphericity assumption of variances. To analyse exploratory activity and aggressiveness, both measured once before and once after diet modification, we quantified the changes observed between the two dates and compared them among groups using anovas (or the non‐parametric Kruskal–Wallis test if data failed tests of normality or equality of variance). anovas and Kruskal–Wallis tests were followed by Bonferroni and Dunn's post hoc tests, respectively. All analyses were performed in pasw statistics 18 and graphpad prism 4 software.

    Results

    Foraging activities

    The percentage of workers patrolling in the foraging area did not present any major change over time, either in the control group or in the protein‐limited group (Fig. 1a). In the carbohydrate‐limited group, this percentage started to increase soon after the beginning of the experimental period, and did so regularly until reaching a maximum level after about 10 weeks (Fig. 1a). As a result, the average ‘food searching’ activity over the whole experimental period was significantly higher in the carbohydrate‐limited treatment than in the others, with the number of foraging individuals reaching 4.0 ± 1.3% of the total colony size (about eight times more than the initial proportion; Fig. 2a, Table 1). Carbohydrate‐limited colonies also promptly responded to the fortnightly small addition of carbohydrate. This small supply of 2% honey solution was apparently enough to effect a small reduction in foraging activity for a few days, before this activity started to increase again (Fig. 1b). Accordingly, the average number of foraging workers was significantly reduced in the 2‐week post‐experimental period (Fig. 2a).

    The ‘food collecting’ activity was affected by both nutrient‐limitation treatments (Table    1). Recruitment to honey was generally low, but was sharply increased in the carbohydrate‐limited group. Recruitment to mealworms tended to increase over time in all groups regardless of food treatment (i.e. the time × treatment interaction term was insignificant, Table 1), but this trend was much more pronounced in the protein‐limited than in the carbohydrate‐limited treatment (Fig. 2b).

    Inter‐group differences in foraging activities did not seem to be related to differences in worker mortality, as the total number of workers decreased similarly in all groups (mean change rate between pre‐experimental period and end of experimental period: −30.92 ± 13.27%, −17.3 ± 26.51% and −27.34 ± 10.87% in the control, carbohydrate‐limited and protein‐limited groups, respectively; F2,12 = 0.75, P = 0.49).

    Ants explored the wooden structures placed in the foraging areas in all cases, except in one colony of the carbohydrate‐limited group before diet modification. After diet modification, the ants' exploratory activity was more efficient and faster in the carbohydrate‐limited group, while no major change was observed in the two other groups (Fig. 3). The index of exploration efficiency (number of areas visited min–1 during the exploration time) increased between the two dates in the carbohydrate‐limited group (0.76 ± 0.11) while remaining relatively stable in the control and protein‐limited groups (−0.31 ± 0.36 and 0.01 ± 0.26, respectively) (Kruskal–Wallis test, H = 8.46, P = 0.01; Dunn's post hoc test: control vs. carbohydrate‐limited, P  < 0.05). The number of ants patrolling outside of the nest box in response to the presence of a novel structure was not influenced by diet. Indeed, the differences between the initial and final number of ants in the foraging area following the installation of the wooden structure (expressed as average ± SE percentage of initial number of ants, before and after diet modification) were highly variable (control group, 11.67 ± 20.68% and −53.50 ± 17.31%; carbohydrate‐limited group, 45.00 ± 48.99% and 132.00 ± 93.71%; protein‐limited group, 51.67 ± 37.68% and 17.33 ± 26.88%, respectively), and the change observed between the two dates did not differ significantly among groups (Kruskal–Wallis, H = 0.64, P = 0.72).

    Aggressiveness

    Intraspecific aggressiveness between P. advenus colonies was relatively low, with only one case of mortality out of all 240 observed encounters. Aggressive acts occurred, however, and the overall level of inter‐nest aggression before diet modification was significantly higher than the intra‐nest level (1.34 ± 0.07 and 1.00 ± 0.00, respectively; Mann–Whitney test, U = 22.00, P  < 0.001). The temporal change in inter‐nest aggression level did not differ significantly according to the diet treatment, although it tended to increase in the carbohydrate‐limited group (Table 2a). The time workers spent in aggressive interactions, however, varied according to the diet treatment (Table 3a): the duration of aggressive acts increased by an average of about 27 s in the carbohydrate‐limited group, remained about stable in the control group, and increased to an intermediary level in the protein‐limited group (Fig. 4).

    Workers of the two Huberia species displayed contrasting behaviours. When interacting with P. advenus, H. brounii workers were always extremely passive and often remained still. In contrast to H. brounii, H. striata workers were frequently aggressive and harmed or killed their opponent. The temporal change in P. advenus aggressiveness towards H. brounii did not differ significantly according to the diet treatment (Tables 2b and 3b). Against H. striata, changes in the aggression level did not differ significantly (Table 2c), but there was a clear trend for P. advenus workers to engage in longer aggressive acts against H. striata when lacking carbohydrate (Table 3c).

    Discussion

    The main finding of our study is that carbohydrate‐limited P. advenus colonies increased their foraging effort through all its behavioural components and, to a lesser extent, their intraspecific aggressiveness. When lacking sugar resources, P. advenus colonies not only shifted their food‐collecting activity towards sugar‐rich food items, but also strongly increased their efforts to discover such food resources. The proportion of the colony actively patrolling out of the nest reached eight times the level of activity recorded at the beginning of the experiment, and remained at this level for several weeks. The ants' efficiency at exploring a novel structure was also substantially increased by carbohydrate limitation. The latter result, however, is simply a direct consequence of the increased number of workers in the foraging areas, because the mere presence of novel structures was not found to trigger any significant recruitment of nestmates. Finally, in addition to being more active, P. advenus ants fed with carbohydrate‐limited diet also engaged in longer aggressive acts during intraspecific encounters.

    Compensatory foraging is well known in ants, under both controlled and natural conditions (Kay, 2004; Dussutour & Simpson, 2008, 2009; Christensen et al., 2010). Therefore, the result that P. advenus colonies adjusted their food‐collecting activity in favour of the least frequent macronutrient was not surprising, but it confirmed that our experimental diets effectively induced different physiological states between treatments. The marked and prolonged increase in food‐searching activities, along with longer periods of intraspecific fights are in line with a number of previous studies, but disagree with some others.

    On the one hand, starvation and reduced carbohydrate availability have been repeatedly observed to trigger increased exploration activities in ants (e.g., Wallis, 1964; Mailleux et al., 2010; Bolek et al., 2012). Grover et al. (2007) found that the per capita activity of Argentine ants during exploration of novel structures in laboratory colonies was higher under low‐ than under high‐carbohydrate diet treatments, as observed here with P. advenus. Several field studies have also pointed towards a similar pattern. For example, Rudolph and Palmer (2013) found that plant‐ants were more efficient at discovering food sources after a long‐term deprivation of carbohydrate‐producing nectaries. In the ant Formica podzolica, colonies strongly reduced their foraging activity in response to carbohydrate supplementation (Petry et al., 2012). As for aggressiveness, both laboratory and field studies showed that aggression can sometimes increase when carbohydrates are limited. For example, experimental colonies of N. fulva were clearly more aggressive towards fire ants when given a low‐ versus a high‐carbohydrate diet (Horn et al., 2013). In wood‐ants, intraspecific aggressiveness has been assumed to increase in response to a decrease in carbohydrate resources (aphids) in their habitat (Sorvari & Hakkarainen, 2004).

    On the other hand, our results do not match the predictions of the metabolic fuel hypothesis, according to which ants would invest excess carbohydrates in higher activity rates and aggressiveness (Davidson, 1997). This hypothesis has been supported by several field and laboratory studies (Yanoviak & Kaspari, 2000; Gibb & Cunningham, 2009; Kay et al., 2010; Kaspari et al., 2012). Interestingly, such a pattern was also found in several invasive ants, whose foragers were more abundant or more aggressive when given access to increased carbohydrate resources in field experiments (Rowles & Silverman, 2009; Savage & Whitney, 2011). Overall, the variety of responses described so far thus makes it difficult to predict how any given species will react when faced with varying carbohydrate or food availabilities. A phylogenetically based comparison of such responses could possibly help to detect specific behavioural traits in different ant groups.

    Except in food‐collecting activity, we did not find any significant effect of protein‐limited diet on the behaviour of P. advenus. This has to be interpreted cautiously, however, because, while larvae are known to be the most protein‐demanding members of ant colonies (Abbott, 1978), brood production was extremely low during all our experiment. Our work was thus mostly in regard to mechanisms by which workers forage to sustain themselves and the queens. Moreover, although no significant effect was found, it should be noted that foraging activity and intraspecific aggressiveness both tended to increase when prey were limiting, reaching levels that were intermediary between those observed in control and carbohydrate‐limited groups (Figs 1a, 3, and 4). Therefore it is likely that natural colonies with larger brood population may respond more strongly to variations in protein availability.

    Our study has several implications regarding the responses of native species, such as the ant P. advenus, to invading competitors. On the one hand, P. advenus ants may enhance their ability to coexist with invasive competitors by increasing their foraging efforts in response to diminished resources. For example, invasive Vespula wasps considerably reduce the amount of sugar‐rich honeydew available in Nothofagus beech forests. Several honeydew consumers suffer from exploitative competition (Beggs & Wardle, 2006), and P. advenus could also be affected, as they commonly use this resource (J. Grangier, unpublished). Our laboratory experiment shows, in principle, how P. advenus colonies could respond to a decrease in honeydew availability by increasing their foraging activity and territory. Conducted over the long term, our study further suggests that such an increased foraging effort could potentially be maintained over the period of several months of wasp activity in summer. Such a phenomenon would explain why P. advenus foragers were found to be more abundant in wasp‐maintained than in wasp‐excluded areas (Duthie & Lester, 2013). On the other hand, increased levels of foraging activities were associated with a propensity to engage in longer fights with conspecifics in experimental carbohydrate‐limited colonies, which could facilitate the progress of invaders in the native community under natural conditions. Indeed, more frequent conflicts with conspecific neighbours could increase the degree of intraspecific competition for resources and result in a lower investment in interspecific competition. Varying levels of intraspecific aggressiveness are likely to play a role in natural P. advenus populations where both one‐to‐one conflicts and, more occasionally, large‐scale fights involving dozens of workers occur (J.G. pers. obs.). In interspecific encounters with sympatric ants, it is noticeable that carbohydrate‐limited P. advenus tended to become more aggressive towards H. striata, a species with which conflicts are frequent in nature, and not towards the passive H. brounii (which is a typically submissive species). In summary, carbohydrate scarcity can have repercussions at least at the population level in P. advenus, and possibly at the community level with the most aggressive competitors (see also Barbieri et al., 2013).

    To conclude, carbohydrate limitation markedly increased foraging efforts of P. advenus ants, but also increased their intraspecific aggressiveness. These two responses may potentially have opposite effects on the ability of the species to resist a competing invader under natural conditions. Therefore, while most current research focuses on linking diet and behavioural dominance in invasive ants, our study highlights the necessity to conduct similar investigations in the native species with which they are confronted.

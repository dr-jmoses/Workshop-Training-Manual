Abstract

enThis link goes to a English sectionesThis link goes to a Spanish section

Discussion of the vertical stratification of organisms in tropical forests has traditionally focused on species distribution. Most studies have shown that, due to differences in abiotic conditions and resource distribution, species can be distributed along the vertical gradient according to their ecophysiological needs. However, the network structure between distinct vertical strata remains little‐explored. To fill this gap in knowledge, we used baits to sample ants in the canopy and understorey trees of a Mexican tropical rain forest to record the ant–tree co‐occurrences. We examined the ant–tree co‐occurrences in the canopy and understorey using complementary network metrics (i.e., specialization, interaction diversity, modularity, and nestedness). In addition, we evaluated co‐occurrence patterns between ant species on trees, using C‐score analysis. In general, we found no differences in the network structure, although the interaction diversity was greater in the understorey than in the canopy networks. We also observed that co‐occurrence networks of each vertical stratum featured four ant species in the central core of highly co‐occurring species, with three species unique to each stratum. Moreover, we found a similar trend toward ant species segregation in the both strata. These findings reveal a similar pattern of ant–ant co‐occurrences in both vertical strata, probably due to the presence of arboreal‐nesting ants in the understorey. Overall, we showed that despite the marked differences in species composition and environmental conditions between understorey and canopy strata, ant–tree co‐occurrences in these habitats could be governed by similar mechanisms, related to dominance and resource monopolization by ants.

Tradicionalmente, la discusión de la estratificación vertical de los organismos en los bosques tropicales se ha enfocado en la distribución de especies. La mayoría de estudios han mostrado que, debido a las diferencias en las condiciones abióticas y la distribución de los recursos, las especies se distribuyen a lo largo del gradiente vertical de acuerdo a sus necesidades fisiológicas. Sin embargo, la estructura de las redes entre los distintos estratos verticales aún es poco explorada. Para llenar este vacío de conocimiento usamos cebos para muestrear hormigas en árboles de dosel y de sotobosque en un bosque tropical mexicano para registrar las coocurrencias árbol–hormiga. Nosotros examinamos las coocurrencias árbol–hormiga en el dosel y en el sotobosque usando métricas de redes complementarias (i.e., especialización, diversidad de interacciones, modularidad y anidamiento). Adicionalmente, evaluamos los patrones de coocurrencias entre especies de hormigas en los árboles usando el análisis C‐score. En general, no encontramos diferencias en la estructura de las redes entre dosel y sotobosque, aunque la diversidad de interacción fue mayor en las redes de sotobosque que en las redes de dosel. También observamos que en las redes de coocurrencias de cada estrato vertical se destacaron cuatro especies en el núcleo central de las especies de alta coocurrencia, con tres especies únicas para cada estrato. Además, encontramos una tendencia similar hacia la segregación de especies de hormigas en ambos estratos. Estos descubrimientos revelan un patrón similar de coocurrencias de hormiga–hormiga en ambos estratos verticales, probablemente por la presencia de hormigas en el sotobosque que anidan en el dosel. En general, nosotros mostramos que a pesar de las marcadas diferencias en la composición de especies y en las condiciones ambientales entre los estratos dosel y sotobosque, la coocurrencia de árbol–hormiga en estos hábitats podría estar gobernada por mecanismos similares, relacionados a la dominancia y monopolización de los recursos por las hormigas.

1 INTRODUCTION

Tropical rain forests present a huge diversity of life forms, being one of the richest habitats on Earth (Basset et al., 2012). One of the main factors promoting this huge diversity is the vertical stratification of the distribution of organisms across the forest landscape, shaping a three‐dimensional environment (Oliveira & Scheffers, 2018; Smith, 1973). This complexity in spatial structure generates niche segregation among species and, therefore, is a crucial mechanism for increasing species coexistence (Oliveira & Scheffers, 2018). Although there are many possible definitions of the vertical strata in tropical forests (Moffett, 2000), canopy and understorey can be used as generalized categories. In this context, the canopy captures most of the sunlight and is composed of trees of different architectures (Barthélémy & Caraglio, 2007), interspersed with gaps created by fallen trees (Schnitzer & Carson, 2001). The understorey is composed of seedlings and treelets from species able to live in this environment (receiving only 0.5%–5% of the sunlight that falls in the canopy) and which are shade‐tolerant until their crowns reach the canopy (Chazdon & Pearcy, 2006). Moreover, from the canopy surface to the ground layer there is a decrease in light and temperature, combined with an inverse humidity gradient, leading to vertical stratification (Madigosky, 2004). Although most ecological studies have focused on biodiversity turnover along horizontal distances, the vertical distribution of organisms in tropical forests has been studied for different taxa (Oliveira & Scheffers, 2018; Popma, Bongers, & Meave del Castillo, 1988; Wilson, 1959). However, despite the increasing number of studies dealing with vertical stratification in the literature, still little is known about how species interact or co‐occur between distinct vertical strata in tropical rain forests (but see: Morris, Sinclair, & Burwell, 2015; Paniagua, Medianero, & Lewis, 2009; Schleuning et al., 2011), and this topic remains to be elucidated in detail.

Ants are among the most conspicuous organisms in tropical forests, mainly due to their high abundance and central role in many ecological processes, including their biotic interactions with different trophic levels (Del‐Claro et al., 2018). Also, ants occupy all vertical strata in a tropical forest, from the soil to the upper canopy (Wilkie, Mertl, & Traniello, 2010). Although ants are in general sensitive to changes in temperature, ants from the canopy or the understorey are subject to distinct ecophysiological constraints (Kaspari, Clay, Lucas, Yanoviak, & Kay, 2015). Furthermore, changes in resource availability across vertical strata, such as nests and food, can determine the ant community, even if narrow (Camarota, Powell, Vasconcelos, Priest, & Marquis, 2015; Philpott et al., 2018). However, hierarchical dominance is the most important force in structuring ant communities (Camarota et al., 2015; Philpott et al., 2018). Unlike the organization of the ant community at ground level, canopy ants are hierarchically structured, being classified as dominant, co‐dominant, sub‐dominant, and non‐dominant (Dejean, Corbara, Orivel, & Leponce, 2007; Majer, 1993). Dominant species exhibit aggressive behavior, large colonies, and high recruitment rates, while co‐dominant and sub‐dominant ant species share the same space and time with dominant ant species (Majer, 1993). Additionally, dominant ant species can be distributed in well‐defined territories, forming “ant mosaics,” that is, the occupation by such ant colony of several adjacent treecrowns, competing for resources and limiting the occupancy by other colonies of the same species or other dominant ant species (Majer, 1976; Room, 1972). Territorially dominant ants can nest in the canopy or at ground level, and they can forage in areas beyond the nest (Dejean et al., 2019; Tanaka, Yamane, & Itioka, 2010). Ant species that nest in one vertical stratum can interact with ant species of the other vertical stratum, influencing the foraging pattern of such species (Dejean et al., 2019). Thus, both abiotic conditions, for example, temperature and characteristics of biotic communities, for example, hierarchical dominance, lead to the vertical stratification of ants in tropical forests.

It is important to note that ants are not isolated entities in the forest and are connected to plants in several types of ecological interactions, ranging from negative (e.g., indirect herbivory) to positive (protecting host plants against herbivores) relationships (Rico‐Gray & Oliveira, 2007), even in the canopy (Blüthgen et al., 2000). However, measuring the type (quality) of the interactions between ants and plants in the field is extremely difficult (Corro et al., 2019; Fagundes et al., 2017), particularly in the canopy (Antoniazzi, Garro, Dáttilo, Ribeiro, & Neves, 2019). Moreover, ants can use plants only as substrates for foraging and patrolling (i.e., “visit event”) (Costa et al., 2016). Therefore, given the multiple types of possible relationships and the high degree of complexity involving ants' occurrence on plants, some studies have used a co‐occurrence approach to understand the organization of ant–plant associations (Corro et al., 2019; Dáttilo & Vasconcelos, 2019). The use of co‐occurrences involving highly facultative interactions, such as those involving ants and trees, proved a useful tool to assess how association patterns involving ants and plants are structured across time and space.

The high diversity of ant–plant co‐occurrences over spatial gradients generates complex networks where plant and ant species are depicted as nodes and their interactions as links (Del‐Claro et al., 2018). Several studies have provided new insights into the ecological and evolutionary processes structuring the organization of ant–plant networks (Del‐Claro et al., 2018). Moreover, we already know that ant–plant network structure can be affected by variations in the environmental conditions in different habitat types (Corro et al., 2019; Del‐Claro et al., 2018; Luna, Peñaloza‐Arellanes, Castillo‐Meza, García‐Chávez, & Dáttilo, 2018). Specifically, changes in environmental factors affect ant–plant interactions (e.g., protective mutualisms) (Rodríguez‐Castañeda, Brehm, Fiedler, & Dyer, 2016) and species co‐occurrence (Dáttilo & Izzo, 2012). However, although no study has evaluated these ant–plant networks in a vertical stratification context, it is expected changes will be found in the organization of these associations mainly due to changes in abiotic conditions and biotic communities between the canopy and understorey. Here, we sampled ants on trees to evaluate how the ant–tree co‐occurrence network structure differs between the canopy and understorey strata in a Mexican tropical rain forest. Because abiotic conditions differ among vertical strata, we made several predictions based on the assumption that both plant and ant species composition also differ among vertical strata, based on their ecophysiological requirements. We postulated that ant–tree co‐occurrence networks would be smaller in the canopy stratum, where a few territorially dominant species tend to exclude one another (Adams, 2016; Dejean et al., 2007), compared to the understorey stratum, where epigeic and arboreal ants tend to opportunistically forage on understorey plants (Brandão, Silva, & Delabie, 2012; Dejean et al., 2019). Since more ant species should co‐occur with more plant species in the understorey stratum (Benson, 1985), we expected to find higher diversity in ant–tree co‐occurrences and less specialization in understorey co‐occurrence than in canopy networks. Finally, we expected that the greater presence of dominant ants in the canopy, whose territory extends over several treecrowns (Adams, 2016; Dejean et al., 2007), would generate higher nestedness and lower modularity (i.e., higher niche overlap in the canopy compared to understorey networks).

2 METHODS

2.1 Study area

This study was carried out at the Estación de Biología Tropical Los Tuxtlas (hereafter called “Los Tuxtlas”) on the coast of the Gulf of Mexico in the state of Veracruz (18°34′N, 95°04′W; 200–350 m a.s.l.). Los Tuxtlas is a 700 ha preserved tropical rain forest located on the eastern slope of the San Martín volcano and corresponds to the northern boundary of tropical forest distribution in the Americas (Dirzo & Miranda, 1991). The climate is warm and humid, with a mean annual temperature of 25°C and annual rainfall between 3,000 and 4,600 mm. During late summer and early autumn (August–September), precipitation is influenced by tropical storms and hurricanes. Winter (December–February) is marked by less precipitation and lower temperatures, produced by the orographic uplift of polar air. Vegetation is classified as evergreen forest, with canopy trees that exceed 30 m in height. Myrmecophyte species (Ibarra‐Manríquez & Dirzo, 1990) and plants with extrafloral nectaries (Aguirre, Coates, Cumplido‐Barragán, Campos‐Villanueva, & Díaz‐Castelazo, 2013) are very uncommon in Los Tuxtlas.

2.2 Sampling ant–tree co‐occurrence networks

We selected six sampling sites separated by 250–800 m (within a total area of approximately 25 ha). At each sampling site, we chose the tallest canopy tree to serve as the central sampling point of each plot. This central canopy tree was the starting point for the delineation of six circular plots with a radius of 30 m (Leponce et al., 2019), such that each plot covered approximately 2,800 m2 (Figure S1). First, within a circular plot with 30 m radius, we labelled all trees with DBH equal or greater than 10 cm. From all the labelled trees, we selected the ones that had their treecrowns directly exposed to the sunlight (hereafter called “canopy trees”). Once we defined the canopy trees of each plot, we chose a posteriori one understorey woody plant (hereafter referred to as “understorey tree”) for each canopy tree, that is, they were paired. The understorey trees were always those closest to the canopy trees, with DBH less than 10 cm, and were never lianas or epiphytes (Table 1). The understorey trees did not touch the canopy trees; thus, we are able to avoid the interference of trees from different strata in the ant community. A total of 135 trees of each category were sampled (Table 1). Canopy and understorey trees were identified by Sr. Santiago Sinaca‐Colín and using the Los Tuxtlas herbarium as a reference.

We sampled ants at the end of summer, in October 2017. To sample canopy ants, we installed an 18‐gauge (1.21 mm) line over a top branch of each canopy tree present within the six plots using a sling shot (Sherrilltree® Big Shot). Then, we tied the two ends of the string together and attached to it a tuna and honey bait wrapped in a paper napkin. The napkin was hoisted on the line to the highest point of the canopy (where the bait touched the branch) and was left there for four hours during the day time, usually between 1,000 and 1,700 hr (adapted from Leponce et al., 2019). After this time, we carefully lowered the bait and collected ants that were foraging on it using forceps and an entomological aspirator. We also used the bait hoisted on the line to sample ants on the understorey trees. Ants were preserved with ethanol in labelled tubes. The temperature was recorded for 10 days in the canopy and understorey of three plots with a Hobo® data logger (n = 6 series of data). Temperatures ranged from 20.6 to 47.2°C in the canopy and between 20.4 and 29.6°C in the understorey.

The collected ants were transported to the laboratory at the Instituto de Ecología A.C., for sorting, mounting, and identification to the lowest possible taxonomic level. Ants were initially identified at genus level according to Baccaro et al. (2015) and then to species level using “Antweb” (Fisher, 2002) and keys provided by John Longino's web page “Ants of Costa Rica” (http://ants.biology.utah.edu/AntsofCostaRica.html). Identifications were later confirmed by experts from the Entomological Collection of the Instituto de Ecología. Ant distribution was confirmed using the most complete and current information available across the Mexican territory (Dáttilo et al., 2020). Nesting habits of ant species were classified according to specialized literature (Ennis & Philpott, 2017; Fisher, 2002; Jiménez‐Soto & Philpott, 2015; Smallwood, 1982; Yamamoto & Del‐claro, 2008) (Table 2).

2.3 Statistical analysis

We calculated the sampling completeness of ant–tree co‐occurrences by rarefying and extrapolating species richness (the number of samples was doubled) (Chao et al., 2014). For the ant–tree co‐occurrences, we used the tree species on the “x” axis and the ant species on the “y” axis to obtain the number of ant species accumulated for each new tree species. Thus, we expected to obtain the completeness of ant species in relation to tree species as a proxy of co‐occurrence among them. We used non‐metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) to visualize differences in ant and tree species composition (each trophic level in a separate analysis), at canopy and understorey levels, using the Bray–Curtis dissimilarity index. After we had examined spatial structure in the ordination, we performed a Permutation Multivariate Analysis of Variance (PERMANOVA) to test for significant clustering of variation in ant and plant species composition between the canopy and understorey, computing 999 permutations. After the NMDS representation and the PERMANOVA test, we standardized the data (ranging from 0 to 1). We performed these analyses using the packages iNEXT (Hsieh, Ma, & Chao, 2016), ggplot2 (Wickham, 2016), vegan (Oksanen et al., 2015), and labdsv (Roberts, 2007) in R (R Core Team, 2017).

2.4 Network analysis

We used the co‐occurrence of ants on trees to infer species associations (Corro et al., 2019; Dormann et al., 2018). Therefore, we analyzed 12 co‐occurrence networks (one for the canopy and one for the understorey of each of the six plots) from quantitative matrices A, where Aij was the number of records in which the plant species i and the ant species j occurred together, where a zero was recorded if they did not co‐occur. The ant–tree co‐occurrence was defined as the number of times in which a tree species co‐occurred with an ant species at each sampling point (bait), in which various ant workers of the same ant species were considered as only one record of that ant species (Miranda et al., 2019). For each ant–tree co‐occurrence network, we calculated the following network descriptors: network size, specialization, interaction diversity, modularity, and nestedness, as described below. These network descriptors cover a useful range of possible structures with complementary biological meanings within ant–tree interaction or, in our case, co‐occurrence networks (Del‐Claro et al., 2018).

Finally, we considered each ant co‐occurrence at each sampling point (bait) to determine whether the ant species settled into a consistent pattern with low co‐occurrences, using the co‐occurrence index (C‐score) (Stone & Roberts, 1990). This index is based on the average number of co‐occurrences for each unique pair of ant species and varies from 0 to 1, in which high C‐score values indicate that the co‐occurrences between ant species are highly exclusive and not randomly distributed within each vertical stratum of each plot, while low C‐score values indicate that the two ant species involved co‐occur more frequently (Stone & Roberts, 1990). The C‐score values were generated using the packages bipartite (Dormann, Gruber, & Fründ, 2008) and vegan (Oksanen et al., 2015) in the statistical program R 3.1.1 (R Core Team, 2017).

We used the paired t test (paired per plot) in R to test for differences in network size, specialization, interaction diversity, modularity, nestedness, and C‐score between canopy (n = 6) and understorey (n = 6) ant–tree co‐occurrence networks. Before performing the paired t test, we analyzed the homogeneity of variance and whether all pairs (canopy and understorey) conformed to a normal distribution.

3 RESULTS

3.1 Ants and plants

We recorded a total of 38 ant species in the canopy and understorey, of which 30 were present in the canopy and 27 on understorey (Figure 1 and Table 2). Ants belonged to 16 genera from five subfamilies, and the three most species‐rich genera were Camponotus Mayr, 1861 (nine species), Pheidole Westwood, 1839 (five species), and Pseudomyrmex Lund, 1831 (four species) (Table 2). Ten ant species were found exclusively in the canopy and eight in the understorey, and 18 ant species were present in both strata. We recorded a total of 73 tree species belonging to 35 families (Figure 1; Table S1): 39 species from 22 families (n = 84) in the canopy and 44 species (n = 109) from 27 families in the understorey. We found 34 tree species exclusively in the understorey and 29 exclusively in the canopy, while the two strata shared 10 tree species. The sample coverage of ant–tree co‐occurrences was 86.2% in the canopy and 90.1% in the understorey (Figure S2). Additionally, we observed that both ant (PERMANOVA F1,11 = 2.43, p = .004) and plant (PERMANOVA F1,11 = 3.96, p = .001) compositions differed between the canopy and the understorey (Figure 2).

3.2 Ant–tree co‐occurrence networks

We found that the individual canopy ant–tree co‐occurrence networks for each plot contained from 6 to 13 (9.7 ± 2.7, mean ± SD) ant species and 7 to 14 (10.8 ± 2.6) tree species. In the understorey, we found from 8 to 14 (11.8 ± 2.3) ant species and 10 to 19 (13.2 ± 3.1) tree species. The canopy networks included 10 to 27 (19.2 ± 7.3) co‐occurrences per plot, while understorey networks presented 18 to 40 (27 ± 8.1) co‐occurrences. The network size did not differ between the canopy (295.67 ± 185.23) and the understorey (519.33 ± 270.45) (tpaired‐test = −2.43, df = 5, p = .06). Ants and plants showed a total of 237 independent co‐occurrences. (Figure 1; Table S2).

We observed four ant species in the central core of highly co‐occurring species (generalist core) in both canopy and understorey networks, representing 13.3% and 14.8% of the total recorded species, respectively (Figure 1). Dolichoderus bispinosus was the only ant species that was part of the generalist core of both canopy and understorey networks. Camponotus sericeiventris, Pseudomyrmex elongatulus, and Pheidole dossena were the remaining generalist core species in the canopy networks, and Camponotus novogranadensis, Pseudomyrmex boopis, and Pseudomyrmex salvini were the remaining core species in the understorey networks. Although the number of generalist core ant species (four) was the same in both strata, the frequency was greater in the understorey than in the canopy stratum, in other words, the number of times that these ant species were core in the networks of each stratum. In the canopy networks (plots), the four ant species were generalist core species seven times, while the four identified in the understorey networks (plots) were generalist core species 12 times (Figure 3).

The specialization of ant–tree co‐occurrence networks did not differ between the canopy (mean ± SD: H2′: 0.16 ± 0.10) and understorey (H2′: 0.14 ± 0.08) (tpaired‐test = 0.40, df = 5, p = .70; Figure 4a). However, we observed a lower diversity of interactions in the canopy (Hi: 2.84 ± 0.40) than in the understorey (Hi: 3.17 ± 0.23) (tpaired‐test = −2.92, df = 5, p = .03; Figure 4b). We did not observe differences in the modularity values between canopy (Q: 0.62 ± 0.10) and understorey (Q: 0.59 ± 0.09) (tpaired‐test = 0.50, df = 5, p = .64; Figure 4c). We also did not find differences in nestedness between canopy (WNODF: 1.29 ± 1.37) and understorey (WNODF: 1.08 ± 1.67) (tpaired‐test = 0.49, df = 5, p = .65; Figure 4d). Network size, specialization (H2′), diversity of interactions (Hi), modularity (Q), and nestedness (WNODF) values were normal between the canopy and the understorey (p > .05). For the canopy, we observed that only 33% of the networks (n = 2) had higher interaction diversity and modularity values than expected by chance, while only one network (16.66%) exhibited a significantly specialized and nested pattern. Moreover, we found a significant nestedness value only in 33% (n = 2) of the understorey networks, while only one network (16.66%) had higher interaction diversity values than expected by chance. In addition, we observed that no understorey network exhibited a specialization and modularity value higher than those obtained from the null models.

3.3 Ant–ant co‐occurrence

We observed that the mean of ant richness per individual plant (sampling point) was 1.37 (SD: 0.60), ranging from zero to three in the canopy stratum. In the understorey, we observed that the mean of ant richness per plant individual was 1.48 (SD: 0.77), ranging from zero to five ant species. In addition, we also found that the C‐score values (based on ant–ant co‐occurrence on trees) did not differ between the canopy (mean ± SD: C‐score: 0.90 ± 0.02) and understorey strata (C‐score: 0.86 ± 0.05) (tpaired‐test = 1.25, df = 5, p = .27). C‐score values of canopy and understorey were normal (p > .05). No canopy network exhibited an observed C‐score value higher than those obtained from the null model, while only one network presented higher C‐score values than expected by chance.

4 DISCUSSION

We found that both diurnal ant species and plant species compositions were different between the canopy and the understorey. Moreover, among the four ant species that were part of the generalist core of canopy and understorey networks, only one ant species was shared between the two strata. However, contrary to what we expected, the structure of ant–tree co‐occurrence networks was similar between the canopy and the understorey, although interaction diversity was higher in the understorey than in the canopy.

We found differences in tree species composition between the canopy and understorey strata, corroborating the seminal study conducted by Popma et al. (1988) in the same study area. Distinct groups of tree species are known to have different ranges of tolerance and strategies for occupying each vertical stratum (or height) within a tropical rain forest, due to the gradient of climatic conditions from the ground level to the treetops (Givnish, 1988). We also found a distinct ant species composition between the canopy and understorey strata, a widely demonstrated pattern in tropical rain forests (Brühl, Gunsalam, & Linsenmair, 1998; Wilkie et al., 2010). An important factor driving the vertical stratification of ants is the difference in the abiotic conditions at different heights, for example, temperature (Kaspari et al., 2015). Here, we observed that the maximum temperature was higher in the canopy than in the understorey. Therefore, when the temperature affects the ants' foraging in the canopy (Kaspari et al., 2015), it should also affect the structure of ant communities and their behavior in the vertical strata, as reported in ground‐dwelling ants (Bestelmeyer, 2000). Moreover, the ant–plant interactions (Rodríguez‐Castañeda et al., 2016) and co‐occurrences (Dáttilo & Izzo, 2012) should also be affected by changes in temperature.

Likewise, the ant species composition of the central core of highly co‐occurring species was generally distinct between canopy and understorey co‐occurrence networks. We found three exclusive ant species that were part of the generalist core of highly co‐occurring species of networks in the canopy and also in the understorey, this being the only ant species shared by both vertical strata, Dolichoderus bispinosus. This ant species is polygynous (i.e., its nests contain more than one reproductive queen) and polydomous (i.e., the ant colony occupies two or more spatially separated nests), often forming nests by fission (Swain, 1977). This nest strategy contributes to the ability of D. bispinosus to present high biomass and abundance in the canopy (Tobin, 1997) and at ground level (Miranda, Oliveira, Baccaro, Morato, & Delabie, 2012) in Neotropical rain forests, although its abundance varies depending on the region (Dejean et al., 2019; Tobin, 1997). Dolichoderus bispinosus is reported to be aggressive, exhibiting exclusion behavior to other ant species in the canopy (Ribeiro, Espírito Santo, Delabie, & Majer, 2013), and is classified as a dominant ant in Neotropical canopies (Adams, Schnitzer, & Yanoviak, 2017; Majer, 1993). Although we have found other Dolichoderinae species, specifically three Azteca species, also known to be arboreal dominant species (Brandão et al., 2012; Dejean et al., 2007), these species were not part of the core of highly co‐occurring species in the canopy and understorey networks. Moreover, we sampled in some Cecropia obtusifolia tree species (Table S1) a myrmecophyte tree species that often host Azteca species. A possible explanation for this unexpected result is that some Azteca species, for instance Azteca forelii, build carton galleries on the surface of trees and feed on honeydew provided by Coccoidea Hemiptera inside these cavities (Longino & Fernández, 2007). This foraging strategy may have led to less activity on the trunk surface, leading to a lower incidence on baits. The other ant species that were part of the central core of highly co‐occurring species were three Pseudomyrmex species (one in the canopy and two in understorey networks), two Camponotus species (one in the canopy and one in understorey networks), and one Pheidole species (in the canopy networks). Some species in these genera tend to be dominant under certain conditions (Brandão et al., 2012). For instance, Pseudomyrmex species associated with myrmecophytes can exhibit high aggressiveness and recruitment rates on their host plants (Janzen, 1966), although that was not our case, since no myrmecophyte with Pseudomyrmex was sampled, for example, acacia–ants' relationship. In general, ant species belonging to the Pseudomyrmex genus have been reported as submissive species, coexisting with dominant species (Philpott, 2010; Yanoviak & Kaspari, 2000), and because they are a widespread arboreal specialist, are frequently sampled on trees (Adams et al., 2017). However, a previous study in a Neotropical rain forest that also used the sampling method we used here (bait line technique) found Pseudomyrmex species very rarely, while Pheidole and Camponotus species were frequent (Leponce et al., 2019), as we found. Some studies have reported that some Camponotus species are dominant in the canopy (Ribeiro et al., 2013) and also hierarchically dominant in ant–plant interactions (Dáttilo, Díaz‐Castelazo, & Rico‐Gray, 2014). Also, some Pheidole species are reported as resource‐dominant in studies using bait (Hashimoto, Morimoto, Widodo, Mohamed, & Fellowes, 2010). Based on these results, we propose that the structure of ant communities in the canopy of the forest studied presents some peculiarities, especially related to the dominance of ants. Future investigations related to the use of food and nest resources by canopy ants and ant–ant interactions in the canopy of Los Tuxtlas should open new insights that will help to understand our findings.

While the canopy and understorey presented distinct ant and plant species compositions and also distinct abiotic conditions (i.e., temperature), there was no difference in the structure of the ant–tree co‐occurrence networks between vertical strata (as defined by network size, specialization, modularity, and nestedness). From these findings, we can infer that the organization of these co‐occurrence networks is shaped by the same mechanisms, probably related to a similar use of resources (Rosumek et al., 2018) in both strata. Also, most of the ant species we found are arboreal nesting and forage in the canopy and understorey, limiting the influence of the ant species that nesting at ground level and opportunistically forage on the vegetation (Andersen, 1997; Dejean et al., 2019). We found that the co‐occurrences between ants and trees were highly facultative, indicated by low specialization values (H2′) (Figure 4a; Table S2). In fact, only a few of the tree species in the study area (Ibarra‐Manríquez & Dirzo, 1990) and the trees sampled by us (Table S1) are myrmecophytes or present extrafloral nectaries, which could increase the specialization in networks. Additionally, we found higher interaction diversity in understorey networks than in canopy networks, indicating that ant species co‐occur more uniformly with plants in this habitat (Benson, 1985). While interaction diversity can be affected by network size [since differences in network size should affect network structure (Blüthgen, Menzel, Hovestadt, Fiala, & Blüthgen, 2007)], we found no difference in network size between the canopy and understorey networks. On the other hand, the canopy is occupied by ant species that monopolize resources and move through large territories (Adams, 2016), generating a high frequency of co‐occurrences with only a few species and, therefore, decreasing interaction diversity in this habitat. In addition, probably due to the low degree of specialization, the topology of the canopy and understorey networks were non‐nested and moderately modular, due to species' relative abundances, increasing the probabilities of species encountering one another (Dáttilo, Marquitti, Guimarães, & Izzo, 2014; Krishna, Guimarães, Jordano, & Bascompte, 2008). Finally, future studies should evaluate other profitable resources for ants, such as the homopteran honeydew of specialized hemipterans, cavities, epiphytes, or structures as nest sites, and lianas (Adams et al., 2017; Blüthgen et al., 2000; Klimes, 2017) in order to evaluate ant–tree associations in a more integrative way.

In this study, we observed that the most important species within the networks of each stratum present almost the same dominance behavior in terms of resource use, with low co‐occurrences at the sampling point (given by C‐score analysis). Specifically, we empirically showed the importance of resource monopolization on the structure of ant–ant relationships. In fact, dominance hierarchy is one of the main factors structuring ant–tree networks (Dáttilo, Díaz‐Castelazo, et al., 2014). Thus, deterministic factors, such as resource monopolization, seem to be important in shaping ant–tree networks, rather than neutral factors alone (e.g., more frequent co‐occurrences among more abundant species) (Dáttilo, Marquitti, et al., 2014; Krishna et al., 2008). It is important to highlight that a potential limitation of the results related to ant–ant and ant–plant relationships is the spatial proximity of the sampled trees, since a same ant colony can occupy contiguous crowns and therefore our samples could be not considered truly independent. Consequently, a potential avenue for future studies is to evaluate the effect of the spatial distribution of ant–tree co‐occurrences on network architecture (Dáttilo, Vizentin‐Bugoni, Debastiani, Jordano, & Izzo, 2019), specifically at canopy level.

In summary, we have demonstrated that despite the differences in the abiotic conditions (e.g., temperature) and biotic communities (e.g., plant and ant species composition) between the canopy and understorey, ant–tree co‐occurrence networks were structurally similar in these vertical strata. These results confirm the generalization of co‐occurrences involving ants that forage on plants, related to resource dominance by some ant species, with lack of intimacy (Dáttilo, Díaz‐Castelazo, et al., 2014), tested here in the canopy and in the understorey. These findings differ from some pollination (Vázquez, Blüthgen, Cagnolo, & Chacoff, 2009) and parasitism (Poulin, 2010) interaction networks, where relationships are marked by co‐evolutionary processes (Herrera & Pellmyr, 2009) and trait matching, that must drive differences in network structure (Vázquez et al., 2009). Finally, our study suggests that the mechanisms governing the patterns of ant–tree co‐occurrence may be similar in the canopy and understorey strata.

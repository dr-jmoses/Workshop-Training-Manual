Abstract

Fluctuating resources and conditions can regulate the community structure of ants, affecting their activities and interactions. This has important implications for the selection of sampling periods during bioindication studies. The aim of this study was to evaluate if the chosen sampling period influences the response of ant assemblage diversity and ecological function to mining impacts and rehabilitation age after mining. We used ant assemblage parameters, including arboreal, epigaeic and hypogaeic ant species richness and composition, seed-removing ant species richness and composition, and seed removal rate in areas impacted by mining and with different rehabilitation ages. We showed that in most cases the response is the same, regardless of sampling period. However, we suggest that ant sampling is best undertaken in the rainy period, when ant species richness reaches higher values and the assemblage composition presents a well-marked difference among the areas. We also indicate that the epigaeic ant assemblage is sufficient for evaluating mining impacts, but arboreal strata should also be sampled in rehabilitation scenarios.

Introduction

Throughout the year there are fluctuations in resource levels and conditions, which influence the activity of living organisms and contribute to the maintenance of biological diversity (Herbers 1985; Wolda 1988). Such fluctuations can lead to species coexistence, changes in competitive interactions, alterations in resource availability, and changes in microclimatic conditions and nesting sites (Wolda 1989; Leal and Oliveira 2000; Neves et al. 2013). Some tropical regions exhibit conspicuous fluctuations, with well-defined rainy and dry periods, that directly affect the assemblages of organisms sensitive to alterations in habitat (Levings 1983; Neves et al. 2010). Changes in temperature and humidity levels influence local insect distribution, foraging patterns and interspecific interactions (Wolda 1978; Neves et al. 2010; Warren 2012).

Although some studies have not found effects of different weather periods on insects (Basu 1997; Grimbacher and Stork 2009), the majority of tropical studies suggest the rainy period as a peak of insect activity (e.g. Wolda 1978; Rico-Gray et al. 1998; Hahn and Wheeler 2002). Among tropical insects, ants are known to respond according to resource quality and quantity, both of which vary between rainy and dry periods (Keroumi et al. 2012). Thus, periods of fluctuation are considered a key factor regulating ant assemblage structure, leading to variations in abundance, richness, composition and evenness parameters of ants (Retana and CerdÃ¡ 2000; Barrow and Parr 2008).

Ant species have differing physiological demands, some of which are favored by the microclimatic conditions in particular periods (rainy and dry), and this can contribute to ant diversity (Keroumi et al. 2012). Different periods also influence ecological functions performed by ants, such as nutrient cycling, biological control and seed dispersal, affecting the magnitude and the outcomes of these functions (Fornara and Dalling 2005; Cook et al. 2011; Keroumi et al. 2012). In addition to this, anthropogenic disturbances can lead to the loss of ant species and their ecological functions, such as seed removal, leading to environmental damage that is difficult to repair (Philpott et al. 2010; Dominguez-Haydar and Armbrecht 2011).

Due to high diversity, wide geographic distribution, sampling ease, relatively well-known taxonomy and ecology, important roles in the ecosystem and sensitivity to environmental changes (Majer 1983), ants can be used as bioindicators for providing information about environmental conditions and ecological integrity of habitats (Majer et al. 2007). Ants have been widely used as bioindicators in studies on mining impacts and recovery programs (Majer 1984; van Hamburg et al. 2004; Ottonetti et al. 2006; So and Chu 2010; Ribas et al. 2012a, b, c; Schmidt et al. 2013). Indeed, ants have been suggested as useful tools to assess the effect of impacts of a wide range of human activities on ecosystems throughout the world, such as the success of recovery in rehabilitation management (Philpott et al. 2010; Ribas et al. 2012a).

Although many studies have reported changes in ant assemblage structure during the year in areas subjected to invasive species (Heller et al. 2008), pest control (Keroumi et al. 2012), habitat degradation (Viana-Silva and Jacobi 2012), agriculture (Philpott et al. 2006) or mining (Read et al. 1996), there is no study specifically addressing whether the response of ants to the same impact changes between different periods along the year. Thus, to improve the use of ants as bioindicators in environmental programs, it is desirable to investigate if the responses of ants to human impacts are affected by sampling period.

Little is known if the sampling period affects the ant assemblage diversity and ecological functions recorded in response to habitat impacts in tropical areas. To a better applying of terrestrial bioindicators, we attempt to address this gap by analyzing how sampling period influences the response of ant diversity and seed removal to mining impact and rehabilitation age after mining. We hypothesized that the response of ant diversity and seed removal is the same, independently of sampling period, in regards to mining impact and rehabilitation age after mining. Species richness and composition of arboreal, epigaeic and hypogaeic ants were used as measures of assemblage diversity, and seed-removing ant species richness and composition plus seed removal rate were used as measures of ecological function.

Methods

Study site

The study was carried out in Nova Lima, Minas Gerais state, southeastern Brazil. Our study sites are situated in a transition area between Atlantic Rainforest and Cerrado (Brazilian savanna) biomes. The average altitude of the region is 1400 m asl, annual average temperature is 21.1 Â°C, and local climate is characterized by dry winters (April to September) and rainy summers (October to March) (IBGE 2013).

The fieldwork was conducted in February/March (rainy season) and July (dry season) 2012 within three units of the iron mining company Vale S.A.: TamanduÃ¡ (20Â°5â²17â³N, 43Â°56â²27â³W), CapÃ£o Xavier (20Â°2â²47â³N, 43Â°58â²59â³W) and Mutuca (20Â°1â²43â³N, 43Â°57â²19â³W) mines. Such units also had remnants of original vegetation (hereafter called non-impacted areas) in their surroundings, which were used as control areas.

Inside each TamanduÃ¡ and CapÃ£o Xavier mine we selected three areas under mining activity (hereafter called impacted areas), and three non-impacted areas with shrubby grassland vegetation (apart CapÃ£o Xavier mine with only one non-impacted area available). In the Mutuca mine, rehabilitation areas after mining had exotic grasses (Braquiaria decumbens and Melinis minutiflora) and Cajanus cajan, and showed the following ages: two (R2), four (R4), six (R6), eight (R8) and ten (R10) years. Moreover, the older rehabilitation areas were also colonized by some species from the riparian forest. In this way, we selected one area of each rehabilitation age and two non-impacted areas with riparian forest.

Ant sampling

To sample the ants in each area, we established one transect positioned 50 m from the edge of non-impacted areas and in the centre of impacted and rehabilitation areas, each transect had ten independent sampling points at 20 m distant from each other. At each point, we installed three plastic pitfall traps, one in each strata, which were 8 cm in diameter and 12 cm in depth. A cover was used to protect against sun and rain. Hypogaeic pitfall traps had four holes (1 cm in the side of container) and were capped and buried at 20 cm depth. Pitfall traps contained a 250 ml solution of liquid soap, water and salt and remained in the field for 48 h. In the impacted areas, we did not use arboreal pitfall traps, because such areas were devoid of trees. We identified the collected ants using the keys in Palacio and FernandÃ©z (2003) and Bolton (1994) and separated them in morphospecies in the LaboratÃ³rio de Ecologia de Formigas na Universidade Federal de Lavras.

To sample seed-removing ants, we installed in each area one transect with five independent points at 40 m distant from each other, where we offered 50 artificial lipid-rich fruits (based on Raimundo et al. 2004) protected from vertebrate predation by a wire mesh (1.5 Ã 1.5 cm). The artificial fruits contained one white fleshy part representing the elaiosome (part of natural fruits attractive for ants) and the âseedâ. The fleshy part consisted of vegetable fat (75 %), fructose (4.8 %), sucrose (0.5 %), glucose (4.7 %), casein (7 %), calcium carbonate (3 %), and maltodextrin (5 %), and was developed in the LaboratÃ³rio de QuÃ­mica, BioquÃ­mica e AnÃ¡lises de Alimentos (Departamento de Engenharia de Alimentos, Universidade Federal de Lavras). To represent the âseedâ, we used orange beads (0.03 g and 2 mm diameter) which were attached to the fleshy part. These characteristics place our fruits in the small and medium categories proposed by Pizo and Oliveira (2001).

Fruits remained in the field from 0900 hours to 1600 hours (the time we could sample was determined by the Vale S.A.) and were checked every 15 min. In each observation period, we collected and recorded the ant species which removed seeds over distances greater than 30 cm from the original location (Christianini and Oliveira 2010). During the same observations, we recorded how many seeds had been removed. After 24 h from installation of the experiment, we recorded the number of seeds removed in order to calculate the removal rates. The seed-removing ants collected were also identified through keys in Palacio and FernandÃ©z (2003), and Bolton (1994), and separated to morphospecies. The identification was checked by Rodrigo Feitosa (Universidade Federal do ParanÃ¡) by comparing with the ant species collection of the Museu de Zoologia da Universidade de SÃ£o Paulo.

Data analysis

We evaluated the effect of sampling period on the response of ants in two scenarios: mining impact (impacted and non-impacted areas) and rehabilitation age after mining (different rehabilitation ages and non-impacted areas).

We verified such effect in each scenario by running the following analyses with the data from rainy and dry periods separately. We used generalized linear mixed effects models (GLMM), with Poisson distribution, to determine how sampling period affects the response of ant species richness and seed-removing ant species richness in impacted, non-impacted and rehabilitated areas. Additionally, we also used the same analysis to verify, in each scenario, if there was a sampling period effect on the richness within each type of area. We used the independent sampling points as random variables and the areas (pseudoreplicates) as fixed variables in each season, and performed the analysis using the mean number of ant species richness per sampling point in each strata separately. Seed-removing ant species richness was also calculated through the mean number of ants per point. The same analysis was used for identifying sampling period effects on seed removal rates in impacted, non-impacted and rehabilitation areas, with Binomial distribution. For the rehabilitation scenario, rehabilitation age was used as a covariate for analyzing if age would influence the response of ants to sampling period. All analyses were performed on the sampling point spatial scale, using the software R 3.01 (R development Core Team 2013) with lme4 package (Bates et al. 2013).

To verify if the response of ant species and seed-removing ant composition in impacted, non-impacted and rehabilitation age areas is affected by sampling period, we performed multivariate analysis of variance with permutation (PERMANOVA), through paired multiple comparisons. In this analysis, we selected the Jaccard index, which uses presence/absence data. Additionally, we also performed the same analysis to evaluate, in each scenario, if there was a sampling period effect on ant assemblage composition within each category of area. The program used was Primer v6 with Permanova+ (Clarke and Gorley 2006).

Results

Mining impact scenario

We recorded a total of 90 ant species belonging to 24 genera and seven subfamilies, of which 70 species were collected during the rainy period and 51 in the dry period. The subfamilies with highest richness were Myrmicinae (44), Formicinae (26) and Dolichoderinae (five), and the richest genera were Pheidole (16), Camponotus (11) and Brachymyrmex (nine). Among these ants, we observed 11 species removing seeds during the rainy period and ten species during the dry period. The most common subfamily and genus removing seeds were Myrmicinae with eight species, and Pheidole with seven species, respectively.

Sampling periods did not influence the response of epigaeic ant species richness to mining impact because in both periods higher ant species richness was found in non-impacted areas (p < 0.0001 in the rainy period; p < 0.0001 in the dry) (Fig. 1a). In addition, we observed that among impacted areas there were no differences in richness between sampling periods (p = 0.08), but in non-impacted areas ant richness was greater in the rainy than the dry periods (p < 0.0001) (Fig. 1a). We did not evaluate if sampling period influenced the response of arboreal ant species richness to mining, since these ants were sampled only in non-impacted areas. Arboreal ant species richness, in non-impacted areas, was higher in the rainy sampling period (p < 0.0001) (Fig. 1b). Hypogaeic ant species richness was similar in both sampling periods in non-impacted areas (p = 0.07). However, it was also not possible to verify the effect of sampling period on the response of hypogaeic ants in impacted areas because only one pitfall trap contained ant species.

The same situation occurred for seed-removing ant species richness and removal rates, because only one ant species (Nylanderia fulva) removed seeds once, during the dry season. The same was found for the seed-removing ant species richness (p = 0.9), and for removal rates in non-impacted areas (p = 0.1).

On the other hand, we found sampling period effects on the response of epigaeic ant species composition, where during the rainy sampling period the composition differed between non-impacted and impacted areas (p < 0.01), but in the dry sampling period, non-impacted and impacted areas presented a similar ant composition (p = 0.1) (Table 1). It was not possible to perform this analysis with arboreal and hypogaeic ants or with seed-removing ant composition for the reasons previously mentioned. However, for the analysis confined to non-impacted areas, we did not find differences in the composition between sampling periods in arboreal (p = 0.1) and hypogaeic (p = 0.4) strata, and in seed-removing ants (p = 0.9).

Rehabilitation age scenario

We collected a total of 131 ant species from eight subfamilies and 30 genera, of which 111 species were sampled in the rainy sampling period and 78 in the dry sampling period. The most common subfamilies were Myrmicinae (67) and Formicinae (33), and the richest genera were Pheidole (21), Camponotus (20), Solenopsis (14), Brachymyrmex (12) and Pseudomyrmex (11). In relation to seed-removing ant species, we collected 21 species during the rainy sampling period and 20 during the dry one. Among these species, Myrmicinae was the richest subfamily, with 25 species, followed by Ponerinae (four), and the genera with highest number of species were Pheidole (21) and Camponotus (three).

Sampling periods did not affect the response of epigaeic ant species richness, which in both sampling periods showed the same result, with ant richness of all rehabilitated areas being equal to that of the control ones (p > 0.05). The response of arboreal ant richness was influenced by sampling period because during the rainy period all rehabilitation areas had the same richness as control areas (p > 0.05), but in the dry period the rehabilitation areas had higher ant richness than the control areas (p < 0.05; except R2). In both strata the ant richness was higher in the rainy than the dry period (p < 0.004). The response of hypogaeic richness was not affected by sampling period, there being no difference in the ant richness among rehabilitated and control areas (p = 0.06). Seed-removing ant species richness (p = 0.1) and removal rates (p = 0.7) also responded in the same way in all areas. Furthermore, rehabilitation age positively influenced ant species richness in both sampling periods in epigaeic (p < 0.0004) and arboreal (p < 0.0001) strata. Hypogaeic ant species (p = 0.06), seed-removing ant species (p = 0.9) and removal rate (p = 0.5) were not influenced by rehabilitation age.

Sampling period did not affect the response of epigaeic and hypogaeic ant composition, since the rehabilitation areas were different in relation to control areas in both sampling periods (Table 2), except the R6 in the hypogaeic strata (Table 3). However, sampling period influenced arboreal ant species composition, which showed differences among all rehabilitated and control areas during the rainy sampling period, but not so during the dry sampling period for R2, R6 and R8 (Table 4). Sampling periods did not affect the response of seed-removing ant species composition, which showed similar species in the rehabilitated areas as in the control areas, except for R2 in the rainy season (Table 5).

Discussion

We observed four patterns in this study. First, in relation to mining impacts, the response of epigaeic ant richness is not affected by sampling period although epigaeic ant species composition is affected. Second, in the rehabilitation age scenario, the responses of epigaeic, hypogaeic and seed removing ant species richness and composition are not affected by sampling period. Third, in both scenarios, the ant species richness of the epigaeic and arboreal strata was greatest during the rainy sampling period. Fourth, in the rehabilitation scenario, sampling period does not affect the seed removal rates.

Mining impact scenario

Despite there being data for some invertebrate groups, studies about ant assemblages, involving species richness and seed removal ecological functions, in different sampling periods are still little explored in tropical forests (Fornara and Dalling 2005; Neves et al. 2010).Our study evaluated these parameters and we did not find sampling period effects on the response of ant species richness for epigaeic strata in the mining impact scenario. For the other strata (arboreal and hypogaeic), we were not able to conclude anything about the response of species richness to mining being affected by sampling period, since there were no ants from these strata in impacted areas. The absence of sampling period effects on the response of epigaeic ant species richness probably occurred because mining causes great changes in the soil profile and offers homogeneity of resources, leading to drastic consequences for ants (Holec and Frouz 2005; Majer 2007), and masking sampling period effects. According to our results, all these changes lead to almost total loss of species in any sampling period in impacted areas.

In relation to epigaeic ant species composition, sampling period affected their recorded response to mining. Impacted areas are populated by generalist species at any time of the year, with little exigencies for food and nest sites (Majer et al. 2007). During the dry sampling period there is an alteration in the quality and quantity of resources and conditions for ants (Kaspari et al. 2003), and this may have favored generalist over specialist species in non-impacted areas, as the former do not suffer from these alterations. These alterations in non-impacted areas, with the increase of generalist species, lead to non-impacted and impacted areas being more similar during the dry sampling period.

However, although the response of arboreal ants was not possible to evaluate (since impacted areas were devoid of trees), arboreal richness was higher during the rainy sampling period in non-impacted areas, when the habitat contains a higher quality and quantity of resources (e.g. leaves with extrafloral nectaries) and better conditions (e.g. temperature and humidity) for ants (Kaspari et al. 2003). The arrival of the dry sampling period alters the resource availability and offers risk of desiccation, causing a decrease in ant activity and switches to other types of resources (Kaspari and Weiser 2000; Rico-Gray 2004). Thus, it is possible that in the dry sampling period arboreal ants have to intensify their activities on the ground to search for other resources or to defend territory (Hahn and Wheeler 2002).

In non-impacted areas, hypogaeic ant richness and composition did not change with sampling period. In this microhabitat, the assemblage structure can be less affected by seasonal variation in abiotic factors, which in the soil show little variation throughout the year. Berghoff et al. (2002) and Schmidt et al. (2013) suggest that physical structure and soil stability are important factors for hypogaeic ant species, thus their assemblage only responds to drastic changes in soil conditions. However, the hypogaeic ant assemblage structure is still little known (Schmidt and Solar 2010).

We could also not evaluate the response of seed removing ant richness, composition and removal rates because just one species was observed in impacted areas. However, seed removing ant species richness and composition and removal rates did not change according to sampling periods in non-impacted areas. This result is surprising, since the dry sampling period is marked by possible decreases in plant reproduction activity leading to consequent decreases in seed availability (Batalha et al. 1997). Further, such similarity can be due to fact that seed removing species generally contain many opportunistic species (Hahn and Wheeler 2002), which may be able to use alternative food sources during the dry sampling period when resources are scarce.

Rehabilitation age scenario

Sampling period did not influence the response of epigaeic and hypogaeic ant richness to rehabilitation. Regardless of sampling period, in rehabilitated areas we observed a high number of ant species belonging to generalist genera like Pheidole and Solenopsis, while in control areas we detected a high number of specialist genera like Odontomachus and Pachycondyla (BrandÃ£o et al. 2012). This is not surprising because rehabilitated areas offer lower environmental complexity than control areas (Madeira et al. 2009), favoring generalist species which suffer less in stressful environments (Majer et al. 2007).This probably results in greater similarity in the number of ant species among areas.

This also possibly contributed to the same response of epigaeic and hypogaeic species composition to rehabilitation in both sampling periods, resulting in a difference in the ant composition between rehabilitation and control areas, since rehabilitated areas were populated by generalist species and control areas by specialist ones. It is also important to think about the influence of vegetation complexity on ant assemblage structure. Control areas offer greater environmental complexity, with denser canopy cover and higher plant species richness than rehabilitated areas. Thus, these different areas support distinct portions of the ant assemblage, regardless of sampling period, illustrating the fact that factors like vegetation are very important to the ant assemblage, particularly in areas that have suffered intensive disturbances (Andersen 2008; Keroumi et al. 2012).The similar response of epigaeic and hypogaeic species composition to sampling periods indicates the fact that mining causes considerable disturbance to the vegetation, which in turn reflects on the ant species which are not able to recover in 10 years.

Interestingly, sampling periods affected the response of arboreal ant species to rehabilitation. During the rainy sampling period (with higher ant richness than the dry sampling period, in all areas), rehabilitated and control areas exhibited the same ant richness, and we again point to the contribution to this result by the existence of a high number of ant species belonging to generalist genera like Camponotus (BrandÃ£o et al. 2012) in rehabilitation areas and the same with specialist genera like Myrmelachista in control areas. These differences of species groups in each area caused a difference in the species composition in this sampling period.

However, during the dry sampling period, control areas had lower species richness and similarity in composition than rehabilitation areas. Most likely, areas with high vegetation complexity, like the control areas, undergo greater alteration in their vegetation structure with the sampling periods. In the dry sampling period there is leaf loss and consequently decrease in resources and conditions for arboreal ants (Campos et al. 2006), and at this moment the availability of nesting sites maybe the major limiting factor regulating the arboreal ant assemblage (Levings 1983; Andersen 2008). This probably leads to a decrease in arboreal ant species richness in control areas in the dry sampling period.

Furthermore, specialist ant species suffer more from reduced vegetation and competition for limited resources during the dry sampling period due to environmental simplification (Sobrinho et al. 2003), causing a general decrease in their activity. Thus, the few generalist species present in control areas maintained their activity, while the specialist ones decreased, and consequently were not sampled. Since rehabilitated areas have a higher number of generalist species, the negative effects of the dry sampling period were less in these areas. Thus, during the dry sampling period, the combination of resource limitation leading to intraspecific competition and reduced activity of specialist arboreal species in control areas probably contributed to similarity among areas in this sampling period. This confirms the abiotic factor effect on the ant distribution due to the intolerance of specialist ant species to changes in conditions and resources (Coelho and Ribeiro 2006).

The response of seed-removing ant richness, composition and removal rates was not affected by sampling periods, since these parameters did not change between rehabilitated and control areas in both sampling periods. Microhabitat differentiation in conditions, such as humidity and temperature, may have little influence on the seed-removing ant assemblage, which can contain some species with discrete abiotic tolerance, leading to trends unable to be reflected at the species richness level (Warren et al. 2012). Thus, seed-removing ant richness and composition among areas was similar, since these ants suffer little influence from changes in seasonal variation. Furthermore, during the dry sampling period the seeds may comprise the greater portion of litter from the last season (Dias et al. 2012). Opportunistic ant species are able to use this resource (seeds) to compensate for the lack of others, such as flowers, fruits and others insects in this sampling period (Leal and Oliveira 2000) and hence do not cause a change in the seed removal rates between sampling periods.

Our study is the first to analyze if there is any influence of sampling period on the response of ants and their ecological function of seed removal to two scenarios: mining and rehabilitation age. We further showed that in most cases the response is the same, regardless of sampling period. Thus, as ant species richness increases in all areas during the rainy sampling period as a result of higher ant activity, this makes it easier to obtain a more precise diagnosis of the impact and rehabilitation process using ants in monitoring programs. Furthermore, sampling during rainy period, ant species composition, considered by Ribas et al. (2012a, b, c) and Schmidt et al. (2013) to provide the most predictable ant assemblage data in environmental monitoring programs, presented a well-marked difference among the areas considered in our study. Even in environments with low vegetation complexity, like in young rehabilitated areas, seasonal variation in resources and conditions occurs, decreasing the ant activity from all strata in the dry period, and consequently impairing an efficient diagnosis of rehabilitation outcomes.

Finally, we indicate that the epigaeic ant assemblage is sufficient for evaluating mining impacts, but arboreal strata should also be sampled in rehabilitation scenarios. The results presented here and their conclusions will certainly contribute to reduce fieldwork time and costs, without losing the quality of information for environmental monitoring programs. In addition, such programs urgently need fast and reliable responses about environmental conditions, and our suggestions certainly help this, besides improving the use of ants as bioindicators for conservation and management.

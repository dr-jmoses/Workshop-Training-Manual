    Abstract

    Aim

    Identifying traits that enhance the success of animals in unhospitable climates helps to understand biogeographical patterns and to predict the consequences of climatic changes. On temperate mountains, traits that increase cold performance potentially allow species to colonize higher elevations. We first tested the importance of morphological traits for the cold performance of bees. Second, we assessed the relevance of these traits for species distributional patterns by analysing intraspecific and interspecific trait shifts along elevational gradients.

    Location

    National Park Berchtesgaden, Germany; Würzburg, Germany.

    Methods

    We determined thermal activity thresholds by measuring signs of immobility along temperature gradients under laboratory conditions and related them to morphometric traits. We sampled bee communities at 34 sites along an elevation gradient in the northern Alps, measured for all species morphometric traits and thermal activity limits in the field, and analysed how trait measures changed with elevation within and across species.

    Results

    Laboratory and field experiments revealed that bees with larger body size, longer hair and relatively short wings tolerated lower temperatures before reaching immobility and started being active in the field at lower temperatures than small, short‐haired and long‐winged species. In correspondence to these results, we found that morphometric traits increased (body size, hair length) or decreased (relative wing length) with increasing elevation in communities and, in case of body size, also within species.

    Main conclusions

    Results of laboratory and field experiments and their reflectance in community‐ and species‐level trends of morphological traits along elevational gradients underscore the adaptive nature of large body size, long hair and short wings for bees’ thermal performance. Morphological traits expanding lower thermal limits probably contribute to the fitness of bees in cold environments and may simultaneously be under selection in a warming world.

    Introduction

    Climatic conditions are highly heterogeneous on earth and dynamic in time (Mannion et al., 2014). Average annual temperature is decreasing with c. 5.4 °C per 1000 km along the latitudinal gradient and with a lapse rate of c. 6.2 °C per 1 km of elevation (La Sorte et al., 2014), shaping the terrestrial surface with habitats ranging from c. −25 to 30 °C in mean annual temperature (without Antarctica). In most areas of the world species face temperatures significantly lower than the optimum temperature for population growth and have evolved traits to maximize fitness under these climatic conditions (Frazier et al., 2006; Angilletta, 2009). The identification of traits increasing the thermal performance of organisms and determining their distribution in natural ecosystems might be valuable for understanding species geographical ranges and to predict their response to climatic changes (Gardner et al., 2011; Buckley & Kingsolver, 2012; Secord et al., 2012; Moritz & Agudo, 2013). While traits increasing the thermal performance of organisms were identified for multiple taxa (e.g. Bishop & Armbruster, 1999; Hodkinson, 2005; Harrison et al., 2012), complementary data on the relevance of these traits for community assembly are rare. In this study we analysed how morphological traits influence the cold performance of temperate bees under laboratory and field conditions and assessed trait distributions in natural bee communities along an extensive climatic gradient in the northern Alps.

    Wild bees (Hymenoptera: Apoidea: Anthophila) are a highly diverse group of insects in temperate and tropical animal communities (Danforth et al., 2013). They are the most important pollinators in natural and agricultural ecosystems (Klein et al., 2007; Garibaldi et al., 2013; Classen et al., 2014) and sensitive to climatic changes along elevational, land use and seasonal gradients (Hoiss et al., 2012; Fründ et al., 2013; Rader et al., 2013; Classen et al., 2015; Kühsel & Blüthgen, 2015). While the activity, life history and species richness of bees is constrained by a variety of biotic and abiotic factors (e.g. availability of flower resources and humidity conditions) across broadly defined environmental gradients temperature appears to be of key importance (Hoiss et al., 2012; Classen et al., 2015).

    At temperate latitudes and high elevations, temperatures are below the thermal optimum of bees through large parts of the year (Stone & Willmer, 1989; Herrera, 1995; Kovac & Stabentheiner, 2011) and significant amounts of floral resources become available in spring when mean daily temperatures are still low (Fitter & Fitter, 2002; Oberrath & Böhning‐Gaese, 2002; Aldridge et al., 2011; Inari et al., 2012). Cold climates constitute a challenging environment for bees, because for flying they have to achieve high operating temperatures (Heinrich, 1974). In true ectothermic organisms this would constrain foraging activities to hours with high temperatures (Bishop & Armbruster, 1999; Buckley et al., 2012). However, many bee species have the capability to significantly elevate and maintain thoracic temperatures by metabolic heat production (‘thermogenesis’) which promotes fast exploitation of resources even at low temperatures (Harrison et al., 1996; Bishop & Armbruster, 1999; Heinrich, 2004; Goulson, 2010). Because of their small size (in comparison to e.g. endothermic mammals) bees have to cope with an enormous heat loss when foraging in cold environments (Bishop & Armbruster, 1999). Heat loss enhances energetic costs of thermoregulation and reduces efficiency of foraging flights (Stabentheiner & Kovac, 2014). Adaptations mitigating heat loss may increase the foraging efficiency and the temporal window for foraging in bees. They are, consequently, expected to be under high selection pressure and to be the basis of filtering effects of cold environments on the regional species pool (Peat et al., 2005; Hoiss et al., 2012).

    Among the large number of mechanisms influencing the thermal performance of organisms (Ramlow, 2000; Sinclair et al., 2003a,b; Angilletta, 2009; Harrison et al., 2012; Kaspari et al., 2015), morphological adaptations (e.g. changes in body size, the length of body appendages or changes in the density and length of isolating hair to reduce heat loss) are possibly the most studied (Hodkinson, 2005; Peat et al., 2005; Meiri, 2011; Harrison et al., 2012; Hoiss et al., 2012). Everything else being equal, organisms of small size lose heat faster than large organisms making them more susceptible to coldness. Due to the same reasons, organisms with long body appendages, like wings [insect wings exhibit a complex system of veins in which haemolymph is circulating from the thorax into the wing and back (Arnold, 1964; Pass, 2000)], are more prone to heat loss than those with short appendages (Ray, 1960; Shelomi, 2012). In bees, cooling is slower and the capability to elevate and maintain thoracic temperatures is higher for large than for small bees (Bishop & Armbruster, 1999). In fact, across a large number of bee species of different families, body size is the main determinant of bees’ thermoregulatory capabilities and bees of larger size started foraging at lower environmental temperatures than small species (Bishop & Armbruster, 1999). This suggests a large influence of body size and other thermally relevant traits on bees’ cold activity and community assembly.

    In this study we first determined minimum thermal activity thresholds of a diverse set of bees (37 species from five families) in the laboratory and analysed how they are correlated with three morphometric traits potentially influencing heat loss, that is, body size, thoracic hair length, and relative wing length (Chown et al., 2002; Peat et al., 2005; Goulson, 2010). Second, in bee communities in the northern Alps we assessed lower thermal limits of foraging activity of species and related them to morphometric traits. Third, we studied the distribution of traits across bee communities along elevational gradients in the northern Alps. Mountains provide ideal conditions to study changes of species traits along climatic gradients (Hoiss et al., 2012; Graham et al., 2014; Leingärtner et al., 2014; Peters et al., 2014) as climatic conditions rapidly change with elevation (c. 1000× more rapidly than along latitudinal gradients). This allows well replicated studies of trait distribution in natural species communities along extensive climatic gradients within feasible amounts of time (Körner, 2007; Sanders & Rahbek, 2012).

    We tested the following three hypotheses: Firstly, that a larger body size, longer hair and short body appendages reduce heat loss of bees allowing them to tolerate cold temperatures for longer time periods before reaching immobility (Peat et al., 2005). We therefore expect a significant correlation between bees’ minimum thermal activity threshold and morphometric traits related to heat conservation (body size, hair length and relative wing length). Secondly, that body traits facilitating heat conservation allow bees to expand their thermal window for foraging to times of low temperature. We therefore expect a significant positive correlation between species’ minimum temperature of activity in the field and morphometric traits that improve heat conservation. Finally, that morphological traits which have a positive effect on the cold performance of bees influence the foraging success and the survival probability of individuals and species at low temperature (Heinrich, 1999). We therefore expect that the frequency of traits increasing cold performance increases in communities or populations from warm to cold habitats along temperature gradients on mountains (Reeve et al., 2000).

    Materials and methods

    Sampling of bees and laboratory measures of thermal activity thresholds

    For determining the relationship between morphometric traits and minimum thermal activity thresholds, we collected bees in the vicinity of Würzburg from mid of April to early July 2013. The area of Würzburg (Fig. 1; c. 49.7833° N, 9.9333° E) is situated at an elevation of 177 m a.s.l. in a region with temperate seasonal climate. Mean annual temperature is 9.2 °C. During the collection time mean monthly temperature changed from 9.6 to 21.3 °C and monthly precipitation averaged 56 mm. To collect a diverse set of species from different families we collected bees in different habitats, for example, on calcareous grasslands, semi‐natural grasslands with fruit trees, vineyards, meadows, forest edges or along small trails in the agricultural land. Bees were collected with sweep nets while slowly moving through habitats. Collected bees were stored in a container with moist tissue and transferred within 1 h to the laboratory. As bees may adapt physiologically to the temperature conditions changing between April and July and this could influence their cold tolerance, for each bee we registered the mean daily temperature at the collection day and used it as a covariable in statistical models (for details see below). The mean daily temperature was thereby assumed to approximately capture the seasonal temperature conditions in the time period immediately (approximately several days) before collection. Thermal activity thresholds of bees were measured by exposing single bees within 2 mL (small bees) or 15 mL (large bees) plastic tubes to decreasing temperatures generated within a programmable thermo block (Eppendorf ThermoStatC, Hamburg, Germany) (Diamond et al., 2012). To increase the speed of temperature declines in the heat block (which is fairly slow for temperatures below 5 °C when the heat block is operated at room temperature), we operated it within a climate chamber regulated at 10 °C. Starting temperature of the heat block was 14 °C (pre‐experiments showed that this temperature was tolerated by all bees). Bees were placed within tubes in the heat block. The tubes were closed with cellulose tissue during experiments which permitted free exchange of gases. The initial temperature was held for 10 min after which the temperature was reduced two times by 2 °C (up to 10 °C) and thereafter at intervals of 1 °C. Each temperature was held constant for 10 min after which we checked bees for signs of mobility. We recorded the minimum thermal activity threshold as the temperature where mobility was no longer visible (Kellermann et al., 2012a,b). After the experiment bees were frozen at −20 °C, mounted for morphometric measurements and identified to species level. Please note that similar methods were used in other taxa (e.g. ants, Diamond et al., 2012) to determine the critical thermal minimum (CTmin) of organisms. However, due to thermogenic capabilities of bees, the temperature at which we observed immobility may not correspond to the body temperature of the bee. Therefore, in bees this measure may not be directly comparable to the CTmin of true ectothermic organisms but should be interpreted as an indicator for the lower thermal limit of bees’ mobility, which is probably influenced by heat loss, the capability of bees to produce metabolic heat and other factors.

    Sampling of Alpine bee communities and measurements of thermal activity in the field

    Bees were collected at 34 study sites (60 m× 60 m) along an elevational gradient from 641 to 2032 m a.s.l. in the western part of the National Park Berchtesgaden, Germany, northern Alps (Fig. 1, see Appendix S1 in Supporting Information; c. 47.5333° N, 12.9167° E). All study sites were located on grasslands. Eighteen of the selected grasslands were extensively managed and 16 grasslands were not managed. Thirty study sites were equipped with temperature loggers which continuously measured temperatures in 2 h intervals over the full field season. Wild bees and honeybees were recorded by sweep netting during transect walks from 8 May to 10 September 2009. Sites at lower altitudes were sampled six times and sites at elevations > 1200 m a.s.l. were sampled five times owing to the shorter snow free season. Sampling was conducted from 09.30 to 18.00 on days with sunny conditions; when temperatures at 650 m a.s.l. exceeded 17 °C sampling was also conducted on cloudy days. More than 300 observations were done at temperatures between 9 and 17 °C. On study sites with temperature loggers, we recorded for each sampled bee the temperature at collection. From these measures we calculated for each bee species per study site the minimum and maximum temperature of their observed activity in the field. All specimens were killed using ethyl acetate, mounted and identified to species level in the laboratory. For details of the sampling see Hoiss et al. (2012). From each species per site, one individual was randomly selected for measurements of morphometric traits (see below).

    Measurement of morphometric traits

    We measured the intertegular distance (ITD) as a proxy for body size, wing length, and different measures of hair length at different parts of the bee body (Fig. 1c). We calculated relative wing length as the ratio between forewing wing length and ITD. Different hair length measures were strongly correlated (r = 0.88–0.94 for different combinations of hair length measures) and we therefore only used the hair length at the mesonotum as a proxy for the ‘hair volume’ of the bee. Measurements were done using a binocular Leica stereomicroscope at a magnification of 8–80× with a calibrated ocular micrometer. Errors of morphometric measurements were estimated by repeatedly measuring the same bee individuals at 10 different days. Measurement errors (measured as the SD of measurements taken from the same individual) were small compared to the variation of trait measures among bee species (SDmeasurement error/SDtrait among species: ITD = 5%, wing length = 4%, wing width = 10%, hair length = 9%) and even among the individuals within species (SDmeasurement error/SDtrait within species; ITD = 25%, wing length = 14%, wing width = 29%, hair length = 28%). All measurements were done by the same observer (J.P.).

    Statistical analysis

    The relationship between the thermal activity threshold measured in the laboratory and morphometric traits were analysed using mixed effects models with thermal activity threshold as the response and one of the three tested morphometric traits (ITD, hair length, relative wing length) as a fixed effect variable. Mean temperature of the day when bee individuals were collected in the field entered as a covariable as we assumed changes in the extent of cold performance during the seasonal change from cold spring climate to warm summer. As we had multiple measurements for some of the species we added species as a random variable in models. We additionally used phylogenetic generalized linear models for analyses which control for the phylogenetic non‐independence of data points not only within species but also within higher taxonomic levels. We constructed a pseudo‐phylogenetic tree with the topology based on taxonomic information and a recent review on bee phylogeny (Danforth et al., 2013). We used the pgls function as provided in the R package ‘caper’ to simultaneous estimate the phylogenetic signal for branch length optimization (λ) and to calculate parameter estimates for all explanatory variables (Freckleton et al., 2002). For comparative purposes, we also calculated trends in thermal activity thresholds for each bee family separately using ordinary linear models. Please note that as our study concentrated on trends of traits at the community level, the number of sampled individuals per family and accordingly the statistical power to detect significant trends for single families was, however, rather low.

    We analysed the thermal activity of bees in the field in relationship to morphometric traits across a total of 30 study sites in the Alpine study region (at four study sites no temperature data loggers were installed). Analyses of trends in the thermal activity were conducted using mixed effect models with the trait as the fixed effect predictor and minimum or maximum temperature of observed activity as the response variable. We entered site as a random intercept term in models to control for variation in mean temperature among study sites. Ordinary linear models in which we entered study site as a second fixed effect revealed quantitatively and qualitatively highly similar results. In addition, we performed phylogenetic linear models as described in the previous paragraph to analyse data on thermal activity patterns while controlling for a potential influence of phylogenetic autocorrelation.

    We analysed the distribution of morphometric traits along elevational gradients at the community and species level. For the community‐level analyses, we calculated for each study site the mean of the respective trait across all species which were collected at the site. We analysed trends in the community mean of traits with elevation using ordinary linear models. Analyses of intraspecific trends were conducted using mixed effect models with elevation as the fixed effect predictor and trait as the response variable. We entered species as a random intercept term and elevation as a random slope term in models allowing for random variation in the slopes and intercepts of individual species. We also used ordinary linear models with species (and elevation) as fixed effect variables or linear mixed effects models allowing for random intercepts but not for random slopes but results were not different to those of the random slope + random intercept mixed effects model. We therefore report only results of the random slope + random intercept model. In addition to the average model of intraspecific trends in traits, we also calculated trends for single species using ordinary linear models but restricted analyses to those species which were sampled at ≥ 5 study sites.

    Results

    We recorded thermal activity thresholds of bees under laboratory conditions and their corresponding morphological traits for 113 individuals from 35 species belonging to five bee families (Andrenidae, Apidae, Colletidae, Halictidae, Megachilidae; see Appendix S2). We found a significant negative correlation between the minimum thermal activity threshold and the body size or hair length of bees, that is, large and long‐haired bees tolerated on average colder temperatures than small and short‐haired bees before becoming immobile (Table 1, Fig. 2). These trends were observed across species and also within multiple but not all bee families. On average, the minimum thermal activity threshold decreased with decreasing relative wing length: bees with relatively short wings showed lower thermal activity thresholds than long‐winged bees. Interestingly, the temperature at the collection day generally had a negative but in most cases only marginally significant effect on the minimum thermal activity threshold, that is, individuals that were collected on warm days tolerated lower temperatures before becoming immobile. The phylogenetic signal in the data, estimated by the λ parameter, was low and results did not qualitatively differ between mixed effects models and phylogenetic linear models.

    In alpine bee communities, we observed 3284 individuals and a total of 84 bee species across the 34 study sites (see Appendix S3). For 1872 of the individuals, the temperature at observation could be derived. The minimum temperature at which active bees were observed in the field was on average lower for large bee species with relatively long hair and relative short wings than for small, short‐haired and long‐winged bees (Fig. 3, fixed effects of mixed effects models: minimum temperature of active bees = 22.13–1.00 × ITD, t = −5.47, P < 0.001; = 21.10–2.57 × hair length, t = −5.7, P < 0.001; = 15.28 + 1.25 × relative wing length, t = 4.00, P < 0.001). When we analysed data with phylogenetic linear models, we found a low phylogenetic signal and highly similar results (compared to simple mixed effects models not controlling for a phylogenetic signal in the data) for the effect of ITD and hair length on the minimum temperature of active bees (PGLS models: minimum temperature of active bees = 22.86–1.13 × ITD, λ = 0.07, t = −2.93, P =0.004; = 21.65–2.93 × hair length, λ = 0.05, t = −3.35, P < 0.001). An effect of relative wing length was not significant (P = 0.20) but the tendency of the parameter estimate remained qualitatively similar (minimum temperature of active bees = 17.74 + 0.67 × relative wing length, λ = 0.13). While morphometric traits were correlated with the minimum temperatures of field activity, no significant correlation was found with maximum temperatures of activity (ITD: t = −0.89, P = 0.37; hair length: t = −1.75, P = 0.08; relative wing length: t = −0.15, P = 0.88; PGLS models for all morphometric variables: P > 0.10), suggesting that in the alpine environments the temperature range at which bees forage is on average larger for large, long‐haired and short‐winged bees than for small, long‐winged and short‐haired bees.

    In alpine bee communities, species’ mean body size (ITD) and hair length increased with elevation while the relative wing length decreased (Fig. 4a–c; ITD: r² = 0.60, F1,32 = 47.7, P < 0.001; hair length: r² = 0.44, F1,32 = 24.7, P < 0.001; relative wing length: r² = 0.49, F1,32 = 30.4, P < 0.001; for all analyses N = 34 study sites). At the intraspecific level, we found the ITD to increase with increasing elevation, while all other traits were not significantly correlated with elevation (Fig. 4d,e; fixed effects of mixed effects models: ITD = 2.20 + 0.0001x, t = 2.51, P < 0.001; hair length = 0.46 + 0.00003x, t = 1.10, P = 0.273; relative wing length = 3.42–0.00003x, t = −0.66, P = 0.51). A comparison of slope parameters derived from community‐ and species‐level analyses revealed that changes in the community mean of traits were > 8 times higher than average intraspecific changes.

    Discussion

    We found that cold performance of bees, measured in the laboratory and under field conditions, was significantly more pronounced for bees with large body size, long hair and relatively short wings. Bees of large body size, with long hair and short wings also became more frequent in bee communities along the elevational gradient in the Alps suggesting that these traits enhance species’ and individuals’ success in cold climates, that is, to be adaptive. While some studies already related trends in animal body traits to temperature, our study is novel by including trait analyses at both the species‐ and community level (Vellend et al., 2014) and by complementing macroecological data on trait distributions in natural species communities with laboratory and field data on thermal activity of insects.

    We found that larger bees with relatively short wings and long hair were able to tolerate lower temperatures than small, long‐winged and short‐haired bees before reaching immobility in laboratory experiments. These results are concordant with thermo‐physical laws predicting increased energy loss from conduction for organisms with larger surface‐to‐volume ratios (Meiri & Dayan, 2003; Shelomi, 2012), that is, for bees of small body size and long body appendages, like wings. Related to this, the metabolic rate I scales with I α mass0.75 so that organisms of smaller size have higher energetic consumption per g of body mass than large organisms (Brown et al., 2004; Darveau et al., 2005). Small organisms, particularly those which can thermoregulate thorax temperature like bees (Stone & Willmer, 1989; Roberts & Harrison, 1998; Goulson, 2010; Stabentheiner & Kovac, 2014), may therefore reach energetic limits much faster than large organisms. We also found that long hair increased the cold performance of bees. In bees, hairs form an insulation layer which mitigates heat loss (Peat et al., 2005). Many insects insulate themselves with scales, air sacs and or setae and many bee species, for example, many bumble bee species, have a thick hair coat all over the body and particularly around the thorax (Heinrich, 1999).

    Morphological traits did not only predict thermal activity threshold of bees in the laboratory but also the temperature at which bees started foraging at field sites in the northern Alps. In agreement with past studies (Corbet et al., 1993; Willmer & Stone, 2004), we found that in bee communities of alpine habitats, bees of large size began being active at lower temperatures than small bees. Moreover, cold activity was higher for bees with long hair and relative short wings. Maximum temperatures of activity were uncorrelated with morphological traits such that the thermal window for foraging can be assumed to be larger for large bees with long hair and relatively short wings.

    We found that traits which were significantly correlated with the thermal performance of bees to become more frequent in communities along an elevational gradient of 1400 m in the Alps where mean annual temperature declines by c. 0.45 °C per 100 m of elevation. Our results underscore findings of Ruttner et al. (1995) and Peat et al. (2005), who report honeybees and bumblebees’ body size and hair length to increase along latitudinal temperature gradients respectively. As our study was not restricted to these genera but included species of 20 genera and six families these traits appear to be of importance for structuring the composition and morphology of bees at the community level along extensive environmental gradients.

    It has to be noted that body size, the length of body appendages and hairs are only three of a larger set of traits that are probably relevant for the thermal performance of bees. Other traits like colour/melanization or the hair density on the thorax were shown to be important in some taxa but remained unstudied in our analysis (Hodkinson, 2005). Including additional morphological, biochemical or behavioural traits in future analyses may therefore contribute to a better understanding of thermal performance of bees.

    We generally found trait trends to be either concordant with community level trends, as in case of body size, or idiosyncratic at the species level leading to non‐significant average intraspecific trends (Cornwell & Ackerly, 2009; Vellend et al., 2014). Hair length and relative wing length increased in some species with elevation but decreased in others suggesting different environmental constraints to drive the evolution of these traits in different species (Ruttner et al., 1995). For example, the decreasing temperature along elevational gradients may select for shorter wings in bees but a reduced air density may favour individuals with larger wings (Hodkinson, 2005). The importance of these potential drivers of selection may differ among species and may strongly depend on the ecology of species (Vellend et al., 2014). Importantly, changes in the community mean of traits were several times higher than the average intraspecific change in traits suggesting that most trends observed at the community level were due to species turnover rather than due to intraspecific changes in trait measures pointing to a strong role of environmental filtering in shaping trait distribution along the elevational gradient (Hoiss et al., 2012).

      Biosketch

      Marcell K. Peters studies animal communities and their interactions along extensive environmental gradients. His current research is focused on describing and explaining elevational patterns of organismic diversity on mountains. He is a postdoctoral research scientist at the University of Würzburg.

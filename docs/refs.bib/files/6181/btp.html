    Abstract

    en

    Species diversity is the variable most commonly studied in recent ecological research. Ecological processes, however, are driven by individuals and affected by their abundances. Understanding the variation in animal abundances along climatic gradients is important for predicting changes in ecosystem processes under global warming. High abundances make arthropods, despite their small body sizes, important actors in food webs, yet abundance distributions of major arthropod taxa along climatic gradients remain poorly documented. We sampled arthropod assemblages in disturbed and undisturbed vegetation types along an elevational gradient of 860–4550 m asl on the southern slopes of Mt. Kilimanjaro, Tanzania. In our analysis, we focused on 13 taxa of arthropods that represented three major functional groups: predators, herbivores, and decomposers. Abundance patterns were unimodal for most of the taxa and functional groups, including decomposer arthropods, and most of them peaked at low elevations in lower montane forest. When we assigned beetles to functional groups, however, decomposer beetle abundances declined almost linearly, and abundances of predator beetles (ca. 2400 m asl) and herbivore beetles (ca. 3000 m asl, undisturbed vegetation) peaked at higher elevations and exhibited unimodal patterns. Temperature, not primary productivity, was the best predictor of abundance for most of the taxa and groups. Disturbance was only of minor importance. Our results revealed different trends in the response of arthropod abundance along the elevational gradient that depended on the level of taxonomic and functional resolution. This highlights the need for more comparisons of different taxa along the same climatic gradients.

    In light of the worldwide loss of biodiversity, species diversity is the most common variable reported in recent ecological studies. The most obvious and often most influential variable, however, is the abundance of organisms (Hooper et al. 2005). This is why invertebrates, despite their small body sizes, are essential for fundamental ecological services, including pollination, decomposition, and pest control (Hooper et al. 2005, Losey & Vaughan 2006), as well as ecological disservices, such as the transmission of diseases (Drakeley et al. 2005) and as agricultural pests (Showler 2002).

    Local declines in the abundance of species are faster and more important for the functioning of an ecosystem than the final loss of an individual species (Dirzo et al. 2014, Winfree et al. 2015). Although the most important drivers for the declines of both the abundance and diversity of species is the change in land use (Sala et al. 2000, Dirzo et al. 2014) and climate change (Chapin et al. 1995, Warren et al. 2001, Parmesan 2006), we still know little about potential changes in the abundance of species in response to such changes (Becker et al. 2007). It has been predicted that the abundance of ectothermic invertebrates declines with declining available energy or productivity (Srivastava & Lawton 1998, Meehan 2006, Yee & Juliano 2007, but see Currie et al. 2004, Isaac et al. 2013) and temperature (Lavelle et al. 1995, but see Meehan 2006). As a result, the rates of ecological processes in which these invertebrates are involved should decrease (Burnside et al. 2014). Furthermore, the changes in abundance with productivity or temperature should vary based on biological differences among feeding guilds, functional groups (Dell et al. 2011, Vucic‐Pestic et al. 2011, Seifert et al. 2014), and/or trophic levels (Holt et al. 1999, Dobson et al. 2006).

    Elevational gradients provide an opportunity to study the relative importance of temperature and productivity on the abundance of ectotherms (e.g., Werenkraut & Ruggiero 2014, Classen et al. 2015). While temperature lapse rates are consistently close to −0.65°C per 100 m of elevation in moist‐adiabatic systems (Dillon et al. 2006), the relationship between productivity and elevation is more variable (Marshall et al. 2012, Lewis et al. 2013). Most of the limited number of previous studies comparing several invertebrate taxa found a decline in the abundance of arthropods with increasing elevation (Lawton et al. 1987, Wachter et al. 1998, Goldsmith 2007, Lee et al. 2012), but some found mid‐elevation peaks (Janzen et al. 1976, Werenkraut & Ruggiero 2014) and even increases in abundance (McCoy 1990). However, the number of studies comparing abundances of different functional groups over elevation is still very low (McCoy 1990, Olson 1994, Smith‐Patten & Patten 2015).

    We analyzed the abundances of 13 common epigeic arthropod taxa sampled with pitfall traps (activity abundances) along an elevational gradient of 3685 m on the southern slopes of Mt. Kilimanjaro and addressed the following questions: (1) are there systematic differences between the elevational patterns of activity abundance between arthropod taxa belonging to different functional groups? (2) Are arthropod abundances correlated with temperature, productivity, or humidity, and do the responses of taxa or functional groups differ? (3) Do arthropod taxa or functional groups respond differently to environmental variables in disturbed and undisturbed vegetation types?

    Methods

    Study Area

    Mt. Kilimanjaro is an inactive volcano that rises from the savannah at about 700 m asl to the summit surrounded by glaciers at 5895 m asl (Nonnotte et al. 2008). It is located in northeastern Tanzania, close to the Kenyan border and just south of the equator (2°45ʹ to 3°25ʹS; 37°00ʹ to 37°43ʹE). This region experiences two distinct rainy seasons, with heavy rains in March–May and mild rains from mid‐October to December, an extended cold dry season in June–September, and a warm dry season in January–February (Chan et al. 2008). The southern slopes of the mountain receive most of the precipitation, with a peak of 2500 mm mean annual precipitation at mid‐elevations around 2200–2500 m asl (Rohr & Killingtveit 2003, Appelhans et al. 2015). The temperature lapse rate changes at 2320 ± 71 m asl (± SE) from dry‐adiabatic to moist‐adiabatic, corresponding to a lower lapse rate of −0.84°C/100 m, and an upper lapse rate of −0.42°C/100 m (Appelhans et al. 2015). The soils in the colline savannah zone at the foot of the mountain have been classified as ferralsols and acrisols (Zech et al. 2011). The soils in the cultivated zone on the lower slopes (ca. 1100–1900 m asl) are mainly anthrosols, while andosols dominate the montane forest belt (ca. 2000–3200 m asl; Downie et al. 1956, Zech 2006, Zech et al. 2011, 2014).

    Sampling Sites

    Along the elevational gradient, sampling sites were established within six elevational zones where the most important natural vegetation types occur, i.e., those that cover the largest area on and around Mt. Kilimanjaro: savannah (870–1155 m asl), lower montane forest (1560–2040 m asl), Ocotea forest (2120–2750 m asl), Podocarpus forest (3830–3900 m asl), Erica forest (3830–3900 m asl), and alpine Helichrysum communities (3880–4550 m asl), resulting in an elevational gradient spanning 3685 m (see also Table S1). In four of these elevational zones, additional sampling sites were established in disturbed environments. The type and extent of disturbance is unique for each vegetation type. Savannah has been, and still is, transformed to agricultural fields (865–1010 m asl), agroforestry replaced most of the lower montane forest (1170–1790 m asl), historical selective logging removed Ocotea trees (2220–2560 m asl), and natural fires destroyed most of the old growth Erica forests and affected the Podocarpus forests situated at even lower elevations (2770–3060 m asl), which form the recent treeline (Lambrechts et al. 2002, Hemp 2006a). We did not sample in disturbed Erica or Helichrysum communities. Furthermore, a land‐use gradient was established in the cultivation zone between 1100 and 2050 m asl, including the major management systems present in this highly productive elevation zone. According to decreasing canopy cover, these are lower montane forest (1560–2040 m asl), agroforestry/homegardens (1170–1790 m asl), coffee plantations (1125–1650 m asl), and grasslands (1305–1750 m asl) (Hemp 2006b, Hemp & Hemp 2009). In each of the 12 vegetation types, five sampling sites of 50 m × 50 m were established with a minimum distance of 300 m between the individual sites (Fig. 1, see also Ensslin et al. 2015). The five sites were chosen to represent the variation within a vegetation type with regard to vegetation structure, canopy cover, and elevational extent. Owing to logistics at the time of sampling, we could not collect samples on one of the Erica forest sites, which reduced the number of sampling sites to 59.

    Arthropod Sampling

    On each site, ten pitfall traps were evenly spaced along two 50 m transects, with a distance of 10 m between individual traps and 20 m between transects. Pitfall traps were filled with 100–200 ml of a mixture of ethylene glycol and water (1:1 vol/vol) with a drop of liquid soap to break surface tension. Traps were exposed for 7 days each during two to five sampling events in both the dry and wet seasons between May 2011 and October 2012. As the number of individuals collected in ten traps was very high, we had to confine the sorting and subsequent analysis to sub‐sets of at least three traps per sampling site and sampling event. The number of sampling events and considered samples is given in Table S2. To address imbalances in the data set, we first calculated the mean abundances per trap per sampling event for each sampling site. We then calculated mean abundance per sampling site from the mean abundances per sampling event such that the number of replicates for statistical testing summed up to a total of N = 59 sampling sites. Abundances are given at the order level, except for Hymenoptera, which were further divided into army ants (Dorylus sp.; their raiding behavior leads to extreme abundances in traps if they happen to raid the plot), other ants, and other Hymenoptera. Here, we present data for the sum of all collected arthropods, and for each of 13 taxa for which pitfall traps are a reliable method for estimating the relative abundance activity. This assumes also that each individual trapped is an independent event—an assumption that is violated for ants. Therefore, the quantitative information for ants needs to be interpreted with care. We further assigned all 32 arthropod taxa to one of three functional groups (predators, herbivores, and decomposers) according to the predominant feeding behavior of the species in each taxon. Taxa comprising predominantly omnivore species or many species belonging to different functional groups were grouped as ‘mixed’ (Table 1). Finally, in a similar procedure, beetles were assigned to one of the three functional groups at the family level. Note that the available data on beetle families was restricted to two sampling campaigns in June–July 2011 and June–July 2012, while the order level data cover up to five sampling campaigns (Table S2).

    Pitfall traps are a common method to sample epigeic arthropods. It is well established that pitfall traps do not measure real density; instead they provide a mixture between density and activity. In our study, this is an advantage rather than a bias: both higher actual abundances and higher activities allow for higher rates of ecological interactions. Although there are a range of factors determining the activity and trapability of arthropods at the species level (Topping 1993, Driscoll 2010) and at the individual level (Topping & Sunderland 1992), the method is still valid to compare activity abundances within taxa across different ecosystems (Olson 1994, Sabu & Shiju 2010, Raub et al. 2014).

    Environmental Data

    All study sites were equipped with sensors for temperature and relative humidity that were installed approximately 2 m above the ground. Details on the design and set‐up of sensors, and on the processing of collected data can be found in Appelhans et al. (2015). We used the normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) as a proxy for primary productivity (Kerr & Ostrovsky 2003). Living green plants reflect light in the near‐infrared spectral region (NIR, 700–1300 nm), but hardly any red light (Red, 600–700 nm). The NDVI uses this difference and helps to distinguish between healthy, full vegetation cover (NDVI = +1), bare ground (NDVI = 0), and waterbodies (NDVI = −1). It is calculated as NDVI =  (NIR − Red)/(NIR + Red). The NDVI estimations used in the study are based on Collection 5 of the Aqua‐MODIS product (MYD13Q1) with a spatial resolution of 250 m × 250 m, because of known problems with the Terra‐MODIS product of the same collection (Wang et al. 2012, Lyapustin et al. 2014). Cloud contamination is a prominent feature at Mt. Kilimanjaro and realistic estimations of NDVI are therefore problematic. To address this issue, we first identified all pixels flagged as cloudy in the companion ‘pixel reliability’ layer and deleted these together with the eight surrounding pixels. Afterward, we followed the approach proposed by Atzberger and Eilers (2011) using the ‘Whittaker smoother’ based on three iterations with a lambda of 6000. We then calculated the overall mean NDVI for the 10‐year period 2003–2012 and extracted the pixel values corresponding to the locations of our study sites. A comparison of NDVI data with on‐site measurements of the leaf‐area index conducted during the growing season revealed a high level of correlation (r = 0.84, P < 0.001).

    Standardization

    Our aim was to compare the patterns of abundance of different taxa and of groups of taxa relative to selected environmental variables. This approach made it necessary to compensate for differences between taxa; after all, abundances of the different taxa covered a range of four orders of magnitude. Therefore, we divided abundances of taxa by the respective standard deviations. The resulting values are directly comparable between taxa, as they share the same scale given in SD units. Furthermore, all sites with zero occurrences are still zero. The standardization was especially important for the analysis of patterns of groups of taxa: the patterns of simple sums of individuals of different taxa are, of course, dominated by the most abundant taxon. We wanted to focus on the sums of trends and patterns; therefore, we summed the standardized abundances of the respective taxa and divided that value by the number of taxa considered. Standardization does not distort data structure. To increase normality in the distribution of errors, we used the fourth root of standardized abundances in all analyses. Original abundances along the elevation gradient are shown in Figs. S2–S4.

    Data Analysis

    The patterns of abundance of the taxa, combinations of taxa (total abundance and ‘others’), the functional groups of arthropods and the functional groups of beetles were analyzed with generalized additive models (GAMs) using the Gaussian distribution for the error, and the default penalized thin plate regression splines to estimate smooth terms (package mgcv in R v. 3.0.1; R Development Core Team 2013, Wood 2003, 2011). The respective patterns of abundance along the elevational gradient were analyzed with separate GAMs for undisturbed and disturbed sites, with elevation as the only explanatory variable. We increased the basis dimensions of the smooth functions where necessary, to be sure that all related variation was covered. The respective model predictions were used to determine the elevations of maximum abundance, which for many taxa was equal to the peak of a unimodal pattern of distribution. Additional GAMs were used to test the influence of temperature, productivity, humidity, and disturbance on the abundances of all 13 taxa, functional groups of arthropods, and functional groups of beetles. For each response variable, we built a full GAM including smoothing functions for the three continuous explanatory variables, and disturbance as a binary factor. The basis dimensions k for temperature were increased from the default 10 to 20 after checking GAMs of all groups and taxa, to ensure that all related variation was covered. Then, we calculated all 16 possible models (2no. of variables, function dredge, package MuMIn v 1.9.5; Barton 2013). Afterward, we applied model averaging to all good models, i.e., models with a difference in AICc <3, compared to the best model. The relative importance of temperature, productivity, humidity, and disturbance was calculated as the sums of Akaike weights over all good models considering the respective explaining variable. Consequently, the values of relative importance range between 0 and 1, with 0 meaning “not relevant” and 1 meaning “occurring in all models with high support”.

    Results

    We collected a total of 179,787 arthropods, with a mean of 271 individuals per trap (Table 1). The most abundant taxa were Collembola (58,507 individuals), Acari (39,696 individuals), army ants (32,913 individuals), and other Formicidae (17,383 individuals). The collected arthropods were sorted into 32 taxa in total, i.e., in addition to the focal 13 taxa, 19 taxa were combined in ‘others’. All 3186 beetles from two sampling rounds in June/July 2011 and June/July 2012 were sorted to morphospecies, which resulted in 330 morphospecies from 23 families. Eleven individuals belonging to six morphospecies could not be assigned to a family and were not considered in the analysis. The mean abundance of beetles per trap from these two sampling rounds (10.1 individuals) was lower than the mean abundance of beetles from all sampling rounds together (16.4 individuals). The most abundant beetle families were Staphylinidae (1354 individuals) and Carabidae (745 individuals), both of which peaked in abundance in the forest belt (1500–3000 m asl; family level not shown).

    Many taxa and functional groups were most abundant in the elevational zone with intense human management, most favorable climate, and highest productivity (Figs. 2 and S1). Considering only undisturbed sites, eight of 13 focal taxa and all 32 taxa combined were most abundant in lower montane forests (Fig. 2). Two taxa were most abundant at higher elevations: Coleoptera in Ocotea forest, and Dermaptera in Erica forest. The activity abundance of other Formicidae, Chilopoda, and others declined exponentially. Owing to the fourth‐root transformation of data, a linear decline in the figures corresponds to an exponential decline in activity abundances with elevation. Regarding disturbed vegetation types, eight of 13 taxa were most abundant in the land‐use zone, homegardens, coffee plantations, and grasslands. Activity abundance of Formicidae and Acari plateaued in maize fields and in the land‐use zone and decreased at higher elevations. The activity abundance of Isoptera and “others” declined exponentially. In contrast to the patterns of other taxa, Blattodea became more abundant with increasing elevation in disturbed sites.

    The abundances of predators and arthropods with a mixed diet declined linearly with elevation in both undisturbed and disturbed sites (Fig. 3). The activity abundance of herbivores was comparably high in the savannah and in lower montane forests and about ten times lower at higher elevations in undisturbed sites. In disturbed sites, herbivore abundance declined linearly and fast. The peaks in activity abundance of decomposers in undisturbed and disturbed sites were both in the land‐use zone, but abundances were higher in lower montane forests than in the three disturbed vegetation types in this elevational zone. The functional groups of beetles reacted differently than the functional groups of all arthropods (Fig. 4). Predatory beetles showed unimodal patterns with peaks in Ocotea forest in both undisturbed and disturbed sites. The activity abundance of herbivorous beetles in undisturbed sites even slightly increased with increasing elevation over most of the gradient, with a peak in Erica forest. In disturbed sites, however, herbivorous beetles declined linearly. Decomposer beetles and beetles with a mixed diet were only abundant below the recent treeline, i.e., below 3000 m asl, with only one exception each. Activity abundance of decomposer beetles declined linearly in undisturbed sites, and peaked in maize fields and logged Ocotea forest in disturbed sites. Beetles with a mixed diet declined linearly in both undisturbed and disturbed sites.

    Although our method of standardization of the abundances of groups of taxa is an approach that gives equal weight to each taxon, the resulting patterns differed only slightly, as did the patterns of common standardized sums of abundances (Figs. S2–S4D). Using the unbalanced raw data did not change overall patterns (Figs. S2–S4B).

    Our study design covered an elevational gradient of 3685 m, with mean annual temperatures ranging between 3.7°C and 24.1°C, extreme temperatures ranging between −4.7°C and 45.1°C, and a mean annual precipitation of 690–2390 mm (Table S1). Among temperature, productivity, humidity, and disturbance, the mean annual temperature was the most important explaining variable for nine of 13 taxa (Table 2). The only composite model that did not include temperature was that for Blattodea; NDVI was the most important variable in that case. The abundance of Coleoptera and Chilopoda was best explained by humidity. Disturbance was the most important variable that explained Isopoda abundance. None of the best models included disturbance (Table 2).

    Discussion

    As noted in the introduction, only a few studies have compared the trend of abundances of several invertebrate taxa along elevational gradients. Using sweep netting, McCoy (1990) recorded a decline in the abundance of predators along an elevational gradient from 100 m asl to 1700 m asl, and an increase in the abundance of herbivores. Janzen et al. (1976) used sweep netting in secondary vegetation sites from 200 m asl to 3600 m asl in Venezuelan and Costa Rican mountain ranges. These authors found mid‐elevation peaks in abundance for some predominantly herbivorous arthropod taxa (Hemiptera, Lepidoptera, Orthoptera, Aphidae) and a decline in abundance of other taxa. In our study, conducted along an extensive climatic gradient, the overall abundance of arthropods declined toward higher elevations. Most of the taxa showed unimodal patterns with a peak in activity abundance in the lower third of the gradient, at least in undisturbed vegetation types. However, taxa belonging to the same functional group did not show more similar responses compared to taxa from different functional groups. The reason for these different patterns lies in the different habitats, food, and life cycles of these groups. Nevertheless, financial and logistic constraints as well as the high complexity of organismic communities in many studies have resulted in the choice of indicator species or allegedly representative taxa for inferring ecological patterns and processes for whole groups of organisms (Kotze & Samways 1999). This approach is very common in conservation (Prendergast et al. 1993), but it can be problematic, as patterns found for single taxa fail to represent those of the full assemblage, especially for arthropods (Prendergast et al. 1993, Lawton et al. 1998, Ruhi et al. 2014, Smith‐Patten & Patten 2015).

    We expected the abundance of predators to decline faster than that of herbivores and decomposers. However, we did not find support for the expectation that the total abundance of predators would respond to elevation differently than other functional guilds. Although the three predatory taxa had an activity abundance peak in the land‐use zone, spiders were common on all sites, army ants only occurred below the recent treeline, and centipedes were rare on Erica and Helichrysum sites. More specifically, while army ants were abundant at low elevations, Carabidae and Staphylinidae were abundant at middle and high elevations. Olson (1994) found in his pitfall trap samples a similar shift from ant‐dominated assemblages in primary forests at lower elevations to carabid‐dominated assemblages in cloud forests at higher elevations in western Panama. The decline in the total numbers of predators with elevation is not due to their function, but to different reactions of taxa. Highly thermophilic predators, such as ants, drop out very quickly with increasing elevation, while the abundances of other taxa either remain almost constant with elevation, e.g., hunting spiders, or exhibit hump‐shaped distribution patterns, e.g., carabid beetles (see also Kotze & Samways 1999). The most important environmental variables shaping these patterns were therefore temperature for army ants, spiders, and all predatory taxa combined, and humidity was most important for predatory beetles and centipedes.

    Primary productivity was not the most important driver of arthropod abundance or even of overall herbivore abundance in our study – only for herbivorous beetles and cockroaches was productivity the most important variable. The distribution of biomass on Mt. Kilimanjaro is distinctly hump‐shaped, with a maximum between 2000 m asl and 2500 m asl in lower montane and Ocotea forest, which is highly correlated with precipitation (Fig. S1). The pattern of the NDVI is also hump‐shaped, with a peak at 2000 m asl, i.e., the transition from areas under intense human management to montane forest (Fig. S1). It is thought that higher primary productivity sustains more heterotrophic individuals, which in turn should lead to a lower extinction risk for populations (Wright 1983). The more individuals hypothesis works well for small, secluded communities which are clearly limited by productivity (Srivastava & Lawton 1998, Yee & Juliano 2007). However, Currie et al. (2004) did not find support for the causal chain ‘more energy/productivity—more individuals—more species’ in a larger context, whereas ‘more energy/productivity—more species’ seems to hold. Probably, one of the reasons we did not find a close correlation between productivity and abundance in our study is our focus on arthropods on the ground. Especially in forests, a large portion of the total number of arthropods can be found in the canopy (Basset et al. 2012). Nevertheless, there can be considerable indirect effects of the canopy on forest floor communities. For example, high productivity in the canopy fuels the brown food web on the forest floor, which results in higher prey availability for predators (Hyodo et al. 2010). Furthermore, some of the prey in the canopy falls to the ground and is available for predators there (Pringle & Fox‐Dobbs 2008).

    Plants at higher elevations, and especially above the treeline as in our study, adapt to the harsh climate by increasing toughness of leaves and supporting tissues or by storing secondary metabolites to prevent freezing (e.g., Cornelissen et al. 1999). More recalcitrant plant litter and lower temperatures result in lower rates of decomposition, owing to the increasing complexity of chemical compounds and lower rates of chemical reactions at lower temperatures (Cornelissen et al. 1999, Burnside et al. 2014). Some of the Erica‐ and Helichrysum‐dominated sites accumulated impressive amounts of organic material (Pabst et al. 2013). Microbial decomposers are limited by temperature on these sites (Wall et al. 2008), and consequently, decomposer arthropods cannot exploit all available resources, which might explain the decrease in numbers of decomposers. Concerning the abundance of decomposers, Olson (1994) found an increase in the total number of decomposers with elevation, i.e., of the total sum of Isopoda, Amphipoda, and Diplopoda, probably because all of his sampling sites were situated in primary forests. However, when we look at abundances of decomposer taxa in undisturbed forest sites, i.e., on a subset of our sites similar to the sites Olson sampled, we found a decrease in Isopoda abundance, and a U‐shaped pattern for Diplopoda. Although the base of the mountain is surrounded by savannah, which is dry for most of the year, mites, ants, and termites, as well as most functional groups of arthropods were most abundant there. We expected humidity to be limiting in this environment, which would lead to lower abundances. After all, arthropods are sensitive to desiccation (Addo‐Bediako et al. 2001, Chown et al. 2011). However, the response to humidity was probably concealed by the large temperature gradient with more than 20°C difference in mean annual temperature between sampling sites. Almost all composite models for taxa, functional groups of arthropods, and functional groups of beetles included disturbance, but its relative importance was low.

    Elevation gradients around the world can be used as replicates to study the influence of climate on ecological processes (Becker et al. 2007). If one wants to understand the role of invertebrates for such processes, it can be misleading to concentrate on a single species or a single taxon as a representative of a functional group. We found different trends in the response of arthropod abundance depending on taxonomic and functional resolutions. Consequently, when one wants to study the relationship between ecosystem functions, climate, and abundance of taxa involved in the functions, more comprehensive studies are needed that consider the full spectrum of occurring taxa as well as key traits that trigger ecosystem functions.

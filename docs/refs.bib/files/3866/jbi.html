Abstract

Aim

We tested the elevational herbivory defence hypothesis, which predicts that plants from low elevations are better defended than plants from higher elevations.

Location

The Qinghai‐Tibetan Plateau in south‐western China—the world's highest plateau.

Taxon

Angiosperms.

Methods

We collated binary spinescence data for 10,622 angiosperm species ranging from 600 to 6,000 m a.s.l. Logistic regression was used to quantify the elevational pattern in spinescence. Because spinescence is thought to be especially effective against mammalian herbivores, we also quantified the association between spinescence and mammalian herbivore richness.

Results

We found a unimodal relationship between elevation and spinescence, with the highest proportion of spinescent species occurring at mid‐elevations. This unimodal relationship was present in perennial herbs, shrubs and trees, but not in annual herbs. Herbivorous mammal richness also showed a unimodal elevational pattern. A positive association between herbivorous mammal richness and the incidence of spinescent species suggests that elevational variation in herbivore pressure from mammals might drive elevational variation in spinescence.

Main Conclusions

Our findings further call into question the elevational herbivory defence hypothesis and shed new light on the potential causes of elevational gradients in plant diversity.

1 INTRODUCTION

Geographical variation in species interactions is central to our understanding of large‐scale patterns in species abundance, diversity and functional traits (Abdala‐Roberts, Moreira, Rasmann, Parra‐Tabla, & Mooney, 2016; Moeller et al., 2017). Traditional theories, such as the latitudinal herbivory defence hypothesis and the elevational herbivory defence hypothesis, are based on the prediction that biological interactions such as herbivory are stronger at lower latitudes and at lower elevations (Dobzhansky, 1950; Pellissier et al., 2012). In response to higher herbivore pressure, greater investment in plant defences is predicted to occur in the tropics and in the lowlands (Raffa, Powell, & Townsend, 2013; Schemske, Mittelbach, Cornell, Sobel, & Roy, 2009). However, the existence and generality of broad gradients in plant–herbivore interactions are hotly disputed (e.g. Anstett, Nunes, Baskett, & Kotanen, 2016; Moles & Ollerton, 2016). A number of studies have found higher levels of defence at lower latitudes and lower elevations (e.g. Callis‐Duehl, Vittoz, Defossez, & Rasmann, 2017; Dostalek et al., 2016; Pellissier, Roger, Bilat, & Rasmann, 2014). However, many other studies report null results (e.g. Tindall, Thomson, Laffan, & Moles, 2017; Zehnder et al., 2009), or higher levels of defence at higher latitudes or elevations (e.g. Kooyers, Blackman, & Holeski, 2017; Moles, Wallis, et al., 2011; Moreira, Petry, Mooney, Rasmann, & Abdala‐Roberts, 2018).

Some authors have suggested that the available evidence for geographical patterns in plant defence is mixed and unconvincing because of a lack of consideration of herbivore species or guilds (Abdala‐Roberts et al., 2016; Rasmann, Pellissier, Defossez, Jactel, & Kunstler, 2014). Others suggest that the evidence is clouded by studies that span insufficient geographical ranges, contrast sites in two or three regions rather than thoroughly sampling gradients, and/or include only relatively small numbers of species (Kooyers et al., 2017; Schemske et al., 2009). Some authors have suggested that the use of inconsistent methods may have obscured latitudinal and elevational gradients in defence (Moles, Bonser, Bonser, Poore, Wallis, & Foley, 2011). Finally, most studies have not considered the confounding effects resulting from sampling across different life‐forms and across defence traits that are effective against different types of herbivores (Abdala‐Roberts et al., 2016; Moreira et al., 2018). For example, trichomes and phytoliths are less effective in deterring large mammalian herbivores than insect herbivores (Barton, 2016). In this paper, we aimed to address these concerns using a large‐scale study that applies consistent methods across a range of plant species sampling continuously along a wide elevational range to quantify the elevational pattern of a single physical defence trait.

Spinescence is a collective term given to sharp projections from the plant surface, including spines, thorns and prickles (Hanley, Lamont, Fairbanks, & Rafferty, 2007). Abiotic factors such as strong solar radiation, low or high temperature, soil nutrient availability and fire can favour spinescence (Nobel, 1988). Spines can also assist with climbing (Grubb, 1992). Although spines can play a range of roles, one of their most important functions is likely to be as a defence against herbivores (Grubb, 1992; Young, Stanton, & Christian, 2003). Because many small invertebrates can avoid spines, spinescence is thought to be largely targeted towards vertebrates, especially mammals (Burns, 2016; Grubb, 1992; Lev‐Yadun, Ne’eman, & Keasar, 2018). Spines can damage soft lips and other body parts of herbivores (Burns, 2016). Some spines also infect herbivores with pathogenic microorganisms (Halpern, Raats, & Lev‐Yadun, 2007). Thus, spinescence has been the main focus of several studies investigating how plants deter mammalian herbivores (e.g. Ford et al., 2014; Rebollo, Milchunas, Noy‐Meir, & Chapman, 2002; Young et al., 2003). For example, spine length or density increases with herbivory (Young et al., 2003).

Despite the importance of geographical gradients in plant defence in understanding plant–herbivore interactions, and the importance of spines as a defence against mammalian herbivores, there have been only two empirical studies and one perspective on the geographical gradient in spinescence (Grubb, 1992; Moles, Wallis, et al., 2011; Tindall et al., 2017). Furthermore, both empirical studies cited above focused on latitudinal gradients. Thus, there is little understanding of elevational patterns of spinescence. Addressing this knowledge gap is the first goal of our study.

Our second goal is to determine whether any elevational gradient in spinescence is consistent across life‐forms. Plants with different life‐forms (e.g. annual herbs, perennial herbs, shrubs and trees) may suffer different levels of herbivory. For instance, perennial species are longer lived, and this presumably results in a longer exposure to herbivores relative to ephemeral annual species (Cates & Orians, 1975; Herms & Mattson, 1992). Furthermore, plants with different life‐forms do not always respond in the same way to similar herbivore pressure, due to differential resource allocation strategies (Kooyers et al., 2017; Mole, 1994; Ronel & Lev‐Yadun, 2012). For example, annual plants may allocate fewer resources to defence and more resources towards rapid development and reproduction than perennial plants, even if herbivore pressure is equal (Kooyers et al., 2017). In accordance with these expectations, recent work has shown that perennial plants have higher levels of defences (e.g. spines) against mammalian herbivores compared to annual plants (Ronel & Lev‐Yadun, 2012; Zhang & Yao, 2017). There is also a possibility that plants of different growth forms may experience different abiotic conditions (e.g. through smaller plants being buried under snow through winter, or through annual herbs surviving winter in the soil seed bank) (White, Pendleton, & Hood, 2011). For all of these reasons, we predicted that perennial plants would show stronger elevational gradients in spinescence relative to annual plants.

Plant defences are thought to reflect historical herbivore pressure, which is predicted to be higher at low elevations, due to the more benign and stable climate (Pellissier et al., 2012; Raffa et al., 2013). However, it remains unclear whether herbivore pressure is actually more intense at lower elevations. Recent syntheses of elevational studies showed patterns that are variable and that often do not support the assumption (Galmán et al., 2018; Moreira et al., 2018). Thus, quantifying the elevational gradient in herbivore pressure is particularly important for understanding any elevational patterns in plant defence (Moles, Bonser, et al., 2011). However, investigations on the elevational gradients in defence and herbivore pressure simultaneously are strikingly scarce and the few studies available have not always confirmed significant associations between defence and herbivore pressure along the elevational gradient (e.g. Pellissier et al., 2016). Unfortunately, it was impossible to gather data on herbivore pressure at the scale of our analysis. However, although plant damage is influenced by many factors, including herbivore‐ and plant‐related characteristics (Abdala‐Roberts et al., 2016; Grubb, 1992), it is widely expected that higher herbivore richness would lead to higher herbivory due to the species complementarity effect or species identity effect (Salazar & Marquis, 2012). In accordance with this expectation, recent experimental or synthetic studies have shown that, concomitant with the increases in herbivore richness, the herbivore pressure also increases (e.g. Cardinale et al., 2006; De Carvalho Guimarães, Viana, & Cornelissen, 2014; Salazar & Marquis, 2012), indicating that in the absence of herbivory data, diversity provide a worthwhile proxy for a first look. Therefore, our final goals are to determine whether there is an elevational gradient in mammalian herbivore richness and quantify the association between spinescence and mammalian herbivore richness.

In this study, to achieve the above goals of testing the elevational variation in spinescence and testing the association between spinescence and herbivorous mammal richness, we collected spinescence data (presence/absence) for 10,622 angiosperm species and elevational range data for 201 herbivorous mammalian species from the Qinghai‐Tibetan Plateau (QTP) in south‐western China, ranging from 600 to 6,000 m a.s.l., an exceptional elevational range that can be found in only a few regions in the world. Specifically, we asked the following: (a) is there an overall elevational gradient in spinescence? (b) is any such elevational gradient consistent across life‐forms? (c) are elevational gradients in spinescence associated with herbivorous mammal richness?

2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1 Study area

Our study covered the geographical range of the QTP within China, including north‐western Yunnan, the whole of Xizang and Qinghai Provinces, western Sichuan, south‐western Gansu and southern Xinjiang. In addition, we extended the range to include the southern Hengduan Mountains. The study area (2,572,400 Km2) stretches between 24°40′ to 39°47′ N, and 73°19′ to 104°47′ E (Figure 1), with diverse vegetation types, including dry valley scrub, broad‐leaved forests, coniferous forests, alpine shrublands, meadows and scree (Zhang, Li, & Zheng, 2002).

2.2 Data compilation

We compiled a list of all of the angiosperm species present in the study area, based on Wu (2008), Flora of Tibet, Flora of Qinghai and Flora of Yunnan, and Wang (1993, 1994). Varieties and sub‐species were treated as independent species. Cultivated and alien species were not included in the dataset. In total, our list included 10,622 angiosperm species.

We collected data on the elevational range for each species from the data sources above, complemented by data from the Chinese Virtual Herbarium, published papers, investigation reports of nature reserves and personal communications with experts. We divided the elevational gradient between 600 and 6,000 m into 55, 100‐m elevation intervals. A species was considered as present at each 100‐m belt between its upper and lower elevation limits (Bhattarai, Vetaas, & Grytnes, 2004). This is a commonly used method (e.g. Bhatta, Grytnes, & Vetaas, 2018; Bhattarai et al., 2004; Grytnes & Vetaas, 2002; Zhang, Boufford, & Sun, 2009), but it may result in an underestimation of species richness at the extreme elevations or an overestimation at mid‐elevations (Grytnes & Vetaas, 2002). Crucially, there is no reason to expect that any under‐ or over‐estimation of species’ richness is likely to be biased in a way that would affect our estimation of the elevational gradient in the proportion of species with spinescence. The number of species per elevation belt ranged from 2 to 3,629, with a median of 1,689 species.

Spinescence data were compiled from the Flora of China (Wu, Raven, & Hong, 1994). Species were defined as spinescent if they had any sharp pointed tip on any aboveground part of the plant. In the species description, species with the term/s ‘pungent tip’, ‘pungent point’, ‘spine’, ‘spinescent’, ‘spinose’, ‘spiniform’, ‘spinulose’, ‘thorn’, ‘thorny’, ‘prickle’, ‘prickly’, ‘armor’ or ‘armour’ were sorted as ‘spinescent’, whereas species lacking any of these features (including species described as not possessing the above features [e.g. ‘unarmed’]) were scored as ‘non‐spinescent’ following Tindall et al. (2017). To avoid misclassifying, we manually checked the records. For example Rhamnus utilis var. szechuanensis was corrected as ‘non‐spinescent’ because of the species description of ‘branches terminating in a bud, but without spine’. Following the rule of interpolation (Bhatta et al., 2018), if a species was classified as spinescent, it is considered as spinescent at all elevations between its upper and lower limits. Across all angiosperms in the study area, 727 species (6.8%) are spinescent.

We classified each plant species into one of four life‐form categories: annual herbs, perennial herbs, shrubs and trees (Moeller et al., 2017). Of the 10,622 species in our study, 860 were annual herbs (7.95%), 6,709 were perennial herbs (62.02%), 2,313 were shrubs (21.38%) and 740 were trees (6.84%). The proportion of life‐forms at species level changed significantly with elevation (see Appendix S1). The proportion of perennial herbs generally increased with elevation, whereas the proportion of annual herbs, shrubs and trees generally decreased as elevation increased.

In order to test the association between spinescence and the potential herbivore pressure from herbivorous mammals, we established an elevational range dataset for herbivorous mammals also ranging from 600 to 6,000 m a.s.l. in this area. We compiled elevational range data for herbivorous mammals found in the study area from the published literature (e.g. Hu et al., 2014; Jiang et al., 2018), the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species (IUCN, 2017), and from personal communications with experts. A total of 201 mammal species with known elevational distribution were used in our analyses, accounting for more than 85% of all wild herbivorous mammals in the study area (Wang, 2003). Elevational ranges for each herbivorous mammalian species were also interpolated using the same method as above and divided into 100 m elevational belts. The number of species per elevational belt ranged from 6 to 121, with a median of 51 species per 100 m elevational belt.

2.3 Statistical analyses

The relationship between spinescence and elevation was analysed using logistic regressions following the methods of Chen, Cornwell, Zhang, and Moles (2017) and Pincheira‐Donoso et al. (2017). To conduct this analysis, the spinescent trait was modelled as a binary response variable (presence vs. absence of spines) with logit link function. First, to test for an overall elevational gradient in spinescence, we performed polynomial logistic regression analyses using the full dataset where elevation predicted spinescence. Second, to test whether elevational gradient of spinescence depended on life‐form, multiple logistic regression was performed where spinescence was predicted by elevation, life‐form (annual herb, perennial herb, shrub and tree) and their interaction. If the interaction was significant, we ran univariate logistic regressions with elevation as a predictor separately to quantify the elevational pattern of spinescence in each life‐form. Model selection was conducted based on the Akaike information criterion (AIC value), with the lowest AIC values indicating that the model allowed for the best fit (Zhang, Zhang, & Ma, 2017). For all logistic regression analyses, both the number of species with spines and the total number of species in each elevational belt were included in the model. Thus, our analyses consider the proportion of spinescent species in elevational belts that are appropriately weighted according to the number of species they contain (Chen et al., 2017), which means that a belt with 400 species gets more weight than does a belt with fewer than 40 species. We also conducted random resampling tests with same number of species being sampled in each elevational belt in each resampling and this resampling process was iterated 1,000 times (Zhang et al., 2017). Before resampling, belts at extremes of the elevational gradient with exceptionally few species (c. <1% of the species number of elevational belt with highest richness) were removed. Resampling tests showed similar results compared with analyses with complete data (Appendix S2). That is the elevational gradient in spinescence is not an artefact resulting from the variation in plant species richness along the elevational gradient.

Relationships between mammalian herbivore richness and elevation were analysed using a generalized linear regression with Poisson distribution. A quadratic term was incorporated in order to test whether a possible elevational relationship was curvilinear. Given that interpolated range data may result in model overfitting, we also conducted analysis with data that did not include belts at extremes of the elevational gradient with exceptionally few species (i.e. few than 10 species), and found similar result compared with analysis with complete data (Appendix S3). Logistic regression analyses were performed to explore the relationships between spinescence and mammalian herbivore richness.

We also examined the relationships between spinescence and elevation in the context of phylogeny using phylogenetic logistic regression (Ives & Garland, 2010). We constructed a phylogenetic tree for our 10,622 study species following Qian and Jin (2016). All 185 families and 77% of the 1,504 genera in our study were included in Qian and Jin's mega‐phylogeny. We used the S.PhyloMaker software (Qian & Jin, 2016) to add each remaining species to the phylogeny following the methods of Webb, Ackerly, and Kembel (2008). For the phylogenetic analysis, the elevation for each occurrence was extracted and the species’ mean value was used. In addition, to test whether there is a significant relationship between spinescence and elevation within each life‐form (e.g. annual herb), we also conducted phylogenetically logistic regression for each life‐form.

All statistical analyses were conducted in the R computation platform (version 3.3.3) (R Development Core Team, 2017).

3 RESULTS

Using the full dataset (all angiosperms included), the polynomial logistic regression (AIC = 309.4, compared to AIC = 1,013.6 of the simple logistic regression) including elevation as both linear and quadratic terms showed that the elevational pattern in spinescence was unimodal, increasing from 1.0% at 600 m a.s.l. to 8.5% at 2,400 m a.s.l. and then decreasing to 0% at elevations above 5,500 m a.s.l. (Figure 2a). Thus, the proportion of plants with spinescence was not the highest at low elevations (Figure 2a).

Multiple logistic regression analysis detected a significant elevation × life form interaction, indicating that elevational gradients in spinescence were contingent on life‐form (Table 1). We therefore analysed data for each life‐form separately. These analyses revealed no significant relationship between elevation and spinescence in annual herbs (Figure 2b); but perennial herbs, shrubs and trees all showed mid‐elevational peaks in the proportion of species with spinescence (Figure 2c‐e).

Across all angiosperm species, the presence/absence of spinescence was more phylogenetically conserved than random (parameter α = .01; see Appendix S4a; Ives & Garland, 2010). The relationship between elevation and spinescence remained significant in phylogenetic logistic regression analysis (p < .001; see see Appendix S4a,). Relationships between the incidence of spinescence and elevation also remained consistent within life‐forms when the evolutionary relationships among species were taken into account (see Appendix S4b‐e).

Polynomial regression (AIC = 335, compared to AIC = 1，604 of the simple regression) including elevation as both linear and quadratic terms also showed that the elevational gradient in species richness for herbivorous mammals was unimodal, with the highest richness occurring at mid‐elevations (between 2,000 and 3,900 m a.s.l.) (Figure 3). Thus, mammalian herbivores did not show higher species richness at lower elevations (Figure 3). Across all angiosperms, there was a significant and positive association between the incidence of spinescent species in each elevational belt and the species richness of mammalian herbivores (Figure 4a). There was also a positive relationship between herbivorous mammal richness and spinescence in perennial herbs, shrubs and trees (Figure 4c‐e), but not in annual herbs (Figure 4b).

4 DISCUSSION

The elevational herbivory defence hypothesis has interested ecologists for decades because of its importance in understanding plant–herbivore interactions (Koptur, 1985; Pellissier et al., 2016, 2014). Our results do not support the hypothesis that plants will be better‐defended against herbivores at lower elevations. The congruence of evidence from our study, the largest empirical study ever conducted for a single physical defence trait, and a comprehensive synthesis of data from the literature (Moreira et al., 2018) strongly suggests that the widely accepted elevational herbivory defence hypothesis needs to be reconsidered.

The main reason people predicted that plants from low elevations would be better defended than are plants from higher elevations was that low‐elevation species were thought to experience stronger herbivore pressure than species at higher elevations (Rasmann et al., 2014). Unfortunately, we do not have data on herbivory. However, herbivore richness, which has been found to be correlated with herbivore pressure (Cardinale et al., 2006; De Carvalho Guimarães et al., 2014; Salazar & Marquis, 2012), was not the highest at low elevations (Figure 3). Our finding is in accordance with several other recent studies of herbivore pressure along elevational gradients (Moreira et al., 2018; Novotny et al., 2005; Rahbek, 1995; Rasmann et al., 2014). We therefore suggest that analyses of plant defence that do not support the elevational herbivory defence hypothesis (or latitudinal herbivory defence hypothesis) may partially be explained by the lack of stronger herbivore pressure at lower elevations (or low latitudes). We further suggest that the hypothesis that plants from low elevations experience more intense herbivory than do high‐elevation plants should be reconsidered.

Our study is to our knowledge one of the first studies to report a mid‐elevation peak in plant defence. Most previous studies have reported defences decreasing or increasing linearly with increasing elevation (Dostalek et al., 2016; Erelli, Ayres, & Eaton, 1998; Pellissier et al., 2016), or no relationship between elevation and plant defence (Rasmann et al., 2014). No studies have reported mid‐latitude peaks in plant defences either. It remains unclear whether unimodal defence patterns along elevational/latitudinal gradient are rare, or just rarely documented. It is possible that our study can capture this pattern because it spans an unusually large elevational gradient (600–6,000 m a.s.l.), and that studies spanning small elevational ranges cannot capture this pattern (but see Hengxiao et al., 1999). Our data would have supported the prediction that the proportion of spinescence decreases with increasing elevation if we had only sampled above 2,400 m a.s.l. However, we would have found the opposite trend if we had only sampled below 2,400 m a.s.l. This highlights the importance of quantifying geographical gradients in plant defence (and any other adaptation) over broad gradients, and supports the idea that the mixed findings of previous studies might be attributable to their limited sampling scale (Anstett et al., 2016; Moles, Wallis, et al., 2011). Another possible reason for the rarity of such unimodal defence patterns may be the fact that previous studies mainly focused on the linear relationships, and did not quantify curvilinear relationships, even if they were present (e.g. Tindall et al., 2017). Through a re‐analysing of the spinescence data used in the previous study (Tindall et al., 2017), we found a mid‐latitudinal peak in the proportion of spinescent species in Australia (see Appendix S5). Therefore, the possibility of unimodal defence patterns along latitudinal/elevational gradients merits attention in future studies.

One potential explanation for the unimodal relationship between spinescence and elevation observed in our study is that elevational variation in spinescence may reflect adaptation to elevational variation in herbivore pressure from mammals. Consistent with several previous elevational studies (e.g. Hu et al., 2014, 2017; McCain, 2004), we found that the species richness of mammalian herbivores in QTP peaked at mid‐elevations. Furthermore, we found a positive association between herbivorous mammal richness and proportion of spinescent species. Therefore, plants from mid‐elevations may experience greater herbivore pressure from mammals than do plants from either low elevations or on mountain summits. As mammalian herbivores prefer less spiny plants (Charles‐Dominique et al., 2016; Gowda, 1996), this might lead to selection for a higher level of mechanical defence at mid‐elevations (Ford et al., 2014). However, it is also possible that abiotic variables such as temperature, precipitation, ultraviolet radiation, soil depth and soil fertility that are associated with elevation may influence plant defences (Hanley et al., 2007; Pellissier et al., 2014). We hope that the intriguing patterns revealed here might stimulate future studies to collect detailed abiotic data (including data on soil, light and climate) and biotic data (including measurements of herbivory by both vertebrate and invertebrate herbivores) to shed light on the relative importance of different factors in shaping large‐scale patterns in plant–herbivore interactions.

Our findings have implications for understanding the elevational gradient in plant diversity in QTP. Previous studies conducted in this area had already found that the highest plant richness occurred at mid‐elevations (Vetaas & Grytnes, 2002; Zhang e al., 2009; but see Bhatta et al., 2018). Spiny plants not only defend themselves against herbivores, but can also be exploited as nurse plants by other various plants without spines (Charles‐Dominique, Barczi, Roux, & Chamaille‐Jammes, 2017; Rebollo et al., 2002). Therefore, the role of spiny plants in defending against large herbivores may also play an important role in structuring plant communities via a positive interaction or facilitation. In addition, the process of co‐evolution between plants and herbivorous mammals can be a Red Queen‐style arms race, in which herbivorous mammals can be expected to impose selection for plant defence, whereas plants should select for enhanced anti‐defence traits of herbivorous mammals (Becerra, 2007). Such Red Queen processes might ultimately lead to higher diversification rates of both plants and herbivorous mammals at mid‐elevations.

The proportion of spinescent species peaked at mid‐elevations for perennial plants (i.e. perennial herbs, shrubs and trees; Figure 2c‐e), whereas there was no elevational effect on spinescence for herbaceous annual plants (Figure 2b). That is elevational patterns in spinescence are contingent on life‐form. The difference between life‐forms may be related to different resource allocation strategies. Annual plants complete their life cycle in a short time span, and thus invest more resources in rapid vegetative growth and seed production and fewer resources in defences. In contrast, perennial plants are commonly slower growing species with high tissue value, and thus increased allocation to anti‐herbivory is favoured (Cates & Orians, 1975; Herms & Mattson, 1992). In addition, annual plants can temporally escape herbivores as a result of their short life spans. Thus, investment in defence seems to be less important in annual plants relative to perennial plants, presumably leading to a less pronounced elevational gradient in annuals (Galmán et al., 2018). Another possibility is that spinescence in annual plants might be partly selected by other selective agents than herbivory, such as ultraviolet radiation or desiccation (Burns, 2016; Mauseth, 2006). This interpretation is supported by our findings of the lack of a positive correlation between spinescence of annual plants and herbivorous mammal richness (Figure 4b). Thus, the fact that abiotic selective pressures may have different responses to elevation than herbivory might also contribute to the lack of an elevational gradient in spinescence in annual plants.

5 CONCLUSIONS

Our findings do not support the hypothesis that plants from low elevations have higher resistance to herbivores than do high‐elevation plants. Rather, we found that the elevational pattern of spinescence is unimodal, with plants from mid‐elevations having the highest proportion of spinescence. Considering that mid‐elevational peaks in herbivore species diversity and richness and herbivory have been reported in a number of studies from different continents (McCain, 2005; Moreira et al., 2018), this elevational pattern in plant defence might be widespread.

    Abstract

    Organisms associated with another species may experience both costs and benefits from their partner. One of these costs is competition, which is the more likely if the two species are ecologically similar. Parabioses are associations between two ant species that share a nest and often attend the same food sources. Albeit parabioses are probably mutualistic, parabiotic partners may compete for food. We therefore investigated feeding niches and dietary overlap of two parabiotically associated ants in Borneo using cafeteria experiments and stable isotope analyses. The two species strongly differed in their food choices. While Crematogaster modiglianii mostly foraged at carbohydrate‐rich baits, Camponotus rufifemur preferred urea‐rich sources. Both species also consumed animal protein. The 15N concentration in Ca. rufifemur workers was consistently lower than in Cr. modiglianii. Camponotus rufifemur but not Cr. modiglianii possesses microbial endosymbionts, which can metabolize urea and synthesize essential amino acids. Its lower 15N signature may result from a relatively higher intake of plant‐based or otherwise 15N‐depleted nitrogen. Isotopic signatures of the two partners in the same parabiosis showed strongly parallel variation across nests. As we did not find evidence for spatial autocorrelation, this correlation suggests an overlap of food sources between the two ant species. Based on model simulations, we estimated a diet overlap of 22–66% for nitrogen sources and 45–74% for carbon sources. The overlap may arise from either joint exploitation of the same food sources or trophallactic exchange of food. This suggests an intense trophic interaction and potential for competition between the parabiotic partners.

    INTRODUCTION

    An association between two organisms can convey benefits or costs to each partner, or even both at the same time. For example, two species may benefit from each other, but nevertheless, as a cost of the association, compete for resources. Competition is the more likely if the partners are closely related and/or occupy similar ecological niches. However, an association may often be inferred to be mutualistic even if its costs are not studied (Bronstein 2001). Costs of mutualisms are generally difficult to quantify and interpret. For example, besides finding an appropriate way to measure these costs, using averages is problematic when the sources of variation are unknown (Bronstein 2001). As a first step, however, the potential for costs, such as competition, can be evaluated.

    An example of an association between closely related organisms are parabiotic associations between two ant species. The ants share a common nest and often use the same foraging trails (Forel 1898). Only few parabioses are known across the world, and the best‐studied ones are associations between Camponotus and Crematogaster species in Neotropical and Southeast Asian rainforests. In both regions, they seem to be mutualistic: Camponotus seems to benefit from the resource‐discovering abilities of Crematogaster, for example, by following Crematogaster pheromone trails (Vantaux et al. 2007; Menzel et al. 2010). Although Crematogaster and Camponotus both engage in nest defence, the smaller Crematogaster strongly profits from nest protection by the much stronger and more aggressive Camponotus (Vantaux et al. 2007; Menzel & Blüthgen 2010). In the Neotropics, an important additional benefit for Crematogaster is that it can inhabit arboreal carton nests constructed by Camponotus (ant‐gardens; Davidson 1988). However, this benefit seems of lesser importance in the Southeast Asian parabioses, which mainly inhabit tree trunk cavities (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010). As both partners belong to the same family (which is extremely unusual for mutualists, Ollerton 2006), it is likely that their diet overlaps at least in part. Hence, they may compete for food unless they employ other ways of niche differentiation. However, in the Southeast Asian association between Crematogaster modiglianii and Camponotus rufifemur, the two species share food resources without aggression or other evidence of interference competition. Indeed, Cr. modiglianii has a wider spatial and temporal foraging range than Ca. rufifemur, which could potentially reduce competition (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010).

    However, the question remains to what degree the natural diets differ between the parabiotic partners, and to what extent they use the same food sources, which would represent a potential for food competition. Ants feed on a variety of food items, including honeydew from plant‐sucking insects, extrafloral nectar, arthropod prey and carrion (Blüthgen & Feldhaar 2010). Nitrogen is obtained from animals (carrion or prey), but also from honeydew or extrafloral nectar, which contain low concentrations of amino acids but – because of their high total abundance on forest plants – may even exceed prey as a nitrogen source for many ant colonies (Blüthgen et al. 2003; Davidson et al. 2003). Certain species, such as species within Camponotus, can also obtain nitrogen from mammal urine or faeces (Blüthgen & Feldhaar 2010). Further food and nitrogen sources include elaiosomes (Gammans et al. 2005; Fischer et al. 2008; Menzel & Blüthgen 2010), which are also harvested by parabiotic ants in our study (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010), and food bodies (Fischer et al. 2002).

    Diet studies in ants are hampered by the fact that most food is transported in the crop and thus difficult to quantify and identify. Recently, however, stable isotope analysis has become a useful tool to estimate the trophic position of ants (Blüthgen et al. 2003; Davidson et al. 2003; Fiedler et al. 2007; Ottonetti et al. 2008). Interpretation of isotope ratios is based on trophic enrichment (fractionation), that is, 15N:14N and 13C:12C ratios of an organism's body tissue are usually higher than those of its food (Deniro & Epstein 1981). In contrast, animal excretions are often depleted in 15N compared with the animal's tissue or even its diet, because 14N is preferentially excreted compared with 15N (Sponheimer et al. 2003). While 13C shows highly variable and often small degrees of trophic fractionation across arthropod species, 15N is usually enriched by 1.9–3.4‰ compared with the animal's diet in arthropods, including ants (Minagawa & Wada 1984; McCutchan et al. 2003; Spence & Rosenheim 2005; Feldhaar et al. 2010).

    In the present study, we investigated the food sources of the parabiotically associated Ca. rufifemur and Cr. modiglianii. Cafeteria experiments were performed to determine food preferences. Moreover, we analysed and modelled stable isotope signatures of parabiotic and non‐parabiotic colonies in order to estimate their trophic levels and the significance of dietary overlap between parabiotic partners.

    METHODS

    Study sites

    The experiments were conducted in August and September, 2008, at the Danum Valley Conservation Area (Sabah/Malaysian Borneo). The area (4°55′N 117°40′E, about 100 m a.s.l.) is one of the major remaining patches of lowland dipterocarp rainforest in Sabah and has a mean annual temperature of 26.9°C and a rainfall of 2700 mm per year.

    For isotope analyses, we collected workers outside their nests (i.e. most probably foragers) in Danum (between 2006 and 2008) and in Kabili Sepilok Reserve and Gunung Mulu National Park (both in 2008). Kabili Sepilok Reserve (5°54′N, 118°04′E, 20–120 m a.s.l., Sabah) comprises coastal dipterocarp and mangrove forest, with climatic conditions similar to Danum Valley. Gunung Mulu National Park is located at 4°57′N, 114°47′E (NE Sarawak). It comprises several forest types, including dipterocarp forest, limestone forest, kerangas and alluvial forest, and has a mean annual temperature of 26°C and a yearly rainfall of 4000–5000 mm (Sarawak Weather Service, pers. comm., 2007). Study sites at Mulu were located at 48–55 m a.s.l.

    Species studied

    We studied parabiotic associations of Camponotus (Myrmotarsus) rufifemurEmery 1900 (Formicidae: Formicinae) and Crematogaster (Paracrema) modiglianiiEmery 1900 (Formicidae: Myrmicinae). These two species live together in shared nests, which are usually located in hollow, living tree trunks. Camponotus rufifemur is mainly nocturnal and has never been found without Cr. modiglianii, whereas Cr. modiglianii is active throughout day and night and commonly nests on its own. Previous studies suggest that the two species are mutualists (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010). Chemical analyses had revealed that there are two chemically distinct, sympatric varieties of Ca. rufifemur (‘red’ and ‘black’ variety, Menzel et al. 2008). Feeding experiments were performed with both varieties. For the isotope analysis, we also included samples of a third variety (Camponotus sp. 5 of Seiki Yamane's reference collection), which is very closely related to Ca. rufifemur and also lives in parabiosis with Cr. modiglianii (Menzel 2009). Voucher specimens of all species are deposited at the Forest Research Centre in Sepilok, Sabah (Malaysia) and at the Department of Zoology III, Biocenter, University of Würzburg (Germany).

    Feeding experiments

    Different ants rely on various categories of resources. Important nitrogen sources include urea, uric acid, amino acids and protein, while carbon is usually obtained via carbohydrates. Plant‐based food sources are usually N‐poor, and may thus be limiting unless the ants are predatory or scavenging to a high degree (Blüthgen & Feldhaar 2010). In a cafeteria experiment, we compared the attractiveness of two possible nitrogen sources (urea and uric acid) and a carbon source. We offered differently treated pieces of rice grains. They provided a useful substrate for applying nutrient solutions, being not attractive per se for these non‐granivorous ants but easy to handle and to be retrieved by the workers. The pieces (grains cut into three pieces each, mass: 5.7 ± 0.2 (mean ± SE) mg, n = 80) were treated with sugar solution (50 g L−1) (as a carbohydrate source), urea (50 g L−1) or uric acid solution (20 g L−1, due to its lower solubility) (as potential nitrogen sources), or distilled water (as control). The pieces were soaked in the respective solution (10 pieces each in 0.15 mL) and then dried for 2 h at 60°C. In each experiment, we offered five pieces per treatment on a plastic platform directly at the nest trunk.

    As ant activity around the nest entrances was often low, we lured out workers before the experiment by offering tuna in a small plastic cap (diameter 2.5 cm) that was removed at the onset of the experiment. Tuna is highly attractive to both species (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010); hence, it seems unlikely that it differentially influenced the subsequent food preferences of the two species. For the following 30 min, we recorded every 5 min how many pieces were carried into the nest by each species, and how many workers were feeding directly at each of the four treatments. The experiments were conducted both during daytime (before 18:00 hours) and at night (after 18:30 hours, under red light) at four parabiotic nests (red Ca. rufifemur: one colony; black Ca. rufifemur: three colonies) (n = 8 replicates per colony and time of day) during 6 weeks. A fifth colony (red Ca. rufifemur), which was not active during the day, was only tested at night (n = 8 replicates), and a further colony (black Ca. rufifemur) was tested during the day instead. The colonies varied strongly in the number of foragers active outside the trunk.

    We used two different measures to assess preferences for the nutrient treatments, namely the total number of rice pieces retrieved to the nest after 30 min, and the number of workers feeding at the pieces on the platform after 5 min. The two ant species showed a highly different foraging behaviour. The larger Ca. rufifemur (about 5–13 mm) usually carried the pieces to the nest and did not stay on the platform for long. Therefore, the mean count of Ca. rufifemur foragers was low (0.2 ± 0.05 (mean ± SE) per replicate and treatment, Fig. 1C), and we decided to use the number of retrieved pieces as a measure of preference. In contrast, the much smaller and weaker Cr. modiglianii (about 2–3 mm) only retrieved 0.07 ± 0.03 rice pieces per treatment and experiment (Fig. 1D,E), but readily foraged at the pieces offered on the platform. We therefore chose to analyse the number of Cr. modiglianii foragers at each treatment after 5 min, thus, before Ca. rufifemur had retrieved any pieces in significant numbers. Analysing the mean counts of Cr. modiglianii foragers between 5 and 30 min yielded similar results. The data were analysed using generalized linear models (GLM) with quasibinomial (seed retrieval) or quasipoisson (number of workers) error distribution. Effects of colony and time of day (day/night) were accounted for by entering these effects as independent variables into the model before the treatment variable. For Ca. rufifemur, one colony with extremely low activity was excluded.

    In an additional set of assays, we estimated the use of various natural possible nitrogen, carbohydrate or phosphate sources. We separately offered various food items at three parabiotic colonies with one to three replicates each, all at night, and recorded the number of foraging ants after 20 min. The items were bird droppings (rich in uric acid), dead grasshoppers (rich in protein), freshly killed rat carrion (protein), and pieces of paper tissue treated with either uric acid suspension (uric acid), urine (urea) or NaH2PO4 solution (phosphate). Furthermore, ant attendance at six trophobioses and two extrafloral nectaries close to two parabiotic nests was recorded during the day (n = 7 times each) and at night (n = 5 times each).

    Stable isotope analyses

    We analysed Ca. rufifemur and Cr. modiglianii samples from 21 parabiotic nests (17 nests from Danum Valley, three from Gunung Mulu National Park and one from Sepilok), and Cr. modiglianii samples from seven non‐parabiotic nests in Danum Valley. All samples had been stored in pure ethanol. For each nest and ant species, three dried ant bodies without gasters were homogenized and placed in tin capsules. For comparison, we included samples from young leaves of five plant species, which had been collected at Danum Valley and dried. These plants were Poikilospermum suaveolens (Cecropiaceae), Brownlowia peltata (Tiliaceae), Leea indica (Leeaceae), Hopea nervosa and Dryobalanops lanceolata (both Dipterocarpaceae).

    Isotopic N and C composition was measured in comparison with standard gases (N2 or CO2 respectively) using an elemental analyser – isotope ratio mass spectrometer (EA‐IRMS). The analysis was performed in a dual element analysis mode with a Carlo Erba 1108 (Carlo Erba, Milano, Italy) elemental analyser coupled via a ConFlo III interface to a delta S isotope ratio mass spectrometer (Finnigan MAT, Bremen, Germany). Stable isotope natural abundances are presented in the δ notation as δ15N or δ13C = (Rsample/Rstandard − 1) × 1000 [‰], where Rsample and Rstandard are the ratios of heavy isotope to light isotope abundance in the samples and the respective standards. Standard gases were calibrated with respect to international standards by using the reference substances N1 and N2 for the N isotopes and ANU sucrose and NBS 19 for the C isotopes (standards from the International Atomic Energy Agency, Vienna, Austria). Reproducibility and accuracy of the isotope abundance measurements were routinely controlled by measures of the test substance acetanilide (Gebauer & Schulze 1991).

    Using data from the 21 parabiotic nests, we tested for differences in the δ15N and δ13C values of the two ant species. We used a manova and controlled for potential differences between the three sites, as well as for potential differences between the three Camponotus varieties (red/black/species 5). For samples from Danum Valley, we additionally tested for differences between parabiotic and non‐parabiotic Cr. modiglianii using a t‐test. The non‐parabiotic colonies had similar cuticular hydrocarbon profiles, and were genetically similar (Menzel 2009), strongly suggesting that parabiotic and non‐parabiotic Cr. modiglianii belong to the same species.

    The isotopic signatures of parabiotic partners might be correlated not only because of diet overlap but also because of spatial variation in the isotope baseline of the habitat (Ponsard & Arditi 2000). To estimate this source of variation, we conducted Mantel tests (using Spearman correlation) between the spatial distances of the nests (in m) and the isotope signature differences for both δ15N and δ13C for each ant species (only using data from the Danum Valley area). Note that a lack of a correlation in this test cannot exclude very fine‐scaled spatial heterogeneity of the baseline.

    Modelling the diet overlap between parabiotic partners

    Parabiotic partners such as Ca. rufifemur and Cr. modiglianii may have an overlapping diet composition if they (i) feed on the same food resources or (ii) directly exchange food through trophallaxis. If compared across the two partners of a series of parabiotic nests, a constant dietary overlap will result in a correlation between the stable isotope signatures of Ca. rufifemur and Cr. modiglianii. In contrast, if they do not feed on the same food items, but on different ones that have similar isotopic signatures, the ants' signatures may be similar by chance but will not correlate across parabiotic nests. We therefore estimated the degree of diet overlap by modelling isotopic signatures of ants with overlapping diets. The correlation coefficients obtained from simulated signatures were compared with the empirical data for 17 parabiotic nests from Danum Valley, which were collected within a radius of approximately 1.5 km.

    In our model, the ants forage on various food resources with 10 different isotopic signatures. The trophic fractionation (ΔCr and ΔCa) is a scalar number that may assume different values for the two species. The vector I represents the isotopic signatures (for any element) of different food items. I comprises 10 elements with the signatures −1.5, −0.5, 0, . . . 3.5. Crematogaster modiglianii forages on food items with various signatures, including different food items with the same signatures. For example, 10% of its diet has a signature of −1.5, 0% the signature −0.5, and so on. The diet vector DCr contains these proportions such that ΣDCr = 1. The overall isotopic signature of the Cr. modiglianii diet is the scalar product I · DCr, and the isotopic signature of Cr. modiglianii can be calculated as δCr = I · DCr + ΔCr, where ΔCr is the trophic fractionation in Cr. modiglianii. Similarly, the signature of Ca. rufifemur is δCa = I · DCa + ΔCa.

    To simulate overlapping diets, calculation of DCr and DCa was modified using an analogous ‘overlap vector’O. Values for O were drawn from a uniform distribution and such that ΣO = ov (e.g. ov = 0.8 for an 80% diet overlap). The diet vectors were then calculated as DCr = O + DCr′ and DCa = O + DCa′, with values for DCr′ and DCa′ both drawn from uniform distributions such that ΣDCr′ = ΣDCa′ = 1 − ov. Using this approach, the per cent overlap of DCr and DCa was at least ov, but could randomly exceed this value. This is realistic when assuming multiple food resources with similar isotopic signatures.

    Using the above equations, we simulated the signatures of Cr. modiglianii and Ca. rufifemur in parabiotic nests. For each nest, O, DCa′ and DCr′ were randomly drawn from uniform distributions, with given values for ov, ΔCr and ΔCa. We calculated Pearson correlation coefficients between the resulting δCr and δCa values. This process was repeated 10 000 times each for 100 ov values between 0 and 1. We ran the simulation (i) for 100 parabiotic nests, to get a better understanding of the expected tendency, and (ii) for 17 nests, to realistically mimic the empirical data (i.e. the number of nests sampled at Danum Valley). Testing various arbitrary values for ΔCr and ΔCa did not affect the correlation coefficients but merely the intercept of the fitted line. Moreover, the simulated coefficients were not affected by number of elements and range of values in I. Plotting the correlation coefficients against the real overlap values (i.e. including random overlap) instead of the coerced diet overlap values (ov) resulted in a similar curve (data not shown). The difference between real (including random) and coerced (ov) overlap decreased towards zero in a linear fashion for ov approaching 1. For ov values close to 1, the fitted line converged towards the function δCa = δCr + ΔCa − ΔCr.

    RESULTS

    Feeding experiments

    The two parabiotic ant species showed strongly different food preferences consistently over different colonies. Camponotus rufifemur retrieved rice pieces of all treatments to the nest. However, urea‐treated pieces were retrieved in significantly higher numbers (Fig. 1A,B, Table 1) and usually before the remaining treatments. During the day, urea‐treated pieces were the only ones retrieved in significant numbers. Only few workers fed on the items directly on the experimental platform (Fig. 1C). In contrast, Cr. modiglianii retrieved only few pieces to the nest (in total: 3–5 pieces per treatment in 80 assays, Fig. 1D,E), but readily fed on pieces of all four treatments (Fig. 1F). Sugar‐treated pieces were significantly more attractive to Cr. modiglianii foragers than any other treatment (Fig. 1F, Table 1), irrespective of time of day. Both ant species were more active at night and showed significant differences between colonies. However, the preferences for certain treatments did not vary significantly between colonies; the colony × treatment interaction term (Table 1) was not significant. No aggressive interactions between the two species were observed during the experiments.

    Stable isotope analyses

    Crematogaster modiglianii had significantly higher δ15N and significantly lower δ13C values than Ca. rufifemur (Fig. 2, Table 3). The pairwise difference between workers of the same parabiotic nest averaged 0.88 ± 0.13‰ (mean ± SE) in δ15N and 0.59 ± 0.11‰ in δ13C. The δ15N values significantly differed among the three sites, but this did not affect the difference between the two species (no significant species × site interaction, Table 3). Relative N biomass content was 13.16 ± 0.09% (Ca. rufifemur) and 13.78 ± 0.10% (Cr. modiglianii, both n = 21).

    The δ15N values of parabiotic partners within the same nest were highly correlated (Pearson correlation: r = 0.76, one‐tailed P < 0.0001; Fig. 3A, Appendix S1). The correlation still holds if only samples from Danum Valley are considered, which were all collected within an approximately 1.5 km radius (r = 0.53, P = 0.015). Some nests with different signatures were as close as 24 m (Fig. 3A). Significant correlations also occurred for δ13C values (r = 0.72, P = 0.0001; Danum only: r = 0.74, P = 0.0004; Fig. 3B). The simulated isotopic signatures of parabiotic partners had median correlation coefficients between zero and one, depending on the simulated diet overlap (Fig. 4). Based on the 95% confidence intervals of the simulated correlation coefficients, we estimated the real diet overlap of parabiotic ants in Danum Valley as 22–66% (median: 52%) for nitrogen and 45–74% (median: 62%) for carbon sources (for 17 simulated nests, Fig. 4). Simulating 100 instead of 17 nests strongly increased the precision of the estimate: while the median estimates remained the same, the width of the confidence intervals decreased to 45–58% (nitrogen) and 57–67% (carbon).

    DISCUSSION

    Feeding experiments

    In our experiments, the two parabiotic ant species showed different food preferences. Crematogaster modiglianii foraged mainly at sugar‐treated rice pieces and often tended trophobioses and extrafloral nectaries, which suggests a high demand for carbohydrates. In contrast, Ca. rufifemur showed less interest in these items and preferred urea‐treated rice and urine. These preferences did not vary among colonies, including one with very high and one with extremely low Ca. rufifemur activity. Hence, it seems unlikely that Ca. rufifemur influenced the observed preferences of the considerably smaller Cr. modiglianii or vice versa, the more so as we never found aggressive interactions between the two species.

    While only Camponotus was attracted to urea baits, both species consumed animal protein (fresh carrion and dead insects). Uric acid powder and bird droppings were also attractive to both species, albeit less so to Cr. modiglianii (Table 2). The relatively low attractiveness of uric acid‐treated rice in our assays may be due to the low solubility of uric acid, such that the pieces contained only little uric acid.

    The preference of Cr. modiglianii for sugar and its high activity at trophobioses suggests that its diet has a relatively high carbohydrate/protein ratio. This excess carbohydrate intake is probably metabolized and respired (Zanotto et al. 1993) and may allow a ‘high tempo’ foraging behaviour (Davidson 1997). This is consistent with the much higher foraging activity of Cr. modiglianii compared with Ca. rufifemur (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010).

    As a general caveat, cafeteria experiments may reflect current demands, that is, the currently most limited nutrient, rather than the dietary niche or overall nutritional requirements of the species tested. For example, ants adjust their feeding preferences according to the currently most limited nutrient (Kay 2004), which can, for example, be influenced by the momentary amount of brood in their nest. This might have biased our results as well. However, as the two parabiotic ant species nest at the same location, they should have access to the same food sources. For example, both species jointly exploited trophobioses and EFN, but these resources were more attractive to Cr. modiglianii than to Ca. rufifemur. Moreover, the preferences observed in our feeding experiments did not vary among colonies. We therefore believe that our results do reflect permanent dietary demands rather than current nutrient limitations.

    Stable isotope signatures

    Camponotus rufifemur had significantly lower δ15N and higher δ13C values than Cr. modiglianii. The difference was independent of study site and Ca. rufifemur variety, and is likely to reflect a consistent difference in diet composition, which varies little across different geographic locations. The average difference between parabiotic partners was 0.88‰, which equals 0.26–0.46 trophic levels, depending on the estimates of trophic enrichment (McCutchan et al. 2003; Spence & Rosenheim 2005).

    Camponotus species harbour the obligate intracellular endosymbiont Blochmannia in their midgut. These bacteria can metabolize urea and ammonia to essential amino acids, and recycle ammonia into the host's amino acid metabolism. It is important to note that neither nitrogen recycling nor the presence of microbial endosymbionts itself alter the trophic enrichment value Δ for 15N (Spence & Rosenheim 2005; Feldhaar et al. 2007). However, Blochmannia enables its host to use a wider variety of nitrogen sources, which is reflected in the stable isotope signature. First, Camponotus species do not need to ingest essential amino acids, and can obtain nitrogen from plant sources such as plant exudates or extrafloral nectaries (which often lack certain essential amino acids, Blüthgen & Fiedler 2004). In contrast, most other ant genera (including Crematogaster) must ingest animal protein to obtain essential amino acids (Feldhaar et al. 2007). Second, and again in contrast to Crematogaster, they can utilize urea sources such as mammal urine. Indeed, Camponotus species frequently forage at urine (Shetty 1982; Pfeiffer 1997). Urine and bird faeces are depleted in 15N compared with their producers' tissue, and have δ15N values similar to, or even lower than their producers' diet (Mizutani & Wada 1988; McCutchan et al. 2003; Sponheimer et al. 2003). Third, because of the internal N recycling, Ca. rufifemur may need a lower overall intake of nitrogen than Cr. modiglianii and can largely rely on the relatively N‐poor, plant‐based sources (Davidson 1997). Thus, the lower 15N signature of Camponotus compared with Crematogaster probably results from a relatively lower intake of (15N‐enriched) animal protein compared with (15N‐poor) plant‐based nitrogen or (15N‐depleted) nitrogen sources like urine and bird faeces.

    The mean 15N signatures of Ca. rufifemur and Cr. modiglianii were 2.8‰ and 3.8‰ above the signature of plants from the same site. Trophic enrichment has been explicitly measured only in a single ant species so far, Camponotus floridanus, where trophic 15N enrichment was 3.0‰ (Feldhaar et al. 2010). Honeydew is enriched by about 1.1–1.4‰ compared with the plant tissue (Yoneyama et al. 1997; Sagers & Goggin 2007).

    Thus, the isotope signatures of both species are consistent with a diet largely based on plants or honeydew, and partly on animal tissue. Fractionation may be even higher in other ants (e.g. a re‐calculated level of 4.8‰ for plant‐based diet consumed by Linepithema, Menke et al. 2010), which would imply a stronger contribution of nitrogen from non‐carnivorous diets when applied to our study.

    Correlation of the isotope signatures

    The isotope signatures of the two partners in a parabiotic nest were significantly correlated, both regarding 15N and regarding 13C signatures. These correlations still held if only data from one site (Danum Valley) were considered. We did not detect any spatial structure in the isotopic signatures of either species. Thus, an environmental effect of small‐scale variation in stable isotope abundances (Ponsard & Arditi 2000) seems unlikely as a sole explanation. The correlations rather suggest that the diets of the two species overlap at least in part, probably through a trophic interaction between parabiotic partners. However, non‐parabiotic Cr. modiglianii had similar 15N and 13C signatures as their parabiotic conspecifics. This suggests that Cr. modiglianii may directly or indirectly affect the diet of Camponotus rather than vice versa. Two pathways seem possible for such an influence. First, Ca. rufifemur follows pheromone trails laid by Cr. modiglianii and thus can forage at resources discovered by Cr. modiglianii, whereas Cr. modiglianii ignores Ca. rufifemur pheromone trails (Menzel et al. 2010). Both species indeed foraged together at trophobioses, EFN and artificial baits (Blüthgen et al. 2006). Second, there may be an intense trophallactic exchange of food. Trophallaxis between the two species has been observed (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010), but up to now its quantitative importance is unclear. However, trophallaxis was usually solicited by Camponotus, which suggests a food flow from Cr. modiglianii to Ca. rufifemur (Seidel 1994; Menzel & Blüthgen 2010) rather than vice versa. Notably, the estimated overlap of carbon sources is higher than that of nitrogen sources. This result is consistent with the food preferences of the two ant species –Cr. modiglianii is mostly attracted to carbohydrate‐rich food sources, which are then exploited by Ca. rufifemur as well, whereas Ca. rufifemur partly uses other nitrogen sources, which it does not share with Cr. modiglianii. Note that the 13C (but not the 15N) signatures could have been influenced by the ethanol storage. However, this should have affected all samples equally (Tillberg et al. 2006). We also emphasize that our estimated diet overlap is only based on correlations so far, and should be investigated in future studies, for example, by experimental manipulation of the stable isotope signatures of food sources.

    CONCLUSION

    Both cafeteria experiments and isotopic analyses showed that the two parabiotic ant species have a different diet composition and, thus occupy at least partly different food niches. In particular, Ca. rufifemur can exploit nitrogen sources inaccessible to Cr. modiglianii and does not depend on animal protein as a source of essential amino acids, while Cr. modiglianii forages more intensively at carbohydrate‐rich baits. Nevertheless, the two species still rely on the same food sources to a large degree, as suggested by their parallel trends in 15N and 13C signatures across nests.

    In earlier studies, two facts indicated that Ca. rufifemur depends on Cr. modiglianii but not vice versa: first, the absence of non‐parabiotic Ca. rufifemur colonies and second, that Ca. rufifemur follows Cr. modiglianii pheromone trails (which usually lead to food sources) while Cr. modiglianii ignores its partner's trails (Menzel et al. 2010). The isotopic data – correlation between signatures of parabiotic partners, but no difference between signatures of parabiotic and non‐parabiotic Cr. modiglianii– now additionally suggest that the diet of Ca. rufifemur is strongly influenced by that of Cr. modiglianii, which corroborates that a major benefit for Camponotus is exploiting resources discovered by Crematogaster (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010). The parabiosis of the two species thus includes a form of trophic exploitation, which may represent one of the major benefits for Ca. rufifemur, and is reimbursed to Cr. modiglianii by an improved nest defence (Menzel & Blüthgen 2010).

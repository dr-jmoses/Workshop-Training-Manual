    Biological Conservation

    Volume 142, Issue 3, March 2009, Pages 668-675

    Species compensation maintains abundance and raid rates of African swarm-raiding army ants in rainforest fragments

    Author links open overlay panelMarcell K.PetersaGeorgFischeraGertrudSchaabbManfredKraemerc
    https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biocon.2008.11.021Get rights and content

    Abstract

    Habitat clearance and fragmentation is increasingly threatening the biodiversity of tropical rainforests, however, the response of many insect species, even of key organisms, is still little understood. Using an extensive data set spanning over four years, we analyzed the effects of clearance and fragmentation of a Congo-Guinean rainforest in western Kenya on the abundance and the raid rates of two species of swarm-raiding army ants, which are considered keystone organisms of tropical rainforests. The abundance of army ants was measured by transect monitoring and by short-term pitfall trapping while raid rates were measured by long-term pitfall trapping over a period of five months. Dorylus wilverthi was the most abundant army ant in undisturbed rainforest and its abundance and raid rate strongly declined in small forest fragments. In contrast, the abundance of Dorylus molestus increased with decreasing fragment size and compensated for the decline of D. wilverthi in terms of abundance and ecological functionality (i.e. raiding rates). D. molestus appears to have a higher ability of using the agricultural land surrounding the Kakamega Forest than D. wilverthi, which may explain the species-specific differences in the susceptibility to habitat fragmentation. Our study demonstrates that habitat fragmentation may have a differential effect on two ecologically highly similar keystone species. Moreover, it shows that species compensation might help in maintaining an important ecosystem function (i.e. raiding by swarm-raiding army ants) in fragmented tropical rainforests.

    1. Introduction

    Less than 50% of tropical forests still exist and remaining forests are vanishing at an alarming rate (Achard et al., 2002, Wright, 2005). In future decades, industrial logging and steadily increasing demands for agricultural land are expected to put a high pressure on the remaining forest blocks (Millennium Ecosystem Assessment, 2005, Primack and Corlett, 2005). In many areas, rainforest animal species will depend on survival in fragmented landscapes, where small protected ‘forest islands’ are embedded in a matrix of agricultural land. Despite expected increases in deforestation and fragmentation of the tropical rainforests (e.g. Flamenco-Sandoval et al., 2007) little is known on the response of insect species and even of key ecological groups to these processes.

    Swarm-raiding army ants are prime invertebrate predators in tropical forests and are well known for their impressive foraging behaviour (Gotwald, 1995): forming a fan-shaped swarm of several meters width, hundreds of thousands to millions of workers search the leaf-litter and lower vegetation for animal prey. In 1 day, a single raid of these ants can affect areas of more than 1000 m2 (Leroux, 1982). Swarm-raiding army ants are highly mobile as they change their nest sites very frequently, a behaviour known as nomadism (Gotwald, 1995). For example, colonies of Dorylus (Anomma) molestus were reported to emigrate from an old nesting site to a new one (mean distance = 93 m) on average every 17 days (range 3–111 days) (Schöning et al., 2005).

    Swarm-raiding army ants are considered keystone organisms of tropical rainforest ecosystems due to several reasons. First, their raids affect the density and sustain the diversity of leaf-litter arthropods (Franks, 1982a, Franks and Bossert, 1983, Otis et al., 1986, Vieira and Höfer, 1994). Second, many invertebrate and vertebrate species are obligatorily or facultatively associated with these ants, such as ant-following birds (Willis and Oniki, 1978, Peters et al., 2008), phorid flies (Brown and Feener, 1998), and hundreds of species living as ‘guests’ within the army ant colonies (Gotwald, 1995).

    Swarm-raiding army ants combine several traits which are generally known to render species prone to habitat fragmentation (Turner, 1996): they are top-predators, have very low colony densities, and require large areas of adequate habitat for survival (Gotwald, 1995). Their queens are wingless and they exclusively reproduce by colony fission (Gotwald, 1995), which strongly reduces their dispersal ability and therefore the ability to (re)colonize forest patches. Indeed, in the well studied Neotropical swarm-raiding army ant Eciton burchellii, field studies and simulations revealed a high vulnerability to the loss and fragmentation of forest habitat (Partridge et al., 1996, Terborgh et al., 1997, Boswell et al., 1998). In contrast, the African swarm-raiding army ant D. molestus was not affected by forest fragmentation, probably due to its ability to disperse through dry matrix habitats, e.g. farmland, dry scrubland, or savannah (Gotwald, 1995, Schöning et al., 2006). However, it remained unknown if the low susceptibility to deforestation and habitat fragmentation of this army ant is a species-specific phenomenon or whether it applies to all African swarm-raiding army ant species.

    In a study spanning 4 years we analyzed the impact of rainforest clearance and fragmentation on the abundance and raid rates of swarm-raiding army ants in a fragmented Congo-Guinean rainforest in western Kenya, the Kakamega Forest. In this area, the distributional ranges of two swarm-raiding army ant species, Dorylus (Anomma) wilverthi and D. molestus, overlap. (i) We evaluated the effects of habitat fragmentation on the abundances of the two species. (ii) We measured how frequent a point on the rainforest floor gets raided by swarm-raiding army ants (i.e. we estimated the raid rate of swarm-raiding army ants) and tested whether habitat fragmentation alters this rate. (iii) In order to explain differences in the response of army ant species to forest fragmentation we analyzed if and to which degree army ants are capable of using the open agricultural land which surrounds and connects forest fragments. We expect that species which are not capable of dispersing through agricultural land are highly prone to disappear from small forest fragments under a particular size at which no viable populations can be sustained. In contrast, species showing high ability of using agricultural land may not be adversely affected by habitat fragmentation since they can easily disperse in fragmented landscapes and reach isolated forest patches (e.g. Sekercioglu et al., 2002).

    2. Methods

    2.1. Study area and organisms

    The study was conducted between October 2003 and October 2007 at Kakamega Forest (0°17′N, 34°53′E) in western Kenya. Kakamega Forest is located ∼50 km north of Lake Victoria at an altitude of approximately 1650 m asl, and is considered the easternmost relic of the Congo-Guinean rainforests (Wagner et al., 2008). The area receives mean precipitations of about 2000 mm per year. Of an estimated rainforest cover of 25,000 ha at the time of British colonization (Brooks et al., 1999), around 13,000 ha remain (Lung, 2004). These are distributed over a large central forest fragment (8245 ha), referred to here as the ‘main forest’, three medium-sized forest fragments (420–1370 ha), and three small forest fragments (65–113 ha; distances of fragments to the main forest: 1.3–9.4 km). Every fragment has been isolated from the main forest for at least 35 years (Mitchell, 2004). We derived fragment sizes by on-screen visual interpretation of a Landsat-ETM7 satellite image from 5 February 2001, with digitizing and subsequent area calculations for the gained polygons, all performed in ArcGIS 8. For a best judgement on forest stages, we used the combination of the spectral bands 5/4/3 and included a contrast enhancement in order to facilitate in particular the distinction between the diverse forest formations. We set apart mature natural forest from the background, comprised of agricultural land, grassland, younger forest formations, and forest plantations. Two medium-sized forest fragments were interconnected by a thin corridor of degraded forest and were additionally interrupted by a wide road, which is assumed to form a barrier for emigrating army ant colonies. We therefore regarded them as separate forest fragments.

    The forest fragments are surrounded in most areas by densely populated agricultural land (∼600 people/km2; KIFCON, 1994). In some areas, grassland, bush vegetation, or young forest regrowth can be found. In the agricultural area, small-scale subsistence farming dominates, with maize, sugarcane and tea being the most dominant crops. The agricultural land is structurally diverse, as most fields and housing compounds are surrounded by trees and bushes. Moreover, insecticides are scarcely used and mostly applied locally in low concentrations.

    The two swarm-raiding army ant species in the study area, D. molestus and D. wilverthi, are morphologically distinguishable by the shape of the soldiers’ heads. A population genetic analysis using microsatellite markers confirmed the presence of these two distinct species, and assignment tests based on the molecular data were highly congruent with morphological species identifications (M.K.P, D.J.C. Kronauer, C. Schöning, J.J. Boomsma, unpublished data).

    2.2. Army ant abundance in forest fragments

    Between April 2004 and December 2005, the abundance of D. wilverthi and D. molestus was monitored on 33 transects of 500 m. The transects were equally dispersed over 11 study sites in all forest fragments of Kakamega Forest (one transect in the smallest fragment had a length of only 350 m). The three transects per study site were at least 100 m away from each other. Five study sites were located in the main forest while additional single study sites were established in each of the six smaller forest fragments. Transects were placed exclusively in forest habitats. Most parts of all transects were situated more than 50 m away from the forest edge. We carefully ensured that the proportion of forest edge habitat (⩽50 m to the forest edge) of transects was not correlated with fragment size.

    Transects were successively monitored for a total of 37–41 surveys per transect. The starting time of monitoring varied between 7:00 am and 3:00 pm. Twenty-two percents of all surveys began before 9:00 am, 57% before 10:00 am and 91% before 12:00 noon. We ensured there were no systematic differences in starting times among transects. During monitoring, we slowly walked along transects at a speed of roughly 2–4 km/h and checked for the presence of army ant swarms or trails (emigration trails, foraging trails). Swarms and trails are easily located on transects and are indicators of overall abundance and density of army ant colonies in an area (Franks, 1982b, O’Donnell et al., 2007, Vidal-Riggs and Chaves-Campos, 2008). In the rare case of finding two trails of one species within a 100 m distance, these trails were treated as belonging to a single colony, in order to prevent multiple counts of one colony produced by the branches of a single raid (the swarm raid is usually highly branched close to its front). We collected a sample of workers from each colony (including soldiers, if possible) and preserved them in 70% or 95% ethanol for subsequent identification. We calculated the mean abundance for each transect and species (colonies/km).

    We used the formula of Franks (1982b) to roughly estimate colony densities in the main forest and subsequently estimate population sizes for forest fragments. The following assumptions were used: average foraging trail length at time of monitoring = 50 m (estimates for mean trail length of 25 and 75 m are also given), number of raids per colony at time of monitoring = 1, percentage of active colonies at time of monitoring = 100%. These assumptions were based on known maximum foraging trail length, time of foraging, duration of raids, and number of parallel raids per colony, reported for the African swarm-raiding army ants Dorylus nigricans and D. molestus in Leroux, 1982, Schöning et al., 2005, respectively. However, the number of raids per colony at the time of monitoring may be slightly higher than one (which would increase the estimated number of colonies in fragments), whereas the percentage of active colonies may be slightly lower than one (which would decrease the estimated number of colonies in fragments). The assumptions pertaining to foraging trail length are very broad (25–75 m) and should cover the true mean length at the time of monitoring.

    2.3. Raid rates in forest fragments

    We measured the raid rates of the two swarm-raiding army ant species between July and December 2005. We set up six pitfall traps (Ø 7.5 cm plastic cup filled with 50 ml 4% formaldehyde solution, with a 12 × 12 cm rain cover at a height of ∼12 cm) along each transect (in 100 m intervals). We checked the traps weekly for the presence of workers of D. wilverthi or D. molestus over a time period of 19 weeks, up to a total 114 trap weeks per transect (76 trap weeks for the 350 m transect). The presence of army ants in a trap either indicates that a swarm raided the area or that workers fell into the trap during emigration. As army ant brood (usually carried by workers during emigration) was never found in the traps, we are confident that the presence of workers in a trap always indicated that a swarm raided the area. We calculated raid rates of swarm-raiding army ants for each transect according to the number of trap weeks in which at least one army ant worker was found, divided by the total number of trap weeks per transect. When finding traps that had been destroyed, we replaced them and did not include the last trap week in the calculation of raid rates.

    2.4. Abundance in forest and agricultural land

    Abundances of army ants in the main forest and in agricultural land were determined between October and December 2003 on eight 500 m transects in the main forest, and another eight 500 m transects in the agricultural land. Several of the forest transects were later used for studying the effects of habitat fragmentation on abundance and raid rates. Transects were successively monitored for a total of 18 surveys in the way described above. Monitoring started at different times of day from morning to late afternoon, but we carefully avoided systematic differences in starting times among transects, which could have influenced the probability of finding army ant colonies.

    We confirmed our results of the transect monitoring, which has been established as the standard methodology for monitoring swarm-raiding army ants, by recording the presence of army ants in habitats by pitfall trapping. This was predominately done because army ants in the agricultural land may be inactive during the day since they are known to be sensitive to humidity. Because transect monitoring was exclusively done at daytime, it might underestimate the abundance of colonies in farmland. In contrast, pitfall traps were active for a total of seven days, night and day, and should have detected the presence of army ant colonies at a locality even if these raid only at night. The pitfall trap survey was conducted between May and October 2007. Eleven 200 m transects were placed in agricultural land, and eight in mature rainforest (six in the main forest, and two in medium-sized forest fragments). On each transect, ten pitfall traps (filled with 50 ml 95% ethanol solution) were placed in 20 m intervals and were checked after one week for the presence of army ants.

    2.5. Statistical analysis

    We compared mean army ant abundances and raid rates among fragment size classes by linear mixed-effect models (the lme function in the R-package nlme). Fragment size (categorical) was used as a fixed effect and study site (categorical) as a random effect in the models. By using study site as a random factor in linear models we controlled for the correlation of errors in army ant abundance among the three transects of each study site. Army ant abundance was log-transformed (or log(x + 0.1)-transformed, see Section 3) and raid rate was square-root transformed in order to improve normality of residuals.

    Using ANCOVA, we tested whether raid rates can be predicted from the army ant abundance data (transect monitoring data). This would allow estimating mean raid rates over the period of 21 month, during which abundances on transects were measured. In the ANCOVA, raid rate (square-root transformed) was used as the response variable and army ant abundance, army ant species, and the interaction term abundance * species were used as explaining variables. Non-significant terms were removed in a stepwise process from the model if their parameter estimates were not significant.

    Army ant abundances on transects between agricultural land and forest were compared by t-tests. Presence of species on pitfall trap transects were compared by χ2-tests for equality of proportions.

    We used R 2.6.1 (R Development Core Team, 2006, http://www.r-project.org) and the R-package nlme, version 3.1-86 (http://www.r-project.org), for the statistical analyses.

    3. Results

    3.1. Effects of fragment size on abundance

    Overall, we found 320 trails or swarms of army ant colonies on 637 km of monitored transects (∼0.5 colonies/km). Fragment size had a divergent effect on the two ant species. The abundance of D. wilverthi was equally high in the main forest and the medium-sized forest fragments but this ant species was almost absent from small forest fragments (Table 1, Fig. 1). In contrast, the mean abundance of D. molestus was higher in small forest fragments than in medium-sized forest fragments or in the main forest (marginally significant), and compensated in terms of mean abundance for the decline of D. wilverthi. Hence, the total mean abundance of army ants was equal among fragment size classes. The estimated density in the main forest of D. wilverthi was ∼8 colonies/100 ha (based on different, more extreme assumptions the true density should be 5–15 colonies/100 ha; see methods for assumptions) and for D. molestus ∼5 colonies/100 ha (4–11 colonies/100 ha). These data suggest that the small forest fragments analyzed in this study (65–113 ha) can hold a maximum of only ∼5–9 colonies of D. wilverthi and ∼4–6 colonies of D. molestus.

    3.2. Effects of fragment size on raid rates

    The analysis of a total of 3544 pitfall trap weeks revealed that the mean probability of a point on the forest floor to be raided by D. wilverthi or D. molestus per week was on average 0.125 (SE = 0.017) and varied between 0.009 and 0.406 among the 33 transects. Raid rates of D. wilverthi did not differ significantly between the main forest and the medium-sized forest fragments, but were significantly lower in small forest fragments (Table 2, Fig. 2). The pattern was opposite for D. molestus (even though the increase in small fragments was only marginally significant), so that the total raid rate of both species combined did not significantly differ among fragment size classes. The raid rate was highly correlated to the abundance of army ants on transects measured over the same time period (p < 0.001), with slightly different intercepts for the two ant species (p = 0.031). The interaction term species * abundance was not significant (p = 0.35) and was consequently removed from the model. The final model explained R2 = 66% of the variation in raid rates (R2 = 0.66, F2,63 = 62.3, p < 0.001; (raid rate D. wilverthi)1/2 = 0.074 + 0.388 * abundance D. wilverthi; (raid rate D. molestus)1/2 = 0.123 + 0.388 * abundance D. molestus). The high predictability of raid rates by transect abundance data suggests that even over larger temporal scales, total raid rates (abundance data has been collected over a period of nearly two years) did not differ among small, medium and large forest fragments.

    3.3. Abundance in main forest and agricultural land

    We found 17 trails or swarms of army ant colonies on 72 km of transects in the agricultural land (mean abundance = 0.236 colonies/km) and 44 on 72 km in the main forest (mean abundance = 0.611 colonies/km). In the agricultural land, more colonies of D. molestus than of D. wilverthi were found (24% D. wilverthi, 76% D. molestus), whereas in the main forest the pattern was opposite (77% D. wilverthi, 23% D. molestus). The mean abundance of D. wilverthi was significantly higher in the main forest than in the agricultural area (t-test, t = 3.355, df = 14, p = 0.005; Fig. 3), whereas for D. molestus no significant differences between habitats were found (t-test, t = 0.118, df = 14, p = 0.908). Pitfall trapping in two study periods indicated significantly higher probability for D. wilverthi to be present on forest transects (63%) than on transects in agricultural land (9%, χ2 = 3.89, p < 0.05). In contrast, no significant differences in presence probability between habitats were found for D. molestus (probability of presence on forest transects = 25%, on farmland transects = 27%, χ2 = 0.00, p = 1).

    4. Discussion

    This study showed that the low susceptibility to habitat fragmentation that was reported for D. molestus (Schöning et al., 2006), the only species which had been previously studied in this respect, does not reflect the situation in all species of African swarm-raiding army ants. D. wilverthi, a species which is widely distributed in Central African rainforests and which is the dominant army ant species in the main forest block of the Kakamega Forest, disappeared from forest fragments of less than 420 ha (although fragments of >113 ha and < 420 ha were not analyzed in this study). Our data suggest that large continuous areas of tropical rainforest are necessary to conserve this ant species.

    The more fragmentation-tolerant D. molestus, the second swarm-raiding army ant species in our study area, compensated for the decline of D. wilverthi by increasing in abundance in small fragments. Because of this species compensation, both the mean colony abundance and the mean raid rate of swarm-raiding army ants were nearly constant throughout the fragment size gradient. Compensation for low abundance of a fragmentation-prone species by higher abundance of a fragmentation-tolerant species may also occur in other fragmented Congo-Guinean rainforests, where two or more species of swarm-raiding army ants often coexist (Deblauwe and Dekoninck, 2007).

    The increase in the abundance of D. molestus in small fragments is best explained by a release from interspecific competition with D. wilverthi. The emigration direction of swarm-raiding army ant colonies, and consequently their spatial distribution, has been shown to be highly influenced by the position of neighbouring colonies: colonies of D. molestus usually emigrate away from their nearest neighbour (Schöning et al., 2005). Depletion of food resources by neighbouring colonies has been considered the ultimate cause for this behaviour (Schöning et al., 2005). As the diets of D. wilverthi and D. molestus appear to be very similar, competition for food resources might also be of importance between these species (based on the formula of Pianka (1974), food niche overlap was calculated to be 0.83; G.F. and M.K.P., unpublished data). The decline in D. wilverthi populations in small forest fragments may increase the availability of food resources for D. molestus and lead to higher densities of this species. Schöning et al. (2008) reported differences in the prey spectra of D. wilverthi and D. molestus. However, in their study food spectra of the two species were not analyzed in the same locality so that the observed differences in food niches probably result from differences in the composition of animal communities between study areas rather than from differences in species-specific feeding preferences. Further experimental and behavioural studies are needed in order to obtain a fuller picture of interspecific interactions among colonies of swarm-raiding army ants.

    The different responses of D. molestus and D. wilverthi to habitat fragmentation are remarkable, as the two species appear to be very similar ecologically; like all African army ants, both species nest in underground cavities, have wingless queens, reproduce exclusively by colony fission, and show low population densities (Gotwald, 1995). Both species emigrate frequently to new nesting sites and thereby use epigaeic emigration trails. D. wilverthi and D. molestus are extremely polyphagous predators, forming swarm raids of several meters in width and searching the leaf-litter and lower vegetation for prey (Gotwald, 1995, Schöning et al., 2008; personal observation). Captured prey is transported to the nest site in epigaeic foraging trails. Despite of a higher probability of D. wilverthi to climb during raids into the lower vegetation (M.K.P., unpublished data), raids of the two species appeared highly similar. In fact, they were so similar that it was impossible for us to identify the species by simply observing the swarm raid or a foraging trail.

    Our data suggest that species-specific differences in the ability to use and disperse through agricultural land, the dominating matrix habitat in the area, is responsible for the differences in the susceptibility to habitat fragmentation. The inability to disperse through matrix habitats was identified as a major factor determining the susceptibility of bird species to habitat fragmentation (Sekercioglu et al., 2002, Palmer et al., 2008). Populations of species which are unable to disperse through matrix habitat become isolated from other populations. If isolated populations are too small, their chances of becoming extinct are higher because of environmental and demographic stochasticity, or due to genetic erosion or inbreeding (Shaffer, 1981, Reed, 2005, Frankham, 2005). D. wilverthi was found in much lower abundances (∼9×) in the agricultural land than in the neighbouring study sites in the main forest, suggesting a very low ability of using matrix habitat. Conversely, the abundance of D. molestus was similar in forest and farmland, and more colonies of this species than of D. wilverthi were found in the agricultural land despite dominance of D. wilverthi in the neighbouring main forest. This finding fits well with our expectation for a species with a high ability of using the matrix habitat and is consistent with other studies which described high ecological flexibility of D. molestus (Gotwald, 1995, Schöning et al., 2006).

    The difference in the ability to use or disperse through open habitats may be explained by the contrasting biogeographic history of the two species. Climatic conditions in the distributional range of D. molestus (East Africa) were and are spatially and temporally more variable and are generally drier than those in the distributional range of D. wilverthi (Central Africa). Hence, selection for tolerance against dry conditions might have been much stronger for D. molestus than for D. wilverthi.

    High susceptibility to habitat fragmentation similar to that observed in D. wilverthi has previously been reported for the Neotropical swarm-raiding army ant E. burchellii (Willis and Oniki, 1978, Partridge et al., 1996, Terborgh et al., 1997, Sekercioglu et al., 2002), which is the best studied army ant species to date (Gotwald, 1995). Workers of this species are susceptible to the high temperatures typical of open farmland habitats, which may hamper migration of colonies between forest patches and consequently increase the isolation of fragmented populations (Meisel, 2006), a mechanism which may also apply to D. wilverthi.

    Swarm raids of army ants and the animals following these raids (e.g. ant-following birds) constitute one of the most significant predatory forces in tropical rainforests (Otis et al., 1986, Vieira and Höfer, 1994, Gotwald, 1995) and have been reported to increase the overall diversity of insect communities by temporally removing dominant species from the leaf-litter and giving less competitive species the opportunity to recolonize patches (Franks and Bossert, 1983). Given the high ecological importance of swarm-raiding army ant raids in tropical rainforests, a worst case scenario from a conservationist’s point of view is the disappearance of these animals from fragmented landscapes and a strong decline of their raid rates. These would probably have drastic effects on the spatio-temporal structure of prey communities and might also affect lower trophic levels, even primary producers, via cascading effects (Schmitz et al., 2000). The increase of the fragmentation-tolerant D. molestus in small forest fragments, which makes up for the decline of the fragmentation-prone D. wilverthi, maintains raid rates on a level similar to those in the main forest. Because the prey spectra of the two ant species are quite similar, we assume that the spatio-temporal structure of prey communities might also be largely maintained in small forest fragments. The maintenance of raid rates may also help to sustain densities of ant-following birds in small forest fragments, as these birds benefit from foraging at army ant swarms (Meisel, 2004), and many follow raids of both ant species (Peters et al., 2008).

    Our study provides the first estimates of the rate at which army ants swarm over the floor of African tropical rainforests and shows that habitat fragmentation did not significantly affect this rate. Moreover, with a total sampling effort of 3544 pitfall trap weeks and 443 documented raid events, our study is the most extensive hitherto conducted to reconstruct patterns of army ant raiding. A mean raid rate of 0.125 raids per week that was found in this study is higher than the estimates for E. burchellii based on computer simulations by (Franks and Bossert, 1983; 50% of the analyzed area is raided within 34 weeks; our data suggests that 50% of the area is raided within ∼7 weeks), but significantly lower than the raid rate of 1.5 raids per week estimated for E. burchellii (Kaspari and O’Donnell, 2003) or a rate of 0.91 raids per week estimated for E. burchellii plus Labidus praedator (O’Donnell et al., 2007).

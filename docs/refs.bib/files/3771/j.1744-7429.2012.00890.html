    Abstract

    Invertebrate communities of the tropical rain forest floor are highly diverse, characterized by patchy species distribution patterns and high variation in species density. Spatial variation in the foraging activity of swarm raiding army ants, prime invertebrate predators in tropical rain forests, is discussed as a mechanism contributing to these patterns, but highly resolved long‐term data on army ant raiding on the local and landscape scale are hitherto lacking. In this study, 196 positions in 11 study sites in a tropical rain forest in western Kenya were continuously monitored over ~4 mo for the occurrence of swarm raids of army ants. Using population simulation analyses, the consequences of army ant raiding for prey communities were assessed. We found an unexpectedly high variation in raid rates at the study site and landscape scale. The weekly chance of communities to become raided by army ants was on average 0.11, but ranged from 0 to 0.50 among the 196 positions. Simulating population developments of two Lotka–Volterra species—showing slight trade‐offs between competitive strength and resistance to army ant raids—in the real raiding landscapes showed that the observed spatial variation in raid rates may produce high prey diversity at larger spatial scales (due to high β‐diversity) and strong variation in species density. Our results indicate that high spatial variation in army ant swarm raiding is a mechanism capable of generating patchy species distribution patterns and maintaining the high biodiversity of invertebrate communities of the tropical rain forest floor.

    The floor of tropical rain forests hold an extremely high number of invertebrate species which are often patchily distributed (Wilson 1958, Levings 1983). The density of species and individuals strongly varies even at spatial scales of a few meters (e.g., Levings & Windsor 1982, Levings 1983, Kaspari & Yanoviak 2009, Silva & Brandao 2010). Disturbance is considered a potential mechanism contributing to these patterns (Sousa 1984, Feener & Schupp 1998, Fraterrigo & Rusak 2008, Mertl et al. 2009). Beyond visually obvious disturbances like treefall gaps, the activity of predators can produce patchy distribution patterns of prey species, strong variation in species density, and the maintenance of high species diversity (Franks & Bossert 1983, Sousa 1984).

    Army ants are prime invertebrate predators in tropical forests (Gotwald 1995). While most army ant species are hypogaeic, often specialized column raiders, a few species of the New World army ants (Subfamily: Ecitoninae), and African army ants (Subfamily: Dorylinae) forage in massive surface swarm raids. Swarms consist of several hundred thousand to millions of ants, which move at a speed of 6–12 m per hour over the forest floor and search through the leaf litter and lower vegetation for animal prey. They are generalist predators, feeding on a large variety of animals, even though invertebrates constitute their most important prey (Gotwald 1995, Schöning et al. 2008). Colonies of swarm raiding army ants can consume 50 g animal dry mass per day, significantly influence the abundance and diversity of soil invertebrates, and are suspected to limit populations of their prey (Franks 1982, Otis et al. 1986, Gotwald 1995, Schöning et al. 2005, 2010, Kaspari et al. 2011). Several animal species evolved costly adaptations to resist army ant raids underscoring the strong predation pressure exerted by army ant raids (Gotwald 1995, Powell & Clark 2004, Huang 2010). Swarm raids are accompanied by opportunistic and specialized ant‐followers, e.g., ant‐following birds, which feed on animals driven out of the leaf litter by the ants, an effect which significantly adds on to the impact of the ants on prey communities (Willis & Oniki 1978, Peters et al. 2008, Peters & Okalo 2009, Schöning et al. 2010).

    Army ant colonies are highly mobile biotic components in local ecosystem. Colonies frequently move to new nest sites, a behavior called nomadism (Gotwald 1995). In contrast to the well‐studied and better known Neotropical swarm raiding army ant Eciton burchellii, which shows a strict regular movement pattern consisting of alternating nomadic and statuary phases (Franks & Fletcher 1983, Gotwald 1995), emigrations of African swarm raiding army ant colonies occur at irregular and much lower frequencies, on average once in about 8–20 d, with emigration distances between 40 and >200 m (Gotwald 1995, Schöning et al. 2005).

    Spatial variation in army ant raiding was suggested to be a mechanism contributing to patchy species distribution patterns and the maintenance of species diversity of tropical rain forests (O'Donnell et al. 2007). However, observational data on variation in raid rates at small spatial scales (e.g., local forest site or landscape scale), and over longer time periods are lacking. O'Donnell et al. (2007, 2009, 2011) found strong variation in army ant raid rates at large spatial scales, i.e., among four neotropical forests. Schöning et al. (2010) found strong spatial variation in raid rates even at small spatial scales (in an area of only 65 ha) in an East African mountain forest, although the distribution of colonies was regular in space. Monitoring was conducted over only a few weeks, however, and it might be possible that raid rates would homogenize over a longer time period.

    In this study, swarm raid events were continuously recorded over ~4 mo at 196 positions in a Guineo‐Congolian rain forest to reconstruct spatio‐temporal patterns in army ant swarm raiding and to assess the degree of spatial variation in raid rates on the local and landscape scale. The study period over which army ant raid rates were measured exceeds those of former studies, allowing more robust measures of mean and variation in raid rates. We tested if the variation in raid rates can be explained by abiotic predictor variables including those related to the human disturbance of the forest (O'Donnell & Kumar 2006, Kumar & O'Donnell 2009, Peters et al. 2009, 2011). Furthermore, using simple simulation models it was inferred if spatial variation in army ant raid rates at small spatial scales might be a mechanism contributing to patchy distribution patterns and the maintenance of species diversity in army ant prey communities.

    Methods

    Raid rates were continuously measured over a time period of 19 wks between 27 July 2005 and 8 December 2005 in the Kakamega Forest, western Kenya (34° 40′ 19″ to 24° 58′ 23″ E and 0° 6′ 20″ to 0° 30′ 14″ N). The Kakamega Forest is situated approximately 50 km north of Lake Victoria at an altitude of ca 1460–1765 m asl. The fauna and flora of the Kakamega Forest is highly species‐rich and dominated by Guineo‐Congolian species (Wagner et al. 2008, Hita Garcia et al. 2009). The ant fauna includes two species of swarm raiding army ants, Dorylus wilverthi and Dorylus molestus. Within the past 30 yrs, the area of diverse old growth rain forest, showing canopy heights of 25–35 m, decreased from ~155 km2 to ~125 km2 (Lung 2004). Agricultural land, forest regrowth in different successional stages, grassland, and forest plantations dominate formerly cleared areas (Lung 2004). The forest receives an annual precipitation of about 2000 mm with a pronounced dry season from December to February with very little rainfall, a long rainy season from March to May, and a small rainy season typically occurring in October/November. Therefore, the time period in which raid rates were measured included months with intermediate to high rainfall. As the foraging activity of D. molestus decreases under dry conditions (Peters & Okalo 2009), extending the study period into the dry season would probably have led to lower average estimates of raid rates.

    Eleven study sites were established in old growth rain forest, five in a large 8245 ha mainforest block, three in medium‐sized (420–1370 ha), and three in small forest fragments (65–113 ha size). In each study site we established three transects of 500 m (one transect in one study site was only 350 m long as the size and shape of one forest fragment did not allow the establishment of three 500 m transects). The three transects within each study site had a minimum distance of 100 m between them, whereas the study sites had distances of 1–36 km between them. Six pitfall traps (Ø 7.5 cm plastic cup filled with 100 mL 4% formaldehyde solution, with a 12 cm × 12 cm rain cover at a height of ~12 cm) were set up along each transect (in 100 m intervals) and, consequently, 18 traps per study site (16 for the study site with the 350 m transect). A distance of 100 m between individual pitfall traps was chosen because army ants colonies often forage over distances of >50 m (Schöning et al. 2005), and pitfall traps placed closer to each other may therefore have a high chance of capturing the same raid events.

    Traps were checked weekly for the presence of workers of D. wilverthi and D. molestus, summing up to a total of 3724 trap weeks over all study sites. The presence of army ant workers in a trap either indicated that an army ant swarm raided the area or that during an emigration workers fell into the trap. Army ant brood, which is usually being carried by the workers during an emigration, was never found inside the traps. Therefore, we are confident that the presence of workers in a trap always indicated that a swarm raided the area. If we found traps that had been destroyed (59 of 3724 trap weeks in total), we replaced them. A monitoring interval of 1 wk was large in comparison to intervals used in former studies (e.g., Schöning et al. 2010), but constrained by the large spatial scale of our study, which did not allow more frequent checkings of traps. Please note that a monitoring interval of 1 wk may underestimate raid rates if army ants raid an area more often than once a week. For each position and study site the raid rate (weeks with raids/total number of trap weeks) was calculated. For calculating raid rates, trap weeks with traps found destroyed were excluded. Using Kruskal–Wallis tests and Levene's test for the homogeneity of variances, we tested differences in the mean and the variation in raid rates among study sites.

    To examine if the variation in raid rates is a pattern related to anthropogenic disturbance or other abiotic factors, we tested the influence of the following environmental variables on raid rates of D. wilverthi, D. molestus, and total raid rates (raids of both species pooled): relative temperature, relative humidity, distance to the next forest edge, forest fragmentation (index), and forest cover within a distance of 1500 m. A detailed description of the methodology used for deriving these measures is described in Appendix S1.

    The predictive power of these variables on raid rates was examined using ordinary linear models. The minimum adequate model was selected based on the Akaike information criterion (AIC), i.e., using an information theoretic approach (Burnham & Anderson 2004). Residuals of ordinary linear models were examined for spatial autocorrelation using the Moran's I test. As significant spatial autocorrelation was detected, in addition, spatial linear models (SLM) were used to analyze the influence of environmental variables on raid rates (Kissling & Carl 2008). The spatial linear models were calculated as ‘spatial simultaneous autoregressive error models’ using the R library ‘spdep’, v. 0.3–25 (2006, R. Bivand, available at http://cran.r-project.org/src/contrib/Descriptions/spdep.html). These models are a special type of simultaneous autoregressive models and assume that the response at each location i is a function not only of the explanatory variable at i but also of the values of the response at neighboring locations j as well. The spatial weights matrix in SLM was calculated with neighborhoods defined by a maximum bound of 250 m (minimum = 0 m) using the dnearneigh function of spdep and a row standardized coding style. The neighborhood defines the extend for which spatial autocorrelation is assumed. For more details on the spatial linear modeling approach see Kissling and Carl (2008). Various maximum bounds for defining the neighborhoods were tested and 250 m was generally selected to be the one best fitting to the data in terms of R2 and AIC as the evaluation criteria.

    Simulation models were used to estimate the potential consequences of spatio‐temporal variation in army ant raiding for the maintenance of diversity in prey communities. Dorylus wilverthi and D. molestus have a highly similar way of foraging (i.e., both are swarm raiding) and a strongly overlapping diet (food niche overlap: Pianka's O = 0.85, GF & MKP, unpubl. data) and for a large percentage of invertebrate species of the leaf litter, raids of both army ant species probably have similar effects. We therefore pooled raiding data of both species for simulating effects of army ant raiding on prey populations. The development of populations of two Lotka–Volterra species were simulated under the real army ant raiding regimes observed at the 196 positions in the field. We here define Lotka–Volterra species as populations which grow and interact following simple Lotka–Volterra equations. We used simple Lotka–Volterra equations for simulating populations as these equations are well‐established in ecology, only few life history parameters have to be defined, which may affect results of simulations, and the simulations are analytically easily tractable.     The species only differ in two parameters, carrying capacity (KA = 50, KB = 70) and the effect of army ant raids on populations (pA = 0.4, pB = 0.8). Species B has a higher carrying capacity and therefore outcompetes species A in the absence of predation. In case of an army ant raid, 40 percent of the population of species A is killed, whereas 80 percent of the population of species B is killed. Values used for simulating the effect of army ant raids on prey species were observed in similar strengths in natural systems. For example, Levings (1983) observed that after a raid of the Neotropical army ant Eciton hamatum the abundance of the prey species Odontomachus bauri dropped by 48–72 percent. Carrying capacity values of prey species were subsequently defined by assuming that the species with the higher resistance to army ant predation has a lower carrying capacity, i.e., lower competitive strength, simulating natural trade‐offs in energetic investments in competitive strength versus resistance to predation.

    For the simulation, for the low number of trap weeks for which no data on army ant raiding was available (due to destroyed traps), it was assumed that no raid occurred. Forty individuals of each species were placed at a time of 0 (corresponding to the time of setting up the pitfall traps, i.e., 20–26 July 2005) at each of the 196 positions. Populations then developed at each position following Lotka–Volterra equations over the time period of 19 wks and were killed during army ant raiding events at defined rates (pA and pB). For comparison simulations on three alternative raiding scenarios are shown, assuming (1) no army ant raids occurring; (2) army ant raids occurring at each position at the average raid rate observed over all positions (two raid events in 19 wks); and (3) army ant raids occurring at each position at the maximum raid rate observed on any of the monitored positions (nine raid events in 19 wk). Please note that for simulations (2) and (3) on each position the same number of raids was launched, but that the time when the raids occurred was randomly chosen. After the 19th simulation week (corresponding to the time of the last checking of pitfall traps, i.e., 30 November 2005–6 December 2005) for each type of simulation the total number of individuals of each species were counted, and the mean and standard deviation in the proportion of species A (and consequently of species B) were determined. In addition, the number of species still surviving at each position, i.e., species density, was calculated. A species was considered to have survived at a position if its population size was ≥1 (please note that simulations can also produce populations sizes of 0–1). As populations might not reach equilibrium states after 19 wks, we also examined if population parameters changed when running simulations over longer time periods. For this purpose, the army ant raiding matrix was simply tripled. We used the R environment (v. 2.11.1, http://www.r-project.org/) for all statistical analyses and simulations of prey populations.

    Results

    Observed patterns

    On average, each of the 196 position had a chance of 0.11 per wk (SD = 0.10) to become raided by a swarm of army ants (Fig. S1). The mean raid rate of D. wilverthi and D. molestus was 0.04 (SD = 0.07) and 0.06 (SD = 0.09), respectively. Army ant raid rates were highly variable among the monitored points—both at the landscape scale and at the scale of study sites (Fig. 1). In total, between zero and nine swarm raids per position were observed over the 19 survey weeks (mean raid rate: 0–0.50 raids/wk). The range in raid rates over the whole study area was similar when analyzed for each species separately, i.e., 0–0.42 raids/wk for D. molestus and 0–0.44 raids/wk in case of D. wilverthi.

    Significant differences in mean (Kruskal–Wallis test: χ² = 45.49, P < 0.01) and the variation in raid rates were found among study sites (Levene's test: F = 2.01, P = 0.03). Differences in mean and the variation in raid rates were even higher when raid rates of each species were analyzed separately (D. wilverthi: Kruskal–Wallis test: χ² = 69.32, P < 0.01, Levene's test: F = 4.40, P < 0.01; D. molestus: Kruskal–Wallis test: χ² = 70.59, P < 0.01, Levene's test: F = 2.41, P = 0.01). Within study sites the extent of variation in raid rates was on a similar scale to those observed over the whole study area, ranging from 0 to 0.50 raids/wk in the study site with the most divergent raid rates (‘IK’) and from 0 to 0.16 raids/wk in the study site showing most homogeneous raid rates (‘CO’, Fig. 1). At all study sites, it was repeatedly observed that swarm raids occurred at the same position in two or three consecutive weeks (up to five). For D. molestus, raids were detected in up to five consecutive weeks, for D. wilverthi in up to four consecutive weeks, underscoring the observation of Schöning et al. (2010) that predation by army ant species may be highly concentrated in space.

    The variation in total raid rates (both species combined) could not be well explained by any of the tested environmental variables. Based on the AIC as the evaluation criterion, the best model included the fragmentation index, relative humidity, and forest cover as predictor variables. The model explained only R2 = 6 percent of the variation in raid rates (F3,192 = 4.34, P < 0.05). For comparison, the global model, with all predictor variables included, also explained only R2 = 6 percent of the variation in raid rates. Residuals of ordinary linear models were spatially highly and positively autocorrelated (for a distance class of 0–1 km: Moran's I = 0.27, P < 0.05). Therefore, it was examined if after controlling for spatial autocorrelation the effect of environmental variables increased. Based on AIC model selection, the best spatial linear model was the null model, i.e., a model in which just the intercept remained. When analyses were conducted for each species separately, R2 = 16 percent of the variation in raid rates of D. wilverthi were found to be explained by a minimum adequate model (AIC = −493, F3,192 = 11.97, P < 0.01). When controlling for spatial non‐independence of data using spatial linear models (AIC = −526), the same variables with similar parameter estimates remained in the model. Raid rates of D. wilverthi increased with increasing forest cover, increasing relative humidity, and increases in the index of fragmentation. For D. molestus, the minimum adequate model included distance to the forest edge and forest cover as explanatory variables, and explained R2 = 23 percent of the variation in raid rates (AIC = −453, F2,193 = 28.33, P < 0.01). When controlling for the spatial non‐independence of data (AIC of SLM = −534), however, only forest cover remained in the final model. Raid rates of D. molestus were predicted to decrease with increasing forest cover.

    In the scenario in which the most extreme raid rate observed at any of the 196 positions was assumed to be true for all positions (nine raids within 19 wk), population simulations resulted in a low number of individuals and a clear dominance of species A (which is more resistant to army ant raiding, but less competitive) at all positions. At 187 positions one species survived, whereas at nine positions the species density was 2. Longer simulation runs (over 57 wk) showed that in equilibrium species density would correspond to 1 at all positions. In the scenario in which the average disturbance frequency observed at any of the 196 positions was assumed to be true for all positions (two raids within 19 wk), population simulations resulted in moderate total populations sizes and relatively even abundances of species (=high diversity), with all positions showing similar proportions of species. A high species density was found at all positions. For the simulation using the real raid patterns, the diversity and total population size estimates were similar to the last mentioned simulation scenario, however, the variation in the abundance of species A and B and consequently the relative proportion of species at the 196 positions was higher. Moreover, species density varied between 1 (few positions) and 2 (most positions) species among position. In longer simulation runs, variation in species density even strongly increased.

    Discussion

    We reconstructed the history of army ant raiding for 196 points distributed over 11 study sites in a tropical rain forest in western Kenya. This study extends findings of O'Donnell et al. (2007, 2009) and Schöning et al. (2010) by showing that high spatial variation in raid rates is not only found at the continental scale but also at the scale of landscapes and even at small spatial scales of single study sites. Within a tropical rain forest, areas frequently raided by army ants are found adjacent to areas remaining unraided throughout a time period of more than 19 wks (the longest time period we were able to observe). High spatial variation was found not only for individual species of army ants but also when raid rates of D. wilverthi and D. molestus were pooled, i.e., for the functional group of swarm raiding army ants. Simple models simulating the development of prey species populations under the real raid regimes suggest that the high variation in army ant raid rates may contribute to the maintenance of patchy species distribution patterns and high levels of diversity in leaf litter invertebrate communities.

    We found significant positive effects of humidity, forest cover, and small‐scale fragmentation on raid rates of D. wilverthi, whereas raid rates of D. molestus were negatively correlated with increases in forest cover. Thus, as already pointed out in an earlier study, the decline in raid rates of D. wilverthi with decreasing forest cover was partly compensated by an increase in raid rates of D. molestus (Peters et al. 2009). Nevertheless, for both species only a small percentage of the total variation in raid rates could be explained by environmental variables (R2 = 16 percent and R2 = 23 percent in case of D. wilverthi and D. molestus, respectively). When raid rates of both species were combined the explanatory power of variables related to human disturbance was even less, i.e., R2 = 6 percent. This suggests that high spatial variation in raid rates is a pattern true for natural ecosystems and not one solely produced by anthropogenic disturbance or environmental gradients.

    Simulating the development of Lotka–Volterra species, slightly differing in competitive strength versus resistance to swarm raids, resulted under the real raid regimes in a high species diversity (here measured as evenness) at larger spatial scales, high degree of patchiness, high variation in species density, and in a high variation in the number of individuals among the 196 positions, i.e., patterns which were repeatedly documented for real leaf litter invertebrate communities of tropical rain forests (Wilson 1958, Levings & Windsor 1982, Levings 1983, Silva & Brandao 2010, own unpubl. data). The importance of the activity of swarm raiding army ants for patch dynamics and the maintenance of species diversity was first identified by Franks and Bossert (1983). In their study, movement of army ant colonies and ecological succession of species communities after raids were considered the major processes maintaining species diversity, whereas spatial variation in raid rates was not explicitly considered by the authors.

    None of the simulations assuming homogeneous raid rates fully revealed these structural characteristics. Very high homogeneous raid rates and the absence of army ant raids resulted in low species diversity, as either species adapted to army ant raids or those of high competitive strength strongly dominated at all positions, respectively. Homogeneous army ant raiding at the average rate found in the Kakamega Forest also resulted in high species richness, reflecting predictions of the intermediate disturbance hypothesis (Krebs 2008). Under this raiding regime, however, distribution patterns of species were more homogeneous and there was much less variation in species density in equilibrium compared to the real army ant raiding regime, where high diversity levels were reached more strongly by species turnover from one position to the other, i.e., by high levels of β‐diversity.

    When we added additional species with intermediate p and K parameter values in simulations, results for the real raid regime and the raid regimes assuming homogeneous average and very high raid rates remained similar. In the scenario assuming a total absence of army ant raids, however, the diversity of species was higher compared to the simulations with only two species. At the end of simulations population sizes of these additional species were in between those of species A and B. Nevertheless, at all positions the composition of prey communities was the same, i.e., there was no patchiness in the distribution of species.

    How realistic are the species simulations used in this study? Due to several reasons we are confident that the patterns produced by simulations of the two Lotka–Volterra species might also naturally occur. First, a number of species are known to be behaviorally or morphologically adapted to raids of army ants. Species vary in their susceptibility to army ant raids, leaf litter invertebrate communities are known to be partly structured by interspecific competition, and to fluctuate after army ants raided an area (Franks 1982, Franks & Bossert 1983, Levings 1983, Gotwald 1995, Berghoff et al. 2003, Huang 2010). For example, Levings (1983) observed that ant species dominating at tuna baits, strongly dropped in activity after an army ant colony raided their nests and took 6–8 wks to recover. Other species in the sample area, which were less affected by army ants, increased in abundance at baits during the time period (Levings 1983). Second, similar patterns as generated by the species equations used in this study should also be produced for trade‐offs in other species traits, e.g., for trade‐offs between development time, size, reproductive capability, or survival probability, which are well known to exist naturally (e.g., see the extensive literature on r‐ versus k‐strategists, Krebs 2008). Future studies should evaluate the predictions of our simulations by experimentally excluding army ants from some study sites and by evaluating prey communities under different raid regimes.

    For simulating effects of army ant raiding on prey populations, we pooled raiding data of both species, i.e., we assumed that both species effect prey populations in the same way. This was done because both species forage in a highly similar way (i.e., are swarm raiding), worker morphology, reflecting the foraging ecology of army ants (Schöning et al. 2005), is highly similar, and we found a high overlap in the composition of prey communities on higher taxonomic level (on the level of orders and families). Therefore, we assume that parts of the species living in the leaf litter are affected by D. molestus and D. wilverthi in a highly similar way, justifying our approach of pooling the raid data of both species. We are confident, however, that detailed species‐level studies of African army ants' diets will reveal that at least some leaf litter invertebrate species are taken as prey by one but not by another army ant species, leading to different species‐specific probabilities to survive army ant raids. As forest sites strongly differ in the relative abundance of army ant species, this may lead to strong spatial variation in the survival rates of these species, a mechanism further increasing the spatial variation in the structure of prey communities.

    It is obvious and theoretically expectable given the large diversity of species—each responding individually to different environmental predictors—that leaf litter invertebrate communities are structured by a variety of factors. While temperature, productivity, and salt limitation appear to be of high importance particularly at large spatial scales (Kaspari et al. 2000, 2004, 2008), variation in moisture, sodium, phosphorus, and litter depth may also help to explain small‐scale variation in the diversity, composition, and density of species (Levings 1983, Levings & Windsor 1984, Kaspari et al. 2008, 2009). Strong spatial variation in predation by army ants should be considered as an additional factor shaping the small‐scale spatial variation in the abundance of invertebrate species in tropical rain forests.
